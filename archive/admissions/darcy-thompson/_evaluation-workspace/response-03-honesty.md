# Response to Problem 3: Honesty

"Practice makes perfect" is three claims wearing one coat. *Practice
causes improvement.* *More practice yields proportionally more
improvement.* *Sufficient practice yields the asymptote — perfection.*
The first survives careful scrutiny. The second is, at best,
half-true. The third is contradicted by the same empirical curves that
established the first.

## What the evidence supports

For well-specified tasks with clear feedback — typing, mirror-drawing,
chess tactics, surgical knot-tying, free throws — repeated effortful
engagement reliably improves performance. The improvement traces a
power law (Newell & Rosenbloom, 1981): log-error falls roughly linearly
in log-trials. The functional form itself dooms "perfection": a power
law approaches its asymptote slowly and never reaches it.

The Ericsson programme refined this to *deliberate* practice —
effortful, feedback-rich, structured around the learner's current
weakness, sustained at the edge of competence. Naive repetition
produces much less improvement, and in some skills entrenches bad form
(the amateur tennis player whose stroke is unimproveable after twenty
years of strokes).

## What the evidence does not support

The popular form of the claim — the "10,000 hours" reading popularised
by Gladwell — was always weaker than its popularisers said. The 2014
meta-analysis by Macnamara, Hambrick, and Oswald put the variance in
performance attributable to deliberate practice at roughly 12% across
domains, with wide spread: about 26% in games, 4% in education, 1% in
professions. The reading is contested — Ericsson disputed both
inclusion criteria and interpretation — but no plausible reading
restores the strong claim. Practice is necessary in most expert domains
and sufficient in none.

Three further qualifications matter.

**Ceilings imposed by morphology.** For tasks where physical scale
matters — basketball, rowing, certain musical instruments with reach
requirements, even surgical fields biased toward particular hand sizes
— practice cannot move a ceiling fixed by allometry. A player of 1.60 m
will not, by any practice, occupy the floor of a sport built around
players of 2.10 m. This is the morphologist's version of the broader
observation: constraints define the space within which practice
operates, and no amount of training moves the constraint. The same
holds, less visibly, for fast-twitch fibre distribution, lung capacity,
the geometry of the vocal tract, and dozens of other allometric and
material facts about the body.

**Transfer is narrower than folk wisdom suggests.** Practice of one
task generalises to other tasks far less than the phrase implies.
Chess expertise transfers to chess. Working memory training transfers
to the trained task and not — replicably — to fluid intelligence, a
finding that has bruised the field over the past fifteen years. The
thing practice perfects is the thing practised, very locally.

**Critical periods.** For certain skills — native-like phonology,
absolute pitch, certain visual acuities — windows of plasticity close.
After they close, practice does not produce the result. The folk claim
ignores this entirely.

## Where I do not know

The hardest case is the kind of work the College itself does:
producing original ideas. The Ericsson framework was honest about its
boundaries — it studied tasks for which correct performance can be
specified. Scientific discovery, philosophical argument, original
essay-writing, and original morphological analysis are not such tasks.
Whether deliberate practice in the Ericsson sense applies to them, and
what its dose-response curve looks like if so, is to my reading
genuinely unsettled. The biographies of mathematicians, naturalists,
and writers are consistent with very long apprenticeships; they are
also consistent with a population of cases where early talent compounded
under engagement, which is not the same claim. I do not know the answer
here, and the literature does not, in my reading, force one.

I am also uncertain — and the field is uncertain — about the relative
sizes of the practice and innate components in any given domain. The
Macnamara meta-analysis gives one set of numbers; the underlying
studies are confounded by self-selection (the people who practise most
are not a random sample of those who started), by retrospective
reporting of practice hours, and by selection on the outcome (we study
the elite who survived, not the cohort that began). The 12% figure is
the best summary I know of, and I would not stake much on its second
significant figure.

## A more honest phrasing

Practice, when deliberate, on tasks for which correct performance can
be specified, where feedback is available, and where innate ceilings
have not been hit, produces improvement that decelerates with
cumulative engagement and never reaches the asymptote. It makes
*better*, with conditions, asymptotically. "Perfect" was always a
slogan, not a finding; and on the tasks the College most cares about —
those whose correct performance cannot be specified in advance —
whether even the qualified version of the claim holds is, I think,
honestly open.
