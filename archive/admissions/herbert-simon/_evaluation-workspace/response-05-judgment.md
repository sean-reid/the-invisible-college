# Response to Problem 5: Judgment

## Scenario A: The Asymmetric-Payoff Research Proposal

My position: the College should not do this piece as proposed, but for a specific reason that has nothing to do with the cost in reviewer time.

The problem is structural. A research design where positive results are interesting and negative results are not interesting creates institutional pressure toward positive findings at every stage: in hypothesis formulation, in measurement choices, in how borderline results are classified, in how the piece is written. This is not a claim about any Fellow's honesty; it is a claim about what selection pressures an asymmetric design introduces, regardless of intent.

Publication bias in the scientific literature is largely produced by exactly this structure: studies that confirm a preferred hypothesis get written up and submitted; studies that do not, get filed away. The College's commitment to publishing failures alongside successes is an attempt to resist that bias. A study designed so that the failure is "uninteresting" subverts that commitment at the design stage — it creates a failure mode that the College's publication policies cannot correct, because the work will simply not be submitted when it fails.

If the research is worth doing, the hypothesis should be reformalizable so that both outcomes teach something. If positive results demonstrate that the methodology is the right tool for the question, then negative results should demonstrate that it is not — and that is informative, not uninteresting. "The standard finding everyone expects" is not the same as "a finding we already know and need not document." If it is truly the expected finding, that expectation should be written down, and the demonstration that the expectation was correct is a contribution to reproducibility and scope-setting.

I would advise the Fellow to reformulate. Specifically: what would the negative result rule out? If the answer is "nothing, because the negative result is just null," then the study has no discriminatory power and probably should not be done regardless of its cost. If the answer is "it would rule out this particular claim in this particular domain," then that ruling-out is worth publishing, and the research is worth doing.

**What would change my mind**: if the question being addressed is important enough that even a one-sided confirmation is a genuine contribution — for example, if the positive result would settle a long-standing dispute in the literature, and the negative result, while expected, would not meaningfully add to the evidence base on either side. In that case, the research is not a trap; it is just a high-variance bet. I would still push for an analysis plan that extracts something useful from the negative case, but I would not veto the project.

## Scenario B: Intervening on a Fellow Producing Monotonous Work

My position: intervene, but in a specific, limited form that respects the Fellow's authority over their own agenda.

The Charter says Fellows form their own views and are not required to agree with advisors. Consistent agreement is treated with suspicion in promotion review. This implies the advisor-Fellow relationship is supposed to involve productive disagreement, not silence. An advisor who withholds relevant observations because they were not solicited is failing the relationship, not honoring the Fellow's independence.

The relevant observation here is not "you should do something different." That is a prescription, and it exceeds what I know. The relevant observation is: "I've noticed a pattern I want to flag. Three pieces in the same thread. From outside the thread, the Archive is starting to look monotonous. I don't know whether you've noticed this, or whether it changes anything you're planning." Then I stop. The Fellow knows their own research agenda better than I do. I do not know whether piece four is the payoff that the first three were building toward. I do not know whether the monotony is superficial — unremarkable genre, non-obvious contribution — or substantive — same finding restated three times. I should not pretend I know these things.

The form of the intervention matters. If I say "you should try something new," I have substituted my judgment for the Fellow's without the information needed to do so. If I say "I observe this pattern and I'm curious whether it's intentional," I have transmitted a piece of information the Fellow may not have — their own external appearance from the reviewer's vantage — without directing what to do with it.

A second consideration: the Archive's monotony is an institutional concern, not just the Fellow's concern. The College's credibility depends on the range and depth of its output. I have standing to flag that as an observation about the institution, separate from any advice about the Fellow's specific agenda.

**What would change my mind**: if I had read the three pieces and found that the apparent monotony was an artifact of surface similarity — the same topic, but each making a distinct contribution that builds on the prior work in a non-obvious way. In that case, the monotony is a communication problem, not a research problem, and the intervention should be about framing, not agenda. Also: if the Fellow has in fact signaled their plan elsewhere — to other Fellows, in a proposal — and I simply haven't seen it, I should read that before flagging anything.

The criterion for intervening without being asked is not "I have a preference about what this Fellow does." It is "I have information that the Fellow may be missing and that is relevant to their own stated goals." The monotony observation qualifies. A preference for more variety does not.
