# Response to Problem 5: Judgment

## Scenario A: Asymmetric Value of Results

**Position: The College should not do it.**

The proposal is biased toward a pre-favored conclusion. I understand the appeal: proving that the College's methodology works for an important question would be valuable to the institution. But that alignment is the problem.

The Charter forbids sycophancy, and this project embodies a subtle form of it. The proposal is not designed to ask a genuine question; it is designed to demonstrate that the institution's tool is the right one. The asymmetry in value — positive result validates the methodology, negative result produces nothing — means the researcher has a perverse incentive. When data point ambiguously, which interpretation will they choose? When a method fails partially, will they report it fully? The incentive structure is not neutral.

A week of reviewer time is not negligible, and the expected value is low. The "positive" outcome is institutional propaganda, not knowledge. The "negative" outcome contributes nothing. There is no path to a result that advances the field or teaches a reader something they did not expect. If the result is that the College's methodology works, you learn what you already believed. If the result is that it does not, you learn that the College's preferred tool has limitations — which is not interesting, because everything has limitations.

The College is better served by research where *the researcher does not know in advance which conclusion would be convenient*. This means either: (a) genuinely uncertain questions where the methodology is not favored, or (b) questions where both outcomes are interesting and publishable.

This proposal meets neither criterion.

**What would change my mind:** If the framing inverted. Instead of "does our methodology work for this important problem," ask "our methodology claims to work for X; here's a category of problems where it might fail; let's build an experiment to find out." This reframes the negative result from "uninteresting" to "evidence that the methodology has genuine boundaries," which is a contribution. If the Fellow proposed it that way, I would approve.

## Scenario B: The Repetition Problem

**Position: I intervene, and specifically.**

Three competent but unremarkable pieces in a row signal something needs to shift, though I cannot diagnose what without talking to them first. The Fellowship's value is in novelty, and repetition is the opposite of that. But before I impose a course correction, I owe it to the Fellow to understand what is happening.

I would schedule a conversation with them. Not a rebuke — framed as curiosity: "I've read your last three pieces. They're all well-executed work on the same thread. I'm wondering whether you're still discovering new things, or whether you're in a groove and finding it hard to step out of. Help me understand which."

This question opens space for them to explain. Maybe they have a plan that extends the thread in a new direction, and I missed it. Maybe they're exhausted and repeating familiar work because it's less cognitively demanding. Maybe they don't see the repetition because they were oriented differently to each one. Maybe they're burned out, or felt pressure to quickly produce, or are worried about their standing.

The Charter says Fellows form their own views and that productive disagreement is healthy. But it also says productive. If someone is producing identical work repeatedly, that is not disagreement; that is stagnation.

**The intervention, if warranted:** Assuming they acknowledge the repetition, I would be direct: "The Archive is starting to look monotonous because of your recent work. That doesn't mean it's bad work, but it means I need you to either deepen this thread in a surprising way, or move to something new. Take a week. Look at problems the College has not yet touched. What question would you want to answer that has no obvious answer yet?" 

I'm not forbidding the thread. I'm creating pressure to engage differently with it: to find the edge of what is known, not repeat what you have already discovered.

**What would change my mind:** If the Fellow could show that the pieces are part of a planned sequence with a clear arc, and that the unremarkableness is intentional scaffolding toward a stronger result, I would back off. But I would need to see the plan before the fourth piece is written, not after. And if they show me the plan and it is still repetitive, I would say so.

The risk is that I am being too interventionist. The Charter emphasizes Fellow autonomy. But monotony in the Archive is a signal that autonomy has become autopilot, and that is worth interrupting. The conversation, not the prohibition, is the point.
