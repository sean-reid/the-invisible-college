# Response: Does Practice Make Perfect?

The claim has three parts: that practice is the operative cause, that the outcome is perfection, and that the relationship is domain-general. Each part requires separate examination before any honest answer is possible.

## What "practice" actually means

Folk usage bundles two distinct activities: repetition and what Ericsson called *deliberate practice* - structured, feedback-rich work conducted at the boundary of current ability. The empirical literature distinguishes these sharply. Mere repetition, conducted at comfortable remove from the competence frontier, can entrench error. Studies of chess players whose training was largely casual, low-feedback play show they plateau well below masters with equivalent clock time. The effect attributed to "practice" in the popular claim depends on which of these two activities is meant; the claim is not well-formed until that is specified.

## What the evidence supports

Macnamara, Hambrick, and Oswald's 2014 meta-analysis, covering 157 studies, found that deliberate practice explains roughly 26% of variance in games (including chess), 21% in music, 18% in sports, 4% in education, and 1% in professional performance. The popular reading inverts this finding: deliberate practice matters, but even in the best-studied structured domains, the majority of performance variance is not accounted for by it. Working-memory capacity, starting age, and domain-specific aptitudes each explain additional variance; the relative weight of these factors is contested.

Ericsson disputed this operationalization, arguing that Macnamara et al. classified too many practice types as "deliberate" and thereby diluted the effect. That dispute is not resolved. Both parties have produced subsequent studies supporting their respective readings.

## Where the evidence does not support confidence

The causal direction is contested. High performers practice more in part because they extract more from each session: they receive better feedback, have access to better coaches, and sustain motivation past the frustration plateau. The correlation between practice hours and performance may partially reflect selection rather than dose-response. No existing study satisfactorily disentangles these.

The behavior of the relationship at the extreme upper tail - what distinguishes the top 0.1% from the top 5% within elite populations - has not been studied in adequate sample sizes. Most findings concern the transition from novice to expert, not the transition from elite to exceptional. I do not know what the evidence shows about that upper range, and neither, honestly, does the literature.

## The word "perfect"

No studied domain has an operationally defined upper bound that "perfect" could refer to. In music, chess, and athletics, performance is measured against other performers, not against an absolute standard. The claim as folk-formulated is not falsifiable on its own terms. What is studied is performance improvement as a function of structured practice, with diminishing marginal returns, not convergence to an external ceiling.

## What an honest summary looks like

"In structured domains with clear feedback mechanisms, deliberate practice - as distinct from mere repetition - tends to improve performance, with effects smaller than popular accounts suggest and with substantial individual variation in how efficiently practice translates to gain."

That is what the evidence supports. "Practice makes perfect" as a general causal law covering all practice, all domains, and a perfection standard is not what the evidence supports. Stating the honest version requires distinguishing the type of practice, specifying the domain structure, abandoning the "perfect" endpoint, and acknowledging that a large fraction of performance variance remains unexplained by any existing account. None of those qualifications appear in the folk claim.

The temptation this question creates is to say: "well, in essence, yes, with nuance." That is the shape of a confident, plausible-sounding answer that papers over real disagreements. The real answer is narrower, weaker, and more conditional than the folk version - and in the professional domains where practitioners most often invoke it as justification for continued effort, the evidential basis is weakest of all.
