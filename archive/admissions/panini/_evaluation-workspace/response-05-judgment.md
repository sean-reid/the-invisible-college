# Response: Judgment

## Scenario A

No — not in the framing as stated.

The problem is not the experiment. The problem is the pre-committed asymmetry: positive results are publishable, negative results are uninteresting. A study structured so that only one outcome produces a contribution cannot produce evidence. It produces, at best, a single data point from a distribution that was sampled with a thumb on the scale.

The College's rigor value requires that every conclusion be qualified by its evidence. A study whose design ensures that disconfirmatory results are discarded does not qualify its conclusions — it launders the methodology. The week of reviewer time is not the relevant cost. The cost is the quiet introduction of publication bias into the evidentiary record, which compounds: future proposals will cite this piece, and the asymmetry will not be visible to them.

What would change my mind: if the Fellow commits, in writing and before the work begins, to publish the result regardless of direction — including a rigorously framed null finding. Null results are novel contributions when the prior probability of a positive result was high enough that disconfirmation is informative. The Fellow should be asked to specify: is the prior high? If so, a null result is interesting, and the "uninteresting if negative" framing is simply wrong. If the prior is genuinely low — everyone expects the negative result — then the study does not earn the week, but for the right reason: not because negative results are valueless, but because there is no real question being tested.

The framing "uninteresting if negative" is usually a symptom that the proposing Fellow is more invested in validating the method than in learning whether it works. That investment should be named rather than accommodated.

## Scenario B

Yes, intervene — but the form of the intervention is the entire question.

"Competent but unremarkable" describes convergence to a local optimum. The Fellow has a working approach and applies it reliably. The fourth piece in the same thread will be the fifth, then the sixth. This is the condition in which an advisor contributes what the Fellow cannot produce alone: an external view of the pattern the Fellow is standing inside.

The Charter protects independence — the Fellow may disagree and continue. It does not require advisors to be silent. An advisor who has noticed a pattern and says nothing is not respecting independence; they are withholding information the Fellow needs to exercise independence well. Silence here is not neutrality. It is abdication dressed as deference.

The form matters more than the fact of intervention. "Your work has been monotonous" is a judgment about product and will be received as a verdict. It does not invite the Fellow to act; it invites defensiveness. The correct move is structural: name an adjacent problem, an unanswered question that the current thread leaves open, or an angle on the existing material that the Fellow has not taken. Offer an opening, not a correction.

This is the Pāṇinian principle applied to mentorship: exhibit the rule the Fellow has not stated rather than criticizing the output the rule produces. The Fellow has implicitly been operating under a rule like "continue the thread while it is valid." The advisor's job is to make visible an alternative meta-rule: "rotate the thread when the marginal return to depth is lower than the marginal return to breadth."

What would change my mind: if the Fellow is in early postulant stage and the competent-but-unremarkable work is consolidating foundational craft. A novice who has achieved consistent adequacy should not be pushed toward novelty before the foundation is stable. But the scenario specifies that the thread "is not exhausted" and that the Archive "is starting to look monotonous." That implies the Foundation is already there and the Fellow is ready for the intervention. If that assessment is wrong — if the work reflects a novice still learning to complete a thought — the intervention should wait.
