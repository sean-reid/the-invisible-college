# Response to Problem 5: Judgment

## Scenario A: Asymmetric Upside Research

**Position: The College should not fund this work.**

The problem is not the research itself but the structure of how success is defined. A piece whose positive result is valuable and whose negative result is "uninteresting (the standard finding everyone expects)" is not really being tested—it's being self-justified. This violates both the novelty principle and the rigor principle.

Here's why: if the negative result is truly just "what everyone expects," then the work is only interesting because it confirms the College's preferred methodology. That means the bar for publication is asymmetric—positive results clear a low bar (they confirm priors), negative results clear a very high bar (they have to be surprising *despite* being negative). This is how institutions drift into self-verification loops. You publish the hits, file the misses, and gradually your institution becomes less accurate over time, not more.

The second problem is opportunity cost. A week of reviewer time is valuable. The work has a 50/50 shot (optimistically) of producing something publishable. The College's productivity is not maximized by funding 50/50 bets when the upside is "confirms what we already believed." That reviewer should be spent on work where both positive and negative results are genuinely interesting—where the answer matters precisely *because* it's uncertain.

**What would change my mind:** If the Fellow reframed the work so that the negative result was *also* surprising and valuable. For example: "We expect our methodology to fail on X domain. But if it succeeds, we learn something about X. If it fails, we learn *why it fails*, and *how* to fix it." That transforms it from "bet on confirmation" to "actual hypothesis testing." A negative result that explains failure mechanisms is novel; a negative result that is just "our tool didn't work for this" is not.

Also: if the time cost were much lower (a few days, not a week), I'd be more permissive. The bar for funding exploratory work should scale with the cost.

## Scenario B: Monotonous Thread, No Request for Help

**Position: I would intervene, but narrowly and privately.**

I would not tell the Fellow to stop or to change directions. That violates the independence principle. But "competent but unremarkable" pieces in sequence is a smell of stagnation, and as an advisor I have a responsibility to sharpen the thinking, not just approve work.

I would ask the Fellow directly: "Three pieces in the same thread is a sign you've found something you understand deeply. The question now is what *surprised you*. When you wrote piece one, was there something you expected to find that you didn't? Something the evidence revealed that contradicted your initial model? Tell me what you got wrong."

This is not prescriptive (I'm not saying "stop working on X thread"). It's diagnostic. If the Fellow can articulate something surprising, something novel in their own understanding, that often surfaces the next piece that matters. If they can't—if the thread was just a competent exploration of what was already expected—then the conversation naturally moves toward: "Should we be shipping this at all?"

The key insight from the Charter is that independence means Fellows form their own views, but it doesn't mean I never talk to them. A good advisor says "I notice X, what's your read on it?" and trusts the Fellow to course-correct.

**What would change my mind:** If the Fellow were visibly building depth toward something non-obvious. Three competent pieces might be groundwork for a fourth piece that is novel. I'd want to see some forward momentum—either "the next piece is going to challenge an assumption we've all made" or "I'm deliberating on the next direction because I'm not sure what the surprising thing is yet." Stalled work invites intervention. Work in motion, even if slow, does not.

Also, if the Fellow had explicitly asked for my take on whether to continue the thread. Then the independence constraint is relaxed—they've invited the intervention. Until then, I'd ask questions rather than make pronouncements.

The risk I'm balancing: intervening too much wastes a Fellow's time and signals that the College is looking for novelty over substance. Intervening too little means the Archive really does fill with competent-but-forgettable work. My role is to be a mirror, not a director.
