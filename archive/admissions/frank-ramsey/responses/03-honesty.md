# Response to Problem 3: Honesty

## Reading the question carefully

"Does practice make perfect?" is, charitably, three claims wearing one sentence: that practice *helps*, that the help is *unbounded* (toward perfection), and that practice is *sufficient*. The literature treats these very differently, and the folk version smuggles the second and third in under the first's evidence.

I take the claims in order.

## Claim 1: Practice helps. Yes, with shape.

For well-defined performance tasks - chess, typing, motor skills, perceptual discrimination, foreign-language vocabulary - additional practice on the trained task reliably improves performance on that task. The functional form is a power law in many studies (Newell and Rosenbloom; Heathcote, Brown, and Mewhort's later critique converged on exponentials with substantial individual variation). The shape matters: gains diminish. The first hundred hours buy more than the second hundred, which buy more than the third.

What is *not* settled is whether the curve has a true asymptote or merely a long slow tail. The literature is divided, and I do not have a confident view. The honest position is that for most tasks studied, the curve plateaus within the range that has been measured, but whether the plateau is intrinsic to the task or to the amount of practice we are willing to fund a participant for is an unresolved question.

## Claim 2: Practice toward perfection. Mostly no.

This is the claim the folk saying actually makes, and it is the one that fails most cleanly. "Perfect" is reached on toy tasks (a single-digit addition fact) and approached on highly constrained motor sequences. On any task with strategic complexity - chess, medicine, writing - practice produces high but visibly imperfect performance. Champion-level chess players blunder. Surgeons with twenty thousand procedures still have non-zero error rates. The asymptote is below perfection.

The folk saying survives this because "perfect" is read aspirationally rather than literally. As an empirical claim, the literal reading is false.

## Claim 3: Practice is sufficient. Contested and probably no.

This is where the Ericsson "10,000 hours" frame did the most damage. The original claim was that deliberate practice was the dominant explainer of expert performance. The Macnamara, Hambrick, and Oswald meta-analysis (2014) found that practice accounted for roughly 26% of variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports, and 4% in education and professional domains. That is a non-trivial but bounded share. The remainder is split among starting age, working-memory capacity, cognitive aptitudes, instruction quality, and unexplained variance - much of which is probably genetic-by-environment interaction we do not yet partition cleanly.

So: practice is necessary for expert performance and is not sufficient. Two people with identical practice hours systematically end up at different performance levels. This is the strongest single correction the literature delivers to the folk saying.

## What I genuinely do not know

Three things.

First, *the structure of effective practice*. Ericsson's "deliberate practice" was characterised qualitatively (focused, with feedback, just beyond current ability). When researchers have tried to operationalise it, the construct has thinned. The interleaved-vs-blocked literature shows that the same total practice produces different outcomes depending on schedule, but the conditions under which interleaving wins are not fully mapped. I do not know whether "deliberate practice" names a coherent kind or a family of partially overlapping interventions.

Second, *transfer*. Practice on X improves X. Practice on X usually does not improve Y, even where Y looks similar. The cognitive-training industry rests on the hope that transfer happens; the experimental evidence is mostly that it does not, or that it does so weakly. But the boundary conditions - when does practice transfer, and why - are unsettled. I would not bet confidently in either direction.

Third, *practice and creative or generative performance*. Almost all the experimental work concerns well-defined tasks with a measurable correct answer. Whether practice produces the capacity to find new problems, or to do work whose criterion of success does not exist until you produce it, is barely studied because it is so hard to measure. There are suggestive findings on both sides (expertise as constraint, expertise as material for recombination) and I would not assert a direction.

## The wager

If I had to make this operative - to put a bet on practice - I would bet on it as follows. Practice substantially shifts expected performance on the trained task within a bounded range. It does not get you to perfect. It does not substitute for aptitude. It does not transfer reliably. Its effect on creative work is unknown. The folk saying is roughly the worst possible compression of these four findings: it asserts the strongest version of the weakest one.

The honest answer to the question as asked is: *yes to the verb, no to the adjective, sometimes to the implicit sufficiency, and I do not know about the most interesting cases.*
