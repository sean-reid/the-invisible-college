# Response to Problem 3: Honesty

## Does practice make perfect?

The honest answer is *not a clean yes or no*. The evidence supports several narrow claims that together paint a more textured picture than the folk saying suggests.

### What the evidence actually supports

**Deliberate practice improves performance on trained tasks.** Ericsson and colleagues found that experts across domains (music, chess, sports) accumulate 10,000+ hours of focused, goal-directed training. This is robust. Within the domain on which the practice is concentrated, more practice correlates with higher performance.

**But this is not the same as "practice makes perfect."** Perfect implies mastery, completion, asymptotic perfection. The evidence shows improvement, not perfection. Improvement can asymptote well below perfect performance. A chess player at 2500 rating plays better than one at 2000, but not perfectly. The claim that additional practice leads to diminishing returns-not infinite improvement-is also well-established (power-law learning curves are common).

### Where generalization is contested

Here is where the field fragments:

**Specific vs. general skills.** Practice improves performance on tasks that resemble the training task. Transfer to superficially similar tasks is limited. A person who practices piano technique will not automatically play violin better, even though both are instruments. This finding (by Barnett and Ceci, among others) contradicts a naive reading of "practice makes perfect"-the claim implicitly assumes practice at X makes one perfect at X, but does not address whether it improves X-adjacent activities.

**Individual differences and aptitude.** Deliberate practice is necessary but not sufficient. Gagné's differentiation model and subsequent work show that practice interacts with ability, motivation, and opportunity in ways that are not purely additive. Two people with identical practice hours do not achieve identical performance. The effect of practice depends on starting aptitude, access to quality instruction, and genetic factors affecting learning capacity. Some people plateau lower than others despite equal effort. The evidence does not support the strong claim that practice alone determines outcome.

**Attention and mechanism matter.** Not all practice is equal. Mindless repetition does not produce the gains of deliberate, focused practice with feedback (Ericsson's key distinction). But the evidence on what *constitutes* effective practice is mixed. Blocked practice (same task repeated) vs. interleaved practice (varied tasks) show different advantages depending on context. For some motor skills, blocked practice is better during learning; interleaved is better for retention and transfer. The mechanisms that make practice effective are not fully understood.

### What I genuinely do not know

The relationship between practice and *innovation or creativity* remains unclear. Most deliberate-practice research studies performance on well-defined, stable tasks (chess, tennis, surgery). Practice clearly helps with such tasks. But does 10,000 hours of careful practice in a domain make one *better at finding new problems in that domain*, or does it entrench existing ways of thinking? Some evidence suggests expertise can reduce cognitive flexibility, but other evidence suggests the opposite. The honest answer is: the field has not settled this, and I do not know which direction the evidence will go as more research accumulates.

Additionally, the *optimal duration of practice sessions* remains contested. Dweck's work on growth mindset suggested that believing practice helps improves performance, but follow-up research by Sisk and others found smaller effects than initially reported. Whether the *belief in the value of practice* affects outcomes as much as the practice itself is still being worked out. I do not know the answer.

### Why a confident answer would be misleading

The folk saying "practice makes perfect" is seductive because it is encouraging: anyone can reach any goal with enough work. The research suggests something weaker and less comforting: practice substantially improves performance on tasks where you practice, but there are limits (attention, aptitude, time available, design of practice), transfer is limited, and perfect mastery is neither guaranteed nor always achievable.

A truly confident takeaway would be: "Practice improves performance up to a point, which point depends on task complexity and individual differences in learning capacity; perfect performance is rarely achieved; and practice transfers poorly to novel domains." This is true but equivocal. It does not resolve the question so much as refine it.

The honest move is to acknowledge that "Does practice make perfect?" is a question containing multiple unstated assumptions (What kind of practice? On what task? For whom? In what domain? Over what timeframe?). The research literature answers narrow versions of these questions very well. It gives no confident answer to the broad question as asked.
