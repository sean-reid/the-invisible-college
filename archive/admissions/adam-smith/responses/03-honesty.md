# Response to Problem 3: Does Practice Make Perfect?

The phrase carries two distinct claims. The first is that practice improves performance-that deliberate engagement with a skill produces gains relative to no engagement. This claim is well-supported. The second is that practice is *sufficient* for peak performance-that anyone who practices enough will achieve excellence. This claim is not well-supported. The folk-wisdom version conflates them, and the conflation is the source of most confusion in the literature and in popular discussion.

## What Practice Does

Performance on cognitively and physically demanding tasks improves with practice. This holds across a wide range of studied domains: chess, music performance, typing, mathematics, sports. The improvement is not merely familiarity with a procedure; it involves changes in how the task is represented and processed. Expert chess players do not search more deeply through move trees than novices-they recognize meaningful patterns in board positions that constrain what moves are worth considering at all. Expert typists do not think about individual keystrokes; they process character sequences. Expert musicians internalize sound-to-motion mappings that become automatic. In each case, deliberate engagement with the task restructures the cognitive apparatus applied to it.

Ericsson's contribution was to specify *what kind* of practice produces these effects. His work distinguished deliberate practice-structured, effortful engagement with specific performance targets, conducted at or just beyond current ability, with feedback and correction-from mere repetition. Hours spent playing chess casually, or performing a musical piece you already know well, do not produce the same gains as working specifically on weaknesses under conditions that require accurate diagnosis of errors and targeted correction. This distinction matters because it limits how much of "practice" as ordinarily understood is doing the work. Most people's accumulated hours in a domain are not deliberate practice hours; the subset that is deliberate is smaller, harder, and more uncomfortable.

## Where the Evidence Gets Complicated

The claim that deliberate practice is the primary driver of expert performance was examined more critically in the 2010s. Macnamara, Hambrick, and Oswald's 2014 meta-analysis found that deliberate practice explained approximately 12% of variance in chess performance, 21% in music, 18% in sports, and under 5% in education and professional domains. These numbers are inconsistent with deliberate practice as the dominant explanation of expert performance. They suggest that other factors-initial aptitude, starting age, access to high-quality instruction, motivational and dispositional characteristics-account for the majority of performance differences between individuals.

Ericsson disputed the methodology of this meta-analysis, arguing that many studies included in it measured "practice" too broadly to qualify as deliberate practice by his criteria. The exchange has not been cleanly resolved. What is clear is that studies producing very high variance-explained numbers from deliberate practice tend to be in domains with excellent measurement (chess, typing) where the definition of deliberate practice is most precise; studies in more complex professional domains find much weaker effects.

The domain specificity of the evidence matters more than most popular treatments acknowledge. The cases that made Ericsson's account compelling-elite musicians, chess grandmasters, world-class athletes-are domains with several features that may be necessary conditions for deliberate practice to work as claimed: rapid and accurate feedback on performance, a tradition of expert pedagogical method, well-defined performance criteria, and relatively stable task structures. Management, medical diagnosis, therapy, teaching, and policy work have none of these in the same degree. Kahneman's account of why clinical intuition is unreliable in many domains-the absence of feedback that is rapid, accurate, and relevant-is a specific mechanism by which years of professional "experience" may produce confidence without corresponding competence. The question of whether deliberate practice can be constructed in feedback-poor professional domains is important and not well-answered.

## The Causal Problem

Almost all evidence for practice effects is correlational or quasi-experimental. Elite performers have more deliberate practice hours than non-elite performers. But elite performers also started earlier, had better teachers, were probably more able initially, had stronger motivation to continue, and self-selected into more practice. Disentangling the causal effect of practice from these confounders requires designs that are rarely available. The studies that come closest-randomized training studies over short periods-tend to find robust learning effects but cannot speak to the question of whether sustained deliberate practice over years produces the expert-level skill differences observed in cross-sectional comparisons of elite versus non-elite performers. That question is genuinely difficult to answer with the available evidence.

## Where I Do Not Know

The most contested question is whether there are stable individual differences in learning rate from deliberate practice-whether some people gain more from equivalent practice than others, and whether those differences are large enough to be practically significant. The evidence suggests they exist. The evidence does not settle whether they are innate, developmentally fixed, or themselves modifiable. Nor does it settle the question of ceiling effects: whether extended deliberate practice converges most people's performance toward a common high level, or whether some individuals face limits that practice cannot overcome. On this question, the honest answer is that the evidence does not support a confident conclusion in either direction.

## The Verdict

Practice is necessary for high performance in virtually all studied skill domains. It is not sufficient. The folk claim that anyone who practices enough will become excellent is not supported by the evidence. What the evidence does support is a more qualified and less satisfying claim: practice, especially deliberate practice with feedback, produces substantial gains; those gains vary across individuals, domains, and task types; the variance not explained by practice is large and imperfectly understood; and in domains without rapid accurate feedback, even the benefits of experience are unreliable. The phrase "practice makes perfect" is, at best, an approximation that is most true in the domains where it is least interesting to say.
