# Response to Problem 5: Judgment

## Scenario A: Research with Asymmetric Value

**My position:** The College should do it, but with explicit acknowledgment of the asymmetry, and only if the experimental design actually controls confounds rigorously.

**Why:** The Charter prizes independence and intellectual honesty. A piece whose result would be uninteresting if negative is a piece the institution is economically incentivized to find positive. That is precisely when rigor matters most. If we run the study only when we expect a favorable result, we are selecting for confirmation rather than for truth.

But this cuts both ways. Uninteresting negative results are often genuinely uninteresting - they confirm what the field already knows. The asymmetry is real. However, a well-designed negative result (one that rules out plausible alternatives, or that shows the methodology fails where it was expected to work) *is* interesting. The distinction between "the hypothesis is false" and "the hypothesis-test is uninformative" is not always clear ex ante.

The harder question: if we take on this project, are we committing to publishing the negative result? The Charter's honesty requirement says yes. A Fellow who runs the study and finds no effect must publish that finding with the same rigor as a positive one. If the commitment is real, I support the work. If there is quiet expectation that a negative result "just doesn't make the blog," then I recommend declining.

The time investment is also relevant. A week of review work is substantial. If this review could instead be allocated to a Fellow whose work is novel regardless of outcome, that is a real cost. I would weigh this against the institutional value of demonstrating that the preferred methodology is reliable even when put to an asymmetric test.

**What would change my mind:** 
- Evidence that the study design is actually flawed (that negative results would not be informative). A well-designed negative result has value; a study that cannot distinguish "the hypothesis is false" from "the test is broken" does not.
- Demonstration that review capacity is genuinely constrained elsewhere. If this consumes a reviewer who would otherwise enable higher-novelty work, the cost changes.
- Evidence that the proposed Fellow has a pattern of confirmation bias in prior work. Independence requires that the researcher actually remains independent in the face of an asymmetric outcome.

## Scenario B: Intervening on Repetitive Work

**My position:** I intervene, but carefully and conversationally, not by directive.

**Why:** The Charter values novelty and intellectual health. Three competent but unremarkable pieces in a row is a signal worth attending to, even absent formal grounds for objection. A Fellow in that position is at risk of slipping into a rut - of pursuing the cognitively lowest-friction continuation rather than the most interesting next question. This is a form of intellectual drift that is hard to see from inside the work.

However, "the Archive is looking monotonous" is not the Fellow's obligation. The Fellow's obligation is intellectual honesty within their domain. If the thread genuinely contains further interesting work, I have no Charter basis to object. My intervention should not sound like "you have to move on"; it should sound like "I notice a pattern. What are you noticing?"

The intervention I would make is *conversational*, not directional. I would say something like: "I've read your last three pieces and they are competent. But I notice they all stay within the same perimeter of the problem. Before you write the fourth, tell me: what makes the fourth piece necessary? What would be missing from the Archive if you didn't write it? And separately: what did you have to *not* explore to stay within this thread?" 

Often, this produces clarity. The Fellow has noticed the pattern too, but was not sure whether it mattered. Sometimes it produces a genuine reframing: "Actually, what I'm really trying to get at is X, and I've been approaching it from the wrong angle." Sometimes it produces a decision to move on. Sometimes it produces a strong defense of why the fourth piece is necessary - which, if honest, I should respect.

The key is that I am not substituting my judgment for theirs. I am making their own implicit judgments explicit, creating the conditions for independence rather than overriding it.

**What would change my mind:**
- If the Fellow has explicitly stated what the fourth piece contributes and I find the answer unconvincing, I would then be more directive. At that point, silence looks like tacit approval for work that both of us know is marginal.
- If this is a Fellow whose independence I have reason to doubt (who tends to pursue what they think others want rather than what they think is true), I might intervene more directly earlier. Protecting intellectual independence sometimes requires intervention.
- If the fourth piece would materially damage the Archive's standing (if, for instance, the three previous pieces had cumulative errors that a fourth would compound), that is a Charter-level concern and grounds for objection.

**The deeper principle:** The Charter says the Fellow "is not required to agree with their advisors." This means advisors must earn agreement through the quality of their judgment, not through institutional authority. Intervening because "the Archive looks monotonous" is not a Charter-level objection; it is an aesthetic preference. My job is to help the Fellow think clearly about their own work, not to impose my taste on their output.
