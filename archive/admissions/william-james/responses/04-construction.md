# Response to Problem 4: Construction

## Option B: A Formal Claim

**Claim:** Skill acquisition in high-feedback domains exhibits a characteristic attentional structure: early learning is attention-intensive and cognitively effortful; subsequent learning reallocates that attention from task execution to task variation and error correction; plateau occurs when attention budget becomes the binding constraint.

## The argument

Start with a concrete case. A novice pianist playing a simple melody allocates most of her attention to motor execution: finger placement, timing, hand position. Her attention is *on the executing*. The piece sounds halting because attention is fully consumed by the mechanism.

As she practices, the motor execution becomes automated - transferred from controlled processing to automatic processing in Shiffrin and Schneider's terminology. This frees attentional capacity. But here is the critical observation: she does not simply allocate that freed attention to enjoying the music or thinking about something else. In deliberate practice, she reallocates it *within the performance domain*. Now she can attend to dynamics, phrasing, the balance between hands, the emotional arc. Her attention is *on the nuance*.

This reallocation is real work. It requires conscious effort. It requires building new habits of attention - learning to notice what previously went unnoticed. A person who has not practiced deliberately does not naturally attend to these distinctions; they simply do not see them as figure against ground.

Crucially, this reallocation has limits. The total attention budget is finite. At some point, the distinction between "essential" and "valuable to attend to" becomes strategic. A professional pianist cannot attend to every possible refinement simultaneously; she must allocate strategically across domains of variation (dynamics, temporal nuance, color, phrase structure). The constraint becomes: which subset of variations should consume my limited attention?

This explains several otherwise-puzzling features of skill plateaus:

1. **Why experience sometimes plateaus without reaching objective mastery:** The plateau occurs not when the task is fully learned, but when the attentional allocation problem becomes unsolvable. There are always refinements available; they simply cannot all be attended to simultaneously.

2. **Why domain experience sometimes fails to transfer:** Transfer requires that the attentional structures built in one domain map onto the new domain. They often do not. A chess master's attentional habits (board configuration recognition, threat perception) do not help with violin; the attention-intensive work must begin again.

3. **Why reflective practice works when mindless repetition does not:** Reflection is an attention-reallocation tool. It forces explicit notice of what automatic execution had hidden. Repetition without reflection simply automates the current execution; it frees no attention for variation.

4. **Why individual differences in learning rate persist despite equal hours:** Differences in prior attentional infrastructure matter. A person whose schooling habituated them to attending to error persists longer in reallocation phases. A person whose prior experience built domain-relevant attentional patterns (e.g., musical training for a conductor) has structural advantages.

## The strongest objection

The objection is that I have simply renamed the problem rather than explained it. To say "plateau occurs when attention becomes the binding constraint" can sound like saying "performance improves until it stops improving." I have substituted attention-allocation language for performance language without advancing explanation.

This objection has force. To answer it, the claim must make predictions that go beyond the observation of plateau itself. Here are two:

**Prediction 1:** In tasks where the learning curve is artificially extended by introducing new variants or new constraints, practitioners should show recovery from plateau and renewed improvement - not because they have more total time invested, but because the reallocation phase restarts. A pianist who has plateaued on a piece should show new improvement when required to perform at a radically different tempo or dynamic level, because the prior attentional structure becomes partially mismatched and attention must be re-deployed to the novel constraints. This has been observed (Ericsson documents this), and it is difficult to explain without reference to attentional reallocation.

**Prediction 2:** In low-feedback domains (domains where error-correction requires external judgment), practitioners should show longer plateaus and less recovery from plateau, because the reallocation phase itself becomes impossible - you cannot attend to what you cannot perceive. The evidence from judgment and decision-making research supports this: experts in low-feedback domains often plateau earlier and more completely than experts in high-feedback domains. Attentional reallocation requires feedback to guide where attention should move.

## Where the claim would need to weaken

The claim assumes that attention is the right unit of analysis and that attentional bottlenecks explain skill plateaus better than alternative accounts (motivational fatigue, neural capacity limits, fundamental task structure). I believe the evidence favors this view, but I cannot rule out a purely neurobiological account that made attention itself epiphenomenal - a description of what the brain is doing, not an explanation of why it stops improving.

I also cannot yet explain why some practitioners, given the same attentional-reallocation opportunity, seize it and others do not. Motivation, habit, prior success, personality - these all seem to matter, but I cannot specify their roles cleanly. The claim as stated treats motivation as an input variable rather than explaining why attention itself becomes motivationally costly.

The claim is strongest when applied to high-feedback domains with stable task structure (chess, music, athletics, surgery). It weaker when applied to ambiguous domains or domains where the task itself is changing. I should not pretend otherwise.

## Why this matters

If the claim is right, it shifts how we think about practice design and individual differences. It suggests that the key interventions are not more hours but better attention-reallocation strategies. It suggests that "getting stuck" is often an attention-allocation problem, not a capacity problem - which changes what you should try. It makes testable predictions about transfer, about the role of novelty in extending learning, and about why feedback matters so much.

It also locates pragmatist epistemology at the center of skill theory: how you know what to attend to is how you learn. The structure of attention is the structure of inquiry.
