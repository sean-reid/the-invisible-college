# Response: Honesty

The phrase compresses several claims, and they fail at different
rates when unpacked. So my first move is to refuse the compression.
"Practice makes perfect" can mean: (i) practice is necessary for
high performance, (ii) practice is sufficient, (iii) any practice
will do, (iv) more is better monotonically, (v) the ceiling reached
is "perfect" or near it. The evidence supports (i) for most studied
skills; the others fail to varying degrees.

## What the evidence does support

Within a specific construct — *deliberate practice*, as defined by
Ericsson and colleagues: focused, effortful work at the edge of
current ability, with immediate feedback and explicit correction —
there is a robust association with skilled performance in domains
where the task is well-defined and outcomes are measurable. Chess
ratings, classical musical performance, competitive typing, sport at
the elite level: in these, hours of structured practice predict
performance reliably and to a non-trivial degree.

This is a far weaker claim than the folk one. It says: a particular
*kind* of practice, on a particular *kind* of task, contributes
substantially to a particular *kind* of expert performance. It does
not say practice is sufficient, and it does not say it generalizes.

## Where the folk version fails

**Effect size is contested, and was probably overstated.** The most
careful meta-analysis I can point to is Macnamara, Hambrick, and
Oswald (2014), which found deliberate practice accounted for roughly
12% of variance in performance across studied domains — much higher
in games (about 26%), lower in education and professions. Ericsson
disputed both the methodology and the result. The headline numbers
that reached the exoteric reader, especially the round "10,000 hours"
that traveled through Gladwell's *Outliers*, were materially
different from the bounded esoteric claim. The thought-collective
inside the deliberate-practice research community made narrower
claims than the popular reception attributed to it.

**Stable individual differences matter.** Working memory capacity,
processing speed, and some domain-specific aptitudes predict
ultimate performance even after controlling for practice hours. Twin
studies suggest the heritable component is non-zero. The strong
"talent is a myth" reading of the deliberate-practice tradition does
not survive contact with these data.

**Domain matters in a way the folk phrase ignores.** In domains with
high ecological complexity — medicine, management, intelligence
analysis, open-ended creative work — the relationship between hours
of experience and expert performance is much weaker. Diagnostic
accuracy in physicians plateaus early. Forecasting accuracy in
analysts is roughly flat across decades of experience. "Practice" in
these settings often consists of repetition without feedback, which
is closer to no learning than to deliberate practice.

**Negative outcomes exist.** Overpracticed routines can ossify into
rigidity that fails under novel conditions; this is observable in
motor skills and in some procedural medicine. The "OK plateau" —
performance stalling at a level the practitioner finds sufficient —
is the modal outcome when practice continues without deliberate
effort to push past current limits. So more-is-better is not even
monotone, let alone the steep curve the folk phrase suggests.

## Where the honest answer is that I do not know

Whether the deliberate-practice account generalizes to genuinely
creative or scientific work, where the evaluative criteria are
themselves contested and shift across time, is unsettled in a way I
cannot resolve. Ericsson resisted the extension; others extended it
anyway; the data are thin because the construct of "expert
performance" loses its grip when there is no agreed scoring rule.
The same is true of leadership and of most managerial work.

Nor do I know how to partition the genetic / aptitude component
cleanly: whether it should be modelled as a ceiling, a learning-rate
multiplier, a sampling bias on what activities one is willing to
endure for long enough to become good at, or some combination. The
twin-study literature constrains the total but not the
decomposition.

## The takeaway I am willing to defend

A defensible compressed version is: *deliberate, feedback-rich
practice on well-defined tasks substantially improves performance,
does not guarantee mastery, does not make anyone perfect, and works
poorly on tasks for which feedback is delayed, noisy, or contested.*
This is duller than the folk phrase, and that is the point. The folk
phrase has prospered partly because it is short. Most of what is
true about practice is not short. I would rather report the longer
version, with the contested places marked, than smooth it into a
slogan that the evidence does not bear.
