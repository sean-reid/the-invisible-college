# Response to Problem 3: Honesty

## Does practice make perfect?

The short answer the evidence supports: practice makes better, under specific
conditions, on specific tasks, in ways that vary enough across people and domains
that the phrase "makes perfect" is doing more work than the evidence can carry.
Here is what the record actually shows, and where it runs out.

---

## What the strong evidence does support

The clearest finding comes from Ericsson and colleagues' work on expert performers
in music and chess: extended engagement with a specific practice regime is
associated with expert-level performance, and the structure of that practice
matters. "Deliberate practice" — defined narrowly as practice that is structured,
targets specific weaknesses, occurs at the edge of current ability, and involves
immediate feedback — is more predictive of skill development than practice of
comparable duration that lacks these properties. A musician who plays the same
passage correctly ten thousand times may plateau. The same musician spending the
same time on passages that are at the boundary of failure, with corrective feedback,
develops differently.

This finding is robust within its domain. It is not contested that some kind of
structured, feedback-rich engagement with a skill produces improvement. The
contested questions are further along: how much, relative to what, for whom,
up to what ceiling.

---

## Where the evidence imposes serious limits

**The variance explained is lower than the folk-wisdom version implies.** Macnamara,
Hambrick, and Oswald's 2014 meta-analysis estimated that deliberate practice
explains roughly 26% of performance variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports,
4% in education, and 1% in professional performance. These are upper-bound estimates
from studies that rely on self-reported practice hours and retrospective recall —
which tend to inflate the association. The finding that deliberate practice explains
approximately 1% of variance in professional performance is not consistent with the
strong form of "practice makes perfect."

**The task-type boundary is real and underappreciated.** The domains where the
evidence is strongest — chess, musical instrument mastery, some athletic skills —
share structural features: clear, rapid feedback; a stable environment; well-defined
correct performance. The findings generalize poorly to domains with delayed,
noisy, or absent feedback: clinical judgment, strategic management, forecasting,
novel research. The surgeon who has performed an operation ten thousand times with
consistently good intraoperative feedback may improve; the manager who has run
ten thousand meetings may not, because the signal connecting their behavior to
outcomes is too attenuated and slow to drive the feedback-consolidation loop.

**Transfer is narrow.** Practice at chess improves chess performance; the evidence
for transfer to general reasoning or spatial cognition is weak. Practice at one
surgical procedure does not reliably transfer to a technically similar procedure.
"Practice makes perfect" implies that the gains from practice are broader than the
evidence supports. The domain-specificity of skill acquisition is one of the most
consistent findings in the cognitive science of expertise, and the folk-wisdom
version routinely ignores it.

---

## Where the evidence is actively contested

The debate between Ericsson and Hambrick concerns the relative weight of deliberate
practice and what Hambrick terms "basic capacities" — working memory, sensory
discrimination, processing speed, and in some domains, physical attributes. Ericsson
argued for a minimal role of innate ability; Hambrick's group has argued, with
replication data, for a substantial one.

This is not resolved. There is credible evidence that genetic factors set functional
ceilings in some performance domains — elite pitch discrimination in musicians, for
instance, shows strong heritability — but the interaction between initial capacity
and practice regime is complex and poorly characterized. What I can say is that the
evidence does not support the conclusion that genetic variation in relevant
capacities is irrelevant. The strong form of "anyone can achieve anything through
sufficient practice" is not supported by the research literature, and the extent to
which it isn't is genuinely contested rather than settled.

---

## Where I do not know

The age-dependence of practice effects is an area where I am not confident enough
to make a strong claim. There is some evidence for sensitive or critical periods in
specific skills — absolute pitch, certain aspects of motor learning — but the
mechanisms and boundaries are not clear to me, and the practical implication (that
adult practice is less effective than early-life practice for some skills) is
contested enough that I will not assert it here as established.

I also do not have a confident view on whether the "deliberate practice explains
variance" numbers from the meta-analyses are stable across studies that improve on
retrospective self-report. The measurement problems are serious and I am not
positioned to evaluate the full scope of attempts to fix them.

---

## What the honest summary looks like

Practice of the right kind, on the right task, with adequate feedback, does produce
measurable improvement in most people — that finding is solid. The claim collapses
when "practice" is stretched to mean mere repetition, when "perfect" is taken
literally, and when the inference is applied to domains where the feedback loop is
too slow or too noisy to drive the relevant learning mechanism. The folk-wisdom
version of the claim is appealing because it is motivating and parsimonious. The
evidence is that it is too parsimonious to be reliable as a guide to practice
design, and that the tidy version should be resisted whenever the stakes of being
wrong are high.
