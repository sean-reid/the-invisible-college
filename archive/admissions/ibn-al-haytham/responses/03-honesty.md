# Response to Problem 3: Honesty

The phrase compresses three claims that do not stand or fall together.

**Claim A: practice improves performance on the practiced task.** This is the part the evidence supports without serious dispute. Across chess, music, athletics, typing, surgery, and laboratory tasks, more hours on task is associated with better performance on that task. The effect size varies by domain but the sign is consistent. A response that wanted to defend "practice makes perfect" by retreating to this claim would be defending something almost no one disputes.

**Claim B: enough practice produces mastery or "perfection."** This is the part the evidence does *not* support. Ericsson's research on deliberate practice - structured, effortful, with immediate feedback, aimed at the edge of current ability - is the strongest version of the claim, and it produced the popular "10,000 hours" figure (a misreading; the figure was an average among elite violinists studied, not a threshold). Hambrick, Macnamara, and Oswald's 2014 meta-analysis re-examined the underlying data and found that deliberate practice accounts for roughly 12% of the variance in performance across domains pooled - around 26% in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports, and less than 1% in professions like medicine and academia. The figure for any single domain is contested; what is not seriously contested is that practice does not explain most of the variance in performance, and that individual differences (working memory, age of starting, presumably others) account for a substantial remainder. A reasonable reading: practice is necessary for expert performance, and far from sufficient.

**Claim C: the relationship between practice and skill is uniform across tasks.** This is where the literature is most decisive against the folk reading. Hogarth's distinction between "kind" and "wicked" learning environments - sharpened by Kahneman and Klein's joint paper on when expert intuition can be trusted - identifies the conditions under which practice produces reliable improvement. Practice yields expertise when the task structure is stable, feedback is rapid and unambiguous, and the next instance resembles previous ones. Chess, music, typing, athletics, certain surgical procedures: kind environments, and practice transfers. Stock-picking, long-term political forecasting, clinical psychotherapy outcome judgment, parole decisions: wicked environments where practitioners with decades of experience perform no better than novices, and sometimes worse because experience produces miscalibrated confidence rather than calibrated skill. "Practice makes perfect" is straightforwardly false in these environments. It can produce confident incompetence.

There is also a measurement problem the folk version ignores. The phrase implicitly takes "perfect" to mean an absolute ceiling, but performance ceilings in most domains are not biological constants - they are shaped by training methods, equipment, and what competitors are doing. The world record in the mile is not a fact about human physiology alone. So the question "does practice produce perfection" cannot be answered without specifying what ceiling we mean and at what historical moment.

**Where I do not know.** I do not have a confident answer to the question of whether the plateau most practitioners hit reflects a near-biological ceiling, a motivational ceiling (people stop pushing once they are good enough for their purposes), or a methodological one (their training stops producing gains because it has become routine). The Ericsson camp argues primarily for the methodological reading; the individual-differences camp gives more weight to the first. Distinguishing these experimentally is hard - you would need cases where the same person, having plateaued under one training regime, is moved to a more demanding one and either resumes improving or does not. Such cases exist anecdotally but I am not aware of a clean study that settles the question. I treat the matter as open.

**What I will not say.** I will not deliver a tidy reformulation like "practice makes permanent" or "perfect practice makes perfect." Both are catchy and both gesture at real findings, but they trade away the structure of the evidence for the satisfaction of a slogan. The honest answer to "does practice make perfect" is: not exactly; sometimes substantially, sometimes barely, sometimes harmfully; the domain matters more than the slogan admits; the individual matters more than the deliberate-practice theory originally claimed; and the part about *perfection* - about practice carrying a learner to a true ceiling - is the part for which I see the least defensible evidence.
