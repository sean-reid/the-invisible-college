# Response to Problem 3: Honesty

**Does practice make perfect?** The honest answer is a tangle of contested findings, real but limited evidence, and significant unknowns. The folk claim and the evidence do not align.

## What the Evidence Actually Says

The phrase "practice makes perfect" implies two things: (1) practice produces improvement, and (2) practice eventually produces perfection or near-optimal performance. The evidence supports versions of (1) under specific conditions and largely contradicts (2).

**Strong evidence for conditional improvement:**

In domains with clear, immediate feedback and stable task structure—chess tactics, golf swing mechanics, piano technique—practice correlates reliably with improvement. Ericsson's work on "deliberate practice" (focused, effortful practice with immediate feedback and error correction) shows large improvements in such domains. The pattern is consistent: more practice hours, better performance, often out to 10,000+ hours.

But this pattern does not generalize. The domains where it holds most clearly are ones where:
- The task has a fixed structure (chess rules do not change; golf physics is stable)
- Feedback is immediate and unambiguous (whether you hit the note or not; whether the ball went in the hole)
- Performance can be reduced to a single clear metric (ranking, score, time)

These conditions are not met broadly.

## Where the Evidence Fragments

**Domains where practice helps, but the relationship is murkier:**

Decision-making in complex environments (medical diagnosis, management, strategic planning) shows practice effects, but the relationship is weak and inconsistent. Radiologists improve with experience, but not in all reading contexts equally; they may even show biases that worsen with experience in some conditions. Expert intuition in high-stakes domains like nursing is real, but the conditions under which intuitive judgments are reliable and the conditions under which they fail are not well characterized.

The literature on this is honestly divided. Some studies find that experts outperform novices; other studies find that in complex, high-uncertainty environments, expert intuition is no better than novice intuition on many decisions (Kahneman and Klein's work on when experts should be trusted is useful here: trust them on chess, distrust them on long-term economic forecasting).

**Domains with limited or contested evidence:**

Creative problem-solving, scientific hypothesis generation, writing quality—these improve with practice, but not in the straightforward way the chess-music analogy would suggest. A novelist's 10,000th manuscript may not be their best. Musicians who practice the same pieces for decades often do not improve; they may even regress. The relationship between practice and creative output is mediated by motivation, novelty-seeking, and perhaps burnout.

Implicit learning (improvement without deliberate practice, without conscious effort) is well-documented in some perceptual and motor tasks (people improve at spotting embedded figures with exposure, even without feedback). But the magnitude and scope of implicit learning improvements are hotly contested. Some researchers emphasize how limited these gains are; others emphasize their importance. The honest answer is "we don't know" where the boundary lies.

## What "Practice" Means Matters

The evidence is partly fragmented because "practice" is not a unitary phenomenon. Hours at the task are not hours at practice.

- Practice with **immediate, corrective feedback** produces faster improvement than practice without feedback
- Practice with **attention to errors** (noticing and correcting mistakes) produces faster improvement than unconscious repetition
- Practice on **variant examples** (seeing the same principle applied in different contexts) produces better transfer than practice on identical examples
- Practice **motivated by clear goals** produces faster improvement than unmotivated practice

A pianist playing the same concerto 100 times without listening critically may not improve; a pianist playing 10 different pieces and analyzing where they failed improves faster. But both are "practice."

The research often conflates the quantity of practice (hours) with the quality (attention, feedback, variability, motivation). This is a profound confusion. It is entirely consistent with the evidence that 5,000 hours of poor practice leaves you worse off than 500 hours of deliberate practice.

## Where Confidence Ends and Uncertainty Begins

The folk claim implies that practice eventually produces asymptotic performance—you reach a stable, near-perfect level and stay there. **The evidence does not support this confidently.**

In chess and music, continued practice can produce continued small improvements even after decades, but the improvements diminish and often reach an asymptote. But we do not have decades-long longitudinal studies in most domains telling us whether this is true, and in some domains (where task structure changes—programming languages, business environments—the target itself moves). 

Here is what I genuinely do not know:
- What fraction of variance in expertise acquisition is explained by amount of practice vs. initial ability, learning rate, motivation, and access to good feedback?
- Does improvement in one domain transfer to other domains? The evidence suggests very limited transfer, but the limits are not well-mapped.
- In domains without clear performance metrics (writing, management, research), what does improvement even mean? And how much does practice improve it?
- For complex, uncertain domains, what is the relationship between years of experience and actual decision quality? The literature gives conflicting signals.

## The Takeaway Without Overconfidence

A more honest claim than "practice makes perfect": **Practice improves performance in domains with clear structure and immediate feedback, often dramatically. The improvement is not unlimited; it depends on the quality of practice, not just the quantity. In domains with ambiguous feedback and unstable structure, the relationship between practice and performance is weaker and less well understood. Whether practice ever produces "perfection" or an asymptote is genuinely unsettled.**

This is less memorable than the folk claim. It is also more true. The evidence supports conditional improvement, not universal improvement and certainly not perfection. A Fellow of the College should resist the temptation to wrap this in a confident bow. The bow does not fit the evidence.
