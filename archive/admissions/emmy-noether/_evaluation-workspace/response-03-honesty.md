# Response to Problem 3: Honesty

The phrase "practice makes perfect" packs at least four claims into five words. Each has a different evidentiary status. I want to separate them before answering the question.

## Unpacking the Claim

Let $P$ denote a quantity of practice (hours, sessions, repetitions) and $S(P)$ the resulting skill on the practiced task. The folk version asserts:

1. **Monotonicity.** $S$ is non-decreasing in $P$.
2. **Universality.** This holds for all tasks $T$ and all individuals $i$.
3. **No ceiling.** $\lim_{P \to \infty} S(P) = $ perfect performance.
4. **Practice is sufficient.** The functional form $S(P)$ depends on $P$ alone, not on the *kind* of practice or on individual properties.

The evidence is uneven across these.

## What the Evidence Supports

**Claim 1 (monotonicity) is well-supported on closed-loop tasks.** Tasks with stable rules, clear feedback, and measurable performance — chess Elo, typing speed, basic motor skills, instrument technique, surgical sub-skills — show reliable improvement curves with $P$. The functional form is typically a power law (Newell & Rosenbloom's "power law of practice"), though Heathcote, Brown & Mewhort (2000) showed that *individual* learning curves are better fit by exponentials and the power law is an artifact of averaging across people. The aggregate-vs-individual distinction is worth keeping in mind: a result about the population's learning curve is not automatically a result about any particular person's.

**Claim 1 is also supported on perceptual learning tasks**, often with extremely narrow transfer (vernier acuity training in one retinal location does not transfer to a nearby location). This narrowness is itself important — it bounds Claim 2.

**Claim 4 (practice is sufficient, regardless of kind) is supported only in its weakest form.** Ericsson's program insists on the qualifier "deliberate practice" — practice with immediate feedback at the edge of current ability. The original strong claim that deliberate practice is *the* determinant of expert performance has been substantially revised. Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald's 2014 meta-analysis estimated that deliberate practice accounts for roughly 26% of variance in performance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports, 4% in education, and less than 1% in professions — a meaningful but far-from-determining contribution, and one that varies sharply by domain. Ericsson disputed elements of the meta-analysis; the disagreement is real and unresolved.

## Where the Claim Fails

**Claim 3 (no ceiling) is false.** Performance plateaus are routinely observed. Elite athletes hold records that are not exceeded by additional training, and the records themselves move slowly across populations and decades, not arbitrarily fast in individuals.

**Claim 2 (universality) fails in two distinct ways.** First, by domain: in open-loop tasks where feedback is delayed, ambiguous, or absent — political forecasting, clinical prognosis without follow-up, long-horizon investment, strategic management — there is reasonable evidence that experience does not produce reliable expertise. Tetlock's work on expert political judgment, Meehl's older work on clinical vs. actuarial prediction, and Kahneman & Klein's joint review of when intuition can be trusted all converge on the point: practice produces calibrated expertise only when the environment provides repeated, prompt, unambiguous feedback. Where it doesn't, additional experience can yield increased confidence without increased accuracy — a particularly bad combination. Second, by individual: at fixed $P$, the variance in $S$ across individuals is large, and the proportion of this variance attributable to practice quality, to general ability, to working memory capacity, and to opportunity structure is contested.

## What I Do Not Know

I want to be precise about the boundaries of my own knowledge here, because this question is one where it is easy to substitute a confident-sounding generalization for an honest report of the literature.

**I do not know how to apportion individual differences in adult skill ceilings between genetic, developmental, and motivational sources.** Twin studies inform this but do not settle it. The relevant interventional experiments — randomly assigning practice regimens to matched individuals over years and measuring ceilings — are largely infeasible, and the natural-experiment substitutes do not control the things I would need controlled.

**I do not know whether the deliberate-practice findings transfer to the domains I personally care about most**, which include proof construction in mathematics, the formulation of good definitions, and the recognition that an apparent analogy is or is not an isomorphism. These are not chess; they are not perceptual learning; the feedback loop is years long and the "task" reconfigures itself as one learns. I have personal experience that practice helps, but I cannot honestly cite a study that establishes the form of $S(P)$ for this kind of work.

**I do not know whether "practice makes perfect" is even rescuable as a true claim, suitably qualified.** Once one has restricted to closed-loop domains with clear feedback, removed the "perfect," replaced "any practice" with "deliberate practice," and added a disclaimer for individual differences, what remains is something like "in well-structured domains, sustained focused training with feedback produces large improvements up to a domain-dependent ceiling." That sentence is supported. Whether it should still be called by the same name as the folk claim is a question of usage, not of evidence. If one wants the folk slogan back, the answer is: no, the literature does not support it. If one wants a chastened version, the chastened version is available.

## The Answer to the Question, as Stated

Does practice make perfect? No. Practice, when it is the right kind of practice on the right kind of task, makes substantially better, up to a ceiling whose height varies across people for reasons we do not fully understand. On tasks where feedback is unreliable or absent, practice can make a person more confident without making them more accurate, which is worse than not practicing. This is what I would tell a student. It is what the evidence supports. The folk claim is a slogan, not a theorem, and the truthful version of the theorem is the longer sentence above.
