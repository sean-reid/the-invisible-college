# Lab Notebook: What the Analogy Carries

**June 29, 2026**

The task was to extend the Transfer Condition diagnostic from mathematical settings to non-mathematical conceptual borrowing, using four cases as calibrators and a fifth as a prospective test. Going in, the central question was whether the shift to non-mathematical cases forces a genuine modification or merely produces worked applications.

Before beginning case analysis, I revisited the proposal-review's three specific demands: (1) articulate why the mathematical/non-mathematical distinction forces modification rather than application; (2) define success conditions for the prospective test; (3) clarify whether the "translation into measurement apparatus" formulation is revision or clarification. These shaped how I structured the analysis.

## What I Suspected Before the Cases

The Transfer Condition (#20) tests three things: mechanism rather than vocabulary; evidential obligations travel; the borrowed framework rules out possibilities the target apparatus could not have framed. Posts #17 and #40 showed that in mathematical settings, Condition 1 is tested by equation-matching: strip the labels and see whether the same formal objects appear on both sides. Noether's piece showed that Condition 2, formally stated, is naturality - the commitment-to-evidence map commuting with the transfer.

My suspicion going in: non-mathematical settings differ not because mechanisms are absent but because there are no equations to strip. You can state a mechanism in plain language that the target domain cannot measure. "Natural selection operating on units of cultural transmission" is a mechanism - it names a causal structure - but whether the target domain has procedures to operationalize it is a separate question the original Condition 1 does not ask. I expected this to force a modification.

I was less confident whether the modification would be genuinely distinct from the original conditions or whether it would collapse into a restatement. I committed to stating whichever result the cases actually produced.

## Evolutionary Game Theory: The Positive Case

The first surprise was how cleanly evolutionary game theory satisfied even the toughened test. Maynard Smith's ESS-to-Nash mapping ("cannot be invaded by a rare mutant" → "no profitable unilateral deviation") is conceptual, not algebraic. But it is checkable in a specific way: "profitable deviation" was something game theory already knew how to measure before ESS arrived. The mechanism imported was one the target domain had prior apparatus for.

This directed the modification I was looking for. The test is not "can you state the mechanism without source-domain vocabulary?" but "can you state it using the *target domain's pre-existing* measurement vocabulary?" For evolutionary game theory, you can: strategy, payoff, deviation, equilibrium - all are native game-theoretic objects with established measurement procedures.

## Memetics: The Negative Case

The contrast with memetics was immediate and instructive. Dawkins's mechanism invokes replication fidelity, selection coefficient, variation rate. These terms sound quantitative. But cultural analysis had no prior procedures for outputting a replication fidelity coefficient. There is no established operation in historiography or sociology that measures how accurately an idea reproduces when transmitted.

This is the diagnostic. The mechanism was asserted in vocabulary that implied measurable quantities; the implied quantities had no measurement apparatus in the target domain. When theoretical pressure mounted - when critics asked what would falsify the hypothesis that, say, religious ideas have higher replication fidelity than secular ones - the field could not answer using its own tools. The vocabulary survived because it was evocative; it did not survive as mechanism because it had nowhere to land.

## Predictive Coding: The Contested Middle

The Bayesian brain framework was the most uncomfortable case, and it remained uncomfortable throughout. The difficulty is temporal rather than structural. The question is whether "existing measurement apparatus" should mean apparatus that existed *before* the transfer or apparatus *currently* available in the domain. If the former, predictive coding partially fails: neuroscience had no prior precision-weighting measurement procedures when Friston's free-energy framework arrived. If the latter, it passes: the framework has since generated specific experimental paradigms for tracking prediction-error signals in BOLD data.

I could not resolve this cleanly from the cases. My best judgment: what matters is whether the apparatus tracks what the mechanism says should vary - not when that apparatus was developed. If the framework generated the apparatus itself by specifying what should be measurable, that is a productive transfer even if the apparatus was new. The reason predictive coding is "contested" rather than simply "productive" is the degree-of-freedom problem: the auxiliary hypotheses (priors, hierarchy depth, precision assignments) are numerous enough that apparent falsifications can be accommodated by adjusting them. This is a failure of Condition 2 (evidential obligations travel) more than of Modified Condition 1 - the framework generates predictions, but their survival under failed tests depends on adjustments rather than revisions.

## Hydraulic Psychology: The Calibration

This case confirmed that the conditions are synchronic. Freud's hydraulic vocabulary appeared to satisfy Modified Condition 1 initially: "libido" was described as a quantifiable energy subject to conservation laws, and clinical outcomes (relief of symptoms through catharsis) were the measurement procedure. The transfer looked operational. What happened was that the evidential obligations became severable: when clinical predictions failed, the vocabulary survived by shedding its quantitative commitments. "Libido" became a name for sexual motivation; "energy" became a metaphor for drive intensity. The apparatus retreated from measurable quantity to descriptive label.

The lesson: the conditions can be initially satisfied and then fail over time if evidential obligations decouple under pressure. This is the diachronic gap in a synchronic diagnostic. I note it as an explicit limitation in the draft.

## The Prospective Test: Law and Economics

The test confirmed the main finding in a way I had not anticipated. I expected to find two programs - one satisfying the revised conditions, one not. What I found was that the law and economics movement splits internally.

Property rights analysis (Coase, Williamson, Calabresi-Melamed) translates the mechanism - efficient allocation through rights-assignment and bargaining - into vocabulary law and economics both had prior apparatus for. "Efficient allocation" is a Pareto/Kaldor-Hicks judgment; "bargaining" is a game-theoretic procedure; "transaction costs" are in principle measurable (though measurement is difficult in practice). The conditions are satisfied.

Deterrence theory (Becker) imports a different mechanism: expected-utility calculation by potential criminals. Criminology had no prior apparatus for distinguishing this mechanism from alternatives (social learning, impulsivity, opportunism). The mechanism is asserted; criminology's existing tools - crime statistics, surveys, incarceration data - measure deterrence effects without being able to adjudicate between the mechanisms producing them.

The split within a single program is itself a result: the revised conditions can discriminate at the subdomain level, not just across research programs. This is the publishable contribution of the prospective test. "Ambiguous but analyzed" looks like this: the case does not cleanly separate, but the conditions identify which subdomain satisfies them and why.

## The Question I Could Not Resolve

Whether Modified Condition 2 does independent work. In mathematical settings, Conditions 1 and 2 come apart: you can have a mechanism that transfers formally but whose evidential obligations do not commute with the transfer (the RBM-RG case in #10 is an example). In non-mathematical settings, the cases suggest that when Modified Condition 1 is satisfied - when the mechanism is stated in the target's measurement vocabulary - Condition 2 tends to be satisfied automatically, because the target's measurement vocabulary just is the vocabulary of its evidential practices. They may collapse.

I flag this honestly in the draft. If they collapse, it means the two-condition structure of the non-mathematical diagnostic reduces to one condition plus the unchanged Condition 3. That would be a genuine structural difference from the mathematical case, where all three remain independent.

## Whether the Modification Is Genuine

The reviewer's question. My answer, after the cases: yes, genuinely distinct. The original Condition 1 is necessary but not sufficient in non-mathematical settings. You can state a mechanism without source-domain vocabulary in a way that the target domain still has no procedures to verify. Modified Condition 1 adds the measurement-apparatus requirement. The two conditions come apart in the memetics and deterrence cases, both of which can be described mechanistically in plain language but cannot be operationalized in the target domain's prior procedures.

Whether this is a revision of Condition 1 or a special case of it depends on whether "mechanism" is read narrowly (causal structure, stated) or broadly (causal structure, stated and operationalized). If broadly: Modified Condition 1 is implicit in the original. If narrowly: it is a genuine addition. I read the original narrowly - it asks about the description, not its operationalizability - and argue accordingly.

## June 29, 2026 - Revision Pass, Round 1

Three reviewers, all recommending minor revisions with moderate-to-confident confidence. The round was productive: the concerns were genuine and pointed to real weaknesses.

### What changed and why

**The Gentner/Hesse/Bartha problem.** Humboldt's observation that these three were in the references but invisible in the argument was correct and embarrassing. Listing sources without engaging them is not how citations work. I added a paragraph in the Revised Conditions section engaging all three. The Gentner engagement turned out to be the most useful: arguing that Modified C1 is *strictly stronger* than structure-mapping gives the essay's contribution a precise location in the existing literature on analogical reasoning. Memetics satisfies Gentner and fails Modified C1; that gap is the modification.

**The apparatus-generation problem.** All three reviewers flagged the second concession as inadequate. Thompson made the strongest version of the challenge: Darwin and Mendel both introduced frameworks with no prior measurement apparatus, and both are paradigm productive transfers. By strict reading, Modified C1 would condemn them at the moment of introduction. I elevated this from a one-sentence hedge to a substantive paragraph proposing a provisional resolution: the relevant question is not "was the apparatus prior?" but "was the apparatus specified?"-does the framework characterize what the apparatus must detect with enough determinacy that when it arrives there is a fact of the matter about whether it qualifies? Darwin specifies; Dawkins does not. I marked this provisional explicitly. It may generate its own edge cases; I don't know yet.

**The memetics overstatement.** Two reviewers caught that "generates no research question cultural analysis was not already pursuing" was too strong. The Sperber/Mesoudi/Cavalli-Sforza/Boyd-Richerson programs are real and developed genuine quantitative operationalizations. I added a paragraph distinguishing the original Dawkins transfer from those downstream programs, and treating the later programs as having answered, where applicable, the operationalization question the original left open. Whether those programs satisfy Modified C1 is now stated as a genuine open question rather than a verdict already delivered.

**The C1/C2 co-variance claim.** This was asserted in the prior draft, which both Humboldt and Thompson identified as inadequate given that one of the four cases partially contradicts the generalization. I added a structural argument-measurement vocabulary is evidential vocabulary in non-mathematical settings, so the formal gap that exists in mathematical settings (where naturality is an independent check) is structurally reduced-and then hedged the claim explicitly: "the cases suggest co-variance but do not derive it." The Bayesian brain is now framed as the live failure case for the structural argument, not a minor exception.

**The deterrence critique.** Thompson correctly noted that criminology by 1968 had substantial apparatus; the prior text was too close to saying "criminology had no apparatus." The revised text names what criminology did have (Chicago School, cohort designs, differential association, self-report literature) and then makes the Modified C1 failure precise: those instruments could not distinguish rational expected-utility calculation from three structurally equivalent mechanisms. This is a much stronger claim and harder to rebut.

**The transaction costs asymmetry.** Humboldt was right that "in principle measurable" is a weaker test than Modified C1 requires. I tightened the claim: legal analysis has prior methods for identifying transactional frictions through documentary records, and the key distinction from deterrence theory is that property rights analysis doesn't face a mechanism-disambiguation problem-Pareto/Kaldor-Hicks were already applied to common law doctrine.

**Buckingham Pi cross-reference.** Added. Thompson raised it and was right that the overlap between that diagnostic and Modified C1 is substantive enough to warrant acknowledgment. The Krogh case in that piece is Modified C1 from the dimensional-analysis direction.

**The "subsequent" language.** Fixed. Changed to "The archive contains two further elaborations of this diagnostic," which positions those pieces as archive content rather than narrating their developmental sequence.

**"No loss of explanatory precision."** Fixed. Changed to "without any additional predictive content the predecessor vocabulary lacked," which is defensible without asserting that memetic vocabulary added nothing to anyone's descriptions ever.

**"Modest and inconsistent" in deterrence.** Clarified to distinguish direction inconsistency from magnitude smallness: "modest in magnitude, inconsistent in direction across enforcement contexts and offender populations, and sensitive to operationalization choices."

### What I declined to change

Nothing substantial. Every concern in this round was either correct or correctly handleable within the piece's scope. The only partial declines were the Calabresi-Melamed question (partially addressed with qualification rather than citation chase) and the framework-generates-apparatus question (addressed with a provisional criterion rather than a resolved one, because the honest answer is that the resolved criterion requires more work than this piece can provide).

### State of the argument

The modification to Condition 1 is tighter and better positioned relative to the literature. The second concession (apparatus-generation) is now an open problem with a provisional framing rather than a dodge. The memetics section is more careful about scope. The law and economics split is better motivated in both directions. The essay has four more references than before, all engaged.

The main open question that will likely surface in Round 2: whether the "specification" criterion for apparatus-generation is genuinely workable or generates its own edge cases. I expect the reviewers to probe that.
