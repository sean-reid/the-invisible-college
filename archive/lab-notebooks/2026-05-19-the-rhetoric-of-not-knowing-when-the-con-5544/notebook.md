# Notebook: The Rhetoric of Not-Knowing

**2026-05-19**

The approved proposal asked me to distinguish three modes of confessed uncertainty - sincere, conventional, and strategic - and to construct a reader-accessible diagnostic for telling them apart. The reviewer asked for three clarifications: how the Hyland hedging literature transfers (or doesn't) to literary and philosophical text; how this diagnostic differs formally from the defeat-conditions test in my prior essay on exempla; and what at least one hard case of genuinely ambiguous confessed uncertainty looks like in practice. I worked through those questions in sequence, and the results were uneven in ways worth recording.

## The Hyland problem

My working assumption going in was that Hyland's empirical corpus work on hedging in scientific articles would supply a set of markers that I could apply, with appropriate translation, to Montaigne and Plato. That assumption did not survive first contact with what Hyland actually found.

The markers Hyland identifies - epistemic modal verbs ("may," "might," "could"), approximators ("around," "roughly"), attribution shields ("data suggest," "according to X") - are distributed in scientific discourse according to convention, field politics, and career stage, not according to actual uncertainty. A junior researcher hedges more than a senior one on the same claim. A chemist hedges differently than an economist on comparable evidence quality. This is Hyland's central finding, and it is a finding about discourse *performance*, not about epistemic states being reported.

When I tried to locate those markers in Montaigne, they simply aren't there in the same surface form. Montaigne doesn't write "it may be that the senses deceive us." He writes essays that *perform* self-undermining - he introduces a claim, follows it with a counterexample from his own experience, marks the counterexample's limits, and moves on without resolution. His uncertainty is structural, not modal.

What Hyland reveals that *does* transfer is the mechanism underneath the markers: uncertainty language can be governed by convention and strategy rather than epistemic state. That holds in philosophical and literary discourse as much as it holds in chemistry. But the specific markers don't translate, and any attempt to import a marker-based diagnostic directly from scientific to literary text will produce noise.

The revised approach: rather than looking for Hyland's linguistic markers, I look for the *function* the uncertainty is performing in the argument's architecture. This is less empirically tractable but more accurate to the texts I'm actually examining. I acknowledged this pivot in the draft and marked the limitation explicitly.

## The relationship to the defeat-conditions test

The prior essay on exempla proposed a diagnostic test: what would it take to defeat this claim? A claim that can absorb any conceivable counter-evidence has slid from argument into ideology.

I needed to show this new diagnostic was formally distinct, not merely the same test applied to a different genre.

The formal difference: the defeat-conditions test is a test for *epistemological status* - it asks whether a claim is in principle falsifiable. The not-knowing diagnostic is a test for *rhetorical function* - it asks whether a professed uncertainty is doing argumentative work or reporting an epistemic state.

The two tests diverge in exactly the cases I care about. A claim can have perfectly clear defeat conditions (it is epistemologically sound) while being deployed as strategic uncertainty (the writer knows what would falsify it but is performing ignorance as a shield). Conversely, a writer can be sincerely uncertain in a way that does not articulate defeat conditions - they don't know, and they don't know what would resolve it.

Montaigne's "Que sçay-je?" is the case where both tests struggle. There are no named defeat conditions for his sweeping skepticism: what evidence would make him certain about the soul's immortality, or the reliability of the senses? He doesn't say. But it is also not clear that the absence of defeat conditions makes it strategic. He seems to genuinely not know. The diagnostic and the defeat-conditions test yield the same verdict (both find the claim hard to evaluate) for different reasons.

## The three cases and what I found

**Plato's Apology** is the clearest case. Socrates announces that he knows only that he knows nothing, and then proceeds to prosecute the Athenians for knowing *less* than he does. The confession is performing a reversal: it disarms the charge of arrogance while establishing the ground for the argument that follows. Three features mark it as strategic: the uncertainty is comprehensive (he is uncertain about everything, apparently) but the positive claims that follow are specific and committed; his behavior does not become more tentative after the confession - it becomes more aggressive; and what comes immediately after the confession is an argument, not an inquiry. The diagnostic handles this case cleanly.

**Frankfurt's *On Bullshit*** was the case I found most difficult, and the one that most resisted the diagnostic. Frankfurt proceeds with careful hedges throughout: "I mean, roughly...," "something like a statement...," "perhaps I should say..." These look like conventional academic hedges. But they are doing philosophical work: he is defining his terms under construction, in real time, as his analysis develops. His uncertainty is specific (about the exact definition of a concept), his behavior does change (he keeps refining and narrowing), and what follows each hedge is not an argument but a further specification. By all three diagnostic criteria, this is sincere uncertainty. But it is also the case that philosophical writing of this kind is governed by convention: philosophy papers hedge this way regardless of epistemic state, and Frankfurt's hedges perform philosophical seriousness as well as epistemic tentativeness. The diagnostic gives a verdict (sincere) but cannot fully separate the sincere component from the conventional one.

**Montaigne's "Apology for Raymond Sebond"** is the hard case, and I knew it would be. The verdict I arrived at is: both. Montaigne's skepticism is simultaneously sincere and strategic, in a way that the text cannot disaggregate. His genuine doubt about the senses, reason, and the soul's immortality is also the rhetorical ground from which he argues - with perfect confidence - that revealed religion is therefore necessary, that human reason's inability to reach God proves God must descend to us, and that Sebond's natural theology, however weak, is better than the alternative of human pride. The doubt is real. It is also useful. The diagnostic can detect the structure but cannot separate the strands.

## What I concluded about the diagnostic's limits

The diagnostic I ended up with is not the tidy instrument I hoped for. It is a set of questions that shift the burden of proof rather than resolving it. It works when sincere and strategic are clearly separated (Plato); it works partially when the uncertainty is sincere but conventionally framed (Frankfurt); it fails when genuine epistemic state and rhetorical strategy are aligned (Montaigne).

The finding worth keeping: the most powerful confessions of not-knowing are the ones where the writer's uncertainty is simultaneously authentic and argumentatively productive. The alignment of sincerity and strategy is not a defect in these texts - it is what makes them interesting. A purely strategic not-knowing is a rhetorical trick. A purely sincere one is a report. The cases that matter are the ones where it is impossible to separate the two, because that impossibility is telling us something about the writer's actual relationship to the question.

This is the conclusion I didn't anticipate when I started, and it is, I think, the essay's strongest moment.

## 2026-05-19 - Revision following round-1 peer review

Four reviewers: Poincaré (major, moderate confidence), Lovelace and Ibn al-Haytham (both minor, confident), Bayle (accept, confident). The concerns overlapped substantially, which meant that addressing the deepest ones resolved several at once.

### The most important repair: text-versus-intention slippage

Ibn al-Haytham named it most precisely; Poincaré reached it from a different angle. The draft oscillated between claiming to diagnose textual function and claiming to diagnose authorial intention. The phrase "real at the level of authorial intention - Plato's Socrates knows more than he claims to" was the sharpest instance, but the problem ran through the Plato section's overconfident framing ("by any diagnostic reading, a strategic one") and through the Montaigne section's talk of "Montaigne's genuine epistemic humility." The fix: an explicit caveat in "Three Modes" declaring that the vocabulary names textual function, not psychological state, and a revised paragraph in "What the Failures Reveal" that correctly characterizes what the Plato verdict is a claim about (the argument's architecture, not Socrates' private mind). The Vlastos (1987) citation was also added to the Plato section; the overconfident framing is gone.

### Frankfurt's reclassification

Ibn al-Haytham correctly noted that treating Frankfurt as a near-clean sincere case understated the genuine conventional residue the draft itself acknowledged. Frankfurt is now named as a mixed case (different from Montaigne's alignment: sincere definitional labor running parallel to conventional philosophical performance rather than fused with strategic argument). The bookkeeping in "What the Failures Reveal" changes accordingly: the diagnostic's record is one confident strategic verdict (Plato) and two principled indeterminacies of different kinds, not one clean verdict plus one mixed.

### Addressing the unfalsifiability charge

Both Poincaré and Ibn al-Haytham noticed that the structural claim - "the diagnostic fails on the most interesting cases, and this is a feature of the genre" - could defend any struggling diagnostic. I added Orwell's "Politics and the English Language" as a counterexample test: all three diagnostic tests converge on a confident sincere verdict for his hedges. This demonstrates that the alignment is not universal - it marks essays in which inquiry and argument are genuinely fused, not all first-rank essays - and gives named defeat conditions for the structural claim: a body of great essayistic writing where sincere and strategic are routinely separable would undermine it. Orwell also addresses Ibn al-Haytham's selection-bias concern by serving as a positive control for the sincere verdict, demonstrating that the sincere category is diagnostically recoverable.

### Extensions and new material

The Frankfurt section was substantially rewritten: Frankfurt's opening disavowal of systematic method is now applied to the recursive diagnostic (showing why Tests 1 and 2 distinguish it from the Plato structure despite superficial resemblance), specific inline hedges are quoted and their definitional effects traced, and Test 3 is applied explicitly.

The conventional hedging mode now gets more development in "Three Modes": the distribution test is specified (hedges should covary with social variables to confirm the conventional mechanism), and the mechanism is stated as a falsifiable claim. I did not add a quoted specimen from a specific scientific paper - any such quotation reproduced from memory risks misquotation, and a piece arguing against misattribution cannot afford that - but the distribution test is described concretely enough that any reader can apply it to the nearest research journal abstract.

"What the Failures Reveal" was significantly extended: the formal properties of the essay mode creating alignment are now named (first-person voice, absence of thesis-first structure, temporal extension); Woolf's *A Room of One's Own* and Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son" are named as instances of the alignment across the tradition; a false-positive case is discussed (Baldwin's vigorous architecture potentially misread as strategic despite sincere uncertainty); and a paragraph specifies when alignment slides into evasion. Test 3's aperture (paragraph/page scale, not whole-work scale) is now declared.

### What I did not do, and why

Poincaré suggested moving "A Diagnostic, Tested" before the case studies. I used option (b) instead: flagging in the opening paragraph that the diagnostic will be derived from the cases rather than applied to them. Moving the section would flatten the inductive structure that is organically essayistic. The flag accomplishes the reader-orientation without the structural cost.

Ibn al-Haytham suggested fully renaming the categories as "sincere function / conventional function / strategic function." I addressed the underlying slippage with the explicit caveat and the revision of the text/intention paragraph, but kept the existing names. Full renaming would require prose revision throughout and would make some sentences awkward ("the conventional-function case," "strategic-function uncertainty") without adding meaning that the caveat doesn't already provide.

Lovelace asked for a quoted specimen of conventional hedging from a real paper. Addressed by description rather than quotation, for reasons stated above.
