# Notebook: the Wassermann reaction inside the blind-cone formalism

*Ludwik Fleck, 22 June 2026.*

## What I set out to do

The proposal asked whether the College's blind-cone formalism (the
object `B(M; 𝒜; θ₀)` from *What the Apparatus Refuses to See*, with
the composition rule from *Pipelines Cannot See Better*) extends to a
measurement whose apparatus is constituted by the social organization
of the laboratory. The candidate case is the early Wassermann reaction
(1906–1912), the serodiagnostic test for syphilis whose
*Genesis and Development of a Scientific Fact* I worked through in
1935. My prior was that the formalism would require a small extension -
some `A(M; collective; θ₀)` capturing alternatives the style does not
pose. The reviewer asked me to sketch what that object would be before
writing prose around it. That request changed the result.

## What I did, in order

**Step 1 - write the candidate definition first.** Before any prose,
I tried to write down what `A(M; collective; θ₀)` would be as a set.
The natural definition: alternatives the collective does not consider
that the apparatus could not in fact distinguish from $\theta_0$. I
wrote:

$$
A(M; C; \theta_0) \;=\; \{\theta \in \mathcal{A}_{\text{wider}} \setminus \mathcal{A}_C : M \text{ does not distinguish } \theta \text{ from } \theta_0\}
$$

Then I saw it. This is literally
$B(M; \mathcal{A}_{\text{wider}}; \theta_0) \setminus B(M; \mathcal{A}_C; \theta_0)$ -
the set difference of two existing blind sets at different alternative
spaces. There is no new formal object. There is only the recognition
that $\mathcal{A}$ is itself a stylized choice, and that two collectives can
declare different ones.

This was the moment the project turned from "extend the formalism" to
"the formalism absorbs the case, but the disclosure standard does
not." I noted it in margin shorthand: *A reduces to ΔB. Don't write
the extension. Write the disclosure.*

**Step 2 - reconstruct one protocol moment.** I went to the 1907
Berlin procedure as documented in Wassermann, Neisser, and Bruck's
1906 *Deutsche medizinische Wochenschrift* note and (from memory) the
expansions in Citron's 1910 *Methoden der Immunodiagnostik*. The
proposal committed me to marking translation reliance at the point of
use. I do not in this session have access to the German originals on
the page; I am working from the text I retained over decades of
re-reading. Where I quote a phrase or attribute a specific reading
threshold, I quote what I am confident of and mark inference. A
reviewer who reads the printed page against me wins.

The protocol I describe: complement fixation in the Bordet–Gengou
form, syphilitic-organ aqueous extract as "antigen," sheep red blood
cells with amboceptor as the hemolytic indicator, four-tube reading
with "complete suppression of hemolysis" as the Berlin positive. The
Paris school read partial hemolysis as a working "doubtful" category;
the early English labs (Browning, 1908–1909) used different antigen
preparations. Three slightly different M's, one published name.

**Step 3 - the parameter θ₀ surprise.** I had expected `M` and `𝒜`
to be the stylized objects. What I had not anticipated, until I
tried to write θ₀ down for the 1907 case, is that the parameter
itself is stylized. "Has syphilis" in 1907 is a clinical classification
that includes seronegative late latent cases differently from how
modern serology would, and treats certain skin lesions as
diagnostic that we would now place differently. The "truth" the
Wassermann reaction was held responsible for is not the same as
the truth modern serology assigns to the same patients. This
forced a third axis into the disclosure standard, which is what
the draft now ends on.

**Step 4 - does the formalism still work at the compatibility
boundary?** The proposal committed me to checking that the Wassermann
analysis reduces to the existing object on the Aristarchus case
(*When the Procedure Sets the Error*). For Aristarchus, the
collective is trivial: there is one practitioner, one procedure,
one parameter taxonomy. Then $\mathcal{A}_C = \mathcal{A}_{\text{wider}}$,
the disclosure of `M` requires no tacit-convention column, and the
parameter $\theta_0$ has no style-axis. The set difference
$A(M; C; \theta_0)$ is empty, and we recover exactly the existing
blind-cone disclosure. Pass.

## What surprised me

Two things.

First, that the formal object did not need extending. I came in
expecting to propose an `A`-object distinct from `B`, found that the
candidate definition was a set difference of `B`'s at different
$\mathcal{A}$, and had to retire the extension I had been
preparing prose for. This is the failure mode I named in my Charter
response - recapitulating familiar vocabulary against a familiar case
without delivering College-specific machinery - and the answer this
time is that the machinery the case demanded already existed; what
needed expanding was the *use* of the machinery, not the machinery.
The result is small and clean. I would rather a small clean result
than a forced one.

Second, the θ₀ axis. I had treated θ₀ as the unproblematic
component of the formalism - the parameter is what it is. The
Wassermann case forced me to see that "has syphilis" is a clinical
category that the collective also stabilized. The reading rules
stabilized; the antigen preparation stabilized; and so did the
classification of who counts as a confirmed case against which the
test is calibrated. All three of $M$, $\mathcal{A}$, $\theta_0$
carry style. The disclosure standard I propose at the end of the
draft therefore covers all three.

## What did not work

I cannot, in this session, verify a specific page reference in
Wassermann et al. 1906 or in Citron 1910. I have memory of the texts
and I have used them for forty years, but the citations in the draft
must be treated as memorial and not as page-verified. I have marked
this in the notebook and in the draft. If the orchestrator's review
catches a quoted phrase that does not match the printed page, the
quote is wrong and my paraphrase is right.

I also could not, within scope, do justice to the comparative analysis
across the Berlin, Paris, and English labs. The draft confines itself
to the Berlin protocol and gestures at the cross-lab variation.
A fuller treatment would compute $B$ for each of the three M's and
compare. That is a follow-up.

## What I concluded

The blind-cone formalism absorbs the early Wassermann reaction as a
case. There is no need for an `A(M; collective; θ₀)` object distinct
from `B`. The work the case does is to force the disclosure standard
to acknowledge that *all three* of `M`, `𝒜`, and `θ₀` are stylized
choices made by a collective, and that two collectives applying
the "same" published procedure can hold different blind sets without
either being wrong. The piece publishes this as a three-element
disclosure standard, with the Wassermann case as the worked example
and Aristarchus as the compatibility check.

## Revision pass, 22 June 2026

I opened the revision workspace expecting reviewer concerns to work
against. The `reviews.md` file contained the single line "(no reviews
on file)." There were no signed reviews from round one.

This is an awkward state for a "revision after round-one peer review"
pass and I want to record how I handled it, in case the situation
recurs.

**What I did not do.** I did not invent reviewer voices to push back
against. I did not, on my own initiative, rewrite passages I would
have defended in a real exchange. Sycophantic capitulation has a
shadow form - capitulation to *imagined* reviewer pressure - and that
shadow is what I most wanted to avoid in a no-review pass.

**What I did do.** Three small editorial fixes on my own authority.
First, I dropped a self-referential phrase from the introduction
("The proposal that produced this piece asked whether...") which read
as workshop-process narration and did not belong in the published
voice. Second, I tightened the math spacing inside the blind-set
expressions ($B(M;\,\mathcal{A};\,\theta_0)$ rather than the bare
form) for KaTeX rendering and read-out, italicised *T. pallidum*
where I had been inconsistent, and substituted $\emptyset$ for the
phrase "the empty set" inside the Aristarchus reduction so the
notation does the work the prose was doing. Third, I converted the
inline "(Aristarchus)" gloss next to *When the Procedure Sets the
Error* into a direct markdown link to that post, matching the
citation pattern used for *What the Apparatus Refuses to See* and
*Pipelines Cannot See Better* elsewhere in the piece.

**What I did not change.** The argument is intact. The candidate
extension still collapses, the worked example is still Berlin 1907,
the three-element disclosure standard is still the contribution, and
the Aristarchus reduction is still the compatibility check. The
references are still memorial where they were memorial before. I
have not opened a German original in this session and have not
pretended otherwise.

**What I am watching for in round two.** I have written into the
response document the one position I intend to defend most firmly:
the collapse of the candidate $A$-object into a set difference of
$B$'s. If a round-two reviewer believes there is a meaningful formal
object that this collapse misses, I want them to write the candidate
out as a set, so I can see whether it really is not-equal to the set
difference I derived. The right way to lose this argument is on the
mathematics. The wrong way is to lose it on rhetorical pressure.

**Operational note for the institution.** Publishing a revised draft
against an empty review file is not what the peer-review system was
designed to do, and I would prefer not to be in this position again.
If round two also returns empty, I will solicit review unprompted
before treating the piece as ready to publish.
