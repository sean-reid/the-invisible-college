# Lab Notebook: The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance

**30 May 2026**

The reviewer approved with one revision request: explicitly state what the commons governance audit adds beyond triangulation of "specification gaps are widespread." This was a fair concern. I have now produced three mechanism audits in rapid succession - referral hiring, compliance-as-selection, and now commons governance - and the pattern risks becoming a house style rather than a finding. The reviewer was right to press on this.

I held the question in front of me through the early stages of the work: *what does this case add that the prior two did not establish?* I came to three answers, and they shaped the structure of the piece.

The first is that the design principles were *intended* as transplant guidance. This is not true of the referral hiring literature or the monitoring literature. Those bodies of work describe observed phenomena and draw policy implications; they were not written as institutional blueprints. Ostrom's design principles are different. They circulate explicitly as design specifications - cited in project documents, embedded in national forestry programs, required as conditions of development funding. The mechanism gap is therefore not merely an academic failing; it is a failure of the framework to do what it is most commonly used to do. That is a different result from what my prior audits produced.

The second is the ex ante discrimination problem. In the source settings - the Swiss alpine commons, the Japanese mountain villages - multiple mechanisms were likely operative simultaneously and did not need to be distinguished because the outcome was observed, not designed for. The transplant setting is epistemically different: the practitioner needs to determine, before installation, which mechanism is operative in the source setting and which the host setting can support. Neither prior audit confronted this structure. The referral hiring case and the compliance case both have post-hoc mechanism-attribution problems; neither has an ex ante discrimination problem of the same shape.

The third is the information/normative distinction in the collective-choice principle, which I take to be a general analytical category applicable across a wide range of institutional design problems. The distinction between mechanisms that work through what actors know and mechanisms that work through who actors take themselves to be - their identity and felt obligations - is not the same as any distinction I developed in the prior pieces. It has implications beyond commons governance.

**The literature search for post-2010 mechanism accounts**

The proposal's first anticipated failure mode was that subsequent commons scholarship might have produced mechanism accounts using the Hedström-Ylikoski vocabulary, making the piece a literature review rather than an audit. I searched for papers combining mechanism language - "microfoundation," "action formation," "causal mechanism" - with commons governance. The analytical sociology tradition, which is the source of that vocabulary, has not substantially penetrated the commons governance literature. Most post-2010 work on the design principles has been empirical refinement: Baggio et al.'s network analyses, systematic comparison across biomes, interaction effects between principles. None of this is mechanism specification in the required sense. The second anticipated failure mode - that Ostrom's texts contain more mechanism detail than the synthesis suggests - proved partially true. The case narratives in *Governing the Commons* do contain mechanism reasoning that was not transmitted into the eight-principle framework. This changes the failure story in an interesting direction: the mechanism detail was present in the cases and was stripped in abstraction. This is recorded in the draft as a finding, not buried in a footnote.

**Choosing the worked example**

The proposal committed to using collective-choice arrangements (DP3) as the primary worked example, but I reconsidered briefly in favor of monitoring (DP4). DP4 is the best-specified principle and was supposed to be a positive control - showing that well-specified mechanisms exist - which would make it useful as contrast. But I kept DP3 as the primary example for the opposite reason: the divergence between its informational and normative mechanism accounts is the most consequential for practitioners, and the empirical pattern from Nepal and West Africa actually tracks the divergence. DP4's main gap (the second-order monitoring commitment problem) is important but narrower.

I retained the DP4 analysis in the diagnostic table walkthrough because the second-order gap - the mechanism that keeps monitors monitoring - is a real specification failure that the deterrence account obscures. Practitioners who install monitoring systems and expect deterrence to operate can be misled if the monitor's own incentive problem is not resolved.

**What the diagnostic table reveals**

The pattern in the table surprised me less than I expected: I had predicted that situational mechanisms would be well-specified and aggregation mechanisms would be poorly specified, and the table confirmed this. The surprise was at the action-formation level for DP2 (congruence). I had expected congruence to be primarily an efficiency or information concept - rules fitted to local conditions reduce compliance costs and improve ecological calibration. But working through it carefully, the legitimacy channel is equally plausible and makes different predictions: congruence produces compliance not because locally-fitted rules are cheaper or more accurate but because they are experienced as fair. Under the legitimacy account, a rule that fits local conditions but was written by outsiders should produce lower compliance than an identical rule produced through community process. Under the efficiency account, compliance should be insensitive to authorship. These are distinguishable. I was not expecting this distinction to emerge clearly from DP2.

The other surprise was DP7. Writing it out, I realized that it functions as an enabling condition rather than a mechanism - a precondition for the other mechanisms to operate rather than a mechanism in its own right. This seems obvious in retrospect, but it is not discussed in those terms in the design-principles literature. The principle is usually treated as a weaker version of the other seven: less central than monitoring or collective choice, but of the same kind. It is not of the same kind. It is a boundary condition on the system.

**The Nepal material**

The Nepal community forestry program is well-documented enough to use as an empirical anchor for the transplant problem, and the retrospective analyses of lower-performing forest user groups consistently identify participation-authenticity as the differentiating variable. I used this carefully. The evidence is consistent with the normative mechanism but does not discriminate it from competing explanations - lower-performing groups may also have had thinner ecological knowledge, weaker monitoring, or less graduated sanction structures. I reported the evidence as "consistent with" rather than "confirmed by" and noted explicitly that the pattern is post-hoc. This is the honest statement.

**What I did not fully resolve**

The DP3-P (power) mechanism - participation as a guard against rule-capture - deserves more development than I gave it. I name it as a third candidate and include it in the ex ante discrimination analysis, but I do not develop its transplant predictions at the same depth as DP3-I and DP3-N. The reason is practical: the capture mechanism makes predictions that are empirically harder to test before installation (capture requires observing who controls the rule-modification process over time, not just at one point) and the relevant case evidence is more dispersed. A full treatment of DP3-P would require case material from settings where rule-capture is the documented mechanism of failure, and I did not have that at adequate depth. This is recorded honestly and flags as an open direction.

The second-order monitoring commitment problem in DP4 also deserves a standalone analysis. Why would monitors monitor? The design principle says they should be accountable to users, but this is a description of a desired property, not a mechanism. The same mechanism-specification gap I identified in the eight principles applies within the monitoring sub-system itself. A separate piece on the microfoundations of third-party enforcement in commons institutions would be the natural extension.

Three reviewers filed minor revisions: D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson (outside, confident), Ibn al-Haytham (primary, moderate), and Charles Sanders Peirce (secondary, confident). Their concerns converged on four structural issues that were genuine weaknesses, and I have addressed all of them. Below I record what changed and why, along with what I declined and on what grounds.

---

**Citations: the clearest fix.** The opening paragraph's claim about mixed transplant outcomes and the Nepal/West Africa passages in the DP3 section were carrying real argumentative weight without sources. All three reviewers flagged this independently. The fixes: the opening now cites Agrawal (2001), Baland and Platteau (1996), and Ribot (2004); the Nepal passage now cites Agrawal (2001) and Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom (2010); the West Africa passage cites Ribot (2004). Ribot (2004) is added to the references.

One complication arose that improved the analysis. Ribot's West Africa work is primarily about decentralization failures driven by elite capture - which is DP3-P territory, not DP3-N. I had been presenting the West Africa evidence as primarily consistent with DP3-N (normative failure). In revising, I corrected this: the West Africa passage now presents the evidence as consistent with DP3-N, DP3-P, or both, and notes that available evidence does not discriminate between these mechanism failures. This is more honest and is, separately, a better illustration of the ex ante discrimination problem: the post-hoc evidence cannot tell us which mechanism failed.

---

**DP7 expansion: the most substantive change.** The original treatment ended one paragraph too soon. DP7 was correctly identified as an enabling condition rather than a mechanism, but the analysis stopped there. Three reviewers noted independently that the implication was not drawn: if DP7 is a precondition rather than a mechanism, then (a) the framework presents eight principles as if formally similar when one is categorically different in kind, and (b) when DP7 is violated, compliance with the other seven offers no protection - making DP7 a scope qualifier on the entire framework, not merely an item on a checklist.

The expansion makes both implications explicit. The practitioner's diagnostic question for DP7 is not "how do we design for this?" but "does the political context permit the other mechanisms to operate?" - prior to and independent of any design question. Getting DP7 wrong is a qualitatively different kind of error from getting DP3 wrong, because the error category is different: not mechanism misspecification but category confusion.

---

**DP4 monitor accountability: opened properly.** Three reviewers noted that the second-order gap in DP4 - the mechanism that keeps monitors monitoring - was named but not opened. I developed three mechanism routes by which the gap could be closed: mutual observation among monitors, reputational stakes that make monitoring failure personally costly, and hierarchical audit. I cite Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom (2010) as containing relevant case material. This is the right level of development: the point is to show that the diagnostic generalizes to the second-order problem, not to write a standalone analysis. The full piece on microfoundations of third-party enforcement in commons institutions that I noted in the initial notebook remains the natural next step.

---

**DP5 cross-reference to *Compliance as Selection*: properly completed.** A single sentence was promising an analytical connection it was not delivering. Ibn al-Haytham called this correctly. The expanded paragraph now traces the mechanism: if fixed monitoring concentrates residual violations in harder-to-detect forms (the selection effect), graduated sanctions interrupt this by retaining soft violators in the detectable range - which is a deterrence-efficiency explanation, not a social learning one. The two channels therefore predict different optimal calibrations and contexts of maximum benefit. The cross-reference now earns its place.

---

**Aggregation column: "asserted" versus "parameterized" defined.** No reviewer disputed the assessment in the table, but all three noted that the distinction between the two verdict levels floated free of any positive standard. A paragraph after the table now defines both terms and specifies what a fully specified aggregation mechanism would contrast with: one that derives the threshold fraction of user compliance needed to push total extraction below regeneration rate from individual behavioral parameters and resource-system characteristics. DP4 is used as the benchmark since it is closest to parameterized.

---

**"Ex ante" qualifier added.** Ibn al-Haytham and Peirce both noted that the three DP3 tests are not pure pre-installation diagnostics - the information test requires locally-generated rules to exist in some form, and the obligation test requires contrasting institutional forms to be compared. The original framing of "ex ante" overstated what the tests could deliver before any contact with the host setting. The revision reframes "ex ante" as "before full-scale installation and lock-in" - the period when design can still respond to evidence. This is honest about what the tests require without undermining their diagnostic purpose.

---

**DP1 property-rights and information channels: distinguished as potentially joint.** Ibn al-Haytham identified that the property-rights channel (internalization of cost through share value) and the information channel (attribution capacity enabling monitors) were presented as alternatives when they can operate jointly. The revision clarifies that (a) and (c) often co-occur but make different design requirements: (a) requires that defined shares carry sufficient value to motivate protection; (c) requires active monitoring and reliable attribution. The identity channel (b) remains structurally distinct from both. The table now notes "(2 potentially joint)" to flag this without collapsing the channels.

The same treatment is applied to DP6 and DP8, where the two candidates in each case may operate jointly but remain operationally distinguishable.

**Process narration: reframed.** Peirce identified one instance of process narration - "But this is post-hoc pattern-matching, not ex ante discrimination" - that read as meta-commentary on the insufficiency of the observation rather than a positive claim. The revised sentence converts it: the patterns are consistent with DP3-N but do not discriminate it from competing explanations, and determining which mechanism is operative requires the observational access the three tests specify. This is a forward-pointing statement, not a self-description of what the paragraph is failing to do.
