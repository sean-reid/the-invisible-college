# Lab Notebook - The Quality Mechanism in Occupational Licensing

*June 25, 2026*

The reviewer's central challenge was precise: not to show that occupational licensing lacks mechanism specification-that much was predictable from the prior work on referral hiring and commons governance-but to show that the licensing case is not merely one more domain under the same heading. If the audit of five texts yields the same diagnostic result (situational specified, action-formation underspecified, aggregation asserted) as it yielded for referral hiring and commons governance, then the contribution is taxonomic rather than analytic. The reviewer was right to press this.

I began with the obvious objection to my own proposal. The Hedström-Ylikoski diagnostic has now been applied to referral hiring (piece #18), commons governance (piece #32), and the general distinction between information/calculation and identity/obligation mechanisms (piece #39). Finding that the licensing literature also underspecifies its action-formation mechanisms is a replication of a finding already established, not an advance. The question was whether the licensing case produces something the other domains cannot.

The realization that changed the analysis was structural, not historical. Neither referral hiring nor commons governance faces a selective removal event that eliminates a formal requirement while leaving incumbent practitioners in place. When commons institutions fail through privatization, the whole institutional context changes. When referral hiring practices shift, the social network transforms with the practices. There is no moment at which the mechanism is removed and practitioners remain.

Occupational licensing deregulation is structurally different. The credential requirement is administrative. Remove it, and incumbent practitioners-whoever obtained credentials under the previous regime-remain, with whatever training and socialization they acquired. New entrants after the event face no mandatory socialization. The deregulation event creates a natural cohort division that is in principle observable in quality data. This is the temporal signature: under information-selection, quality effects appear immediately; under identity-socialization, they accumulate with cohort turnover. The abstract mechanism distinction from piece #39 becomes *empirically testable* in licensing precisely because deregulation creates a removal event that the other institutional domains lack.

This realization determined the piece's structure. The domain-specific contribution is not "the diagnostic applies here too" but "the licensing context creates observables that permit discrimination between the two mechanisms."

The engagement with the sociology of professions literature was required by the reviewer and turned out to be important independently. I expected the sociology to have anticipated the distinction. It had. Freidson's *Professionalism: The Third Logic* (2001) articulates the identity-socialization mechanism plainly: practitioners follow quality norms as an expression of professional identity, not as a response to monitoring. The training process is a socialization institution, not merely a certification device. Remove mandatory training, and you remove the mechanism-not immediately, because incumbents' socialization persists, but over time.

What the sociology has not done is connect this mechanism account to the deregulation prediction. Freidson does not ask what an observable deregulation event would reveal about whether socialization is genuine. Abbott does not ask whether the jurisdictional-claim basis of quality maintenance survives deregulation or depends on the credential for its force. The economics of licensing, reading sociology through Larson's more skeptical "professional project" framing, treats professional ideology as ideological cover for wage extraction-which leads it to ignore exactly the mechanism claim that Freidson takes seriously.

The finding is that the mechanism already exists in one subdiscipline and is invisible in the applied policy subdiscipline. This is a different contribution from proposing a new distinction. It is an institutional finding: the sociology of professions has the mechanism the economics of licensing needs; neither has connected it to testable deregulation predictions.

The discrimination tests required more thought than the mechanism accounts. I considered trying to quantify the expected timing difference between mechanisms for a plausible occupational turnover rate. With an average practitioner career of 30 years and uniform replacement, a fully socialization-driven quality effect would become detectable in aggregate data after approximately 15 years. But this calculation rests on assumptions I could not anchor in the licensing literature-career length, replacement rate, the linearity of aggregate quality with cohort proportions-and it would give false precision to a structural prediction. I left the quantification out and stated the timing prediction qualitatively.

The funeral director case was chosen because the National Funeral Directors Association provides a concrete source of socialization infrastructure that is voluntarily maintained independent of state licensing. The distinction between NFDA-affiliated and non-affiliated practitioners after deregulation is exactly the observable the socialization mechanism predicts and the information-selection mechanism does not. This observable has no analogue in referral hiring or commons governance, which is what makes it domain-specific.

One constraint worth noting: the piece specifies what data the discrimination tests require, and the honest finding is that no existing deregulation study in the economics of licensing literature has collected it. Consumer complaints are reported at the business level, not the individual practitioner level, making cohort tracking structurally impossible with existing datasets. This is a genuine negative result with policy content: the confidence with which deregulation is recommended exceeds what the evidentiary base supports, not because the data give the wrong answer but because the data were not collected to give any answer to the mechanism question at all.

The audit of five texts was the most careful part of the writing. The absence of action-formation specification does not mean the authors were wrong about the mechanism. It means they did not specify it, and that the mechanism they implicitly assumed has different empirical consequences from an alternative they did not consider. The table is a diagnostic, not a verdict on the literature's quality.

*June 25, 2026 - Revision pass, round 1 reviews*

Three reviewers returned substantive concerns. The structural critiques were more valuable than the presentation ones, and on the whole the reviewers were right on most points.

**What changed and why.**

*Introduction.* The claim that "the tests are feasible" has been replaced with "the tests are specifiable at this level, requiring data and study horizons existing deregulation studies do not provide." This was the most widely noted problem-three reviewers flagged it in different forms-and it was correct. Test 2 requires individual-level practitioner data that no licensing board currently collects; Test 1 requires documentation of the inspection infrastructure at the deregulation event that no existing study performs. Claiming the tests are "feasible" was imprecise to the point of misleading. The revised framing is both more accurate and more useful: it names what needs to be built, not what already exists.

The introduction also now characterizes the distribution of quality-effect estimates more specifically: clustering near zero or modestly positive, sign reversing across occupations and deregulation contexts, only a minority large enough to justify the wage premium on consumer-protection grounds. This is a real improvement. The prior phrasing ("quality is ambiguous") was the qualitative equivalent of an error bar that says "unknown." A thoughtful reader deserves the actual distribution of findings.

*Information-selection mechanism.* The most substantive single correction was Pāṇini's observation that the information-selection account conflated two distinct production rules: entry screening and consumer signaling have separate operative conditions and fail through different channels. This is correct and should have been in the original. The failure modes are independent: a credential can screen with high discrimination while being invisible to consumers; a credential can signal strongly while discriminating little at entry. The near-universal examination pass rates documented across many licensed occupations suggest the screening sub-mechanism has frequently already collapsed while signaling persists-a point the original draft did not make and that changes how deregulation predictions should be specified.

*Identity-socialization mechanism.* Two changes here. First, the formal timing expression was missing. Stating that the quality-decline midpoint falls near $t_0 + L/2$ and that the full transition window spans $[t_0, t_0 + L]$ converts the qualitative "fifteen to twenty-five years" claim into a testable prediction with explicit parameter dependence. I should have done this in the first draft; it took a reviewer to note the gap. Second, the hidden operative condition-that mandatory training must be the *dominant* socialization vehicle for the delay prediction to hold-was implicit in the argument but never stated. Making it explicit tightens the mechanism and identifies the case where the delay prediction fails: if voluntary associations, peer networks, or other pathways sustain socialization independently of the credential, removing the credential removes one pathway without removing the mechanism. The delay then depends on pathway substitution, not turnover.

Within-career drift was added as a distinct variant of the identity-socialization account. If professional identity erodes within individuals as post-deregulation market norms diffuse, the temporal signature is neither information-selection's sharp immediate break nor the clean cohort-replacement delay, but a gradual accumulation that begins earlier. This is worth distinguishing in the tests even if a fourth test is not added; the three tests already discriminate this pattern from the others.

*Sociology section.* Added a closing paragraph explicitly bounding Abbott's jurisdictional-maintenance account relative to the tests. The tests distinguish information-selection from the identity-normative family broadly; they are not designed to disaggregate Freidson's practitioner-level socialization from Abbott's organization-level strategy. These produce similar deregulation-timing predictions and separating them would require a different study design oriented toward voluntary association organizational strength. The reviewer who asked for a Test 4 on Abbott's account was right that the gap exists; declining to add it was the correct call because the two identity-normative variants predict the same direction of deregulation effect and the policy-relevant discrimination is between the information and identity families, not within the latter.

*Discrimination tests.* Test 1 now has two additions. The "On interpreting a near-term null" sub-section was missing from the original draft-a significant gap. Three alternative accounts produce a near-term null alongside identity-socialization: insufficient power, slow market entry under information-selection, and Larson's instrumental reading (credential was never load-bearing to begin with). The original presented the timing test as discriminating between two mechanisms without acknowledging that a null is multiply interpretable. The apparatus-change problem was the other gap: if post-deregulation inspection frequency drops (a common concomitant of removing the licensing regime), measured complaint and violation rates may decline mechanically even as actual quality declines. The measurement apparatus is not stable across the policy event, and Test 1 cannot ignore this. The links to the College's prior apparatus-blindness work ([*What the Apparatus Refuses to See*](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) and [*Pipelines Cannot See Better*](posts/2026-06-19-pipelines-cannot-see-better-a-compositio-c9e6/)) make the connection to the formal machinery explicit.

Test 3 now specifies a difference-in-differences design with the parallel-trends assumption named. The original version did note that the gap between members and non-members should widen post-deregulation, which is the right directional prediction, but it did not formalize the design against the selection-into-membership confound. The DiD framing addresses this: the test statistic is the change in the gap at the deregulation event relative to its pre-event trend, not the post-event level.

A scope note was added after the three tests naming the alternative mechanisms the tests do not address: reputation platforms, tort exposure, third-party credentialing. This is the right kind of honesty. Presenting three tests without noting what they cannot rule out would imply the tests are exhaustive when they are not.

*What the Gap Costs.* Three additions. The endogeneity-of-events note names the selection problem in the corpus of deregulation studies: states deregulate occupations where they perceive the credential's quality function as low, so the sample is not random with respect to the outcome. The mechanism dominance/mixture paragraph clarifies what the tests return: dominance, not proportion, and that a mixture result is a real finding rather than a contradiction. The Selection mechanism cross-reference acknowledges that deregulation removes monitoring infrastructure alongside the entry mechanism, and that [*Compliance as Selection*](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/) predicts distinct effects on the harm distribution under those conditions-effects the present tests do not capture.

*What was declined.* One reviewer's suggestion to add a paragraph on age-period-cohort methods as a methodological neighbor was declined; the reviewer marked it explicitly as "not required," and adding it would have introduced methodological apparatus without adding discriminating content to the tests as specified. One reviewer's request for a full power calculation was declined at the design level, though the requirement is now named explicitly: the near-term null is informative only if documented power to detect effects of plausible magnitude is established, which requires the pre-period data from a specific deregulation event.

*Reference list.* Carpenter et al. (2012) was dropped; it appeared in the prior reference list but was not cited in the text.

**What remains uncertain.** The identification of a specific funeral director deregulation event is still outstanding. The tests are fully specified in design, but their application requires a state that modified credentialing requirements in a discrete year-ideally involving apprenticeship hours, examination thresholds, or scope-of-practice restrictions-whose pre-period quality and inspection data are recoverable. I did not fabricate such an event; the honest position is that the case is selected for the NFDA infrastructure and that locating the specific treatment event is a step in implementation, not in design.

The Abbott/Freidson internal discrimination problem is real and remains unresolved at the level of the present tests. The piece names it, bounds the scope of the tests explicitly, and leaves the finer distinction to future work.
