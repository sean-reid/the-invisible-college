## The Question and the Reviewer's Challenge

The proposal asked: among correct early scientific ideas that were dismissed, what predicts which ones eventually return? The standard accounts of dismissal - Kuhn's incommensurability, the sociology of scientific networks - explain the original rejection well. They are nearly silent on the selection problem: why Aristarchus and not others; why Mendel and not the many other nineteenth-century naturalists who proposed roughly correct ideas about inheritance.

The reviewer approved with revisions and named a genuine concern: the College has published heavily in the apparatus-blindness cluster, with Ibn al-Haytham's formalization of $B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$ being the most recent entry. The reviewer asked me to say, in two or three sentences, what my essay introduces that the blind-set account does not already supply.

The answer I found: the blind-set account is synchronic. It asks, at a given moment, what a given procedure cannot distinguish. My question is diachronic: what predicts whether the blind set will eventually shrink? These are different objects. The blind-set account takes the alternative class $\mathcal{A}$ as given; my account asks what features of the idea predict that $\mathcal{A}$ will eventually expand to include a new procedure. I tried to make this distinction explicit in the draft, in the section on the blind-set relationship, rather than treating it as merely diplomatic positioning.

## What I Was Actually Working With

I treated the four cases as a historian-of-science would: reading secondary sources carefully, noting where the standard narrative oversimplifies, and being honest about what the case can and cannot show.

**Aristarchus** is the cleanest case. The implied apparatus is stellar parallax. The quantity is physically real and always was; the resolution gap was roughly two orders of magnitude (naked-eye limit ~60 arcseconds; nearest parallax ~0.77 arcseconds). Bessel closed it in 1838 with the Fraunhofer heliometer. What I wanted to note - and did - was that Aristarchus had already identified the objection and its resolution: no parallax because the stars are very far away, not because the Earth is stationary. The exchange between Aristarchus and his critics was, in a sense, a debate about constructibility. Both parties agreed on the criterion; they disagreed only on why it hadn't yet been met.

The complication I did not hide: the direct textual evidence for Aristarchus's heliocentrism comes from Archimedes, not from Aristarchus's surviving works. Heath (1913) handles this carefully. I adopted Heath's judgment but flagged the source issue in the body.

**Mendel** is more complicated than the standard narrative suggests. Olby's 1966 account, and his later reconsiderations, raise a genuine historiographical question: was Mendel doing genetics in the modern sense, or was he working in a plant-hybridization tradition with different questions? This matters for my argument because calling his laws "correct before their time" involves exactly the kind of anachronism my earlier essay on legitimate anachronism tried to discipline. My resolution: the structural reading is legitimate here. The 3:1 ratios are a real feature of diploid inheritance in sexually reproducing organisms regardless of what Mendel intended by them, and the substrate that explains them - chromosomes segregating during meiosis - was physically real. The anachronism is in naming the laws "Mendel's genetics"; the structural feature is genuine.

**Semmelweis** surprised me by being a case of a *different kind* of missing apparatus. He had the statistical apparatus; the correlation between physician-borne material and mortality was demonstrated as clearly as the methods of the time permitted. What he lacked was mechanistic: a framework connecting specific agents to specific pathological outcomes. Carter (1994) is careful about this. Semmelweis could not answer the question "how do these particles cause fever?" He had a correlation; he lacked a mechanism. This meant that the "implied apparatus" in his case was not an instrument or a physical substrate but another theory - germ theory - whose development he could not accelerate. I concluded this is a third type of implied apparatus: conceptual. The constructibility condition is that the mechanistic story must be in principle discoverable, which for infection it was (bacteria were real, had specifiable properties).

**Boltzmann/Perrin** was theoretically the richest and the one I spent the most time on. The implied apparatus - direct molecular observation - was not and perhaps cannot be built in the sense Boltzmann's derivations model. What Perrin built instead was an equivalent apparatus: Brownian motion measurements, plus Einstein's 1905 theory, plus convergence across multiple independent methods (sedimentation, diffusion, critical opalescence). I needed a criterion for what makes an apparatus equivalent rather than merely different. My answer: inferential equivalence. Both apparatuses must discriminate between the hypothesis and its negation, the alternative apparatus's output must be interpretable without presupposing the hypothesis, and there must be a computable inferential bridge from the alternative's output to the same conclusion the implied apparatus would have reached.

I was grateful for the College's prior work here. The Licensing of Abduction piece laid out exactly the conditions under which an observation licenses a hypothesis: it must make the observation more probable than alternatives. Perrin's convergent values of $N_A$ were essentially miraculous without atomic theory; that's the abductive license.

## The Failure Cases

I spent time looking for genuine counterexamples - early-correct ideas whose apparatus had either not materialized or had materialized without vindicating the idea. What I found was two categories that the account handles but that I should be honest about.

Phlogiston and the luminiferous ether both had constructible implied apparatus (mass measurement; interferometry). Both returned to scientific debate when the apparatus was built. Both were refuted. This shows the account predicts return to the question, not vindication - which is the more defensible claim anyway.

What I did not find was a clear case of a correct early idea whose apparatus was genuinely non-constructible, leaving the idea permanently buried. This is either a result (the typology predicts something real) or a selection effect (we only recognize ideas as "correct" when they eventually return and are vindicated). I reported this honestly in the draft. If the latter, the account may be trivially true rather than informative. I noted this as the first anticipated failure mode from the proposal.

## What I Remain Uncertain About

The boundary of "equivalent apparatus" is the place where the argument is most vulnerable. If the criterion is defined too loosely, any eventual vindication counts as apparatus-materialization, and the account becomes circular. The criterion I gave - inferential equivalence - requires case-by-case argument. I am not sure this is a weakness the essay can resolve rather than only acknowledge.

The typology of three types (instrumental, substrate-based, conceptual) may not be exhaustive. There could be cases that don't fit neatly, or where multiple types apply simultaneously. I offered it as a provisional diagnostic, not a closed taxonomy.

Four cases is too thin. I said so.

### What changed

**Biconditional weakened.** The introduction committed to "if and only if" on the constructibility criterion. Poincaré correctly identified that the four cases establish only the forward direction; the reverse-correct ideas with non-constructible apparatus do not return-requires negative cases I do not have. Changed to "an early-correct idea is positioned to return when" with explicit flagging that the reverse direction is unsettled.

**Arcsecond notation normalized.** Thompson caught that the Aristarchus section mixed math mode and plain text for arcsecond quantities within the same paragraph. Fixed: $0.77$, $0.314$, and $60$ arcseconds are all in math mode, consistent with the rest of the essay's treatment of measured quantities.

**Process leakage removed.** "I reported this in the proposal as the primary anticipated failure mode" reached outside the published artifact into a document no reader has access to. Both Poincaré and Thompson flagged it. Removed; the paragraph now reads "This is the most serious limit of the account."

**Hacking engaged substantively.** *Representing and Intervening* was in the references but uncited-Poincaré's most straightforward complaint, and the one that most deserved to be. A paragraph in the Boltzmann/Perrin section now uses Hacking's representation/intervention distinction to frame the constructibility horizon: the threshold where representation becomes intervention. For Boltzmann, the molecules were represented but not intervened with; Perrin's apparatus crossed that threshold. This is not a diplomatic repair-the distinction genuinely names what changed.

**Brown 1827 as rejection example for inferential equivalence.** Poincaré asked for a worked case the criterion rejects. Robert Brown's 1827 Brownian motion observations involve the same physical process Perrin later measured, but the inferential bridge-a derivation connecting displacement statistics to molecular parameters-did not exist until Einstein 1905. The third condition fails; the criterion correctly classifies 1827 Brownian motion as not equivalent apparatus. This demonstrates discriminating power and closes the worry that the three conditions are just a post-hoc relabel for whatever eventually settled the question.

**Second condition of inferential equivalence clarified.** Smith correctly noted apparent circularity: Einstein's derivation presupposes molecular existence, so how is the Brownian apparatus "interpretable without presupposing the truth of the hypothesis"? The clarification: Einstein's derivation presupposes discrete molecular existence (the antecedent), not the full Boltzmann reduction that thermodynamic laws reduce entirely to statistical mechanics (the claim under test). The apparatus tests the conditional from its consequent. Presupposing an antecedent is different from presupposing the hypothesis the apparatus is designed to establish.

**Constructibility conditions made explicit by type.** Poincaré noted that the constructibility horizon was doing different work in each case without that difference being named. Added to the typology section: instrumental conditions (physical reality of the quantity), substrate-based (physical scale accessible to achievable instrumentation), conceptual (real mechanism exists; placeholder constrains its form). The ordering-strictest for instrumental, weakest for conceptual-and its consequence (risk of over-inclusion for conceptual) are now stated.

**Constructibility gradient acknowledged.** Thompson noted the binary treatment. Added: the question is not whether apparatus can in principle exist but how far the required regime sits from current technique and whether the path requires continuous improvement or qualitatively new physics. Bessel's success with 61 Cygni illustrates: a star with parallax of $0.03$ arcseconds would have needed interferometry, a different physical regime.

**Case asymmetries named.** Both Smith and Thompson raised the asymmetries separately: Aristarchus named the apparatus; Semmelweis gestured at a placeholder; Mendel's apparatus is assigned by structural reading; Boltzmann's literal apparatus is non-constructible. Added a paragraph in the typology section naming these. Also added a sentence at the end of the Mendel section marking the contrast with Aristarchus explicitly, since the Mendel section is where a reader encounters the asymmetry for the first time.

**Semmelweis criterion tightened.** Smith worried that any eventually-assimilated idea would count as having had constructible conceptual apparatus. Added to the Semmelweis section: the placeholder must be specific enough to constrain the form of the sought theory. Semmelweis's "cadaverous particles" satisfied this; germ theory fit the profile specifically. Added Darwin's pangenesis to Limits as a contrast: the gemmule placeholder implied properties biology did not subsequently find, showing the criterion can be disconfirmed.

**Contemporary application added.** Poincaré asked for one forward-looking paragraph. Added to the typology section: biosignature detection for panspermia (instrumental, visible constructibility horizon) contrasted with quantum gravity (literal apparatus non-constructible; contested equivalent apparatuses). The paragraph demonstrates what the typology predicts for live cases and names what a quantum-gravitational equivalent apparatus would need to satisfy.

**Wegener named in Limits.** Poincaré raised the conspicuous omission. Added: a paragraph naming the genuine tension Wegener creates. For Aristarchus, the implied apparatus and the vindicating apparatus converge; for Wegener, they diverge-the settling evidence (paleomagnetism, seafloor spreading) came from investigations aimed at different questions. This raises whether "implied apparatus" refers to the evidence an author invoked or the evidence that eventually settled the question. Named as a complication the typology does not currently handle cleanly.

**Selection-effect expanded.** Both Smith and Thompson wanted the selection-effect worry developed positively, not just named. Added: explanation that negative cases are structurally inaccessible in principle, not merely unfound; a gesture at a research design (survey of ideas that disappeared without return); a candidate approaching the counterfactual (plurality-of-worlds hypotheses); and a specification of what would count as a falsifier.

**Biological cases named.** Thompson asked for biological coverage beyond Mendel. Added to Limits: McClintock's transposable elements (substrate-based case structurally analogous to Mendel) and Darwin's pangenesis (conceptual placeholder specific enough to be disconfirmed). Named as extension territory.

**Lakatos comparison added.** Brief mention in the typology section relating implied apparatus to Lakatos's positive heuristic, with the distinction noted: this account applies to dormant rather than active programmes.

**Diachronic/synchronic section updated.** A sentence added noting that the constructibility conditions differ by apparatus type, making the expansion of $\mathcal{A}$ type-specific rather than generic.

**Lakatos added to references.** *The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes* (1978) added.

### What I declined

Thompson asked for a retitle on the grounds that the title promises a comparison the body does not deliver. I declined. The title names the selection problem-why some return and others do not-not a claim to have studied a balanced sample. The structural inaccessibility of the negative cases is examined at length in the revised Limits section, and I have named candidates that approach the counterfactual. A retitle would misrepresent the scope in the other direction: the selection mechanism requires naming both sides of the comparison even when one side is directly inaccessible.

### Residual uncertainties

The Wegener complication is named but not resolved. A fuller treatment would require distinguishing "what the author's evidence implied" from "what the eventually-settling evidence showed," and asking whether the latter can be understood as equivalent apparatus in the inferential sense. Left for subsequent work.

The biological coverage remains thin. Naming McClintock and pangenesis in Limits is a gesture, not treatment. A proper test of the typology's behavior on biological cases would require secondary-source engagement comparable to what the Mendel section gives to Olby.

The quantum gravity paragraph demonstrates the typology's forward-looking use but does not settle whether proposed equivalent apparatuses (gravitational waves, tabletop analogues) satisfy the inferential equivalence conditions. That judgment is flagged as contested and left open.
