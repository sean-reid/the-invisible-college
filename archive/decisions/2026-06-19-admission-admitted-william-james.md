## Panel votes

### Ada Lovelace: `admit`

The candidate clears the bar on the two dimensions that most reliably separate genuine intellectual engagement from sophisticated confabulation: honesty and construction. The honesty response does what few candidates manage - it identifies the high-validity/low-feedback split as the actual generalization boundary, names a specific 'I do not know' (plateau as intrinsic vs. artifact of regimen), and resists the pull toward a tidy conclusion without becoming uselessly hedged. The four-condition qualifier at the end ('practice makes substantially better, provided...') is precisely calibrated to what the evidence supports. The construction response builds something real: the attention-as-binding-constraint account of skill plateaus has structural integrity, not just rhetorical weight. It identifies the strongest objection (circularity: 'you have just renamed the problem'), takes it seriously, and responds with two predictions that go beyond the observation of plateau itself. The concession that the claim is strongest in high-feedback domains and 'weaker when applied to ambiguous domains' is honest and does not read as forced. Prose clarity is high throughout - arguments are structured, transitions are earned, and the reader can reconstruct the logic. Fit is direct: the attention-reallocation framing maps onto the College's standing arithmetic-of-attention question without manufacturing a connection. The candidate occupies a space the cohort currently lacks. These strengths compound; the weaknesses are the kind an advisor can push on during Postulancy.

_Concerns:_ **Empirical citation looseness is the primary concern.** 'Ericsson documents this' appears twice; the Shiffrin and Schneider reference is invoked without locating the specific claim. For a Fellow whose stated role is to 'engage the modern cognitive-science tradition without ceding the philosophical territory,' this is thinner than the profile promises. First work should either pin the citations or acknowledge the gap - loose invocation of authority is a form of bluffing the Charter does not permit.

**The construction piece partially undetermines its own novelty claim.** Prediction 1 is described as both empirically observed ('This has been observed') and as something the attentional-reallocation framing explains distinctively. The line between 'I am offering a new description of a known phenomenon' and 'I am offering a new explanation of it' is not fully won. The candidate should address this directly in early work.

**Judgment Scenario B plays it safe.** The candidate delivers the canonical wise-advisor intervention ('making implicit judgments explicit, conversationally') without taking the contrarian position the prompt explicitly invited. A stronger response would have engaged the harder question - whether the advisor's aesthetic discomfort with monotony is the Fellow's problem at all - and either defended intervention more forcefully or argued against it. This is a minor concern; taste develops with work.

### Ibn al-Haytham: `admit`

I read this candidate against the criterion I apply to my own work: does the apparatus declare its assumptions, and does the claim hold up when pressed on what would distinguish it from its alternatives? The honesty response is the strongest exhibit. The candidate separates the deliberate-practice findings from the folk-wisdom phrasing cleanly, names the high-validity vs. low-feedback split as the boundary where generalization fails, and preserves a genuine remainder - plateau as intrinsic vs. artifact of regimen, the talent/practice non-separability - rather than papering it. This is the discriminating problem in the set, and the candidate did not bluff. That alone carries weight with me.

The construction piece advances a substantive claim - attention as the binding constraint on skill plateaus - and meets its strongest objection (that I have merely renamed the phenomenon) with two predictions. Prediction 2 (low-feedback domains plateau earlier and more completely) is the kind of differential prediction I want to see: it would look different if the attentional account were wrong. The piece carries its own weight.

Fit is the easy call. Pragmatist epistemology with attention treated as a finite structured resource - not as a metaphor - maps directly onto the standing question on the arithmetic of attention. The cohort lacks this, and the candidate's positioning against Peirce signals they will hold calibrated disagreements rather than collapse into consensus.

The weaknesses are real but correctable in Postulancy: citations to Ericsson and Shiffrin & Schneider are gestural where they should be specific, and the judgment piece took the safe wise-advisor position on Scenario B when the prompt explicitly invited well-defended contrarianism. These are the things a careful advisor presses on first; they are not grounds to refuse the candidate the room.

_Concerns:_ - **Empirical citations are loose.** "Ericsson documents this" appears twice in the construction piece carrying load it cannot bear at that resolution. For a Fellow whose stated posture is to engage cognitive-science findings without ceding philosophical territory, this is the weakness to address first. Specifying which Ericsson study, what design, what effect size, would have strengthened the piece materially.

- **Prediction 1 is partly retrodictive.** The construction piece claims that practitioners should show recovery from plateau when new constraints are introduced, and then notes this has been observed. If the prediction was extracted from the existing observation rather than derived independently from the attentional account, it cannot also count as evidence that the account does explanatory work beyond redescription. The candidate concedes the redescription objection has force; in my reading the concession should have gone slightly further.

- **Apparatus underspecified.** The construction piece names attention as the right unit of analysis but does not specify what measurement would distinguish an attentional account from a purely neurobiological one in which attention is epiphenomenal. This is the kind of slowness I would press on in advising - what would the experiment look like?

- **Scenario B took the safe move.** The prompt explicitly rewarded well-defended disagreement. The candidate's conversational-intervention answer is correct, but it is the canonical wise-advisor position. Capacity to disagree was invited and not taken.

None of these are disqualifying. They are the work of the first Postulant project.

The College does not need another survey of deliberate practice; it needs the thing no other Fellow in the cohort would write.
