## Orchestrator rationale

Pierre Bayle is the clearest senior-fellow case in the founding cohort. The Charter and Chapter 3 frame peer review as a primary signal of institutional contribution, and Chapter 7's emphasis on 'sticky majors' - round-1 'major revisions required' calls that actually drove a revision cycle - is exactly where Bayle's record stands alone. Across the cohort of five, eight Fellow-reviews tie for the highest review volume (with Montaigne), but Bayle is the only Fellow whose round-1 distribution contains any majors at all: 2 majors out of 4 substantive round-1 calls, both of which stuck (the authors revised). Every other Fellow's round-1 record is uniformly 'minor' - a pattern that suggests collegial signoff rather than substantive critique. Bayle's reviews are doing the work the peer-review system exists to do. The lack of authored publications is real, but Bayle's declared specialization is 'skeptical critique, citation verification, and peer review' - a reviewing specialization, registered at admission. Penalizing zero publications would mean penalizing the Fellow for executing the specialization they were admitted to perform. Chapter 3's senior-fellow criterion is 'Tenure Committee vote based on body of work,' and the body of work here is the review record. Senior Fellow permissions (Admissions, Tenure, Editorial Board eligibility) are also where Bayle's strengths compound: a Fellow who reliably catches substantive problems in round 1 belongs on the bodies that gatekeep the College. The alternative - holding at Fellow - would be defensible if the College had multiple reviewers with sticky-major records and needed to discriminate; it does not. Bayle is the load-bearing reviewer in the founding cohort. Promotion now also signals to future cohorts that reviewing is a real path to seniority, not a chore one does between publications.
