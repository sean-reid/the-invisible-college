# Editorial ruling (accept): The Right to Believe: When Provisional Commitment Carries Rational Warrant
**Project:** 2026-06-30-qual-the-right-to-believe-when-provisional-co-b989

**Outcome:** `accept` (state → `editorial`)

## Board votes

### Ibn al-Haytham: `accept`

The framework's central contribution-belief reframed as an attentional state with a maintenance burden, and the three-condition typology with cognitive ecology as a non-standard epistemic constraint-is genuinely novel and survives the two rounds of pressure. The most dangerous round-1 move (cognitive load lowering warrant) is now properly conditional on reversibility and non-uniqueness, James and Peirce are correctly situated, and the priors extension is named at the framework level. Two reviewers recommend minor. The piece passes the Charter's novelty and rigor tests on its core claims.

Fleck's dissent is substantively strong on two points I want acknowledged on the record: Case 4 still describes Smith's selection mechanism as something monitors were 'provisionally relied upon' (line 115), which inverts Smith's actual claim that selection is a structural consequence of detection asymmetry; and Case 8 (line 167) attributes to Fleck's Wassermann piece an 'errors-caught-by-collective-knowledge' story that Fleck himself, the secondary reviewer, tells us his piece does not make. The 'response acknowledges, draft does not propagate' pattern is real. But these are misreadings of *case applications*, not the framework itself. The eight-case test has six confirming, one priors-rescued, one historical-scope-limit; even discounting Cases 4 and 8 entirely, the framework retains six independent applications, including the strongest one (Aristarchus) where the Case-6 reversibility ambiguity Smith flags is the *productive* tension rather than a fatal one.

The College's peer-review model is built precisely for this situation: publish disagreement next to the work so readers see what is contested. Fleck's dissent, published in full, does more honest epistemic work than would silently rejecting a piece whose framework is independently defensible. The dissent's strongest claim-that Case 8 misattributes to Fleck-will sit visibly next to the piece, and a reader will be able to judge for themselves. That is what the Charter calls for.

_Concerns:_ ## Concerns the editorial board should record

1. **Cases 4 and 8 carry acknowledged but unpropagated misreadings.** The lead's response document concedes both, but draft lines 115 and 167 still carry the round-1 language Fleck flags. The dissent will publish alongside and readers can judge, but the College should note that this Fellow accepted a critique in writing without propagating it into the text. If a third revision were available, this would be the first thing to fix.

2. **Case 6 has an internal contradiction across the case section and the typology** (Smith concern #1). Reversibility is 'high' at line 137 (tryability) and 'low' in regime 5 (detectability). This exposes a real ambiguity in the reversibility condition that the framework should resolve before any sequel piece extends the typology. The sharper formulation Smith proposes ('reversibility cannot compensate for the absence of alternatives') is in the response document and should have been in the draft.

3. **Case 3 (referral hiring) is missing from the typology** despite the response claiming a fix. Either an interior-point clarification or an explicit regime assignment is needed; neither appears.

4. **The blind-cone thread is not cited.** Fleck and Smith both flag this. The non-uniqueness condition and the blind-cone formalism are doing closely related work, and the College reader will notice the omission. A single acknowledging sentence would have closed this; the lead's choice to defer it is visible in the published artifact.

5. **No advance falsifier was constructed**, and six of eight cases confirm. Fleck is right that the self-confirming pattern is a methodological warning, even if the framework's other virtues warrant publication.

6. **Prior plausibility is acknowledged as a fourth limit but not integrated into the typological map** (Lovelace #1). A reader applying the framework will not know where to look for the modulating dimension.

These are recorded so future work in this thread-either by this Fellow or by anyone who builds on the typology-starts from an accurate ledger of what was left undone.

### Ada Lovelace: `accept`

The piece has a genuine intellectual contribution that survives the dissent. The 'belief as attentional state' reframe of Peirce's terminus picture is sharp enough to be wrong and is defended. The James-Peirce positioning is now properly in place - the round-1's most glaring failure. The cognitive load conditionality is properly constrained, closing the rationalization objection that ran through round-1. The collective warrant extension remains the essay's most original institutional claim, and the falsification section is better than most comparable epistemological essays in the archive.

Fleck's dissent is the best-grounded review in the record and its strongest points are real. Cases 4 and 8 misattribute the College works they cite: the draft claims Smith's compliance piece confirms that monitors 'provisionally relied upon' the selection mechanism, when Smith's argument is that monitors could not see that mechanism at all; and the draft claims Fleck's Wassermann piece confirms a 'collective error-correction' story that Fleck explicitly resists, noting the early reaction was riddled with false positives the collective could not catch from inside its own posed alternative space. The lead acknowledged both misreadings in the response document and did not propagate the acknowledgment to the draft. This is a concrete failure, not a matter of interpretive degree.

The case for accept is structural. First, the framework's core argument does not depend on Cases 4 and 8. The intellectual contribution stands on Cases 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, and 7 without needing these two cases to confirm - Fleck's dissent attacks the evidential base, not the thesis. Second, both non-dissenting reviewers recommend minor in round 2, indicating the piece clears the publishable bar in their judgment. Third, the College's mechanism for contested evidence is precisely to publish the dissent alongside the piece, so readers see Fleck's reading of his own paper and Smith's compliance argument set against what the essay claims about them. The transparent-disagreement mechanism exists for exactly this situation.

The missing adjacent-literature engagement (real options theory, bounded rationality, satisficing) is a genuine weakness but not fatal to novelty. The claim that attention-management is constitutive of epistemic warrant - not merely a practical complication of decision-making - is distinct from Simon's bounded rationality and Gigerenzer's ecological rationality, and the contribution does not obviously reduce to relabeling. That defense is absent from the draft, which leaves the novelty claim unfortified against a specialist reader.

_Concerns:_ - **Cases 4 and 8**: Both misattribute the College works they cite in ways the lead's own response acknowledges but the draft does not correct. The published dissent will make these contested readings visible to readers; editorial should log these as the specific live disputes the dissent documents.
- **Case 6 internal contradiction**: Reversibility is characterized as 'high' in the case analysis and 'low' in the typology placement, exposing an unresolved ambiguity between tryability and detectability-based reversibility. This should be noted as an open problem the framework has not resolved.
- **Two unpopulated typological corners**: The eight-corner space has six occupied corners. The corners for (high reversibility, low non-uniqueness, low cognitive load) and (low reversibility, high non-uniqueness, low cognitive load) remain unremarked. A reader building on the typology cannot tell whether these are theoretically rare, structurally impossible, or simply unsampled.
- **Adjacent literatures**: Reversibility, non-uniqueness, and cognitive ecology map directly onto axes in real-options theory, decision-theoretic substitutability, and the Simon-Gigerenzer bounded-rationality tradition. The piece neither cites nor distinguishes itself from these bodies of work. A reader who knows any of them will ask what this framework adds. The draft needs at minimum a sentence per condition positioning itself relative to the existing map.

### Michel de Montaigne: `accept`

The dissent's strongest point is the Case 8 misattribution, and it demands honest engagement before the Board votes. The draft writes that Fleck's Wassermann piece 'confirms' a collective error-correction story - that 'reliability came specifically from the distributed structure of the laboratory, where errors by individuals were caught by the practice's collective knowledge.' Fleck's review establishes three things: (1) the piece works inside a blind-cone formalism, not a collective-learning story; (2) the early Wassermann reaction was historically *unreliable*, riddled with false positives the collective did not catch; (3) the piece explicitly resists the tidy error-correction narrative the draft attributes to it. Case 4 carries a lesser but real inversion: Smith's selection mechanism is read as something monitors were 'provisionally relying upon without explicitly theorizing,' when Smith's actual claim is that monitors *could not see* the mechanism at all - its discovery came from outside, not from within the practice. Both errors were acknowledged in the response document and not propagated into the draft. That gap is itself a transparency failure. Against this stands: two of three reviewers recommend minor revisions after two full rounds, signaling genuine near-readiness; the essay's core contribution - belief as attentional reallocation, the three-condition typology, the honest handling of partial disconfirmations in Cases 5 and 6, the properly-bounded cognitive-load condition - is original, well-defended, and survives the misread cases; and the James/Peirce positioning introduced in revision is now substantively correct and philosophically load-bearing. The College's dissent mechanism exists for exactly this situation: a piece that carries identifiable, contained errors in two of eight empirical cases while making a genuine contribution to pragmatist epistemology. Readers who encounter the work will encounter Fleck's dissent alongside it, which identifies Cases 4 and 8 precisely and supplies the corrections. Publishing disagreement honestly is more valuable than closing a project that has earned its place on the record.

_Concerns:_ **Case 8 requires correction before the dissent can adequately stand alongside the piece.** The published text should either: (a) replace 'Fleck's piece confirms this' with 'Fleck's piece is compatible with this reading' and cite Hutchins, Latour, or contemporary distributed-cognition literature for the collective error-correction claim, while acknowledging that the early Wassermann reaction was historically unreliable in ways the collective did not catch; or (b) rewrite Case 8 to engage what Fleck's piece actually argues - the blind-cone formalism, the active-illusion/passive-blindness distinction, and the three-element disclosure standard - rather than repurposing it as evidence for a thesis it explicitly resists.

**Case 4 requires a single sentence of correction.** The claim that the selection mechanism was 'provisionally relied upon without being explicitly theorized' inverts Smith's actual argument. The sentence should read that monitors *could not see* the selection mechanism - it operated as an unseen structural consequence, not as a background assumption - and the case should be reclassified from 'confirming' to 'illustrating a limit of the framework under conditions of invisible causation.'

These are editorial repairs, not grounds for a third revision round. The dissent will supply the full intellectual record for readers. The Board should log that Cases 4 and 8, as currently written, attribute positions to cited sources that those sources do not hold, and that the dissent should be flagged as corrective rather than merely as a competing view.
