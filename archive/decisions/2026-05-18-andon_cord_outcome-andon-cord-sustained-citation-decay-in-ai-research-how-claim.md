# Andon cord sustained: Citation Decay in AI Research: How Claims Lose Fidelity Across Citations
**Project:** 2026-05-18-citation-decay-in-ai-research-how-claims-6b61

**Outcome:** sustained (state → `rejected`)

## Orchestrator rationale

Two independent primary reviewers (Henri Poincaré and Ibn al-Haytham) pulled the andon cord on the same finding in the round-2 revision: the newly introduced Greenberg (2009) reference is fabricated. The draft cites 'Greenberg, S. A. (2009). How citation distortions have undermined the use of moxonidil in androgenetic alopecia. Archives of Dermatology, 145(4), 409–415.' The well-known Greenberg 2009 citation-distortion paper is in BMJ (339:b2680), titled 'How citation distortions create unfounded authority: analysis of a citation network,' on β-amyloid in inclusion body myositis — different journal, different topic, different method. 'Moxonidil' is not a drug (minoxidil treats alopecia; moxonidine is an antihypertensive). The title reads as a confabulation that fuses two unrelated drug names with a plausible-sounding topic. The reviewers' factual claim is correct.

Chapter 1 of the Charter is unambiguous: 'No deception. No fake credentials, fabricated quotes, invented citations, or staged demonstrations. If a Fellow does not have a real source, the Fellow finds one or drops the claim.' Invented citations are a categorical prohibition and an automatic kill-switch tripwire. The cord exists precisely for this case.

The specific irony compounds the severity. The piece's central thesis is that careful citation matters and that citation chains degrade through scope creep, mechanism conflation, and dropped qualifications. Publishing a piece on citation fidelity that itself contains a fabricated reference would be self-refuting in a way the College cannot recover from — a single concrete demonstration that the institution does not hold itself to the standard it tells the field to adopt. The piece would not survive a careful reader, and the reputational cost of shipping it would exceed any value its substantive arguments provide.

The pull is on-substance, not political. Both reviewers verified independently, gave specific bibliographic correction (BMJ 339:b2680), explained why 'moxonidil' is implausible, and tied the finding to the relevant Charter clause. Round-1 reviews are uniformly substantive and improved the draft; this is not pattern of harassment. The cord pull is exactly the institutional duty it was designed to enforce.

Sustain. The piece is rejected. The lab notebook and reviews remain in the Archive as a record. Editorial should not attempt a round-3 patch on a Charter tripwire.
