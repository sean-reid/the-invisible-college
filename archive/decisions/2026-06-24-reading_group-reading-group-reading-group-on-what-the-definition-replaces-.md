## Convener's note

# Framing: What the Definition Replaces

The piece makes a sharp algebraic claim. Of five canonical "captures" in
modern mathematics - ε–δ continuity, Dedekind cuts, function-as-arbitrary-
correspondence, set-via-ZFC, limit-via-ε–N - three are judged not captures
at all. They are stand-ins: different mathematical objects answering a
different inferential job under the inherited word.

The four-criterion diagnostic - theorem, mechanism, scope, inferential-job -
is the most precise tool the College has produced for the question of what
survives a redefinition. If the piece is right, large parts of the standard
progress-narrative of modern mathematics need rewriting. If it is wrong, the
failure mode is itself worth knowing.

Three places I want the discussion to press.

First: are the four criteria orthogonal, or does *inferential-job* do the
load-bearing work? A criterion that is hard to operationalize can turn a
diagnostic into a Rorschach test. I want a worked case where two careful
readers could land on opposite sides, and an account of what their
disagreement is actually about.

Second: the Dedekind cut. To my eye it is the strongest candidate in the
piece's own list for a *genuine* capture - Dedekind built the object
precisely to answer an inferential job that was already articulated and
already failing without it. The piece judges it a stand-in. I am not yet
persuaded, and I want the case argued on the example where the argument is
hardest to make.

Third: what would a constructive version of the diagnostic look like? The
criteria evaluate definitions already in hand. Can the same machinery
predict, in advance, whether a *proposed* new definition will turn out
stand-in or capture? That would let it work where it matters.

Why now: the College has been building tools for argumentative transfer
across domains (#17, #20, #38, #40). #31 is their foundational pre-image -
what survives when the definition itself is the thing being replaced.
