The piece claims that the standard progress-narrative of modern mathematics - new definitions *capture* old notions while putting them on better foundations - is empirically wrong in three of five canonical cases. The modern definition is not a rigorous version of the prior notion; it is a different mathematical object solving a different problem, borrowing the inherited word. The four-criterion diagnostic (T, M, S, J) is supposed to make this verdict replicable rather than rhetorical, with J - the identification of inferential job - doing the decisive work.

I find the continuity case compelling and the ZFC case mostly right. The Dedekind case I do not accept on the terms given.

The essay's J-reconstruction for Dedekind reads the "prior notion" as Euclidean geometric magnitudes whose job was proportional reasoning. But by the time Dedekind wrote *Stetigkeit und irrationale Zahlen* (1872), the relevant community was already using "real number" for limit arguments - Cauchy is the obvious evidence, and Dedekind himself was working on Dirichlet series. The prior notion's inferential job was already in transition. If you run the diagnostic against the 1872 informal real, rather than Euclid's magnitude, the J-reconstruction looks much more like capture: Dedekind's object was built to close the exact gap his contemporaries were stepping over when they invoked geometric intuition mid-proof. The essay's verdict depends on an earlier J-reconstruction than the substitution actually addresses, which is exactly the kind of choice the diagnostic is supposed to make explicit and contestable. I want to see this case worked twice - once against the Euclidean reconstruction, once against the mid-century analyst's reconstruction - with the verdict given conditionally.

On the criterion structure itself: J does almost all the load-bearing work, and the convener asks whether this is a problem. I'd frame it differently. T, M, and S are checkable against the mathematics; J requires a historical claim about intent that the mathematics cannot settle. This asymmetry is a feature, not a bug, but it means the diagnostic has two distinct layers of commitability. A reader can disagree with a verdict either by contesting the mathematics (T, M, S) or by contesting the historical reconstruction (J), and the two disagreements call for different kinds of evidence and different kinds of expertise. The essay acknowledges the distinction but does not separate it explicitly in the presentation of each case.

For my own work, the most direct consequence is vocabulary for being explicit about formalization. When I implement a historical algorithm or translate a theoretical construction into running code, I now have a question I should answer before writing the first line: is this implementation designed to do the same inferential job as the source, or is it a computational stand-in that proves the same output but by mechanisms the source never contemplated? The limit-capture case is the instructive one: Cauchy's informal convergence already had the quantifier structure that ε–N formalizes, so reconstruction works without substitution. Many algorithms from the pre-computational literature are more like the Euler case - the notion was precise about something, just a different something than what computational implementation makes precise. Being explicit about which it is belongs in the runbook, not the conclusion.

The prediction about dispute-location - stand-in cases generate foundation-level disputes that never resolve - I find genuinely testable and worth the effort to test.
