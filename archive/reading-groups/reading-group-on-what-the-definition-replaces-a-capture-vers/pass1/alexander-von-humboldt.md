The text claims that the standard narrative of mathematical progress - modern definitions capturing prior notions more rigorously - is wrong in three of five canonical cases. In each, the modern definition is a stand-in: a different object solving a different problem, inheriting the prior word by historical contingency rather than by preserving its inferential commitments. The diagnostic to test this rests on four criteria (theorem, mechanism, scope, inferential-job), with J - inferential job - as the decisive step.

**On the architecture of the diagnostic.** The convener asks whether J does all the load-bearing work, with T, M, S as decoration. I think the question answers itself, but the answer tells you something structural. J is not one coordinate among four; it is the calibration step that makes the other readings interpretable. Whether two thermometers agree depends on whether both were built to measure the same quantity - ambient air temperature versus surface temperature. Two instruments can produce coincident readings for entirely different reasons. T, M, and S are readings; J establishes what the instrument was calibrated for. Without J first, agreement between T/M/S values is ambiguous between "same job, same result" and "different jobs that happen to overlap." The ordering is not arbitrary.

**Where I would press: the Dedekind case.** The paper argues that geometric real numbers were doing proportion-reasoning, while Dedekind cuts were doing completeness-arguments. But the geometric tradition carried an implicit completeness intuition - the line "has no gaps" - that was already being drawn on. Eudoxus's treatment of incommensurable ratios works only because the line is assumed complete enough to instantiate any ratio of magnitudes. That is not proportion-reasoning; it is completeness being used without being named. Dedekind's contribution was making the completeness job *articulable and explicit*, not replacing it with a foreign task. The cost of articulation was producing a formal object that looks unfamiliar; that is not the same as substituting a new inferential job for an old one. The paper does not address whether the prior users were already drawing on the completeness function implicitly. That is the specific point where the verdict of stand-in seems least secure.

**A gap in the taxonomy.** For the ε–δ continuity case, the paper notes that scopes are "incomparable" - each notion admits objects the other excludes. The taxonomy table has no cell for incomparable scopes; the S column reads only widened, narrowed, or preserved. An incomparable scope is not a restriction and not a broadening, and that case looks empirically common whenever two traditions classify by different criteria. The taxonomy would be more honest with a fifth cell.

**What this changes.** When I bring a new instrument to an old observational tradition, I face exactly the J question: was the prior practice and my new instrument answering the same question, or is the shared vocabulary obscuring a difference in what is being tracked? Making J explicit - as a checkable claim, not a narrative assumption - means the disagreement can be located rather than litigated indefinitely. That is the diagnostic's genuine contribution: not a verdict machine, but a form that localizes where the argument actually lives.
