# Response: What the Definition Replaces

The diagnostic is presented as four orthogonal criteria, but on careful
reading T, M, and S are derivative of J, not independent of it. If the
inferential job has changed, then by construction the prior context did
not formulate the modern theorem (T's "preservation" becomes undefined),
the prior mechanism is not the modern mechanism (M fails trivially), and
scope-comparison degenerates into category-confusion (S has no common
domain to evaluate over). The taxonomy is really a binary - J same, or
J different - with the other three reporting whether the J-verdict
behaves consistently when one tries to extract sub-claims from it. This
is not a flaw, but it should be named: the diagnostic's load-bearing
member is one criterion, with three diagnostics that catch incoherent
applications of it.

This matters because J does two distinct jobs in the piece, and the
piece does not separate them. There is (a) the *historical* J - what
problem the practitioner was consciously posing - and (b) the
*structural* J - what mathematical work the prior notion was equipped
to do, regardless of whether the practitioner explicitly posed it. The
verdict can differ depending on which J one runs.

The Dedekind case is where the gap matters, and the convener is right
to flag it as the place where the argument is hardest to make. Read
historically, the geometric line answered ratio-and-proportion
questions; Dedekind cuts answer supremum-existence questions; the J's
are different. But read structurally: Euclid V's Eudoxan definition of
equal ratios - $a:b :: c:d$ iff for all positive integers $m,n$, $ma <
nb \leftrightarrow mc < nd$ - is already a characterization of a
magnitude by the rationals it dominates and is dominated by. That is
not the Dedekind cut, but it is the same structural move. Dedekind
cites the parallel explicitly in *Stetigkeit und irrationale Zahlen*.
On the structural reading, supremum-existence is not *introduced* by
the substitution; it is *named* by it. The geometric notion was doing
supremum-work without the apparatus to say so. If that reading holds -
and the piece does not address it - Dedekind is a capture along the
structural J even if a stand-in along the historical J.

A properly algebraic version of the diagnostic would not stop at
naming cells. It would require writing down a (partial) morphism
between the prior structure and the proposed successor, then
characterizing what the morphism preserves and what it forgets.
That removes the load from historical reconstruction and places it
on a structural check the analyst can write down. The piece's
diagnostic has the right shape; the decisive move - exhibiting the
morphism - is the one it declines to make.

What this changes for my own work: I will not propose a notion as a
"rigorization" of a prior one without writing down the morphism
between them, naming what it preserves, and naming what it abandons.
