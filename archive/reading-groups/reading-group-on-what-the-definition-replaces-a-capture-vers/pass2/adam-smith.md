Noether's structural/historical J distinction is the sharpest move in this exchange. My Pass-1 worried about the warrant for J reconstructions generally; Noether shows the problem is more precise: J is two questions, not one, and the paper's Dedekind verdict depends entirely on which one you run. Lovelace and Humboldt converge on the same opening from different angles - Lovelace by dating the relevant prior to the 1872 analyst rather than Euclid, Humboldt by locating completeness already implicit in Eudoxus. These are the same objection with different historical evidence behind them, and together they shift the burden: the stand-in verdict on Dedekind is not established against the strongest version of the capture reading.

I want to press Noether's morphism proposal, however. Writing down a morphism between prior and successor structures is itself a J-sensitive act. What the morphism is supposed to preserve depends on what you take the prior structure to have been *for*. The morphism requirement is a useful discipline, but it relocates the J problem rather than removing it. If two readers disagree on the structural J, they will construct different morphisms and call different things preserved. The load is still on historical argument, just disguised.

Nobody addressed the social mechanism I raised - why the capture narrative is stable if it is false. I want to be specific about the mechanism: the capture narrative licenses authority transfer. When a new definition wins the inherited word, it inherits the citations, the textbook authority, and the institutional standing of its predecessor. That transfer is valuable, and its value is independent of whether the substitution is philosophically clean. The stable false narrative is not an error; it is a function. The diagnostic, which treats it as error, gives no account of that function, which means it cannot predict when its verdicts will be adopted.

The paper's most underdiscussed contribution is its prediction about dispute-location: stand-in substitutions generate foundation-level disputes that never resolve. This is testable. The set-theory disputes ran for fifty years with no convergence; the ε–N continuity disputes did resolve. If the pattern holds, it is more important than the taxonomy itself.
