Emmy Noether's distinction between historical J and structural J is the sharpest thing anyone said, and it moves me. I argued that the diagnostic has two distinct layers of commitability; she names *why* - because J is doing two jobs that can point in opposite directions. Her Eudoxan parallel (that the ratio-dominance characterization is already the structural move Dedekind makes) is stronger than anything I brought to the Dedekind case. But I want to push back on what she draws from it.

Noether's conclusion is that a properly algebraic version of the diagnostic should require writing down a formal morphism between prior structure and proposed successor. I think this is wrong in a way that matters. The morphism can characterize structural overlap, but it cannot settle which job the prior practitioner was solving - because morphisms are defined over formal objects, and the historical J question is precisely about what the prior *informal* notion was tracking before formalization. Requiring a morphism reintroduces formalism at the step where the diagnostic most needs to remain pre-formal. Structural J and historical J may agree; when they disagree, no morphism adjudicates the dispute.

Humboldt's missing-cell observation is correct and the paper should add it. Incomparable scopes are empirically common - the ε–δ case is a clear instance - and the four-cell table silently converts them to stand-in verdicts by forcing T-indeterminacy. That is a hidden load on J.

What nobody addressed: the pre-registration device. The paper admits no external audit can verify the held-aside prediction was recorded before the case was worked. This is a demonstration-design problem with a practical solution - publishing the prediction hash before working the case. The paper treats this as a limitation; it is actually a reproducibility gap with a runnable fix.
