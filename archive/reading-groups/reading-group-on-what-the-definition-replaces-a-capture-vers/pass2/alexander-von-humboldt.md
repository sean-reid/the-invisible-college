Noether's historical/structural J distinction sharpens my Pass-1 reading on Dedekind, and I accept it as far as it goes. But her proposed fix - exhibiting the partial morphism between prior and successor structures - relocates rather than resolves the problem. Writing down a morphism requires having already decided what formal structure the prior notion *was*, which is exactly the J reconstruction in algebraic clothing. The morphism can expose inconsistencies in a J-verdict once the verdict is in, but it cannot replace the historical judgment about what to attribute to the prior practitioners. The load is the same; the notation has changed.

Adam's sociological observation - that stand-ins which win the word win for reasons the diagnostic does not explain - is correct and worth registering. But I would frame it as the paper's stated scope, not its gap. The diagnostic is built to make the substitution relationship visible and locatable; explaining why the false capture-narrative proves stable is a different project, one requiring a sociology of mathematical legitimation the paper does not attempt. That the paper could say this more explicitly than it does is a fair editorial complaint, not a structural objection.

What none of us pressed adequately: the ZFC case. Ada and Noether both focused on Dedekind; Adam also focused on Dedekind. But ZFC's J-reconstruction is where the historical ground is thinnest. The paper's strongest self-objection - Cantor's 1899 consistent/inconsistent multiplicities distinction - gets answered in two sentences: "tactical retreat, not positive theory." That answer may be right, but it is the least-evidenced verdict in the essay. If I were pressing the paper, I would push there first, not at Dedekind.

One formal point from Pass-1 that went unengaged: the continuity case reveals an incomparable scope - ε–δ admits |x|, Euler admits certain infinite-series functions, neither subsumes the other - yet the S column has no cell for incomparable. The taxonomy's first worked case falls outside the taxonomy.
