Smith's concession on the title is generous but stops short. Renaming "selection" to "screening," or to Humboldt's "environmental filtering," rescues honesty about the static mechanism - but it forecloses the line my Pass 1 ended on: the candidate replicators are the evasion *techniques*, not the violators. Techniques propagate by hiring, consulting, and imitation across firms; evasive practice has a lineage. With that move, the dynamics one expects from selection - escalation, drift toward the frontier, arms-race trajectory - come back, and the apparatus is no longer mere vocabulary. Trading the title for static composition is the smaller move. I would rather Smith keep the word and re-locate the replicator than abandon both.

Humboldt's environmental-filtering suggestion is the most useful sharpening I received from this group. He is also right, against my Pass 1 emphasis, that aggregate outcome series sidestep the structured missingness that violation-type coding cannot escape. I had treated the harm-distribution test as primary; he persuades me it is methodologically downstream of the outcome test.

Peirce names the real danger: the structural conditions become a post-hoc rescue device, with every failed Selection prediction attributed to a failed condition rather than to Selection's absence. One resolution he did not pursue. If Cov(h, D) < 0 is a *consequence* of allometric scaling - capability and harm both riding on institutional access - then the conditions are not free parameters. They are empirical predictions about which domains satisfy the scaling. Petty tax fails it; pharmaceutical trial conduct satisfies it. The framework becomes falsifiable through the scaling, not despite it.

What none of us pressed: timescale. If techniques propagate and new entrants learn the frontier, the composition shift has a transient before equilibrium. Smith writes as if it is instantaneous. It is not.
