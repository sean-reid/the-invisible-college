# Pass 2: Response

Thompson's correction on vocabulary is decisive: violators do not reproduce, so calling this "selection" imports temporal dynamics the mechanism does not license. Smith concedes correctly. The title should change; the compositional claim survives. Humboldt's suggestion-environmental filtering-is more precise and avoids the biological entailments.

But both Thompson and Humboldt name something deeper than terminology. The detectability-harm correlation that undergirds the argument is unsupported empirically in all three cases. More urgent: Humboldt identifies a confirmation problem I want to sharpen.

Smith's mechanism produces exactly the condition that defeats observation. Violations outside the detection frontier remain unobserved; when they surface through secondary channels (post-hoc audits, follow-up studies, changed disclosure rules), they are a biased subsample-they are the violations sophisticated enough to leave traces. Smith's three proposed tests (harm distribution shift, sophistication trend, detection-lag pattern) are all indirect, inferring the residual pool from the cases that leak through. These are not representative samples of the violations the mechanism predicts dominate the residual.

The deeper issue: the piece does not confront the alibi built into the framework. If the harm distribution test fails-if newly detected violations are not more harmful-the response can be "that is because we are seeing only the violations that became visible, not the population Selection actually produced." The mechanism is internally consistent; but it admits a standing explanation for negative evidence. Smith is honest that the evidence is "consistent with" rather than "proving" the claim. But the piece proceeds as if this honest admission settles the matter, when it is precisely where the inferential work would need to be done.

I agree with my Pass-1 reading: monitoring programs measuring only frequency are missing information about harm composition, and collecting better data is defensible on grounds independent of whether Channel C is operating. But the policy implication slides from "the mechanism is plausible" to "therefore, programs should be designed to test it." That is weaker once you acknowledge the direct test may be structurally unavailable.

The piece asks a real question and specifies a plausible answer. It names conditions under which you could distinguish the mechanism from alternatives. But it understates the cost of the measurement frontier it maps.
