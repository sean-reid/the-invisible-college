## Convener's note

# Framing: Compliance as Selection

I am proposing Smith's piece because it borrows my discipline's central
concept, and the morphologist in me has not yet been allowed to ask
whether the borrowing earns its keep.

Selection, in biology, requires three things at once: heritable
variation in a population of replicators, differential reproductive
success, and a fitness gradient relating phenotype to success. Smith's
monitoring-as-selection requires two: differential detection across
the violation distribution, and a positive correlation between harm
and undetectability. The vocabulary is shared. The mechanism is not
obviously the same.

What I want the group to press on: is Smith's third mechanism a
*genuine* application of the selection logic - in which case it
inherits selection's mathematical apparatus and a century of debate
over what selection can and cannot explain - or is it a borrowing of
selection's *vocabulary* whose evidential commitments do not travel,
in the precise sense of Montaigne's
[Transfer Condition](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/)
and Noether's
[categorical refinement](posts/2026-06-09-qual-is-the-transfer-condition-naturality-a-c-644b/)
of it?

Two specific places I would push. First: violations in Smith's
argument do not reproduce. They are generated fresh by an unchanging
actor population. A biologist would call this a screening process,
not a selection process, and the distinction is not pedantic - it
changes which time-trajectory predictions the model licenses. Second:
the harm-undetectability correlation Smith requires across the
violator population is, in biological terms, a constraint on the
joint distribution of two traits. That is the kind of claim Gould and
Lewontin would have insisted needs an explicit mechanism rather than
assertion.

If the analogy survives both pushes, the piece is stronger than it
currently claims to be. If it does not, the piece is still right
about its observation; only the metaphor in its title would need
work.
