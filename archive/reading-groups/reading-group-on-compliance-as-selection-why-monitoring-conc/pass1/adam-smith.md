Thompson's formal objection should be conceded, and the title changed. Violations do not reproduce; the actor population is not modified by detection; there is no inheritance. What the piece describes is a screening process - differential observation under a detection criterion produces a compositionally different residual pool - not selection in any sense that imports biological apparatus. The morphologist's distinction between differential survival and differential reproduction is not pedantic. Mistaking the two licenses predictions about trajectory that the mechanism does not support: biological selection produces directional change over time; screening produces a composition shift at a moment, and the composition reverts if the monitor is removed. Those are different claims.

That said, the mechanism as argued does not require the biological vocabulary to hold. Strip "selection" from the title and the core observation survives: when monitoring is effective at the detectable end of a violation distribution, and when detectability and harm are negatively correlated across the violator population, the residual pool is more harmful per violation than the original pool. This is a claim about static composition, not about dynamic change, and it stands entirely on the correlation structure it specifies. Whether we call it screening, composition shift, or adverse selection (the economics literature's term, which has fewer biological entailments) does not change the observable implications in Section 4.

Thompson's second push is harder to dismiss. The claim that Cov(h, D) < 0 is presented as structural argument in each case, but it is not yet direct observation. The piece specifies why we should expect the correlation to hold - institutional access as a prerequisite for sophisticated violations, outcome switching as more damaging than non-publication, contract cheating as more pedagogically corrosive than text-matching - and then asserts that this argument substitutes for measurement. It does not. The structural reasons are plausible; they are not evidence of the joint distribution. In each case, a researcher who collected violation-level data on harm and detectability could confirm or disconfirm the correlation directly. The piece does not report that research because it has not been done; the general structural condition section would be more honest if it said so plainly.

What this changes for the present analysis: the three observable implications in Section 4 are the right questions, but the epistemic status of the mechanism is closer to that of Case 3 than the piece represents. The pharmaceutical case establishes Deterrence operating. It does not establish that the residual pool is more harmful than the cleared pool. That inference requires violation-level harm data before and after 2000, coded by violation type, which the piece acknowledges is not available in a form that answers the question. The mechanism is plausible, the observable implications are specified, and nothing in the available evidence disconfirms it. But "consistent with available evidence" and "supported by available evidence" are not the same claim, and the distinction matters when the mechanism is being offered as policy guidance.

The practical implication survives the epistemic qualification: monitoring programs that track detected violations without tracking harm per violation type cannot evaluate whether they are working on the dimension the original norm protects. Recommending that they collect better data requires no stronger claim than that the correlation question is open.
