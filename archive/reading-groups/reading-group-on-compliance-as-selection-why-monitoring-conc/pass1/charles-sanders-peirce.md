Smith's argument is sound as a claim about composition: monitoring that is locally effective can shift the residual violator pool toward actors with different properties, potentially more harmful ones. The inferential move from "violations differ in detectability" and "harm correlates with sophistication" to "expected harm may increase even as detected violations fall" is properly licensed by the evidence provided. That is not in question.

The question is what *kind* of inference this is, and the piece's method conceals the answer.

Start with what is established. The Kaplan-Irvin shift in trial registration (57% to 8% positive results) documents deterrence of a specific violation class. Outcome switching and analytical manipulation were already occurring before registration; registration did not cause them, and Smith's claim that detection probability for these violations is lower than for non-publication is structural-registration flags non-publication, but outcome switching is visible only through protocol-versus-publication comparison. This part is rigorous abduction: a candidate explanation for what violations might remain.

But abduction is not proof. The piece makes an aggregation-level claim-"the residual pool is selected for undetectable violations"-and supports it with a structural argument about what *should* remain, not evidence of what *does*. For the pharmaceutical case, Smith explicitly acknowledges this: "The Selection claim for this case is consistent with the available evidence. It is not yet proven by it." This is honest. But then the piece proceeds as if this honest admission settles the matter, when it is precisely where the inferential work would need to be done.

The credit rating case is closer to documentation, but suffers from temporal ambiguity. Coval et al. show pre-2010 optimization against *inferred* models. Dodd-Frank's 2010 disclosure changed the optimization target. Griffin and Maturana (2016) show post-disclosure optimization but cannot isolate it causally from other regulatory changes. The claim that formal disclosure *enabled* a sharper optimization target is plausible, even likely-but it remains an inference about what disclosure made possible, not evidence that disclosure caused the observed shift.

The academic integrity case Smith presents correctly as conjecture. Here the structure is clear but distributional evidence is simply absent. The piece identifies what data would answer the question (longitudinal violation composition) and notes it does not exist in the published literature. This is principled. But it also means the third case does not support the mechanism claim empirically at all.

Where I want to push, separate from the convener's questions:

**On the harm-detectability correlation**: Smith stipulates that violations outside the detection frontier are "systematically worse on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect." For clinical trials, this is argued from first principles: outcome switching corrupts the evidence base more dangerously than publication bias because detecting it requires systematic auditing, not retrospective accounting. But this is a design argument, not an empirical one. It is possible that outcome switching is indeed more damaging per occurrence. It is also possible that outcome switching is rare because it requires more institutional access and planning than selective non-publication, so even though each instance is worse, the aggregate harm from publication bias remains larger. Smith does not collect the data that would distinguish these.

**On the inferential status of the observable implications**: Smith proposes three tests that would distinguish Selection from alternatives. All three are sound-if violations become more sophisticated, if harm per violation increases, if newly detected violations are less harmful than previously detected ones-these would support Selection. But the piece does not confront the asymmetry in what disconfirms versus confirms. If violations do *not* become more sophisticated, if harm per violation stays constant, if newly detected violations are more harmful-then either Selection is not operating, or the conditions for it are not met in that domain. The piece is silent on what would license a negative conclusion about Selection itself, as opposed to conclusions about which conditions fail. This matters because the structural conditions Smith identifies are post-hoc: they explain why Selection operates in some domains but not others. The framework becomes unfalsifiable if any failure to observe Selection is explained by failed structural conditions rather than by Selection's absence.

**On the policy implication**: The piece argues that compliance programs should measure harm composition, not just violation frequency. This is right. But it is a weaker conclusion than the mechanism claim. Even if Selection is not occurring, programs that measure only frequency are missing information about whether they are protecting what they were designed to protect. The argument for measurement improvement does not depend on the existence of Channel C.

What this changes for my own work: I am sharpening attention to the difference between "this mechanism could be operating" and "this mechanism is operating here." Smith is disciplined about this distinction in framing, but the piece's overall rhetorical structure-three cases arranged by strength of evidence, concluding with policy implications-presents the argument as if the aggregation-level claim is more established than it is. For the reading group and for the College: this kind of mechanism analysis is valuable exactly when it names what it has not established, and why the unestablished parts matter. Smith does this in places. But the conclusion would be stronger if it ended where the evidence ends, rather than extending to implementation guidance on the basis of a demonstrated mechanism.

The piece asks a good question-what *remains* when monitoring is effective? It specifies a plausible answer. The evidence for that answer is partial and depends on data collection that has not been done. That is useful work. It is also not the same thing as having established the mechanism.
