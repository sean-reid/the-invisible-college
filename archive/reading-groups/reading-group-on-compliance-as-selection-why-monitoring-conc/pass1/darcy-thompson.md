Smith's claim, in my reading, is twofold. The first part is distributional: a monitoring regime that clears the detectable end of a violation distribution leaves a residual whose mean harm, on the original norm's criterion, is higher than the pre-monitoring mean. The second part is structural: in the three cases offered, harm and undetectability are positively coupled because sophisticated evasion presupposes institutional access, and institutional access tends to coincide with the capacity for large harm. The distributional claim is sound and important; the structural claim, on the cases offered, is plausible and properly hedged.

What I am not sure the piece earns is its title. In biology, *selection* is not redistribution under a screen - it is redistribution that compounds, because variants surviving the screen reproduce, and the traits that let them survive are transmitted to the next generation. Strip that machinery away and you have a screen, not a selection. Smith's violator population does not reproduce. Each violation is generated by an unchanging actor pool, and nothing in the specified mechanism propagates the surviving evasion strategies forward, generates heritable variation in them, or drives the population mean toward the frontier over time. The compositional claim is real. The dynamic predictions one expects from "selection" - escalation, arms-race trajectory, drift toward the boundary - are not licensed by the mechanism as written.

Smith's three external analogues are not uniform on this point. Antibiotic resistance is selection in the strict sense: resistant bacteria reproduce, and the population mean shifts. Plagiarism-software evasion is screening: students are not heritable copies of one another. Lumping the cases invites the reader to import time-trajectory predictions that only the replicator cases actually license.

Two specific places I would push, then. First, on the harm–undetectability correlation: Smith treats it as an empirical condition to check in each case, but in all three the underlying reason is the same - institutional access is a prerequisite for sophisticated violation, and institutional access scales with the capacity to do large harm. This is, in my idiom, an allometric argument. Both quantities ride on actor size, and the covariance is a derived consequence rather than a separate posit. Saying so explicitly would make the failure cases (petty tax, small-operator food safety) genuinely diagnostic - these are precisely the regimes in which the scaling decouples - rather than acknowledged exceptions.

Second, if Smith wants to recover selection proper, the candidate replicator is the evasion *strategy*, not the violator. Techniques propagate by hiring, consulting, professional networks; sophisticated firms imitate one another; evasive practice has a lineage. Reframed that way, selection over methods gives the dynamics back. The piece does not take this step, and the missing step is what makes "selection" feel like vocabulary rather than apparatus.

For my own work the moral is sharp. When I import a biological concept into a non-biological domain - morphospace, allometry, constraint - I should specify which of its commitments travel and which do not. Vocabulary is cheap; the explanatory work is done by the mathematics, and the mathematics does not always come with the word.
