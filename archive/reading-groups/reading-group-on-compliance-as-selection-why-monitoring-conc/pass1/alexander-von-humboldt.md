The central claim is compositional: effective monitoring does not merely reduce violation frequency - it selects a residual pool whose harm distribution differs from the original. The formal condition requires Cov(h, D) < 0: harm and detectability negatively correlated across the violator population. The policy implication follows: detected-violation count is not a sufficient statistic for expected harm when that covariance holds, and monitoring programs should be designed to test it rather than assume it.

The convener's pressure on the selection vocabulary is correct, but I would push differently. Biological selection requires replication - it accumulates over generations through differential reproductive success. Smith's violators do not reproduce; the mechanism is a one-time sorting of a static population under a detection criterion. The more precise description is environmental filtering, which ecologists have modeled formally without the generational requirement. That apparatus preserves everything useful in Smith's argument without the replication liability. The piece would be stronger reaching for the filtering literature than the evolutionary one.

What concerns me more than the metaphor is the measurement structure. Smith names the fundamental difficulty - violations outside the detection frontier are unobserved - but may underestimate how structural it is. Even violations "eventually discovered through other channels" are a biased subsample: they are the ones that left external traces. The most sophisticated violations, exactly those Selection predicts dominate the residual, are least likely to appear in any secondary source. The missingness is structured, caused by the property being measured. Collecting more data from the standard monitoring apparatus does not fix this.

This changes which empirical test to prioritize. The three observable implications treat before-after violation-type comparisons as primary. But aggregate outcome series - patient harm rates, structured product default rates - avoid structured missingness by not requiring violation-type coding. Smith mentions this as a secondary check for the pharmaceutical case. I would invert: aggregate outcome data is the most defensible test precisely because it sidesteps the detection frontier. The argument for prioritizing violation-type data should explain why the inference problem there is not fatal.

The harm-detectability covariance is the load-bearing claim. The three favorable cases share a structural feature - institutional access concentrates both capability and harm magnitude - and the two failure cases (petty tax, food safety small operators) are acknowledged in a sentence. The boundary between regimes where the covariance is negative and where it is positive deserves more than passing treatment. Mapping that boundary is empirical work, not merely structural argument, and it is the work the piece opens without completing.

For my own observational programs: any instrument with a detection frontier sorts phenomena toward those it can reach. That sorting bias is always present. Smith has specified the general form precisely enough that I can now ask, of any field program including my own, whether the detection frontier correlates with the dimension the program was designed to measure. That is a genuine clarification of a problem I had been working around without naming.
