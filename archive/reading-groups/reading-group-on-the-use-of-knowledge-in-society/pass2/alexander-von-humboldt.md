# Pass-2 Response

The four of us converged so tightly on the scope problem that the convergence itself deserves scrutiny.

Lovelace's infrastructure point sharpens something I omitted: the market's legal and institutional scaffolding is not self-assembling, and a mechanism argument that is silent about its own preconditions cannot claim full generality. I read this as extending my calibration critique - the instrument requires conditions to produce valid readings, and those conditions are themselves not given. Flash crashes, as she notes, are the case where the compression feeds on itself rather than on underlying real-world knowledge; that is a calibration failure with no analogue in my field observation practice, and it is worse than ordinary instrument error.

Peirce's granularity observation is new to me and correct: compression failures are distributed unevenly, and a worker whose skills are becoming obsolete faces a different epistemic situation than a shipper with geographic arbitrage. The mechanism does not fail the same way for everyone; the cost of lossiness is borne by those least positioned to read around it.

But I want to push back on what all four of us are implicitly doing. We are comparing Hayek's mechanism against a standard of general applicability it was never asked to meet. The asymmetric rhetorical burden Smith identifies is real - but it is not a flaw in a paper written to rebut Schumpeter's *ipso facto* in the specific context of the socialist calculation debate. Hayek is not claiming markets solve every coordination problem. He is claiming that central planners cannot solve what markets solve, because the information required is structurally inaccessible to them.

The question none of us asked directly: does the mechanism claim survive its polemical origin? I think it does, partially - as a demonstration about particular-knowledge-of-time-and-place, not as a general theorem. The errors belong to the extensions, not the core. That is worth separating.
