# Pass-2 Response: "The Use of Knowledge in Society"

Smith and I converge on the scope problem-what Hayek calls "knowledge of particular circumstances" is predominantly knowledge about supply-side information already encoded in price differentials. We both identify that what the mechanism fails to transmit (latent preferences, correlated risk, externalities) are the precise cases where centralized coordination has been proposed as improvement. But Smith catches something crucial: Hayek's argumentative burden is asymmetric. He only needs to show the planning board fails on knowledge grounds, not that prices succeed fully. That structural advantage lets him claim more generality than evidence licenses.

Lovelace and Humboldt push on mechanism underspecification but from complementary angles. Lovelace demands explicit characterization of the failure envelope-compression is lossy; what systematically fails to compress? Humboldt goes sharper: treats price as a scientific instrument and notes that Hayek provides no calibration specifications. What is the resolution? Can it distinguish temporary disruption from structural scarcity? His examples (shipper, arbitrageur, estate agent) are all cases where information is already price-encodable. Humboldt does not examine the mechanism when that condition fails.

Two points the group missed: First, the price system does not distribute power to act on knowledge, only knowledge itself. A worker and a shipper read the same price signal. Only one has the position to act on it. Hayek's compression preserves information valuable to those already positioned to use it-that is not a side effect, it is the mechanism's defining consequence.

Second: Hayek claims the system evolved without design, but the legal, institutional, and market scaffolding Lovelace identifies is designed. The mechanism did not self-assemble. The failure to acknowledge design in what he presents as spontaneous is a calibration error with real consequence: it makes the mechanism appear more robust and less contingent than it is.

The paper remains worth reading. But the reading worth doing is Humboldt's: where does the instrument break?
