# Response to "The Use of Knowledge in Society"

Hayek's central claim is clear: the price system solves the fundamental problem of coordinating dispersed, particular knowledge (knowledge of time, place, immediate opportunity) that no single mind can possess. Prices act as a compression algorithm-a thin signal that encodes what matters and lets each actor adjust without needing the origin story of the price movement.

The inferential structure is sound within its scope. But I think the scope is narrower than Hayek claims, and he does not adequately mark where the mechanism breaks.

## What the mechanism actually handles

Prices efficiently coordinate decisions about *particular resource allocation given stable preferences and known technologies*. The tin example works precisely because everyone already knows tin is useful, the question is only how much less to use it. This is genuinely valuable-most economic activity sits in this space. But Hayek presents it as solving the economic problem of society, which is broader.

Consider three kinds of knowledge prices fail to transmit well:

**First, externalities and correlated risk.** A price for steel tells me how scarce it is; it does not tell me that a price spike reflects temporary shortage versus permanent supply destruction, or that my increased demand will feedback on competitors' costs, or that my competitor is subsidized. These are exactly the cases where "you don't need to know why" becomes a liability. Decentralized actors optimizing on prices alone can systematically underprice externalities or move together into crowded trades.

**Second, emergent preferences.** The price system assumes people know what they want. But substantial economic coordination is about *discovering* what becomes wanted (new technologies, new combinations of existing goods). Prices can signal existing demand, not the demand that would exist if people knew what was possible. This is why markets in entirely new categories often fail to form-there is no price to signal what people don't yet know they want.

**Third, the granularity problem.** Hayek admits compression is lossy but treats this as unproblematic. In practice, compression failures are distributed unfairly. A shipper who knows of half-empty tramps benefits from the price signal; a worker whose skills are becoming obsolete also sees a price signal, but lacks the particular knowledge to act on it. The compression preserves information valuable to those already positioned to use it.

## The contemporary substitution

The convener asks whether the mechanism survives substitution of "algorithmic market with endogenous price-setting" for 1945 central planning. I think the question is even sharper: *if prices are now set algorithmically rather than discovered through dispersed human judgment, has the knowledge they encode changed?*

Hayek's story requires that prices reflect the actual circumstances of time and place-supply bottlenecks, shipment routes, quality variation. If prices are now determined by automated trading engines responding to historical patterns and inferred demand, they encode a different kind of knowledge: not the present particular circumstances but statistical projections about future price movements. The compression algorithm is still elegant, but it is now compressing financial behavior rather than real constraint.

## Implication for my own work

The paper reinforces a principle I should apply more strictly: when I propose a mechanism for coordination or inference, I must specify its scope exactly. "Prices coordinate resource allocation" is a sound claim. "Prices solve the knowledge problem of society" is too broad. The failure is not in the argument but in the scope-creep from solving a particular sub-problem to claiming universal applicability.

I also need to attend harder to what compression loses. Hayek treats thinness as an advantage. It can be. But lossy algorithms have failure modes, and the burden of those failures falls unevenly. Specifying which actors benefit from information loss and which bear its costs is not peripheral-it is central to evaluating whether the mechanism actually coordinates, or only coordinates for some and disadvantages others.

The paper remains worth reading for its mechanism claim, even though I doubt the claim in the form Hayek advances it.
