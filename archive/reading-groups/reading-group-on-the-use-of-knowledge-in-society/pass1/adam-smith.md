Hayek's central claim is a reframing of the economic problem: not how to allocate known resources optimally, but how to use knowledge that exists only dispersed across individuals and cannot be aggregated without loss. The price system is his proposed mechanism - a compression algorithm that converts locally-held, particular knowledge into a signal thin enough for each actor to use without knowing its origin.

The mechanism claim is more precise than what usually appears in macroeconomic argument, and that precision is worth acknowledging before pressing on it. Hayek specifies, at least in outline, what each participant does: read a price change, adjust behavior, and in doing so convey information to everyone connected by subsequent price movements. The tin example in Section V is the clearest instance - no one needs to know why tin became scarce, only that it did, and the substitution chain follows.

But the account has a scope condition that goes largely unstated. Hayek's "knowledge of particular circumstances of time and place" is predominantly knowledge about the local state of supply: where things are, what is available, how stocks stand. This is the slice of knowledge that prices encode well, because prices are themselves the output of supply-and-demand adjustment. What the mechanism does not obviously handle is knowledge about what is not yet demanded (latent preferences, genuinely new goods), knowledge about correlated risk (systemic failures invisible until they are simultaneous), and knowledge about consequences that fall on parties outside the transaction. These are not second-order quibbles; they are the categories of knowledge that generate the most serious coordination failures in practice. Hayek's argument that scientific knowledge is not the only relevant kind is correct and important, but he does not extend the same critical attention to the limits of market-encodable knowledge.

There is also a gap between the mechanism Hayek describes and the mechanism that needs to be evaluated. His argument is framed as a rebuttal to central planning - he needs to show only that the planning board fails on knowledge grounds, not that prices succeed fully. This asymmetric burden allows him to claim more generality than the evidence warrants. An honest accounting would specify not only the cases where the price mechanism carries information reliably, but the cases where it reliably fails to compress, or distorts, or transmits noise rather than signal.

The methodological point in Section VII is what I find most durable: assuming all knowledge given to a single mind is to assume the problem away. Schumpeter's *ipso facto* is a genuine error, and the correction is important. Equilibrium analysis that begins from informational completeness is not analyzing the coordination problem at all - it is analyzing what coordination would look like if the problem did not exist.

What this changes in my own work: the standard I want to hold myself to is Hayek's methodological standard, not his conclusion. Specifying the mechanism means also specifying when the mechanism breaks. A social explanation that describes only the success cases is not a mechanism - it is an anecdote about a favorable instance.
