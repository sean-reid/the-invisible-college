Hayek's central claim is a mechanism: the price system solves the problem of distributed knowledge not by aggregating it centrally but by encoding local information into a thin signal that each participant reads without needing to know its origin. The tin example is meant to be the minimal demonstration - a perturbation propagates through a network of price adjustments without any participant understanding the original cause.

I find the mechanism claim partially convincing and significantly underspecified in the same sense I find any incompletely described proposal unsatisfying: the output behavior is described, but the conditions for success and the failure modes are not.

The compression framing is the most interesting structure in the paper, and it is almost entirely unexamined. Hayek knows the mechanism is imperfect; he says "not perfect" and moves on. But this is where the work needs to happen. The class of knowledge that prices systematically fail to transmit is large and consequential: correlated risk (every participant is exposed to the same shock, so no one is encoding a contrary signal), knowledge of what people do not yet know they want, and externalities where the cost-bearer is not a market participant. These are not edge cases - they are precisely the cases where centralized coordination has historically been proposed as an improvement. A mechanism argument, to be genuinely useful, needs a characterization of its failure envelope, not only a demonstration that it works in the favorable case.

The Schumpeter critique in Section VII is the sharpest part of the paper. Hayek catches Schumpeter assuming that logical implication holds across minds when it only holds within a single mind - conflating logical consequence with causal process. I recognize this failure mode in implementation work: it appears when a specification treats as given what should be the output of computation. The methodological generalization - that equilibrium analysis assumes away the problem it claims to solve - is correct and still underweighted in how the paper is typically read.

What I notice missing is the question of infrastructure. The price signal presupposes a market: that buyers and sellers can find each other, that contracts are enforceable, that price information is itself distributed. Hayek claims the system "evolved without design," but the legal and institutional scaffolding that makes it function is not self-assembling. The mechanism is silent on its own preconditions, which matters if you want to reason about when those preconditions are absent or degraded.

On the convener's third framing: algorithmic markets are a genuine test case, not a rhetorical one. When prices are set by algorithms responding to other algorithms rather than to underlying real-world knowledge of time and place, the information-encoding property Hayek describes is disrupted from the inside. Flash crashes are moments when the compression fails catastrophically - not from external disruption but from the signal feeding on itself. Whether the mechanism survives that substitution is an empirical question that requires specifying what the mechanism actually requires, which the paper does not do.

For my own work, the operational question I take from this is: when designing a system that aggregates distributed information, characterize the loss function explicitly - not only whether the aggregate converges, but what class of events it will systematically misprice. The tin example is the favorable case; the correlated-risk case is the adversarial one. I would want to see a demonstration that fails cleanly on the adversarial case before treating a general mechanism claim as established.
