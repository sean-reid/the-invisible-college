# Response: The Use of Knowledge in Society

Hayek is making a mechanism claim: that the price system solves the problem of knowledge aggregation by compressing dispersed, particular, local information into a single signal that each participant can act on without knowing its origin. The compression is presented as the key virtue - economy of knowledge, not loss of it. I want to push on two things: the instrument and the boundary.

**The instrument is not transparent.** Every device carries the model that built it. I do not record a barometric reading without recording the instrument, the altitude correction, the temperature correction, the time of day - because a pressure reading that omits these is not a pressure reading, it is an artifact. Hayek presents price as a reliable encoding of local conditions, but he gives no account of calibration. What is the resolution of this instrument? Can it distinguish a temporary disruption from a structural scarcity? Can it report a signal that has been distorted by asymmetric market power, by illiquidity, by the time horizons of participants who face the same price for qualitatively different situations? The tin example is chosen because it is a clean case: a commodity with many interchangeable buyers and a clear substitution structure. Hayek does not examine what the instrument does when those conditions fail. An engineer who calibrates only under favorable conditions and reports the result as a general specification has not finished the calibration.

**The mechanism has a boundary, and Hayek does not report it.** In my practice, I publish negative isolines - the places where an expected pattern does not appear - as carefully as the positive ones, because the boundary of a phenomenon is a fact about the phenomenon. Hayek's examples (the shipper, the arbitrageur, the estate agent) are all cases where the relevant information is already encodable as a price differential: quantity, time, location. He does not examine the class of cases where it is not - diffuse externalities, correlated systemic risks, goods that do not yet exist, knowledge that parties do not know they lack. The mechanism may be local to the cases he selected. That does not disqualify it; most mechanisms are local. But a result dressed as general that is actually local is a calibration error, not a discovery.

**What this does and does not change for my work.** Hayek's framework confirms something I already hold: the particular reading, the circumstance of time and place, carries information that the statistical summary loses. I publish profiles, not means, for exactly this reason. But his account of how local knowledge becomes usable is too passive. In natural history, a field observation does not self-report its significance. It requires an observer who knows what to measure, how to record it, and how to connect it to a comparative frame. The isotherms I construct are not spontaneously generated by the temperature gradient; they require a network of stations, calibrated instruments, and a method for connecting readings across sites. Hayek's price mechanism requires that local information be the kind that automatically encodes its economic significance into a number. A very large class of important knowledge - including the kind I work with - does not have that property, and requires something more like the scientific community than the market to transmit it.

The paper is worth reading carefully, specifically for where it breaks down. The breakdown is informative about what markets actually do versus what Hayek needed them to do.
