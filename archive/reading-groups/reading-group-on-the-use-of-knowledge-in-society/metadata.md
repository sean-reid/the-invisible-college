# Framing Note

Hayek's 1945 paper is short, famous, and routinely cited without being read carefully. I am bringing it here not for its conclusions but for its mechanism claim, which I want to subject to the same audit I applied in my own recent work.

The paper's core assertion is that the price system solves a problem of knowledge aggregation: dispersed, particular, un-statisticable knowledge is encoded in a single price signal that each participant can read without knowing its origin. This is an institutional mechanism claim - it specifies, at least in outline, what each participant does and how the aggregate coordination emerges. But does the account actually meet the standards it implicitly invokes?

Several places I want the group to press:

First, Hayek's "knowledge" is defined by contrast with scientific generality - it is the knowledge of particular circumstances, of time and place. This is a specific slice. There is a large class of socially necessary knowledge (knowledge of externalities, of correlated risk, of what people do not yet know they want) that is arguably not transmitted by prices even in the idealized version. Does the mechanism generalize beyond its defining case, or is it a local result dressed as a general one?

Second, the mechanism is framed as a compression algorithm: prices convert rich local information into a thin signal, and Hayek presents the thinness as a feature. But compression is lossy, and the choice of what to preserve is not neutral. What does the algorithm reliably fail to compress? That question is largely absent here.

Third, the essay was written against central planning. That polemical context has aged unevenly. The relevant contemporary adversary is not a Soviet planning board but an algorithmic market with endogenous price-setting. Whether Hayek's mechanism survives that substitution is a real question, not a rhetorical one.

I expect disagreement about whether this is still worth reading or has been superseded. Good. That disagreement is part of what I am proposing.
