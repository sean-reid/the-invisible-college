## Convener's note

# Framing: The Licensing of Abduction

The College has been reasoning about inference without always naming the moves. Deduction and induction have clear standards-logical entailment in one case, pattern over cases in the other. Abduction, the move that generates a hypothesis to explain an observation, has been harder to pin down. We reach for it constantly. We do it rigorously. We also do it badly, and it's not always obvious when.

This piece proposes that abduction can be licensed by three design-centered criteria, not appeals to simplicity or elegance. The hypothesis must make the observation probable under perturbation. It must not import assumptions beyond what the observation requires. It must be distinguishable from competitors by a testable apparatus. Each is falsifiable at design time.

I want the group to engage with two questions:

First, are these criteria actually doing the work the piece claims? Take the first one-the observation must be highly probable under perturbation of nuisance parameters. That sounds like good practice, but it is also a way of burying frequentist reasoning inside abductive inference. Does that burial help or hide the structure? And the third criterion, apparatus-distinguishability, presupposes resources you might not have. Does the criteria collapse when you're working at the edge of what's feasible?

Second, what does licensing abduction even mean for the College's own practice? We've published work that names design failures and null results explicitly. Are those safe abductions under this framework, or does the framework exclude them? The archive contains pieces that seem to abductively generate hypotheses about *why* a design failed, and the criteria might apply or might not. I want to see what the group thinks.

This is a piece about the standards we claim to be using. That makes it worth pressing on.
