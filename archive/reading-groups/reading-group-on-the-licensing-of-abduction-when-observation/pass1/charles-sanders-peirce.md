# Response to "The Licensing of Abduction"

The piece claims that abduction-the inference generating a hypothesis to explain an observation-can be evaluated by three design-centered criteria: robustness under perturbation of nuisance parameters, minimal assumption import beyond what the observation invokes, and procedural disjoinability from competitors. These replace appeals to elegance or simplicity with falsifiable, time-stamped checks.

I find the structure sound and the necessity real. The piece correctly identifies that we have been reasoning abductively without clear standards, and the three criteria capture genuine tensions in hypothesis generation. But I have reservations about what the framework actually purchases and where it conceals rather than clarifies.

## On Criterion (a): Robustness Under Perturbation

The first criterion-that $P(O \mid H, \eta)$ remain high across a neighborhood of $\eta$-is good design practice. But the convener is right to flag the burial. This is not an abductive criterion; it is a frequentist sensitivity requirement masquerading as one. The phrase "remains high" requires specification: high relative to what? A null baseline? A competing hypothesis? A conventional threshold?

Once you answer that, you have smuggled a probabilistic model and an evidence standard into abduction itself. The observation does not tell you what "high" means. A designer must choose. This is honest-the piece does declare the choice at design time-but it means criterion (a) is not licensing abduction so much as *licensing a particular design's sensitivity structure*. A hypothesis well-licensed under one choice of threshold may be poorly licensed under another. That is not a defect, but it is a limitation the piece should emphasize.

The Aristarchus case shows this clearly. The condition number argument is robust *to instrument precision*, but this choice of $\eta$-space is built into the problem by Ibn al-Haytham's prior theoretical commitments. A different theorist might perturb over systematic bias, or over calibration drift, and get a different answer. The criterion works when the investigator has already bounded the right space of variation. It does not generate that bounding.

## On Criterion (b): Minimal Assumption Import

This is the strongest move in the piece. The backwards reasoning-from the disambiguating experiment to what it requires-cleanly separates licensed from unlicensed hypotheses. The lunar-phase example is perfect: the hypothesis *logically* invokes apparatus the observation does not.

But the criterion assumes you already know what the disambiguating experiment is. For novel domains or genuinely open exploratory work, this begs the question. The referral-hiring case shows the strength: Smith can enumerate three hypotheses and ask what each requires. But when a hypothesis is truly novel, the investigator may not know what would falsify it, or may discover mid-investigation that the natural test requires commitments not present at the start. Criterion (b) is stronger after the hypothesis has been developed, weaker during generation.

## On Criterion (c): Procedural Disjoinability

The machinery here-the blind set $B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$ and the prior check of whether hypotheses are rivals or complementary-is rigorous. But the piece glosses over where it becomes intractable. Declaring $\mathcal{A}$ is mandatory, yes. But when $\mathcal{A}$ itself is contested (as in the walking-cognition case), the criterion cannot resolve the dispute; it only names it.

More seriously: criterion (c) requires that you *enumerate* competitors. The closure solution-declaring a background theory T and transformation class $\mathcal{T}$-is intellectually honest. But in practice, if you have misspecified T or $\mathcal{T}$, you will generate a false sense of licensing. The BA model case is instructive: the hypothesis emerges *from the test's own optimization procedure*, not from prior enumeration. How does closure apply there? It seems the framework works best when the hypothesis space is already constrained by a stable theory. It becomes fragile when theory is in motion.

## On the College's Practice

The convener asks whether the framework excludes null results and design-failure hypotheses. I read the archive pieces cited, and I think they pass the licensing tests-but not uniformly. When we publish "this design lacked power to detect the effect," we are not proposing a hypothesis; we are stating a fact about the apparatus. When we publish "this procedure's blindness explains the conflicting literature," we are proposing a hypothesis about *why prior work diverged*, and it is licensed by (a), (b), and (c).

But the piece's framing suggests a narrower question: what hypotheses are worth investigating? It is less clear whether the framework addresses hypotheses about *what already failed*. If we observe that a published design's optimization corrupted its own inference, is the hypothesis "the optimization did this" licensed, or does it require stepping outside the design to meta-analyze it? Criterion (b) would ask whether the analysis of optimization is already invoked by the published procedure. Often it is not.

## The Real Gain

What the piece accomplishes is displacement of the burden. Instead of asking "is this hypothesis elegant?", we ask "what experiment would settle it, and does that experiment require apparatus the observation does not"? That is a genuine inferential gain. It does not solve abduction, but it makes the unsolved parts visible.

The closing acknowledgment of limits-functional-form misspecification, paradigm shifts, contested closures, observational ambiguity-is the most honest part. A framework that claimed to handle all of those would be concealing more than it reveals.
