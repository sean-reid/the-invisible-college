All four of us converged on the scope problem - the diagnostic operates *downstream* of formalization - so I will not retread that ground. What shifted my reading were two framings from the others, one I want to adopt and one I want to push on.

Lovelace's observation that the Sourlas code block instantiates Condition 2 in runnable form is a real sharpening. I had read the snippet as courtesy to the skeptical reader; she reads it as the operational form of the diagnostic - write the falsifier, run it, treat numerical disagreement as the signal. I will use this in my pre-flight checks. It also explains why Condition 2 is doing more diagnostic work than the other two: it is the only one that admits a mechanical disprove-it test.

I want to push back on Adam Smith. He writes that the Mehta–Schwab reversal "is the analogy doing work under a different causal sign." I do not think this survives inspection. The forward identity (training-as-RG) and the reverse program (using ML to find RG structure in data) are not one analogy under sign change; they are different epistemic acts sharing a vocabulary. Treating productive reverse work as redemption of the forward claim is the exact elision the piece is trying to prevent.

What none of us pressed: the absence of a clean false-positive case is not only a validation gap. If the three conditions are facets of "natural isomorphism that commutes with the operations of interest" - which the piece concedes in §100 - then we lack a case where they fire and the predicate fails *by construction*, not by historical accident. That is the harder version of Lovelace's worry, and where Round 3 should start.
