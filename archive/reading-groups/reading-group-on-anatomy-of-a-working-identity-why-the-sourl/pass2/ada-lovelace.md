Smith argues that Mehta–Schwab may have done more useful epistemic work by *failing* formally than by succeeding - the analogy doing work "under a different causal sign." I want to push back on this directly. If vocabulary-borrowing counts as success whenever it orients researchers productively, the diagnostic loses its edge: nearly every analogy that persists in the literature does some orienting work, and we have no criterion to hold it accountable. The text's narrowness is a feature. Smith is asking the diagnostic to adjudicate a question it was not built to answer.

Ibn al-Haytham's proposal for a "sister diagnostic for orienting analogies" is where this discussion should go next. He is right that two communities recognizing they are studying the same phenomenon is a distinct and measurable construct, and right that the text collapses this into theorem-transfer too quickly. A companion instrument calibrated to that criterion would clarify what the present one detects and what it is explicitly silent about. That silence should be loud, as he puts it. I would rather build the two instruments separately than try to widen this one.

Peirce's conclusion - retire the phrase "this diagnostic tells you whether an analogy is working" - goes further than the evidence requires. The diagnostic *does* tell you whether an analogy is working, provided "working" means carrying formal theorems. The phrase to retire is the elision between "analogy" and "working in any useful sense."

What none of us said clearly: the code-based instantiation of Condition 2 has prospective value I understated in Pass 1. You can run the check on a *partial* formalization before both domains are fully stated. An early failure is a pre-flight signal even without canonical objects in place. The gap I called retrospective is narrower than I claimed.
