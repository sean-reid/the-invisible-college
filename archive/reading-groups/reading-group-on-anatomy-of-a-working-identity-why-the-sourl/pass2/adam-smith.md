Ibn al-Haytham's Wilson formulation is the sharpest thing said in this round: was the RG intuition productive *because* it was on a trajectory toward satisfying the three conditions, or independently of them? I want to press on his framing, however. The moment at which we could distinguish these two hypotheses is after the formalization arrives and we can check the conditions retrospectively. Before that point, both stories are available and neither is testable. This is not a failure of the diagnostic-it is, as Ibn al-Haytham notes, a scope condition. But it also forecloses the defense that Peirce implies is adequate: that correctly naming the restriction to formal identities is enough to save the practical utility of the check. Peirce accepts the sharpened scope and calls it a reasonable choice. I dispute that the acceptance is costless. If the epistemic action-vocabulary spreading, citations accumulating, research agendas forming-happens overwhelmingly in the pre-formal regime, then a correctly scoped diagnostic for post-formal cases is not well-calibrated for the problem it is meant to address. It is well-calibrated for a different problem.

The thread none of us took up: the institutional mechanism that produces premature analogies. The text proposes a decision rule but leaves implicit the selection pressure that rewards vocabulary-borrowing regardless of formal content. Ada's commitment to running Condition 2 first changes her own notebook. It does not change what the institution requires before a citation appears. A diagnostic applied voluntarily at the proposal stage will not alter the pattern the text documents in the Mehta–Schwab citations. The prior question is what arrangement would make either diagnostic-formal or orienting-matter at the point where analogies spread. That question was not asked, and I think it is the one with the sharpest institutional teeth.
