Reading the four other participants, I see genuine convergence on a scope issue, but not the scope issue the piece nominally claims.

The convener and all three others converge on this: the diagnostic is described as a pre-flight check on "candidate identities," but it is actually a post-flight diagnostician. It works downstream of formalization, not upstream. One cannot check the three conditions before writing the equations. This is not a limitation the piece hides; it is explicit in the final paragraph. But it changes what the diagnostic *is*. It is not a gating procedure for "whether to spend three weeks on formalization." It is a procedure for "whether to trust the formalization once you have it."

That is useful work. Ada's point about Condition 2 is exactly right: term-by-term matching without limits is computationally accessible; it is the first check to run on any proposed identity. But it is a post-hoc check, not a prospective one. The institutional question Adam raises-that researchers will not use the check unless institutions enforce it-compounds this. A diagnostic that becomes available only after the work is done, and whose use depends on institutional enforcement that does not currently exist, is not a gating procedure.

The harder point: Ibn al-Haytham asks whether the scope condition (both sides must be formalized) should be declared as prominently as the cultural-uptake and engineering-deployment caveats. He is right. It is the same kind of limit. But I want to push back on the consequences everyone draws from it. The fact that Wilson's RG intuitions worked *before* formalization tells us something about what kind of work analogies can do at different stages. It does not tell us the three conditions are narrow or parochial. It tells us the conditions apply to a specific stage of inquiry-the stage where both sides have equations-and are silent about other stages. That is exactly what a diagnostic should do: name the stage where it works.

The piece names all three limits in the final sections. What it could sharpen: the detection-threshold limit (the conditions cannot fire if one domain lacks formalization) belongs in that list, not as an apology for the diagnostic's scope but as a definition of when the diagnostic is the right tool. Not earlier, not later.

One thing unaddressed: Adam's institutional observation. If researchers in 2014 had run the Mehta–Schwab mapping against the three conditions, they would have caught the extension failure. The citation pattern would not have changed unless editors or reviewers enforced it. This is not a failure of the diagnostic; it is a failure of the review gate. But the piece treats the diagnostic as though making it available changes practice, and it does not, without structural enforcement.
