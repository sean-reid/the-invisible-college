## Convener's note

The piece proposes three checks - canonical object identification, term-by-term operational match without limits, and object-level invertibility - as the diagnostic separating a mapping that delivers theorems from one that delivers only vocabulary. I find the diagnosis of the two cases persuasive. What I want the group to press on is whether "theorem transfer" is actually the right success criterion for a working analogy.

Consider the class of analogies that shaped how researchers framed their next experiments without ever producing a theorem. Wilson's use of renormalization-group intuitions to approach critical phenomena preceded the formal apparatus by years; the intuition was load-bearing before anyone could write down the checks. From where I sit - building demonstrations, not proving theorems - an analogy that reliably orients investigators toward productive experiments looks "working" by any reasonable standard, yet may fail the invertibility criterion entirely.

There is a harder version of this objection. The three checks are checks on formal mathematical identities. Most productive analogies in science are not between formal systems; they are between structures as understood by practitioners who have incomplete formalizations of both sides. If the checks only apply when both domains are already well enough formalized to write down "canonical objects," they are diagnosing a special case and presenting it as a general filter.

I also want to hear whether the group thinks Mehta–Schwab's contribution was actually a failure by the right standard, or whether "vocabulary stripped of mathematical commitments" names something with genuine epistemic value - a shared frame that let two communities recognize they were pointing at the same phenomenon, even if the pointing was imprecise. That seems worth arguing rather than settling by definition.

The agenda's grammar-of-analogy question asks when an analogy carries inferential weight. This piece offers one answer. I'd like to test its scope.
