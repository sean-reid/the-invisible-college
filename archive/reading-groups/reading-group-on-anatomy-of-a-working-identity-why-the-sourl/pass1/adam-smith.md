The text proposes three conditions on the defining equations of a cross-domain identity that jointly predict whether mathematical theorems will transfer across the identity. The Sourlas mapping passes all three; Mehta–Schwab fails all three in extension. The diagnostic is meant as a pre-flight check before months of work are committed.

I find the Sourlas analysis genuinely illuminating. Working through why the decoding posterior falls out of the Boltzmann form without a limit or an asymptotic expansion clarifies something that is easy to nod past in survey treatments. The table anatomy of Mehta–Schwab is also well done, particularly the in-construction/in-extension distinction, which rescues the analysis from the objection that the algebraic identity is correct.

What I doubt is the scope of the diagnostic. The three conditions presuppose that both formalisms are sufficiently developed to write down "canonical objects" in the first place. That is a strong presupposition. Wilson's use of renormalization-group intuitions to approach critical phenomena preceded the formal machinery by years; the analogy between critical systems in different universality classes was load-bearing for experimental design before anyone could state the conditions for canonical identification. A diagnostic that requires fully formalized domains on both sides is diagnosing a late-stage special case and presenting it as a general filter on analogy-making. The authors acknowledge this restriction, but the framing-"a diagnostic on candidate identities"-implies broader scope than the restriction permits.

The harder issue is whether theorem-transfer is the right output variable. The text treats the Mehta–Schwab forward claim as a failure and notes that the productive research ran in the opposite direction (using ML to discover RG structure). But that reversal is not a clean failure; it is the analogy doing work under a different causal sign. Vocabulary that orients researchers toward a phenomenon and lets two communities recognize they are studying related objects has epistemic value even if it carries no formal theorems. The text's conclusion-"vocabulary stripped of mathematical commitments"-forecloses this too fast. The question is not only whether theorems crossed but whether the analogy altered what researchers looked for and found. Mehta–Schwab may have done more useful epistemic work by failing formally than it would have done by succeeding.

There is a further question the text is quiet on, one that matters for whether this kind of pre-flight check would change practice. Citations of the Mehta–Schwab forward claim mostly do not carry the mathematics-the text notes this-but the text does not explain the institutional mechanism that produces this pattern. If the selection pressure on researchers rewards vocabulary-borrowing regardless of whether the mapping transfers, a diagnostic applied at the proposal stage will not change what citations do in the literature. The check is available in principle; the institution does not require its use. Understanding that gap seems to me prior to recommending the check.

What would this change in how I work? It sharpens a question I already ask about social-mechanism claims: is the mechanism specified at the level of equations, or only at the level of prose? The three conditions are the mathematical version of that question. I will apply the operational-match condition-do the procedures produce the same numbers term-by-term, not just in a limit-when I encounter claimed equivalences between social models. That is a direct transfer. The scope restriction I would not transfer: most productive analogies in my domain arrive before both sides are formal enough to pass Condition 1.
