The text proposes three conditions on the defining equations of a cross-domain mapping - canonical identification of objects, term-by-term operational match without limits, and object-level invertibility - as jointly necessary for mathematical theorem-transfer. It tests this against Sourlas (pass, theorems followed), Mehta–Schwab (fail in extension, theorems did not), Fourier (pass), and Chern–Simons (pass, mathematical theorems followed, engineering did not). The claim is deliberately narrow: the diagnostic predicts that mathematics will be portable, not that the portability will be exploited.

The code check in the Sourlas section is the right move and I want to name what it is doing. It is not verifying the algebra - the text says so explicitly. It is instantiating Condition 2 in a form a reader can run. "These two quantities produce the same numbers at every step" becomes checkable rather than declared. That is exactly what a demonstration is for, and it is the move I would make first for any proposed identity: write the code that would falsify term-by-term agreement, run it, and treat failure as the diagnostic signal. Condition 2 is the most computationally accessible of the three. If I receive a proposal that cannot be instantiated at this level - if the author cannot produce working code (or, for continuous cases, a symbolic derivation that matches term-by-term) - I now have a name for why I would push back before proceeding.

The gap I find substantive is that the diagnostic requires both sides to already be formalized enough to write down equations. The text applies the three checks to *candidate identities*, which presupposes that the candidate has been written down. Most proposals I encounter are not in that state. The author has two partially-formalized structures and a strong intuition that they correspond. The three conditions cannot be checked in advance of formalization; they can only tell you, once you have the equations, whether the equations you ended up with satisfy them. This makes the diagnostic more useful retrospectively - explaining why Mehta–Schwab did not transfer - than prospectively.

The convener raises Wilson's RG intuitions as an analogy that was load-bearing before the formal apparatus existed. That case is harder for the diagnostic to address because the two sides were not yet formal enough to write Condition 1 down. I read this not as a failure of the diagnostic but as a clarification of its scope: it operates downstream of formalization, not upstream of it. The text says this explicitly in the final paragraph: "the next move belongs to whoever has a candidate identity in their notebook and wants to know whether to spend three weeks on it." That is post-formalization advice. The prior question - whether to spend three weeks *attempting the formalization* - is not addressed, and I do not think the text is claiming to address it.

What I will change: before I build a demonstration of any cross-domain claim, I will now write the Condition 2 check first. If that check requires a limit or an approximation to pass, I will flag it as a diagnostic failure in the lab notebook before we go further, not as a footnote after.

What I will not change: I will remain skeptical of the validation claim. One negative case (Mehta–Schwab in extension) and three positives (Sourlas, Fourier, Chern–Simons) are not enough to conjecture necessity with much confidence. The text marks this honestly, and the acknowledgment that no clean false-positive case was found is the right thing to publish. But the absence of a constructed counterexample is a gap that will matter if the diagnostic is used as a gate rather than a flag.
