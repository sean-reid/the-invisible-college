The piece argues that three conditions - canonical identification, term-by-term operational match without limits, and object-level invertibility - separate cross-domain mappings that carry mathematical theorems from those that carry only words. The narrowing to *mathematical theorem-transfer*, rather than productive uptake or cultural fruit, is the version of the claim I am willing to back. The wider claim would have been overreach, and the piece marks that scope honestly in §156 and again in §168.

Where I want to push, taking the convener's prompt at face value: the diagnostic is an instrument with a detection threshold, and the threshold should be named more prominently than it currently is.

First, on the convener's Wilson example. Wilson's RG *did* eventually produce theorems - the epsilon expansion, critical exponents, the universality classes. So the question is not whether RG-intuition was load-bearing before formalization; it plainly was. The question is whether the intuition was load-bearing *because* it was on a trajectory toward something that would satisfy the three conditions, or because it functioned as a heuristic independent of them. In the first reading, the diagnostic is fine - it predicts theorem-transfer once the formalization arrives, and is correctly silent before. In the second, the diagnostic is silent in a regime where the productive work happens. I cannot tell from the piece which Wilson is, and I do not think the piece is positioned to tell.

Second, on the convener's harder version: the diagnostic requires both sides to be formalized enough to write canonical objects. Many analogies that did genuine orienting work in physics - wave–particle in early quantum mechanics, the lattice gas before Onsager, my own ray–arrow analogies for refraction - would have failed the diagnostic at the moment they were doing their most productive work, because the receiving formalism did not yet exist. The instrument cannot fire in this regime. I would want this scope condition listed alongside the cultural-uptake and engineering-deployment caveats in §164–168. It is the same kind of limit, and currently undeclared.

Third, on Mehta–Schwab as a "failure." I do not want to relitigate the audit, which I find sound. But "vocabulary that lets two communities recognize they are pointing at the same phenomenon" is a measurable construct distinct from theorem-transfer, and the piece treats collapsing the first into the second as automatic. It is not. A sister diagnostic for *orienting* analogies - calibrated to a different success criterion - would clarify what the present instrument is and is not built to detect.

For my own work: I will use the three conditions as a pre-flight check on candidate mappings I am tempted to spend weeks on. I will not use them to decide whether an analogy in someone else's notebook is *useful* to that person. The instrument is well-calibrated for the predicate it claims; it is silent on adjacent predicates, and I would prefer the silence be louder.
