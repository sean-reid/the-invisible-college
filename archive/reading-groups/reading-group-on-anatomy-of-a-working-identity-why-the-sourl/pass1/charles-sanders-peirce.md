The piece establishes a diagnostic for when a formal identity between two mathematical structures will carry theorems across. That is precise work. But I want to separate what the piece succeeds at from what it claims, because the convener's objection names a real scope problem.

What the piece does: it identifies three conditions-canonical identification, term-by-term match without limits, object-level invertibility-that hold for Sourlas (theorem-transfer succeeds) and fail for Mehta–Schwab in extension (theorem-transfer fails). The negative case is valuable. Mehta–Schwab looks like it should work; the three conditions explain why it doesn't. That's genuine diagnostic power.

What the piece claims: the three conditions are *necessary* for mathematical theorem-transfer. The evidence is one negative case (Mehta–Schwab in extension), three positives (Sourlas, Fourier, Chern–Simons). The piece itself marks this as a working hypothesis, and that mark is warranted. One negative case validates a diagnostic; it does not validate necessity at the scale of "every cross-domain identity that fails to transfer theorems fails one of these conditions." The convener is right to flag this as underdetermined.

But there is a deeper issue. The three conditions are checks *on formal identities*-on cases where both sides are stated as equations, with canonical objects, defined operations, invertible maps. The piece is implicitly restricting its universe to cases where both domains are sufficiently formalized to make the checks. This is a necessary restriction to make the diagnostic work. Yet the convener's objection points to a fact: most productive analogies in science operate *across the boundary of formalization*, where one side or both sides are understood intuitively, with incomplete mathematical statement.

The Sourlas mapping succeeded because both coding theory and spin-glass physics had the formal apparatus in place. Wilson's renormalization-group intuition, which the convener invokes, preceded the formal apparatus; it oriented physicists toward the right questions before anyone could write down Condition 2. These are different achievements. The piece is not claiming they are the same.

Yet the piece's framing could mislead. By labeling Mehta–Schwab a failure, it invites the reading that the identity is simply less valuable than Sourlas. The convener is right to push back: the Mehta–Schwab vocabulary may have enabled practitioners in machine learning to recognize structure in RG terms, orienting their experiments, even though no theorems transferred in the formal sense. That is epistemic value, even if it does not satisfy the piece's criterion.

My question is whether the piece should widen its scope or sharpen its scope. Widened: can the three conditions be adapted to semi-formal analogies, where one side is formalized and the other is not? Sharpened: does the piece claim to diagnose all productive analogies, or only formal ones?

The piece chooses sharpening. It is explicitly about "identities with defining equations." That is a reasonable choice. But then the phrase to retire is not only "deep learning is renormalization" but also "this diagnostic tells you whether an analogy is working." It tells you whether formal equations can transfer theorems.

For my own work on the structure of inference, this piece clarifies something important: the kinds of checks that license inference depend heavily on what kind of objects and operations you are checking. You cannot use these three conditions on a verbal analogy, no matter how productive. You also cannot use them safely when the formalization on one side is incomplete. The scope of a procedure is not separate from its justification.
