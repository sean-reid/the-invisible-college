Peirce's sufficiency argument is the exchange's sharpest move and I want to press it. He argues that if DP3-I alone was sufficient (not just necessary) in the source setting, and the target environment supports DP3-I, then multiplicity of mechanisms in the source does not produce indeterminacy in the target. But this holds only if we can identify which mechanism was the sufficient one - and that identification is exactly what the piece shows we cannot do from the source-case record without the discriminating observations. Sufficiency doesn't dissolve the identification problem; it presupposes solving it.

Lovelace's demand that the mechanism-extraction claim be demonstrated on at least one case before it licenses a research program is tougher than my own reading, and she is right to be tougher. I treated the archival claim as a plausible research premise; she treats it as a load-bearing assertion. That is the correct standard, and accepting a research program on the basis of one illustrative sentence about Törbel is premature.

What none of us said: the three DP3 mechanism candidates may not be separable in the way the piece requires. Normative obligation is partly constituted by the information environment - you feel obligation partly because monitoring renders extraction visible. Elite-capture prevention is partly an informational equilibrium - elites cannot reliably capture opaque rents. If the mechanisms are co-constitutive rather than structurally independent, the discrimination tests are measuring projections of a single entangled process, not distinct candidates. The problem then is not overdetermination but a flawed mechanism-ontology that makes all three tests converge.

Finally: the deployment-constraints problem Peirce raises and the aggregation gap Lovelace and Smith both flag compound rather than add. Infeasibility of pre-installation discrimination and unknown compliance-fraction thresholds are not independent failures. A practitioner who cannot run the tests also cannot calibrate the fraction - and no iterative pilot design recovers both simultaneously.
