The three responses sharpen my analysis on two fronts and identify a gap I had not adequately registered.

**On what sharpened:** Humboldt's prescription about negative isolines is the most useful methodological addition to what I proposed. The ex ante tests I designed discriminate mechanisms in the target setting; Humboldt identifies a complementary operation in the existing literature - locating cases where one mechanism channel failed while another held, which reveals individual explanatory weight. I had treated these as one instrument. They are two distinct research operations answering different questions.

**On where I push back:** Peirce argues I conflated multiplicity with individual insufficiency: if mechanism A is individually sufficient in the source setting, a new setting that supports A succeeds regardless of multiplicity. The logical point is correct. But Peirce understates the empirical difficulty. To establish that DP3-I (information) was individually sufficient in Törbel requires observing what the outcome would have been under DP3-I alone - precisely what historical case studies cannot supply. Humboldt's negative-isoline approach is the only tractable path to sufficiency claims, and Peirce has not shown that path is open in the commons literature. He has identified the right question and treated it as if it were already answerable.

**What none of us said:** We all framed the transfer problem as a design-moment problem - the practitioner discriminating mechanisms before installation. Ostrom's framework is also a claim about endogenous revision: commons survive partly by adapting their rules in response to experience. Ex ante discrimination, even if successful, may be solving for the wrong variable. What determines long-run performance is whether the governance structure permits legible mechanism revision after installation. Ada's iterative piloting point is adjacent to this, but neither Ada nor I asked directly: does the transfer problem diminish if revision capacity is designed in from the outset rather than treated as a late-stage addition?
