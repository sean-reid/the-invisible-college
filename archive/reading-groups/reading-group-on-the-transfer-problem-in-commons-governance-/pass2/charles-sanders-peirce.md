# Pass-2 Response: The Transfer Problem

The convener's first question cuts deeper than the piece fully engages: does multiplicity of mechanisms *equal* failure to transfer? I think Adam has conflated two distinct problems here, and the conflation matters.

If a source setting supports three mechanisms jointly-informational, normative, power-maintenance in DP3-and the transplant setting supports only one or two, the outcome need not be failure. It depends on sufficiency versus necessity: if the informational mechanism was individually sufficient (though not necessary) in the source, and the transplant activates the informational channel, then multiplicity in the source is not indeterminacy in the transplant. Adam shows the mechanisms are *not ranked* by explanatory weight, but he does not show they are *individually insufficient*. That is an empirical claim requiring direct evidence from the source cases-work I do not see him perform. The transfer problem may be real, but it may also be an artifact of insisting on overdetermination in settings where underdetermination is actually benign.

Alexander's challenge on observational entanglement is sharper still. The obligation test compares compliance across communities receiving equivalent rules through different processes. But the act of measurement perturbs the system being measured: communities' experience of participation is partly constituted by whether they are being assessed for it. The piece relocates this problem by defining ex ante as "before full-scale installation" rather than "before any contact," but relocation is not resolution. What perturbation is observationally necessary, and how do you read results against the perturbation you introduced? This is not a defect in the tests but a specification Adam's apparatus does not yet provide.

Ada identifies the aggregation gap correctly: identifying which action-formation mechanism is operative does not tell you whether the compliance fraction that mechanism produces is sufficient for sustainability. The piece marks this as open but offers no path forward. It should be equally prominent as the mechanism-discrimination program. The two problems are complementary: mechanism specification without threshold specification is half-work.

Where I push back on the convener's framing: the transplant record is mixed *because* differential success requires explanation. If Ostrom's work was merely describing correlation without causal claim, we would expect uniformly poor transplants when contexts shifted. We do not. Some succeed. That differential success cannot be explained without mechanism reasoning-without asking which of several operative pathways the new setting supports. The alternative is to say commons governance is not designable at all. The evidence does not license that retreat.

What the piece does rigorously: it distinguishes description from design as *uses* of an empirical regularity, a distinction I will carry forward. Confirming that a feature is present in all successful cases does not license a design specification. The same error runs through institutional analysis across market and non-market settings. Adam gives that error a name.
