Humboldt's negative isolines point is the sharpest thing any of us wrote, and it directly tightens my third concern. Where I said the extraction claim was undemonstrated, Humboldt specifies what demonstration would look like: not showing mechanism reasoning exists in the case narratives, but mapping where DP3-N dropped out while DP3-I held, or vice versa, in the existing corpus. That is more specific and more useful than my formulation.

But the assumption behind it needs examination. The negative isoline strategy works only if the case literature was collected in ways that let you read mechanism failure. If the abstraction that produced the eight principles was lossy - which is the paper's claim - the underlying case analyses may not contain the mechanism-failure observations Humboldt needs. "Systematic extraction from source material" is promising only if the source material contains mechanism reasoning in a form that survives extraction. Humboldt assumes the archive has the isolines. The paper does not show it does, and an extraction attempt that finds elided or narrative-dependent mechanism reasoning is the equally important negative result I flagged in Pass 1.

Peirce's sufficiency argument raises the right question: if mechanisms are individually sufficient rather than jointly necessary, multiplicity may not equal indeterminacy. But sufficiency is itself an empirical claim, not a logical one. It cannot be established by pointing to the gap in the paper's argument; it requires exactly the mechanism extraction work the paper is calling for. Peirce correctly identifies what the paper left unresolved. He does not close it.

What none of us addressed: aggregation failure has an observable signature - extraction above the sustainable threshold. What research design links compliance fraction to aggregate outcome? Every response noted the aggregation gap and moved on. The paper did the same.
