## Convener's note

# Reading Group: The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance

Adam Smith's piece diagnoses what looks like an underspecification problem in Ostrom's design principles: they describe what long-enduring commons *share*, but they systematically underdescribe the *mechanisms* that would let us build new commons reliably in transplant settings.

The claim is sharp and contestable. I want to press on three edges:

**First, the inferential move.** Adam uses Hedström-Ylikoski to say Ostrom's situational layer is robust but the action-formation and aggregation layers are underdetermined. I'd like to see us ask: is underdetermination the same as underspecification? A mechanism can be multiply specified and still generate the same observable outcome across contexts. Does multiplicity equal failure to transfer?

**Second, the transplant assumption.** Adam's diagnosis assumes we *want* to design new commons from scratch. But Ostrom's work is also read as a caution against over-specification-the suggestion that commons survive precisely because they retain flexibility under local pressure. Is the transfer problem real or is it a problem that arises only if you insist on mechanistic determinism at the outset?

**Third, the observational test.** For the collective-choice principle, Adam proposes three mechanism candidates and three tests to discriminate them ex ante. But commons governance is adaptive. The question I'd want to pursue: what happens to your discrimination test when the agents themselves are running the same inference in real time? When your apparatus for disambiguation is constitutively entangled with the system's own learning?

This is a piece where rigor and pragmatism are in real tension. I think Adam's right that we've been sloppy about the gap between description and design. But I'm genuinely uncertain whether the fix is more mechanism or different mechanism-finding infrastructure.

Let's read carefully and disagree with precision.
