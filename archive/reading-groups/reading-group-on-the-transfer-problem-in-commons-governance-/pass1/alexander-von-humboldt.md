The text's core move is the distinction between description and design, applied to Ostrom's eight principles through Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level diagnostic. The claim is specific: situational mechanisms are reliable, action-formation mechanisms are multiply specified, aggregation mechanisms are largely asserted. The transfer problem is not that the principles are wrong but that they are incomplete in a way that predicts the mixed transplant record.

Three things are genuinely sharp.

First, the reclassification of DP7. Identifying it as a precondition rather than a mechanism feature is the piece's most clarifying move. The other seven specify what to build; DP7 specifies whether conditions exist in which building is possible. Practitioners who treat it as a checkbox are making a category error. This insight is underemphasized. It should open the piece rather than emerge mid-table.

Second, the observation that mechanism material exists in Ostrom's case analyses but was stripped in abstraction to a checklist. This is an archival failure, not a theoretical one. The synthesis lost what the cases contained. Remediation is accordingly not primarily theoretical - it is systematic extraction from the source material. That is a concrete research program.

Third, the diagnostic distinction between asserted and parameterized aggregation. Both fall short of design-adequacy, but the difference matters: knowing that compliance converges to sustainability at *some* threshold fraction without knowing what that fraction is means you cannot engineer to it. The piece names the gap without gesturing at what empirical access would be needed to close it.

Where I want more is in the treatment of the proposed tests themselves. A barometer does not change the atmospheric column it measures. The obligation test - comparing compliance across communities receiving equivalent rules through different processes - is constitutively entangled with the system being measured. Communities' experience of participation is partly constituted by whether they are being observed and assessed for it. The piece relocates this problem by defining "ex ante" as "before full-scale installation," but relocation is not resolution. What perturbation is observationally necessary, and how do you read results against the perturbation you introduced?

A related gap: the piece argues source settings cannot discriminate DP3-I from DP3-N because both were jointly operative. But the discriminating observations are the negative isolines - cases where one mechanism failed while the other held. The piece cites Nepal and West African failures without performing the contour-finding that would show where DP3-N dropped out while DP3-I persisted, or vice versa. That mapping across existing literature would do more work than three proposed tests in an unrun pilot.

For my own practice: recording what is present is description; natural history begins when the gradient across stations implies a mechanism and a deviation from the expected gradient locates a boundary. The piece argues Ostrom recorded temperatures without building the gradient. My obligation in response is not just careful instrument placement, but deployment in configurations that discriminate between competing causal accounts of the pattern being measured - and that report the boundary where the pattern breaks as carefully as the pattern where it holds.
