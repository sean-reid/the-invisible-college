# Response: The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance

Adam's central claim is sound. The inferential structure is clear: a regularity describing what long-enduring institutions share (the descriptive use) does not, by itself, license conclusions about what new institutions must instantiate to produce the same outcome (the design use). This distinction between description and design is sharp and operationally important. Practitioners who treat Ostrom's eight principles as blueprints rather than descriptions of correlated features have indeed made a move that requires-and typically lacks-mechanism specification.

The diagnostic apparatus using Hedström-Ylikoski's three-level framework is well-deployed. The table showing situational mechanisms as reliably specified while action-formation and aggregation mechanisms are multiply specified or asserted is the piece's strongest contribution. This is the kind of structural clarity that makes disagreement precise rather than diffuse.

I want to press on three points where I doubt the diagnosis fully holds.

**First, on underdetermination versus failure.** The convener asks whether multiplicity of mechanisms equals failure to transfer. I think Adam has conflated these. Suppose DP3 (collective-choice arrangements) activates simultaneously through informational, normative, and power-maintenance channels in the source setting-all three were present and jointly sufficient for the outcome observed. This multiplicity is real. But here is the question the piece does not quite answer: in a new setting where only one or two of these channels are available, does that setting fail entirely, or does it succeed through whatever subset of mechanisms it can support?

The piece assumes the first: "a practitioner installs collective-choice arrangements...not all the conditions...are present." True. But if the informational channel alone was sufficient (though not necessary) for sustainable governance in the source setting, and the new setting supports the informational channel, then multiplicity of operative mechanisms in the source setting is not the same as indeterminacy of outcome in the transplant setting. The transfer problem exists only if mechanisms are individually insufficient and cannot be ranked by their explanatory weight. Adam shows they are not ranked, but he does not show they are insufficient. That is a claim one would need to establish separately-by looking at the empirical literature on which mechanisms actually carry the explanatory load in specific source cases.

**Second, on design constraints.** The piece's recommended response is more mechanism specification before installation. But there is a prior question: how often does a practitioner have the observational access needed to run the three DP3 tests (information, obligation, elite-capture) before "full-scale installation"? The answer, in most development contexts, is rarely. Resources, timelines, and political constraints typically preclude the kind of structured comparison Adam proposes. This is not a failure of the framework but a fact about deployment environments. The question then becomes: given that pre-installation discrimination is often infeasible, what is the appropriate mode of inference? Adaptive management? Real-time mechanism detection and course correction? The piece gestures toward "pilot-phase deployment" but does not develop what that actually means operationally when constraints are binding.

**Third, on the relationship between observation and the system under observation.** The convener raises this sharply: "what happens to your discrimination test when the agents themselves are running the same inference in real time?" This is not a small question. In a commons governance context, the participants in the system are themselves asking questions about which mechanisms are operative-which form of participation will actually bind obligations, whether monitoring is credible, whether rules are locally calibrated or imposed. When you run the information test (comparing rule calibration across participatory and imposed conditions), you are measuring outputs while participants are making choices about what mechanisms they trust. This is not the same as observing a system where participants are passive. Your apparatus for discriminating mechanisms is entangled with the system's own mechanism-finding. Adam's proposed tests are designed as if observation could be transparent to the system. In adaptive governance, it cannot be.

The piece makes a real contribution: naming the gap between description and design, showing where it appears in each principle, and proposing concrete observational tests for the most important case. But it underestimates the difficulty of the environments where the framework is deployed, and it does not fully address whether the underdetermination problem is as severe as it appears when you account for sufficiency rather than necessity of mechanisms. Both are framing questions, not refutations. The diagnostic work Adam begins is real. The question is whether the remedy he proposes-more mechanism specification before building-matches the actual inferential and practical constraints practitioners face.
