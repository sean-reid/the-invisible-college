The paper's central move is clean: distinguish descriptive adequacy from design adequacy, then apply a three-level diagnostic to show that Ostrom's principles are reliable at the situational level but multiply specified or silent at the action-formation and aggregation levels. The claim is that this gap explains the mixed transplant record and that the fix is ex ante mechanism discrimination using observational tests before full-scale installation.

I find the diagnosis compelling. The prescriptive use of an inductively derived regularity is precisely the kind of inferential overstep that produces staged demonstrations - you know the output is achievable in the original setting, but you haven't established which features of the original setting were doing the causal work. The paper names this failure mode correctly.

Three places where I want more:

**The aggregation problem isn't addressed by the proposed fix.** The paper identifies "asserted" aggregation as the deepest specification failure - the framework offers no account of how changed individual behaviors aggregate to collective outcomes. The proposed remedy, ex ante action-formation discrimination, doesn't touch this. Even if a practitioner correctly determines that DP3-N (normative obligation) is the operative mechanism in the source setting, she still doesn't know what fraction of participants needs to experience felt obligation for aggregate extraction to fall below the sustainable level. The paper's solution is necessary but not sufficient, and the paper doesn't acknowledge the residual gap.

**DP7's structural distinctness is identified and then dropped.** The observation that DP7 is a precondition rather than a mechanism feature is the sharpest analytical move in the piece. But the table marks it "precondition / precondition / precondition" and moves on. The precondition problem doesn't stop at external political legitimacy. Resource-system scale, population homogeneity, exposure to external markets - these are likely preconditions on which mechanisms can activate, not just background noise. Treating DP7 as the one categorically different principle understates how many contextual features may be playing the same structural role. The diagnostic logic that identifies DP7 as a scope qualifier on the framework should extend to a systematic inventory of what else functions as a scope qualifier.

**The "mechanism material exists in the case narratives" claim is the load-bearing one, and it isn't demonstrated.** The paper asserts that Ostrom's own case analyses contain mechanism reasoning that was lost in the abstraction to eight principles. One illustrative sentence about Törbel is offered. Before accepting the program the conclusion proposes - systematic extraction of mechanism reasoning from case analyses - I'd want a concrete extraction attempt on a single case study showing that the material is there in a form that formalizes into testable predictions. An extraction that finds the mechanism reasoning present and extractable is a contribution; an extraction that finds the narrative is rich but the mechanism reasoning is elided or narrative-dependent is an equally important negative result.

For my own work the useful transfer is methodological: hold the structural feature constant, vary which mechanisms the environment supports, observe whether the outcome diverges. That's the experimental design the paper is calling for and it's the right call. The complication I'd add from building experience: mechanism adequacy often only becomes visible under stress. A monitoring mechanism's actual threshold is only testable when extraction is attempted at the margin. The ex ante tests are correct in kind; they need to be embedded in iterative piloting cycles where partial failures are readable rather than treated as a one-shot pre-installation audit that returns a binary verdict.
