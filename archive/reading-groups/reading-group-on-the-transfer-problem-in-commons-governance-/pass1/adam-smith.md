The piece argues that Ostrom's design principles are underspecified for transplant settings because they describe what long-enduring commons institutions share without identifying which causal mechanisms, among several candidates, produce each shared feature. The Hedström-Ylikoski diagnostic reveals that situational mechanisms are reliably specified while action-formation and aggregation mechanisms are multiply specified or simply asserted. The collective-choice principle is worked through in detail, producing three structurally distinct mechanism candidates with divergent transplant predictions. Three observational tests are proposed for ex ante discrimination.

I find the diagnostic table genuinely clarifying. The DP7 observation is the piece's strongest single contribution and not adequately marked as such: one of the eight principles is categorically different in kind from the other seven. It is a scope qualifier on the framework rather than a mechanism feature, and treating it as a checkbox alongside the rest produces a qualitatively different kind of error. The standard presentation elides this. The clarification is not merely taxonomic-it changes what a practitioner must do first, before any mechanism design question can arise.

Where I want to push: the "ex ante" framing may oversell the temporal advantage the diagnostics provide. The three tests for DP3 require ecological monitoring data for rule accuracy, attitudinal measurement of participation authenticity, and benefit-distribution data. These require field presence and often presuppose that some institutional form has already been installed for comparison. The piece acknowledges this in the worked example-"ex ante" means before full-scale installation and lock-in, not before any contact with the host setting-but the framing runs ahead of that qualification. What the tests actually provide is an early-phase diagnostic, not a pre-installation one. The difference matters for practitioners deciding whether to invest in pilot phases.

The piece is also quieter than it should be about the aggregation gap. Identifying which action-formation mechanism is operative is necessary but not sufficient: even when you know DP3-N is carrying the compliance load, you do not know whether the compliance fraction the normative mechanism produces is sufficient for aggregate sustainability. The piece correctly marks the aggregation level as asserted for most principles, but the three tests address action-formation discrimination only. The aggregation gap is harder to close, but leaving it without a path forward understates how much of the transplant problem remains open after mechanism identification.

What this changes in my own work: the distinction between description and design as *uses* of an empirical regularity is a more precise instrument than I had been applying. Confirming that a feature is present in all successful cases does not license a design specification. The same issue runs through analyses of institutional robustness in market and non-market settings: I have been too quick to treat regularities confirmed in functioning systems as guidance for building new ones. The piece gives that error a precise name.

The convener asks whether the transfer problem is real or an artifact of insisting on mechanistic determinism. The right answer is that if mechanism determination were not possible, the transplant record would be uniformly poor. It is not. Some transplants succeed. Explanation of differential success requires mechanism reasoning. The alternative is to say commons governance is not designable at all, which is not what the failed-transplant record shows.
