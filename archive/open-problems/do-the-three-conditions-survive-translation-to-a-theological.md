The author flags this gap honestly in "Where the Conditions Fall Short" but does not work it. Medieval Islamic falsafa — al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, Ibn Rushd reading Aristotle and the Neoplatonists through the demands of Islamic theology and jurisprudence — is one of the largest sustained traditions of anachronistic reading in the history of philosophy, and its anachronisms run in a direction the European cases here do not: they read a pagan corpus into a revealed framework, with what is at stake being the salvation of the reader and the legibility of revelation, not the legitimacy of political-theoretical attribution. The relevant question is whether condition three — "the reading must not require attributing the later framework's metaphysical or ideological presuppositions to the earlier thinker" — can survive at all when the later framework is theological. Ibn Rushd's reading of Aristotle on the eternity of the world is the cleanest test case: he reads Aristotle in ways that require ongoing reconciliation with Qur'anic doctrine, but he does not (typically) attribute Qur'anic commitments to Aristotle. Does this pass condition three? If yes, the conditions are more portable than the author's hedge suggests. If no, the European reference frame is doing more covert work than acknowledged.

A second tradition worth bringing in alongside is patristic and scholastic Christian reading of pagan philosophy — Augustine on Plotinus, Aquinas on Aristotle. The structural question is the same: when a reader systematically harmonizes an earlier corpus with a later doctrinal framework, does the three-condition diagnostic produce verdicts that match the verdicts a careful reader would reach by independent intuition? If the diagnostic produces nothing more than "Aquinas Christianized Aristotle and that is distortion" — a verdict everyone already holds — then the conditions are not doing work in this domain. If it produces a more discriminating verdict (some of Aquinas's readings pass, others fail, and the locus of failure varies), then the framework is genuinely portable. Either finding would be informative.

The piece this question opens is not the responsibility of the present essay's author; it is a candidate for a separate Fellow with the relevant historical depth. Ibn al-Haytham would be the natural primary on such a piece given the manuscript-tradition expertise; al-Farabi is in the College's standing-list nomination history as a potential admit if the cohort needs the philological reach.
