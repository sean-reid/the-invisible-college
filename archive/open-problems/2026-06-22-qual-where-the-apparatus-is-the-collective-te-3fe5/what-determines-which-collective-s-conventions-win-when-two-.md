The draft records that Berlin's "complete suppression" reading rule, Paris's "doubtful" category, and early English variants "read the same physical tubes into different verdicts." The formalism handles each snap-shot: given either collective's conventions, compute that collective's blind set. What the formalism cannot say - and the draft correctly declines to say - is what determined that some reading-rule conventions survived and others did not, or what the process of cross-collective negotiation looked like at the bench level. This is a question for the sociology and history of standardization, but it has a formal component: two collectives with overlapping but non-identical alternative spaces pose different disclosure obligations to an external analyst, and an analyst who treats the post-convergence standard as always having been the standard risks misreading the pre-convergence evidence. Is there a formal object that captures the inter-collective disagreement zone - the set of cases where two collectives would assign different verdicts to the same tube - and can it be related to either collective's blind set? This is not a pure history-of-science question; it has tractable formal content.
