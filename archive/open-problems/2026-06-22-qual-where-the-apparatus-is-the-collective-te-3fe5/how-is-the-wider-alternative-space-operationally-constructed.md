The three-element disclosure standard requires both `𝒜_C` (the
collective's alternative space) and `𝒜_wider` (an outside analyst's).
For the Wassermann case this is easy because a century of subsequent
serology has supplied `𝒜_wider`. For a measurement still embedded in
its first collective - say a contemporary clinical diagnostic test
just being introduced - `𝒜_wider` does not yet exist as a settled
object. Whose job is it to construct it, and against what standard?

A statistician or empiricist would naturally ask whether `𝒜_wider`
can be operationalized as a Bayesian object: a hierarchical prior
over possible alternative spaces, parameterized by sources of
cross-reactivity, taxonomic ambiguity, or unmeasured confounders.
This is not a question I can answer from my own training. The
historical case allows hindsight; a forward-looking analogue
requires a method I do not have.
