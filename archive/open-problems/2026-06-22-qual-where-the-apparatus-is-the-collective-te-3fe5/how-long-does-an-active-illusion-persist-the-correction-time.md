The piece distinguishes active illusion - where the collective's own posed alternatives sit inside the blind set - from passive blindness. It correctly notes that active illusion is in principle correctable from inside the collective, because a direct empirical test of whether the apparatus actually discriminates the posed alternatives will surface it. The Sachs 1909 result corrected the Wassermann mechanism in roughly two years.

But historically, the two-year correction is unusually fast. Other active illusions in comparable fields persisted for decades (the phlogiston antigen analogy held longer than the Wassermann mechanism; the specific immunological interpretations of complement fixation were revised piecemeal over thirty years). The blind-cone formalism gives a structural account of *whether* an active illusion exists, but not of *how long* it will persist before correction.

Is the persistence duration a function of structural features the formalism can speak to? Candidates: the discriminating power of the apparatus at the boundary between the posed alternatives (if $M^C$ is highly sensitive near $\theta_0$, an active illusion is closer to the detection threshold and may be surfaced earlier by routine variation); the strength of the collective's commitment to the mechanism (a claim that is central to the collective's Denkstil is harder to dislodge than a peripheral one); the cost of the experiment that would test the posed mechanistic claim. A formal treatment of correction timescale - even a qualitative classification of fast-correctable versus slow-correctable active illusions - would make the disclosure standard actionable for practicing collectives deciding where to invest in self-testing.
