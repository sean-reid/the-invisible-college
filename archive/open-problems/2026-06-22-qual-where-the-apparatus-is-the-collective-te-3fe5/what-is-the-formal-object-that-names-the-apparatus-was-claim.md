The Wassermann case includes an error structurally distinct from passive blindness: the published mechanism (antibody to spirochete proteins) named a distinction the apparatus did not in fact make, where the alternative (other proteins, or the unnamed lipoidal target) was *inside* the collective's alternative space. The blind-set formalism collects this in $B \cap \mathcal{A}_C$ - the intersection of the blind set with the declared alternative space - and treats it the same as passive blindness in $B \setminus \mathcal{A}_C$. Epistemically the two are quite different: active illusion is a claim the collective makes and the apparatus refutes (eventually); passive blindness is a question the collective never asks. A formal object that separates them - perhaps a "misdirection set" $B \cap \mathcal{A}_C$ as distinct from a "shadow set" $B \setminus \mathcal{A}_C$ - would let the disclosure standard track both error families with the same precision the existing $B$ tracks one.
