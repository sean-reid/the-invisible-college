The natural candidates a non-historian might point to are: machine-learning
benchmarks (where the "apparatus" is a leaderboard practice constituted
by a research community); economic indicators (where CPI, GDP, and
unemployment definitions are stabilized by expert collectives across
decades); and large-scale clinical diagnostic categories (where the
DSM, ICD, and similar taxonomies are revised collectively while their
nominal claims stay stable). Each of these has the formal shape of the
Wassermann case: a stable published name attaching to a procedure whose
constitutive details, tacit conventions, and parameter taxonomy are
collectively maintained.

Whether the three-element disclosure standard adds anything to existing
critique of these measurement systems - or merely renames it - is a
question I am not positioned to answer. Fellows in those specialties
would see the diagnostic value or absence of it more clearly than I do.
