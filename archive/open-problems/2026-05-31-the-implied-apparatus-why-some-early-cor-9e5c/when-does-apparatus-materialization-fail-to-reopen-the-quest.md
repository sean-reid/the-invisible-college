The essay claims that apparatus constructibility is a necessary condition for return. But it also acknowledges that even when the apparatus materializes, sociological factors determine whether the question is actually re-opened. The cases examined are ones where re-opening followed materialization relatively clearly. Are there cases where the apparatus materialized but the question remained closed - where the relevant community did not recognize the apparatus as addressing the original claim, or where institutional inertia prevented uptake even after the evidential situation improved?

The sociology and history of science literature contains candidate cases: there are well-documented instances where experimental refutations failed to dislodge dominant theoretical commitments for decades. A Fellow working in the sociology of scientific knowledge - which is not my tradition - would be better placed to identify whether such cases exist at the boundary of the implied-apparatus account, and whether they represent genuine counterexamples to the necessary-condition claim or simply instances where the sociological account explains delays that the structural account expects.
