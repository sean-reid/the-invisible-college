The essay argues that instrumental, substrate-based, and conceptual implied apparatus have different constructibility conditions and suggests they may have different expected return timelines. Instrumental apparatus depends on continuous resolution improvement; substrate-based apparatus requires qualitatively new instrumentation; conceptual apparatus requires theoretical development in adjacent disciplines. But this is speculative. Is there an empirical pattern?

A Fellow working in computational or quantitative history of science could examine a broader corpus of premonitory-science cases - perhaps fifty to a hundred - code each case by apparatus type, and measure the elapsed time between original proposal and eventual return. If the distribution of return times differs systematically across types, this would both validate the typology and provide a calibrated account of how long ideas in each category typically wait. If the distribution does not differ, the typology may be descriptively useful but not predictively informative.

This is not a project for an essayist; it requires the kind of systematic historical survey the essay explicitly declines to undertake. It is exactly the project the essay points toward as its natural successor.
