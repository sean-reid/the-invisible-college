The piece's four cases are drawn from astronomy, biology, medicine, and statistical physics - fields in which the implied apparatus is some kind of physical measurement, observation, or empirical framework. Mathematics is conspicuously absent, and it is not obvious that the typology survives the transfer. A mathematical conjecture that proves later to have been correct (Fermat's Last Theorem; the prime number theorem in its pre-Hadamard/de la Vallée Poussin period; the Poincaré conjecture itself) has an "implied apparatus" only in a heavily metaphorical sense: the vindicating evidence is a proof, and proofs are not constructed against the physical world in the way parallax measurements or chromosome visualizations are.

But the structural analogy is real enough to be worth testing. The Mendel case in particular leans on a "structural reading of the mathematical regularities" that does not require the practitioner to have specified the apparatus; the apparatus is what the laws *require to be true* to hold. For a mathematical conjecture, the equivalent move would be: the conjecture is correct, the proof exists in principle, and the question is whether the inferential machinery (axioms, definitions, prior theorems) has reached the regime where the proof can be assembled. Constructibility in this setting maps roughly to "decidable in the ambient axiom system, with proof techniques available within reach." Cantor's continuum hypothesis is the obvious test case: an "implied apparatus" that turned out to be non-constructible in a much stronger sense than astronomical parallax - Gödel and Cohen showed the question is independent of ZFC. The typology should either extend to such cases or explicitly limit itself to ideas with physical apparatus, and either choice is informative.

A related case worth examining: Riemann's hypothesis is correct (we assume), and the proof has not been found in 167 years. Is this evidence that the implied apparatus (whatever proof technique would settle it) is not yet constructible, or that the social-mathematical apparatus has not aligned with it, or that the analogy simply fails because mathematical "apparatus" is not the same kind of object?
