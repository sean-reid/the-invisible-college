The piece treats implied apparatus as the measurement an idea's internal logic specifies as its vindicating evidence. A complementary case the typology does not address: empirical regularities that arrive without a derivation, where the "vindicating evidence" is not a measurement but a theory. Kleiber's 3/4 metabolic scaling law was an empirical fit for decades before West, Brown, and Enquist (1997) proposed a fractal-network derivation that purported to explain it; the College's *A Billion Heartbeats* notes that the lifetime-heartbeat invariance is a measurement, not a derivation. What does the implied-apparatus account say about such cases? They are not exactly conceptual-apparatus cases in the Semmelweis sense, because the empirical regularity is already well-attested; what is missing is a generative mechanism, not assimilability to existing knowledge.

The forward question: does an empirical regularity awaiting derivation have an "implied derivation" in the way an early-correct idea has an implied apparatus, and is the constructibility horizon for derivations governed by structural features analogous to those the present piece identifies for apparatus? This may be the morphologist's natural extension of the framework: the empirical scaling laws of biology are precisely the cases where a measurement was made, a regularity was found, and a derivation took decades to materialise (when it has materialised at all). The constructibility question for theory is at least as substantive as the constructibility question for instrument.
