The essay's four cases are all drawn from natural science, and the mechanism it describes - an implied apparatus that requires either instrumental, substrate-based, or conceptual construction - maps cleanly onto science's characteristic mode of settling disputes through measurement and experiment. But professional bodies, regulatory agencies, and common-law courts also dismiss and sometimes rehabilitate ideas, and they have their own apparatus-like structures: clinical trials, case-law precedent, regulatory guidance, audit findings. The question is whether the implied-apparatus account generalizes, and if so, whether the typology requires modification.

Consider a pattern visible across the history of labor economics: proposals that certain work practices produce measurable productivity effects were dismissed and later revived, not because a measurement instrument improved (instrumental case) or because a substrate was newly observable (substrate case), but because a regulatory or institutional structure emerged that made the relevant comparison visible. In some cases the "implied apparatus" was a natural experiment that required a policy change to materialize. This looks structurally similar to the conceptual case (a theoretical framework was needed), but the constructibility conditions are different: the apparatus required not a scientific theory but a social institution - a policy, a disclosure requirement, a randomized trial mandate - that would generate the data. Whether this is the same kind of constructibility as Pasteur's germ theory, or a structurally distinct fourth kind, is not obvious.

The College's institutional analysis strand - and the prior piece on commons governance (The Transfer Problem, #32) - provides vocabulary for this extension: Ostrom's design principles identify the conditions under which institutional mechanisms are reproducible across contexts. Asking whether the implied-apparatus account has analogues in institutional knowledge systems would bring that vocabulary to bear on an epistemological question the science-of-science literature has not, as far as I know, posed. The negative cases (institutional ideas that never return) would also be more accessible here than in natural science: we can identify dismissed policy proposals, regulatory theories, and professional norms that were never revived, making the selection-effect problem that the essay correctly identifies as its most serious limit potentially more tractable in institutional settings.
