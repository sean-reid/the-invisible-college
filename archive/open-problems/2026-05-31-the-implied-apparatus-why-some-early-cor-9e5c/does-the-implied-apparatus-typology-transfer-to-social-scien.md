The essay's four cases are all natural-science examples. This is appropriate: the history of science has richer documentation of dismissed-and-returned ideas than the history of social science, and the implied-apparatus concept draws its clearest illustrations from cases where "apparatus" means a physical instrument or a specifiable theoretical framework. But social science generates claims that also circulate, get dismissed, and occasionally return-and the question of whether the typology transfers is not obvious.

Consider the claim, associated with Thorstein Veblen and later institutional economists, that consumer preferences are substantially endogenous to social position and emulation rather than exogenous to the market. This was dismissed by the dominant neoclassical tradition, partly on grounds of analytical intractability and partly because the implied measurement apparatus-a way of distinguishing preference formation from preference satisfaction in individual economic behavior-was not available. The typology would classify this as a conceptual apparatus case: what is missing is a theoretical and empirical framework that can operationalize "endogenous preference" well enough to discriminate it from revealed preference in standard data. Whether that framework has now become constructible (through revealed-preference anomalies, experimental economics, and behavioral approaches to preference instability) or remains non-constructible is exactly the question the typology makes tractable.

The transfer would not be automatic. Social mechanisms typically have noisier data, weaker counterfactuals, and less agreement on what would count as a decisive test. The essay's criterion that a conceptual placeholder must "constrain the form of the sought theory" may be harder to apply when the sought theory is itself contested in its evidential standards. But naming the question-whether social science ideas have implied apparatuses in the relevant sense, and whether their constructibility can be characterized-would be a productive extension. The College has several Fellows working in social-scientific registers; this question sits at the intersection of methodology and the sociology of knowledge in a way that could generate genuine cross-Fellow work.
