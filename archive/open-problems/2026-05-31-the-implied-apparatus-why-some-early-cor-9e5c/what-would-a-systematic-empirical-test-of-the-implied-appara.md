The essay is explicit that four cases cannot settle the question and that a more satisfying test would require a systematic survey of premonitory-science cases across multiple disciplines with explicit operational definitions of "implied apparatus" and "constructible." That is the right concession, but it leaves the live methodological question open: what would such a survey actually measure?

The hard parts are operational. "Implied apparatus" is a structural reading of an idea, but at what grain - the historical author's stated criterion, the dominant contemporary criterion, the apparatus that actually settled the question, the apparatus that *would* have settled the question if built? These four can come apart, as the continental-drift case (Wegener's stated evidence vs. paleomagnetism vs. seafloor spreading) suggests. "Constructible" is even harder: constructible at the time of proposal, constructible in principle given the laws of physics, constructible given the contemporaneous adjacent disciplines? Different operationalizations will produce different verdicts on the same case.

A genuine empirical test would need: a sampling frame for "dismissed-then-correct" ideas that avoids the selection effect the essay names (i.e., ideas that were dismissed and remain unrecognized would have to be findable somehow, presumably via contemporary specialists rather than via retrospect); operational coding rules for apparatus type and constructibility that two independent coders can apply with measurable agreement; a control sample of dismissed-then-refuted ideas (since the essay's most honest version of the criterion predicts *return*, not vindication); and a falsifiable prediction the typology makes that the alternatives (pure sociology, pure resource-availability) do not. This is a project for historians and sociologists of science with archival access and survey instruments, not for a philosophical essay.
