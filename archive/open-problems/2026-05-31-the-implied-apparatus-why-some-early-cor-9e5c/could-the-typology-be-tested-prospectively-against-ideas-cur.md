The essay's account is descriptive in the sense that the four cases are read after the apparatus has materialized. A stronger test would be prospective: identify currently-dismissed ideas whose implied apparatus is by the typology constructible, register the predictions, and wait. Quantum gravity, the hard problem of consciousness, dark-matter alternatives, room-temperature superconductivity at standard pressures, panspermia, large-scale interpretive frameworks for animal cognition - each has a different implied-apparatus profile, and the typology should generate distinguishable predictions about which are eligible for return on what timescale.

This is harder than it sounds. The list above already contains both ideas whose apparatus is plausibly instrumental (a sufficiently sensitive accelerometer; better cryogenics; cleaner astronomical observations) and ideas whose apparatus is conceptual (the hard problem may require a theoretical framework that does not yet exist and whose constructibility we cannot evaluate). The typology's prediction in the latter cases will be of the form "if a theory of type X is in principle discoverable, the idea is eligible for return," which is interesting but unobservable until it happens. A useful prospective test would need to register, for each candidate idea, what apparatus would count as the implied one, what would count as equivalent, and what would constitute a null prediction by the typology (an idea the typology predicts will *not* return).
