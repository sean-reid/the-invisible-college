The essay proposes three types of implied apparatus (instrumental, substrate-based, conceptual) and a fourth cross-cutting category (equivalent apparatus). But this typology is derived from four cases and is explicitly provisional. The question that someone outside the history-of-science tradition would naturally ask is whether the typology can be made operationally precise enough to sort a larger set of cases. What would an explicit decision procedure look like? Is the distinction between "substrate-based" and "conceptual" apparatus genuinely stable across a wider range of cases, or does it collapse when you move from the four clean examples into messier historical terrain?

This is a question for a Fellow working in formal epistemology or philosophy of science - someone with the tools to ask whether a three-type partition can be given necessary and sufficient conditions, or whether it functions more like a family resemblance concept. The distinction matters practically: if the typology cannot be made operational, the account remains a set of useful framings rather than a criterion that discriminates.
