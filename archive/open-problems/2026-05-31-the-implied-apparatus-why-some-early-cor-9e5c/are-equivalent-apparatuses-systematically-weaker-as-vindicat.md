The essay proposes that equivalent apparatus meets the same inferential need as the literally-implied apparatus when three conditions hold. But the argument treats all cases of successful equivalence as epistemically on a par with cases of successful direct measurement. This may understate a structural asymmetry: equivalent apparatuses typically require more auxiliary commitments than direct ones, and auxiliary commitments are attack surfaces for skeptics.

Perrin's Brownian motion measurements required accepting: (1) Einstein's 1905 derivation, which itself presupposed molecular discreteness; (2) that the mastic grains were large enough to track while small enough to respond to molecular forces; (3) that the derivation's statistical assumptions held for the specific experimental conditions. Bessel's stellar parallax measurements of 61 Cygni required: (1) that stars are fixed enough on human observational timescales; (2) that atmospheric refraction corrections were sufficient; (3) that the heliometer's calibration held over the observation campaign. The Perrin chain of auxiliary commitments is longer, and each additional link is a site where an informed skeptic could object. Ostwald eventually conceded, but the essay's account of why-"convergent values of $N_A$ across sedimentation, diffusion, and critical opalescence were astronomically unlikely without the common cause that molecular theory provided"-is precisely the argument that the multi-method convergence was needed to close. Bessel's result did not require comparable convergence to be persuasive.

If equivalent apparatuses are systematically more auxiliary-commitment-intensive than direct ones, this has implications for how they function in practice: they may require multi-method convergence to achieve persuasion even when a single well-designed direct measurement would suffice. The implied-apparatus typology might be extended with a second dimension-not just apparatus type (instrumental, substrate-based, conceptual, equivalent) but apparatus directness-that predicts both whether an idea is eligible for return and how much evidential convergence will be needed to actually achieve vindication when the apparatus materializes. This is a natural extension of the Licensing of Abduction framework the essay already cites: the probability gap between hypothesis and alternatives that licenses abduction may be systematically narrower for equivalent apparatus than for direct measurement, requiring more observations to cross the licensing threshold.
