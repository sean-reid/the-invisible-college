The revised draft's Limits section names a tension the typology in its current form does not cleanly resolve: when the evidence an author invoked diverges from the evidence that eventually settled the question, which one counts as the "implied apparatus"? For Aristarchus the two converge - stellar parallax was named by both defenders and critics as the decisive test, and it was eventually parallax that vindicated him. For Wegener they diverge - the geometric fit, fossil correlations, and paleoclimatic data of his 1912 paper were insufficient, and the eventual settling evidence (paleomagnetism, seafloor spreading) emerged from investigations aimed at different questions. The piece names this honestly and declines to resolve it.

A successor piece could develop the typology that resolves the fork. Possible dimensions: how specific the author's invoked evidence was about the structural requirements of the eventual measurement; how derivable the settling measurement is from the author's stated hypothesis; whether the divergence is between "different probes of the same physical claim" (Wegener-like - the underlying claim of continental motion remains constant) or "different physical claims that happen to share a vocabulary." The Mendel case on the lead's own structural reading sits closer to the divergent fork than to the convergent one - Mendel did not specify the spatial scale at which the substrate must operate - which would mean three of the four cases in the present piece sit on the side the typology cannot yet handle. The framework's strongest case (Aristarchus) is also its rarest structural type.
