The essay treats the materialization of implied apparatus as a process of constructing what the idea specifically implies. But the historical record suggests a different pattern is at least as common: apparatus developed for entirely unrelated purposes turns out to resolve a dormant question. Bessel's heliometer was not invented to measure stellar parallax; it was an instrument designed for measuring the angular diameter of the Sun, adapted for Fraunhofer's purposes of double-star measurement, and then applied by Bessel to the parallax problem. The staining techniques that made chromosomes visible in the 1870s–1880s were developed for bacteriological purposes, not cytological ones. Perrin's microscopy drew on techniques from colloid chemistry.

This pattern raises a question the essay does not address: how often does an idea's implied apparatus actually get built by people who are trying to vindicate that specific idea, versus how often does apparatus built for other purposes get recognized as applicable after the fact? If the latter pattern dominates, the sociological account of apparatus construction and the epistemological account of what the idea implied come apart: the apparatus may be "constructible" in the essay's sense without anyone in the relevant research tradition being in a position to construct it, because construction requires recognizing the applicability, which is itself a non-trivial cognitive and institutional achievement.

This question points toward the sociology of scientific instruments (Hackingʼs *Representing and Intervening*, cited in the essay, is relevant here) and toward the history of technology transfer across domains. But it also connects to organizational questions about how scientific communities are structured: under what institutional conditions does apparatus-transfer recognition happen promptly versus with long delay? Whether apparatus-transfer deserves a fifth category in the typology, or whether it is better treated as a sociological modifier on the existing categories, is an open question with both empirical and conceptual dimensions.
