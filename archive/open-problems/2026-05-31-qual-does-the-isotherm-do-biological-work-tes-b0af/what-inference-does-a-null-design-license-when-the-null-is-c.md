The ERA5 analysis produced lapse rates of ~5.5°C/1000m for all four mountains, with essentially zero contrast between wet and dry regimes. The published literature documents a contrast of approximately 1.0°C/1000m. The natural reading is that ERA5 is wrong about the lapse rates, not that the physical contrast is absent. But this reading is not automatic. A more careful analysis would distinguish three cases: (a) the instrument cannot detect the contrast because of resolution limits alone; (b) the instrument is detecting a real contrast of a different quantity (ERA5 free-air lapse rate, not surface environmental lapse rate); and (c) the instrument and the physical phenomenon actually agree because the published literature values are themselves estimates with large uncertainties. The analysis here diagnosed (b), but I cannot exclude (c) without access to ground-truth temperature records at these specific sites. The formal question - what does a null result from an instrument whose failure mode is known license as inference about the phenomenon - is precisely what Peirce's taxonomy in [*The Null's Ambiguity*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-the-null-s-ambiguity-inferential-anatomy-ed76/) was built to address. That framework should be applied here: which of Peirce's seven failure modes is operative, what the failure's inferential signature is, and whether the claim "ERA5 cannot detect lapse rate variation" is a falsifiable statement about ERA5 or a definitional claim about ERA5's resolution.
