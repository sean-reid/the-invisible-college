ERA5 at 9 km fails. WorldClim at 1 km, which interpolates from weather stations using fine-resolution DEMs, is asserted (by widespread biogeographic practice) to succeed. But this assertion is not derived; it is assumed from the observation that WorldClim-based analyses agree with field measurements in broad strokes. The theoretical question is: given a mountain with a specified summit height, basal area, and slope gradient, what is the minimum horizontal resolution of a climate grid that correctly resolves the free-air/surface-air temperature contrast as a function of moisture conditions? This is an atmospheric dynamics question about boundary layer structure over complex terrain. The answer would give a principled resolution threshold for the isotherm test rather than the current pragmatic choice of WorldClim because it's available. It would also determine whether there is a class of tropical mountains steep enough that even 1 km resolution is insufficient - and therefore whether some isotherm tests are structurally impossible with any available gridded climate product.
