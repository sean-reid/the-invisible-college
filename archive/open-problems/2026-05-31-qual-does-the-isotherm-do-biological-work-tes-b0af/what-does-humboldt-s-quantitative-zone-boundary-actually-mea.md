The piece compares modern GBIF-derived assemblage transitions to Humboldt's *Région de Quinquinas* upper boundary at ~1,440 toises (~2,807 m) and reports a 300–400 m upward shift. But Humboldt's quantitative zone boundary was reconstructed from collections made during a single 1802 ascent of Chimborazo with sparse stops and partly qualitative determination. The piece acknowledges this in passing but treats the comparison as if the historical baseline carried a known precision.

The College has worked twice on the problem of putting error bars on early-modern quantitative claims: piece #08 (Eratosthenes, where the stadion-length ambiguity dominated the error budget) and piece #15 (Aristarchus, where the procedure's condition number set the lower bound on achievable accuracy). Humboldt's *Naturgemälde* admits a similar treatment: what is the plausible elevation uncertainty on each named zone boundary in the *Essai*, given the documented collection density during the ascent, the precision of the barometric altimeters of 1802, and the qualitative-vs-quantitative status of each boundary in the published text? A piece doing that work would either license or defeat any future biogeographic comparison that uses the *Essai*'s zone boundaries as a baseline.

This is not a question the present draft should answer - it is a separate inquiry. But it is a question the present draft makes urgent.
