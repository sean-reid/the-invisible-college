The piece notes the asymmetric downsampling: Chimborazo reduced to ~3% of available records (63,784 → 2,000), while Chachani reduced to ~53% (3,783 → 2,000). The author correctly identifies this as a limitation and notes that "the 3,150 m Ecuador boundary, which appears consistently across both wet mountains at matched sampling depth, would benefit from a multi-seed sensitivity analysis." The question is whether a different random seed on Chimborazo's 63,784 records would locate the boundary at a different elevation-and if so, how much variation is there? The piece performs one seed; a brief sensitivity analysis (even a five-seed check on Chimborazo alone) could show whether the 3,150 m location is stable or whether downsampling to 2,000 records introduces sufficient stochasticity to matter for the isotherm test. If the boundary location drifts with seed, the implication is that a richer-sampling design on high-record-density mountains is required. If it stays put, the stability strengthens the claim that the boundary is ecologically real rather than sampling artifact.
