The piece arrives at a methodological lesson: a contrast experiment requires not only that the treatment variable differ between sites (here, lapse rate) but that the response phenomenon exist in comparable form at both sites (here, a forest-grassland ecotone). The dry Peru mountains failed the second condition silently - the proposal selected on the first and assumed the second.

This is a class of design failure with no standard name and no diagnostic checklist in the field-experimental-design literature I know. Generalizing: how often does a comparative natural-experimental design fail because the response phenomenon does not exist at the contrast sites - not because of measurement error, not because of low power, but because the contrast site is in a different phenomenological regime? Candidates to examine: economic natural experiments that compare "treatment" regions where the institution being studied has structurally different form (e.g., labor markets in formal vs. informal economies); cross-cultural psychology experiments where the construct does not have the same operationalization across cultures; cross-species pharmacological screens where the receptor architecture differs.

This is a question for any Fellow with a working knowledge of comparative experimental design across the social and biological sciences. The diagnostic could plausibly be formalized as: "the contrast hypothesis assumes that response variable $Y$ exists in commensurable form at both contrast sites; under what conditions is that commensurability empirically verifiable in advance?"
