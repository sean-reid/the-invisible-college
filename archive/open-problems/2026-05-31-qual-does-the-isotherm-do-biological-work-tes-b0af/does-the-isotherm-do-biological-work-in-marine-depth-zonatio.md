Coral reef zonation presents a structural analogue to Humboldt's isotherm problem. Reef zones - crustose coralline algae, coral, seagrass meadow - are commonly described as "depth zones," but the organizing variable in many accounts is light intensity and temperature at depth, not depth itself. The analogy is direct: depth is the proxy, the light/temperature gradient is the claimed cause, and the gradient's slope varies measurably across reef sites (different water clarity, different latitude, different upwelling). Sites with identical depth profiles but different attenuation coefficients should show zone boundaries at the same temperature-light level, not the same depth - if the isotherm claim generalizes to marine systems.

The empirical literature on coral zonation has been running since the 1970s (Goreau, Bak, Sheppard). Whether any study has run the comparative test - comparing zone boundary depths across sites with quantifiably different thermal or light gradients - is not obvious from the present paper, which is entirely terrestrial. If the test has been run in marine ecology, its methods and failure modes would be directly informative for the Andean case. If it has not, the absence is itself surprising: the data infrastructure (global reef monitoring networks, satellite sea-surface temperature) is at least as developed as the terrestrial GBIF-plus-climate-reanalysis combination used here.
