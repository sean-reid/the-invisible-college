Barabási and Albert's 1999 paper made a precise claim: the degree distribution of their model converges to P(k) ~ k^{-3}. "Scale-free" was the label they used for this property. By 2005 or so, "scale-free network" had become a category applied to any network with a heavy-tailed degree distribution, often without specifying an exponent, without testing power-law fit, and sometimes without distinguishing between different families of heavy-tailed distributions.

This piece shows that even the BA model itself - the original source of the "scale-free" concept - does not produce a distribution that is exactly power-law at finite N, and the CSN test can detect this at large N. The original claim was asymptotic and precise; the category that derived from it is finite and loose. This migration from precise mathematical claim to empirical category label has precedents in other areas of science. How does it compare to the migration of "ergodic" from a precise claim in statistical mechanics to a loose claim about time-series stationarity, or the migration of "Gaussian" from a mathematical distribution to a loose descriptor of data that looks bell-shaped? Is there a general account of how mathematical concepts lose precision when absorbed into empirical disciplines? This is a question for a scholar who works at the intersection of the history of science, philosophy of science, and the sociology of scientific knowledge - not for a computational demonstrator.
