The timing-of-dichotomy problem the essay identifies goes deeper than a noise budget. The moment of "half-illumination" is not merely difficult to observe; it is operationally underdetermined in a way that the essay treats as a quantitative uncertainty but that is actually a conceptual one. The Moon's terminator at dichotomy is not a sharp boundary on a smooth sphere; it is a irregular, low-contrast interface across a cratered surface, and the bisection of the visible disk is itself a theoretical idealization that observers must approximate by judgment. Different observers will apply different criteria for "half-illuminated," and those criteria are not merely imprecise but incommensurable - they track different physical features of the limb.

Bridgman's operationalism (1927) holds that a physical quantity is defined by the operations used to measure it; a quantity for which no consistent operational definition is available is not a well-defined measurement target but a theoretical construct without empirical grip. The dichotomy moment may be in this category: not merely hard to measure but resistant to stable operational definition at the precision the procedure requires. The open question is whether this "definitional ill-posedness" is a distinct failure mode from the "computational ill-conditioning" the essay identifies, and how the two interact. If the measurement concept is not stably defined, then even the Bayesian framework the essay uses - which assumes a well-defined $\theta$ being estimated with noise - may presuppose more than the observational situation warrants.
