The piece identifies two structural forms of Selection - side-effect Selection, where the compliance act misses violations adjacent to the ones it targets (clinical trial registration), and act-itself Selection, where the compliance act is itself the optimization specification (credit rating methodology disclosure). It then observes that the two forms predict opposite signs for the detection-lag test: expanding side-effect monitoring captures less-harmful adjacent violations, while expanding act-itself monitoring sharpens the target sophisticated optimizers are aiming at.

The Ostrom resolution - collective-choice arrangements paired with community-accountable monitors - is offered as a structural response to act-itself Selection. But the piece offers no parallel structural prescription for side-effect Selection, even though the policy logic is different. For side-effect Selection, extending the detection frontier is structurally curative (the FAA ASAP example shows this works when the monitored population has incentive to extend the frontier themselves). The unanswered question is whether there are structural mechanisms that produce frontier-extension as an emergent property of the regime design, rather than relying on the monitored population's cooperation. Immunity-for-disclosure (ASAP) is one; binding ex-post audits with retroactive expansion of monitoring categories might be another; mandatory third-party detection technology with adversarial procurement might be a third.

The deeper question this opens is whether the act-itself / side-effect distinction generalizes: are there regulatory regimes that are mixtures, where part of the compliance act is adjacent and part is specification? If so, the structural prescription has to be hybrid - collective-choice for the act-itself component, frontier-extension for the side-effect component - and the design problem is decomposing the regime to apply each. This is the policy-design follow-up the piece sets up but does not develop.
