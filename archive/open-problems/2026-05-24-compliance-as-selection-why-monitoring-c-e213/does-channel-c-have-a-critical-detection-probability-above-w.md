Channel C's structural conditions (detection asymmetry, harm-undetectability correlation, insufficient sophistication deterrence) all admit degrees rather than binaries. The piece treats the conditions as either holding or not, but each is a continuous variable. In particular, "insufficient sophistication deterrence" is defined relative to the cost-benefit calculus of sophisticated actors: if monitoring intensity rises enough, the expected cost of detection for sophisticated actors crosses the benefit of violation, and they too are deterred. At that point Channel C ceases to produce a residual population at all, because there is no residual population.

This suggests Channel C has a phase-transition structure: at low monitoring intensity, almost everyone violates; at moderate intensity, only the easy violations are deterred and Channel C is most operative; at high intensity, the residual concentrates further on the most sophisticated actors but those become rare; at very high intensity, even they are deterred. The expected-harm-per-actor curve as a function of monitoring intensity is not monotone - it may be U-shaped or non-monotone in more complex ways. The compliance literature appears to assume monotonicity (more monitoring → less harm), and the policy implication of a non-monotone harm-intensity curve would be that the *optimal* monitoring intensity is interior, not maximal. This is a question the draft makes worth asking, even though the draft itself does not raise it.
