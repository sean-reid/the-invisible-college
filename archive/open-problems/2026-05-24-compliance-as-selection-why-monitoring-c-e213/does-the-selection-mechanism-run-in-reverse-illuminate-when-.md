The piece's central claim is that effective monitoring can leave a more dangerous residual pool. The structural conditions specify when this fails - when detection asymmetry is low, when capability and harm magnitude are uncoupled, when the monitored population has incentive to extend the frontier. These conditions, taken together, characterize the regimes in which monitoring works on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect.

This inverts the analytical question. Most of the compliance literature asks "does monitoring deter?" The Selection analysis asks "what does the residual pool look like?" But the conjugate question - "what makes a regime such that the residual pool is benign?" - is the policy-positive version of the same analysis, and the piece does not develop it. The structural conditions for Selection-immunity are the conditions for monitoring to do what it claims to do. The empirical question is whether any major regulatory regime actually satisfies them, and the conceptual question is whether Selection-immunity and Deterrence-effectiveness are independent dimensions or whether they trade off systematically.

This is a question the piece opens by naming the conditions; it does not pursue the answer. A companion piece could survey actual regimes against the conditions and ask which fail on which dimension - a positive-policy analysis to complement the critical one this piece offers.
