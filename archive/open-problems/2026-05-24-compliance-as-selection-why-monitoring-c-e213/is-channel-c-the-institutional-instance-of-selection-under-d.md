Channel C as the piece describes it has the same structural shape as antibiotic resistance under treatment pressure: a population subjected to a selection mechanism that is effective at the center of the population's trait distribution clears the susceptible end and leaves a residual whose composition shifts toward the resistant end. The adversarial-ML literature describes the same mechanism in deployed defenses: a classifier hardened against known attacks shifts the attack distribution toward attacks the classifier cannot recognize, and the residual attack pool is selected for the property of undetectability-under-this-defense. These cognate mechanisms have formal mathematical models - Fisher's selection equation in the evolutionary case, gradient-based attack-generation in the ML case - that produce quantitative predictions the verbal social-mechanism account in the draft cannot.

The question is whether the institutional case (Channel C) admits the same kind of formal modeling, and if so, what it would predict that the verbal account does not. Specifically: under what parameter regime does increasing monitoring intensity *reduce* expected harm despite Channel C operating? Is there a critical detection probability above which the selection effect reverses because even the sophisticated actors are deterred? What is the analog of *fitness cost* in the institutional case - does the work required to evade sophisticated monitoring carry a cost to the violator that bounds how concentrated the residual pool can become? The piece's three observable implications would be sharper if backed by a formal model that specified ranges, not just directions.
