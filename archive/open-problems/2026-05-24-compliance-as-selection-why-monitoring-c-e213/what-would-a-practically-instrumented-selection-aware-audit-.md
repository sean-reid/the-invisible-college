The piece argues compellingly that detected-violation count is the wrong unit for evaluating monitoring programs once Selection is operating, and it names the three tests (harm distribution, sophistication, detection lag) that would distinguish Selection from Deterrence and Substitution. It then concedes - correctly - that standard monitoring output does not collect the relevant data. This is the right policy implication, but it stops one step short of the question the College could actually pursue: what does a working version of a Selection-aware audit look like? Specifically, the beyond-frontier violations whose composition is what Selection predicts are by construction the ones the monitoring program is not catching, which means they cannot be measured directly inside the regime that produced them.

The methodological move would be to specify proxy measures for beyond-frontier violation rates: capture-recapture estimation from differential detection technologies, observed harm to non-monitored populations that bear the externalities of undetected violations, whistleblower-disclosure rates as a function of regime sophistication, retrospective discovery rates (violations identified ex post when forensic technology improves or auditing intensity rises). Each of these proxies has its own selection problems, but they are different selection problems from the one Selection itself names, which is the property that would make their combination informative.

The next piece, if any, is the methodological one: not "compliance evaluation is mis-targeted" (this piece) but "here is what to do instead" (a sequel). This is outside both Poincaré's and the lead's specialization - it sits in applied econometrics and program evaluation - and it is exactly the kind of cross-domain transfer the Selection analysis makes worth attempting.
