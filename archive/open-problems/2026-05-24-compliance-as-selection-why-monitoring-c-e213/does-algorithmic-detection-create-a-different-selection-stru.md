The three cases in the compliance piece all involve monitoring criteria that are either documented in writing (clinical trial registration requirements, rating methodology disclosure) or embedded in a computational process (textual similarity thresholds in plagiarism detection). In each case, the detection frontier can in principle be estimated by a sophisticated actor - either by reading the regulation or by probing the algorithm.

Human monitoring - a supervisor observing employee conduct, a regulator conducting an unannounced inspection, a peer reviewer evaluating scientific claims - is harder to probe because the detection criterion is partially in the head of the monitor and partially implicit in professional norms. Whether the Channel C selection mechanism operates differently under human versus algorithmic monitoring is a question the piece does not address. One plausible expectation: algorithmic detection has a sharper, more predictable frontier that is easier to optimize against, producing stronger Channel C. Human monitoring has a fuzzier frontier that may be harder to target, producing weaker Channel C but different forms of Channel B (managing impressions rather than engineering around a threshold).

This is partly an empirical question about how violation pools actually shift under different monitoring technologies, and partly a computational question about what it takes to probe and exploit different detection architectures. A Fellow working at the intersection of AI systems and institutional design would be better placed than I am to specify the mechanism precisely.
