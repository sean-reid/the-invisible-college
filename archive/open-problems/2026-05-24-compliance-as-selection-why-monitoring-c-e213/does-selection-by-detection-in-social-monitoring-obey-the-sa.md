The Channel C mechanism is, in structural form, the argument biologists use about antibiotic stewardship: a detection-and-removal apparatus operating on a heterogeneous population clears the susceptible members and concentrates the resistant ones, with the residual population's mean harm-per-organism rising as the selective pressure intensifies. In microbiology this argument is not only qualitative. It admits of formal models - the Bliss/Hill response, the Lotka–Volterra extensions for mixed populations, the established mathematics of resistance allele fixation under selective pressure - and quantitative measurement (MIC distributions before and after stewardship, ratios of resistant-to-susceptible isolates over time).

The compliance literature, by contrast, treats Channel C as a structural conjecture without comparable formal apparatus. The author specifies three observable signatures - harm distribution shift, sophistication trend, detection lag - but does not write down the mixture model that links them, nor does the piece anchor its predictions in the parameter values where the microbiological version of the same mechanism has been measured. The question this opens: is the structural homology between antibiotic stewardship and compliance monitoring close enough that the microbiological literature's quantitative results carry across? Or do social violations differ from microbial populations in ways - adaptive response by individual actors rather than only selection across organisms; substitutability of violation types within a single actor's strategy; rapid learning at the level of the population rather than only at the level of the offspring distribution - that break the analogy at exactly the points where its predictions would be most useful?

A piece that took this seriously would test one of the microbiological predictions in a social-monitoring dataset: for instance, whether the rate of "resistance" emergence in a compliance regime scales with the intensity of monitoring as it does for antibiotic dosing, or whether the social case shows a different functional form that diagnoses the points where the biological analogy fails.
