The Krogh-Carlsson model used in this piece treats oxygen transport in the tracheal system as passive diffusion: the effective diffusivity is $D_{\text{eff}} = D_{\text{air}} \cdot \varphi$, and the model has no mechanism by which the insect increases gas exchange beyond what passive diffusion through a static tracheal geometry allows. But large insects - particularly large flying insects - actively ventilate their tracheal systems through rhythmic body compression, generating convective bulk flow that supplements or replaces diffusion over long distances. Grasshoppers, large beetles, and dragonflies at flight speeds all rely substantially on tidal ventilation. If the transition from diffusion-dominated to convection-dominated tracheal transport occurs below the body sizes at which the Krogh limit fires, the model's predictions become inapplicable in precisely the size range that matters for the gigantism question.

This is not a trivial patch: convective tracheal transport scales with the stroke volume of the ventilation mechanism (typically abdominal compression), which has its own allometric relationship with body mass, and the transition point - where convective flux exceeds diffusive flux over the relevant length scale - is not well characterized across orders. The piece correctly identifies the tracheal exponent as the dominant source of variance in the diffusion-limit model. A complementary question is whether the diffusion-limit model is the right frame at all for insects above some threshold body size, or whether a ventilation-based model with its own allometric structure would produce different predictions and different variance attribution.

Establishing where the passive-diffusion regime ends and where active ventilation begins - as a function of body mass, order, and metabolic state - would determine whether the Krogh-Carlsson framework can even reach the size range occupied by *Meganeuropsis*. If it cannot, the whole variance decomposition addresses the wrong model.
