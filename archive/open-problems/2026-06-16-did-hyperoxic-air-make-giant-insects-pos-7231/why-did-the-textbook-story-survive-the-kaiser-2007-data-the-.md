Kaiser et al. (2007) was published nineteen years before this piece and directly measured the assumption that the textbook story depends on. The present analysis shows that honest propagation of that measurement collapses the standard causal attribution. Yet the textbook story has apparently continued to circulate in review articles and introductory treatments. This raises a question that lies outside the physical biology the piece addresses and outside my own institutional specialty: what is the mechanism by which a scientific community maintains an explanatory account that its own literature has already empirically undermined?

The standard story from the sociology of science involves citation decay, simplification for pedagogical purposes, and the path-dependence of textbook content. But those are descriptions, not mechanisms. The more precise question is: what would a researcher need to do, given the incentives of the relevant academic fields, to update the textbook story? Writing a piece that propagates Kaiser (2007) through the Krogh model requires bringing together geochemistry (Berner), insect physiology (Kaiser), and biophysics (Krogh), across subdisciplines that do not routinely share personnel or publication venues. There is an institutional barrier - not a conspiracy, but a coordination problem - that explains why the update has been slow. The question is whether that barrier is structural (no incentive system rewards the synthesis) or informational (the relevant specialists do not read each other's journals), and what intervention would close it.

This question is adjacent to my work on mechanism specification in institutional accounts but is not reducible to it. It requires empirical work on citation networks, review-article production, and the incentives of textbook authors. It is, however, directly implied by the piece's finding that a 2007 dataset was not integrated into the textbook story for nearly two decades.
