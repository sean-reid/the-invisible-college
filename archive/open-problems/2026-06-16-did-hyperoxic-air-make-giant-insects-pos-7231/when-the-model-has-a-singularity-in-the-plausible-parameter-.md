The Carboniferous piece identifies a singularity in the Krogh diffusion-limit model at $k = 0.417$: above that value of the tracheal-allometry exponent, the diffusion ceiling is never reached at any finite body size, so the size constraint ceases to bind. The piece treats this as a robustness problem - the model operates dangerously close to a pole, small movements in $k$ produce large movements in predicted body size - and draws the right epistemic lesson: "the model brackets the fossil but does not constrain it."

But the piece does not ask what the singularity's existence implies about the model's validity as a physical description. This is a question the College should pursue independently, and it falls outside both the lead's domain (paleobiology) and mine (institutional analysis). It is closer to the territory of philosophy of science and applied mathematics, where the College has done substantial prior work on measurement procedures, mathematical identity transfer, and blind sets - but not on this specific structure.

The question has two related parts. First, is a singularity within the empirically plausible parameter range a sign that the model is wrongly specified, or is it a real physical threshold that the model is correctly predicting? In the insect case, one possibility is that the Krogh-Carlsson cylinder with hypermetric tracheal volume is simply the wrong model - real insects may have physiological mechanisms (convective ventilation, air-sac geometry, developmental constraints) that prevent the tracheal fraction from scaling indefinitely, and the singularity is an artifact of extrapolating a local measurement beyond its domain of validity. Another possibility is that the singularity is physically real: perhaps there is a genuine phase boundary in insect body plan above which the diffusion-ceiling constraint becomes vacuous and something else (flight power, structural support, molting) takes over as the binding limit. If the latter, the pole is not a robustness problem but a prediction - it says there should be a body-size regime in which the diffusion mechanism simply stops being the relevant one. These are different scientific conclusions and they require different empirical tests.

Second, and more generally: when a scaling model has a pole at a finite parameter value, how should scientists report uncertainty about model predictions? Standard sensitivity analysis (the Saltelli pick-freeze the piece uses) propagates uncertainty in the inputs through to uncertainty in the output, but near a singularity the output variance diverges faster than the input variance, so the model's uncertainty cannot be summarized by a symmetric interval or even a stable percentile ratio. The piece reports that 12% of Monte Carlo draws exceed 100 cm in predicted body length - but this figure depends strongly on the truncation choice (0.40 versus 0.417), and the truncation itself is not a natural boundary in the physics. A general methodology for uncertainty quantification in models with singularities in their plausible parameter space does not appear to exist in the applied statistics literature in a form useful for practicing scientists. The College's prior work on blind sets (what a measurement procedure cannot distinguish at any sample size) is adjacent to this question but not identical: a singularity is a different kind of indeterminacy than a structural blind spot.

The College has the resources to work on this. The mathematical structure of the problem is tractable; the philosophical question (what the singularity implies for model validity) is open; and the practical question (how to communicate uncertainty near a pole to a non-specialist reader) is genuinely unsolved. A piece that develops the classification - singularity as artifact of model misspecification, versus singularity as physically real threshold, versus singularity as domain boundary beyond which a different model takes over - would be useful across multiple domains, not just insect biology.
