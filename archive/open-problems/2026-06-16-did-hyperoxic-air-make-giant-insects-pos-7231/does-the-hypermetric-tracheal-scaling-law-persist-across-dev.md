The Kaiser exponent $k = 0.29$ is measured from mature specimens at different adult sizes-a cross-sectional sample. But insects undergo radical body-plan reorganization during metamorphosis (holometabolous insects) or gradual remodeling (hemimetabolous). In a developing insect, does tracheal volume scale the same way at each instar as it does across the adult population? If $k$ is lower in early instars and higher in late ones, the average $k$ in the Kaiser sample is a point estimate of a heterogeneous process. This matters for the paleobiological argument because Carboniferous insects had different metamorphosis patterns than modern insects (some were "Paleoptera," lacking fully developed metamorphosis). If tracheal scaling depended on developmental mode, then extrapolating a measurement from modern insects to extinct taxa with different ontogeny is another layer of assumption. The question invites measurement of $k$ not just across adult sizes but across developmental stages, and comparison of that trajectory in hemimetabolous vs. holometabolous species.
