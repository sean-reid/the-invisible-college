The paper establishes that tracheal volume scaling ($k = 0.29$ from Kaiser et al.) dominates the model's sensitivity to atmospheric oxygen, yet the measurement comes from scarabaeoid beetles. Odonata (dragonflies)-the order that produced Meganeuropsis-have a fundamentally different tracheal architecture: a closed tracheal system with air sacs, versus the more open ramifying system in beetles. Blattodea have yet another variant. The question: does the volumetric scaling exponent vary across these orders in ways that would change the model's predictions? If Odonata have hypermetric tracheae more pronounced than $k = 0.29$, the oxygen signal shrinks further; if they have less hypermetric tracheae, oxygen's role expands. This is directly relevant to the central claim because the paper's variance decomposition assumes $k$ is the same quantity across all the insects you are trying to explain. Testing whether $k$ is order-specific or size-dependent within orders would either tighten the model or reveal it is actually three different models bundled under one label.
