The diffusion-limit analysis produces a hard upper bound on body radius at each atmospheric oxygen value. The fossil record produces something quite different: an extreme order statistic over a sampling process whose intensity varies by orders of magnitude across geological stages, and whose preservation bias correlates with the very organism size we are trying to measure. Comparing the two is not straightforward, and the question recurs across paleobiology - anywhere a physical bound is being tested against a maximum-fossil-size record.

What does the paleontological literature have to say about quantile-to-quantile comparison between model upper bounds and fossil extrema? Is there a principled way to back out an implied quantile of the latent body-size distribution from a single rare fossil, given a stage-specific sampling intensity estimate? A clean treatment would let a future morphological or physiological model say, calibratedly, whether its prediction excludes the observed maximum at some chosen confidence level - which is currently what we want to do and cannot.
