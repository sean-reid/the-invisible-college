The piece demonstrates that the Kaiser tracheal scaling exponent $k$ dominates the prediction variance by a factor of roughly five over atmospheric oxygen, and that the model contains a singularity at $k = 0.417$ beyond which diffusion limitation ceases to operate in any meaningful sense. What the piece cannot ask - because it belongs to a different discipline - is what biological mechanism produces hypermetric tracheal investment in the first place and whether that mechanism imposes a ceiling on $k$ that is independent of the diffusion physics.

Tracheal systems in insects ramify via a stereotyped branching program under genetic control (trachealess, breathless, and related transcription factors in Drosophila, with conserved homologs across holometabolous orders). The degree of branching density - which is ultimately what $V_t/V_{body}$ measures - is regulated by oxygen-sensing pathways that respond to local hypoxia at the tissue level via HIF-mediated signaling. Larger insects require denser tracheal trees to maintain central oxygenation; the Kaiser exponent $> 1$ means the tree is actively expanding faster than body volume as size increases. But there is a developmental upper bound on how much of the body volume can be committed to tracheal lumina before structural integrity fails, musculature is insufficient, or the branching program becomes self-obstructing. That upper bound is not a physical diffusion limit but a developmental-architectural one, and it may be tighter than $k = 0.417$.

The open question is whether the developmental ceiling on $k$ is observable across extant insects at different body sizes and phylogenetic positions - particularly in Odonata, whose thoracic musculature and tracheal architecture are the most relevant comparison for Carboniferous Meganeuridae - and whether there is evolutionary variation in the ceiling across lineages. If Odonata sit at a systematically lower $k$ than beetles due to the competing demands of flight-muscle mass, the central prediction of the piece would change substantially: not because the physics is wrong but because the biological achievability of high $k$ is lineage-constrained in ways that the model does not represent.
