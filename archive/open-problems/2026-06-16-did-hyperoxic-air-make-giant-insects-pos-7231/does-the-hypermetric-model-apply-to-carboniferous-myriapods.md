*Meganeuropsis permiana* is the largest known Permian insect, but the Carboniferous also produced *Arthropleura armata*, a myriapod (millipede) reaching an estimated 2.5 meters in length - the largest known terrestrial arthropod in Earth's history. Myriapods are tracheate: they deliver oxygen via a tracheal system broadly analogous to the insect system the Krogh-Carlsson model addresses. If the hypermetric tracheal investment scaling is a general feature of tracheate arthropods rather than an insect-specific one, the model should in principle apply to myriapods, and *Arthropleura* would become an even more demanding test case than *Meganeuropsis*.

But myriapod tracheal anatomy differs from insect tracheal anatomy in significant ways: the tracheal openings (spiracles) are distributed along a longer body axis, the main tracheal trunks connect to a different segmental architecture, and the body geometry - many short segments rather than thoracic and abdominal divisions - changes the relevant length scale for the Krogh diffusion problem. Whether the Krogh-Carlsson cylinder idealization, already stretched to cover insects of different orders, extends further to myriapods is not obvious. Whether a tracheal investment exponent $k$ has ever been measured for myriapods is, to my knowledge, unknown.

The question matters because the oxygen hypothesis is applied to all Carboniferous tracheate gigantism, not only to insects: the same atmospheric argument is used to explain *Arthropleura* as is used for *Meganeuropsis*. If the model only applies to insects, the myriapod case requires a separate account. If it applies more broadly, the predicted body length for a creature with *Arthropleura*'s dimensions - requiring a ratio $R_{\max}^{\text{peak}}/R_{\max}^{\text{mod}}$ of perhaps an order of magnitude above any extant myriapod - would test the hypermetric model in a regime where it is near its singularity. This is a question for comparative invertebrate physiology and biomechanics, and it is one the College's current Carboniferous oxygen work does not address.
