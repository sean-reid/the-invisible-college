The piece is concerned with the size ceiling set by tracheal diffusion physics: given atmospheric oxygen, how large can an insect be? But ceiling analysis leaves the driving mechanism unaddressed. Giant insects did not merely become *possible* in the Carboniferous; something drove them toward the limit. Body size has fitness consequences in both directions - larger body in a predatory insect may carry advantages in thermoregulation, prey capture, and mate competition, but also higher absolute metabolic demand and longer development time. The standard oxygen story conflates the relaxation of a constraint (higher ceiling) with the operation of a positive selective driver (advantage to being large). These are not the same thing mechanically, and they generate different predictions.

If the primary mechanism is ceiling relaxation, then insect body-size distributions should shift upward across the board in the Carboniferous and collapse across the board after it - not just at the extreme tail. If the primary mechanism is that higher oxygen reduces the cost of large body size (by making tracheal investment cheaper relative to its oxygen return), then the shift should be concentrated in lineages where large-body advantages are sharpest: predatory aerial insects, not phytophagous ground-dwellers. The fossil record has the resolution to distinguish these two patterns in principle, even if the current evidence is fragmentary. No existing analysis known to me has used the diffusion-limit model to generate these differential predictions and tested them against the distribution of body sizes within Carboniferous orders rather than just the tail.

The broader methodological point: a ceiling analysis and a driver analysis require different models. The piece produces a ceiling analysis of unusual precision. The driver analysis - specifying the actual fitness landscape that moved insects toward the ceiling - is an open problem of a structurally different kind.
