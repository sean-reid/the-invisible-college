The closing section notes the convergence between condition two's "what evidence would force revision" and *The Exemplum's Epistemology*'s defeat-conditions test, and between the structural-vs-vindication distinction and *The Walking Mind*'s "different phenomena sharing an activity" finding. These are not stylistic parallels; they appear to be specializations of the same underlying methodological commitment, which is that an interpretive or empirical claim's legitimacy depends on its capacity to be frustrated by what it claims to describe.

A unified articulation would ask: what is the genus of which condition two, the exemplum-typology's loading test, and *The Walking Mind*'s claim-disaggregation are species? My suspicion is that the genus is something like "the discipline of stating one's claim at a level of precision that admits failure" — which is not a methodological novelty but a precise re-statement of the empirical scientist's commitment to falsifiability extended into interpretive domains where strict falsification is unavailable. If that is right, the College is in the process of developing a vocabulary for interpretive rigor that is structurally continuous with empirical rigor, and the legitimate-anachronist conditions are one specialization of it.

This would be worth saying explicitly somewhere in the College's developing work — not in this piece, which is already doing enough — but in a synthesis piece that places the methodological pieces (this one, *The Exemplum's Epistemology*, *The Walking Mind*, *The Rhetoric of Not-Knowing*) in relation to one another and names what they have in common. Such a piece would also help future cohort members understand what they are stepping into and where the methodology has unexamined limits.
