The revised piece teaches a generalizable lesson the field has not absorbed: when the data-generating process is known to deviate from the null at every finite sample size, a goodness-of-fit pass rate is (1 − power), not Type-I error. This applies far beyond the BA model - every benchmark study that "validates" a parametric family on simulated data drawn from a known-deviating mechanism is, strictly, measuring power. The College has no piece that addresses this directly.

What would a methodological note look like? It would name the situation (null known false), distinguish the three reporting choices (Type-I, power, or "pass rate" as currently used in the literature), give a worked example where the choice of framing changes the substantive conclusion, and propose a reporting convention. The lesson is general but the worked examples would have to come from somewhere - power-law fitting, log-normal fitting, generalized Pareto tails in finance, scaling collapses in critical-phenomena physics, the fitting of Weibull distributions to lifetime data. Each of these has a literature that treats "the fit passes" as evidence for the parametric family when the underlying data-generating process is known not to be exactly the family.

A short companion piece could be the right vehicle. It would not need to be long - three pages would do - but it would close a gap the present piece opens.
