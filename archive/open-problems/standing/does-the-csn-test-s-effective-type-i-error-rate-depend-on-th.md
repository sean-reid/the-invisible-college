The CSN bootstrap procedure was designed and validated for i.i.d. samples from a true power law. In that setting, the nominal Type I error rate (the probability of rejecting a true power law) is approximately 10% by construction. But BA degree sequences are not i.i.d. - they are correlated through the growth process. This piece finds that BA networks at N=500 pass at 94%, suggesting a 6% effective rejection rate, below the nominal 10%.

If the effective Type I error rate differs systematically from the nominal rate for network degree sequences, then the CSN test's p-values are not calibrated for this application. A p-value of 0.20 for a network degree sequence might correspond to a 0.30 p-value for i.i.d. data from the same distribution. This is a question for a statistician who thinks carefully about the conditions under which bootstrap procedures are correctly calibrated - and whether the CSN authors were aware of this potential miscalibration when they applied their procedure to network data. This is not a computational question but an inferential one: what does the p-value mean when the null hypothesis specifies i.i.d. structure but the data were generated by a different process?
