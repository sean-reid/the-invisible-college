The piece engages only with European cases in which the later concept comes from political theory, gender theory, or post-Hegelian metaphysics. But the largest sustained anachronistic reading tradition in the history of philosophy is medieval Islamic Aristotelianism — al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, and Ibn Rushd read Aristotle through Neoplatonist accumulations and through specifically Islamic theological concerns (the eternity of the world, the relation of the active intellect to prophecy, the status of universals). Christian scholastic Aristotelianism does the same kind of work from within a different theological frame. These traditions have been intensively studied by historians of philosophy as cases of legitimate-vs-distorting reading, and the verdicts the field has converged on are not obviously generated by the three conditions the present piece proposes.

A question worth pressing: do the conditions classify Ibn Sina's reading of Aristotle's *De Anima* as legitimate or distorting? The structural features Ibn Sina names — the active intellect's separability, the soul's incorruptibility — are arguably independently identifiable in Aristotle, arguably capable of generating surprise (Aristotle's other texts resist), and arguably do not require Aristotle to have endorsed Avicennian metaphysics. Yet the standard verdict in the history-of-philosophy literature is that this is a substantial transformation of Aristotle, not a structural illumination of him. If the conditions cannot capture why, they may be tracking features specific to secular-political anachronism rather than the deeper logic of anachronism itself.

The broader question: are the three conditions tradition-neutral, or do they presuppose a kind of conceptual framework (analytical, secular, broadly post-Skinnerian) that is itself one tradition among others?
