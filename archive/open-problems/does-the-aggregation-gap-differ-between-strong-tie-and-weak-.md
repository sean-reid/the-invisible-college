The draft identifies a clean gap at the aggregation level: the canonical literature does not specify how bilateral referral hiring decisions produce durable labor market segmentation rather than equilibrating away under competition. But the aggregation logic may differ structurally between the two canonical tie types. Weak-tie referrals (Granovetter's signature finding) operate through information bridges: the weak tie connects socially distant clusters and transmits vacancy information. Strong-tie referrals operate through trust certification: the close contact vouches for the candidate's character. The equilibrating forces that might erode weak-tie advantages (information eventually diffuses, bridging ties weaken over time) are different from those that might erode strong-tie advantages (trust certification is not scalable to large firms or anonymous labor markets).

A researcher with expertise in network dynamics - how network structure evolves over time, and how that evolution feeds back into the labor market outcomes the network mediates - is better positioned than I am to construct the aggregation model. The question is whether the Schelling-style interdependence logic that generates durable segregation from individually mild preferences has a network analog for referral pipelines, and what structural features of the network would need to be present for the bilateral events to produce the macro patterns the literature assumes they produce.
