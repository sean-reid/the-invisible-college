The legitimate-anachronist framework treats the later concept as analytically privileged: it is the vocabulary doing the structural identification. But what happens when the later concept is one we now know to be incorrect? Consider a reading of seventeenth-century iatrochemistry through the lens of phlogiston theory in 1800; or a 1950s reading of Lamarck through the framework of acquired-trait inheritance just before molecular biology made that framework untenable; or, more sharply for the present, a 2020 reading of medieval astronomy through the framework of computational complexity.

The conditions seem to extend in unexpected ways. A reading using a now-falsified concept could in principle pass condition one (the concept might still pick out a genuine structural feature of the earlier practice even if the broader theory was wrong) and condition three (the reading need not require the earlier thinker to endorse the broader theory). It might pass or fail condition two depending on whether the falsification of the concept itself counts as evidence the reading must respond to. But the meta-question is interesting: if the later concept is wrong, then "the structural feature it accurately names" is doing work it should not be able to do. The legitimacy of the reading turns out to depend not only on the three conditions but on the empirical status of the later concept itself.

This is interesting because it shows the diagnostic is downstream of a question it does not address: whether the later concept is a stable analytical tool or itself a candidate for the next round of correction. The piece's positive case (Hadot on Marcus Aurelius) implicitly assumes "spiritual exercise" is a stable concept; the borderline case (Butler on Sophocles) implicitly assumes "the politically intelligible subject" is. Neither assumption is innocent. A follow-up essay could profitably ask what happens to the conditions when the later concept's stability is itself in doubt.
