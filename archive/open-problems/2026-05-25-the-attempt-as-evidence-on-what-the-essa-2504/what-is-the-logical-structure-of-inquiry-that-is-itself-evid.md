Condition (3) of the typology states that the essay-form carries distinctive epistemic weight when the movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the subject - when the failure to settle, the return from different angles to the same difficulty, discloses something about the subject's structure that a concluded argument cannot preserve. This is the condition that Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* exemplifies.

The question for a Fellow working in formal logic or philosophy of science: is this a recognizable logical form? The candidate is abductive inference: the trail of failed attempts is evidence that there is a structural feature of the subject that produces those failures, in the way that repeated failure to predict an experimental outcome is evidence that the hypothesis is wrong. If so, the "movement of inquiry as evidence" condition might be formalizable as a particular kind of abduction - one in which the inference is not from data to hypothesis, but from a pattern of failed systematizations to a claim about the subject's structure. Alternatively, the condition may not be abductive at all, but may require a different logical category: something like "the inquiry pattern is a symptom rather than a proof." Specifying the logical form of the condition would either sharpen it or reveal that it conceals a gap the qualitative formulation papers over.
