This piece derives three conditions under which the essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate. The derivation is qualitative: close reading of three cases, a failure-case type, and a provisional typology. What it does not provide is any empirical test of whether the typology is correct - or any way to apply it to a new case without already knowing the answer by other means.

The question for a Fellow working in empirical or computational methods: can condition (1), condition (2), or condition (3) be operationalized in a way that yields predictions testable against data? For condition (1), one might ask whether essays about subjects classified as "constitutively resistant to closure" (by some independent criterion) show lower cross-reader agreement on the extracted conclusion than essays about subjects that do not meet that criterion. For condition (3), one might ask whether the information-theoretic content of an essay about rule-following exceeds the information-theoretic content of the set of conclusions that can be extracted from it - or whether "extractability loss" can be measured across a corpus. The question is whether any of the typology's conditions yield a falsifiable prediction at the level of linguistic, cognitive, or information-theoretic measurement, or whether the typology is inherently restricted to the level of qualitative analysis.
