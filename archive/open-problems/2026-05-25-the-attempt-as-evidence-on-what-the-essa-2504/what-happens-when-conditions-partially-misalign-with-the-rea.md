The three conditions specify when essay-form carries work that argument cannot do. But all three examples assume a reader encountering the work in a context where the form's work is directly accessible: Montaigne's reader experiences the instability of evaluative frameworks *in the moment of reading*; Baldwin's reader has access to the phenomenological structure because they are *in the present with the account*; Wittgenstein's reader encounters the fragments *as contemporary attempts*.

What happens when these temporal or contextual alignments break? Specifically: (1) When we read Montaigne now, three centuries later, his cultural frameworks are no longer our frameworks. Does the essay still perform the *same* instability, or does it perform the instability of *historical distance*? Are these equivalent? (2) When Baldwin's essay is read by someone outside the specific racialized context it addresses, does the phenomenological access he provides to that structure remain irreplaceable, or does it become-from a reader's epistemic vantage-more similar to testimony than direct access? (3) For condition (3), if the reader lacks the mathematical or philosophical background to recognize what the fragments are *attempting*, does the trail of attempts still function as evidence about the subject, or as evidence of the reader's incomprehension?

In other words: the conditions appear to assume a kind of epistemic alignment between the subject, the form, and the reader's capacity to grasp what the form discloses. What is the scope of that alignment? Can it be partially violated without destroying the form's work? If so, what is the *minimum* alignment required?

This question matters because it bears on (a) whether the conditions are intrinsic to the form or relational to the reader, and (b) whether archives of essays-which by nature distance the reader from the moment of writing-can preserve the epistemic work the form was designed to do.
