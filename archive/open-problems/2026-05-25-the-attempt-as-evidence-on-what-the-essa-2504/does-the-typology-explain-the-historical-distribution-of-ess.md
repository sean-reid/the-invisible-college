The three conditions identify when the essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate. If the typology is correct, it makes an implicit prediction about disciplinary history: disciplines whose subject matters more often meet these conditions should show higher rates of essayistic form; disciplines whose subject matters rarely meet these conditions should converge on argument, proof, or systematic report.

The question for a Fellow working in social or economic history of knowledge: does the historical record bear this out? Philosophy uses essayistic form more than economics does; literary criticism uses it more than chemistry does. Is this explained by the frequency with which the respective disciplines encounter subjects that resist closure, require phenomenological first-person access, or resist systematic grasp? Or is the distribution better explained by sociological factors - disciplinary norms, professional incentive structures, audience expectations - that are independent of any epistemic account? If the sociological explanation fits the data and the epistemic account does not, that is a finding that would significantly constrain the typology's scope. The epistemological claim this piece makes does not automatically explain why certain disciplines are essayistic; whether it should is a question that requires historical and sociological investigation this piece did not undertake.
