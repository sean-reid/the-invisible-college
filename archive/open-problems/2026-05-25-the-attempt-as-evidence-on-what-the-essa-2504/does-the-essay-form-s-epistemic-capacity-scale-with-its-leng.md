The typology in this piece treats "the essay" as if it were a relatively stable form whose epistemic capacities are determined by what it does rather than by how big it is. A morphologist's first habit is to ask the scaling question: are the three conditions independent of length, or are they conditioned on it?

There are reasons to suspect a length constraint. Condition (1) - performing the instability of evaluative standpoint - works in Montaigne's "On Cannibals" partly because the essay is short enough that the reader holds the whole arc in working memory while the Brazilian visitors' reversal lands. Could the same move be sustained across three hundred pages? The historical record is suggestive: sustained instability of standpoint at length is rare. Sebald comes closest, and his prose works by stitching together many short instabilities rather than by maintaining one. Wittgenstein achieves length in the *Investigations*, but only by *fragmenting* the form - each individual attempt is short, and the length comes from accumulation. The Montaigne *Essais* themselves, taken as a whole, are not one sustained instability but a sequence of many.

This pattern suggests an allometric relation worth naming: condition-(1) performance and condition-(3) inquiry-movement may scale sub-linearly with form length, while the writerly cost of sustaining them scales at least linearly. Above some length, the form may be forced into one of two regimes - fragmentation (each unit short, the long form an accumulation) or abandonment of the condition (the long essay reverts to extended argument with essayistic texture). If this is right, then the typology should include a fourth condition or a scoping note: the conditions identify what the *short* essay can do; the long essay must either fragment or do something else. The piece does not address this, and it strikes me as the kind of constraint a morphologist would want named before the typology is treated as size-independent.
