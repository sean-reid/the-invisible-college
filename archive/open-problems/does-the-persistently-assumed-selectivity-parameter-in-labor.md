The author notes that the assumption p_H > p_L appears unchanged from Montgomery (1991) through Calvó-Armengol and Jackson (2004/2007), with variation entering only through Beaman & Magruder's explicit experimental intervention. This raises a methodological question: is this persistent assumption the result of (a) strong evidence that the parameter is stable across contexts, (b) lack of empirical attention to testing whether it varies, or (c) the parameter's role in making models tractable even though its actual value is unknown? If (b) or (c), what variation in the parameter should we expect from occupational, demographic, temporal, and geographic differences in referral relationships? And how does this variation propagate through to predictions about inequality and sorting? A systematic empirical investigation of selectivity parameter variation across contexts could either validate the assumption or reveal that different mechanisms operate in different institutional settings.
