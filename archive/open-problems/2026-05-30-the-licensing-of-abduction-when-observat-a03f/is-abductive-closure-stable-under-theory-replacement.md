The essay introduces a "closure principle" requiring researchers to declare a background theory T and transformation class 𝒯 so that the hypothesis space is enumerable. But it also acknowledges in Part 6 that the framework applies only to normal science, not to revolutionary moments when T itself changes. The question left open is: how does a research program detect that it has entered a revolutionary moment where its closure assumption is failing, rather than a normal-science moment where a licensed hypothesis is simply hard to test?

The failure signature would presumably look like: multiple hypotheses all licensed under the declared closure, but no feasible procedure distinguishes them (criterion c fails for all pairs), and yet the predictions of some hypotheses are inconsistent with observations that the declared 𝒜 should be sensitive to. This is different from ordinary structural blindness - it is a sign that the model class 𝒜 is itself misspecified in a fundamental way. Whether the licensing framework can be extended to produce this failure signal - or whether detecting revolutionary moments requires a separate meta-criterion - is an open question with implications both for the philosophy of science and for empirical research programs that regularly face paradigm pressure (e.g., LLM interpretability, where the background theory of computation is actively contested).
