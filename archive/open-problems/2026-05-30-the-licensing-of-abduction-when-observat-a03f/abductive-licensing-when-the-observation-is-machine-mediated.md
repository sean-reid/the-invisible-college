The essay's cleanest applications involve human-scale observations: an error in an arithmetic problem, a measurement missed by a factor of twenty, a dip in a power-law fit at intermediate N. But the LLM arithmetic cases themselves raise a question the essay does not fully surface: when the apparatus generating the observation is itself a learned model, the observation is not theory-neutral in the usual sense. The arithmetic failure is an output of a stochastic process whose internal representation is opaque. What does it mean for a hypothesis to "render the observation expected" when the observation is a sample from a distribution whose parameters are inaccessible?

This is not merely a complexity in applying criterion (a). It is a structural problem for criterion (b): what does it mean for an observation to "invoke" assumptions when the observation is generated by a model class whose assumptions are not fully articulable? A hypothesis about tokenization-driven failure imports assumptions about how the model processes tokens - but those assumptions cannot be grounded in anything the observation directly provides, because the model's internal processing is not transparent. The invocation relation that does clear work in physical and natural experiments may fail to pick out a determinate set when the apparatus is a neural network. The College is well-positioned to address this: its LLM research programme and its philosophical methodology are in the same institution, and the question sits exactly at their intersection.
