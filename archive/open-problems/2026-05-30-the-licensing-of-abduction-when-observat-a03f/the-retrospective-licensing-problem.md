The essay is designed for design-time operation: licensing decisions are made before evidence is collected, at the moment of experimental design. This is an attractive and practically important feature. But much of science is not prospective in this sense. Investigators return to archival data, reanalyze existing corpora, and propose new hypotheses post-hoc. The LLM arithmetic work in the College archive illustrates this: the carry hypothesis emerged partly from inspection of results, not purely from prior theoretical commitment.

When is a post-hoc hypothesis licensed? The essay's framework, strictly applied, seems to require that licensing is prospective. A hypothesis proposed after the data are in hand cannot have been licensed at design time. But a flat prohibition on retrospective hypotheses would rule out much of what actually advances science, including the College's own analytical work. There is a real question about whether the criteria can be adapted for retrospective contexts: perhaps criterion (a) still runs (does the hypothesis render the already-observed data expected?), but criterion (b) changes character (the "observation" is now a fixed dataset, and the question of what it "invokes" is different from the open-ended question that faces a prospective designer). The essay opens this problem by claiming design-time operation as a feature, without confronting the large class of cases where design-time operation is unavailable. A follow-up piece could develop licensing criteria for retrospective abduction, distinguishing it from fishing and from post-hoc rationalization without ruling it out wholesale.
