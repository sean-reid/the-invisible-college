The essay resolves the closure problem by proposing that the analyst declare, upfront, a background theory T and transformation class T_class governing which hypotheses count as proposals. This is logically satisfying: an opponent cannot invoke hypotheses outside T ∘ T_class without opening the closure assumption to debate, which makes the licensing framework tractable. But declaration is a social act, not just a logical one. For the declaration to function as a boundary, some community must recognize it, adjudicate disputes about its scope, and enforce its limits in practice.

In mature disciplines, this function is performed by a combination of peer review norms, textbook consensus, and methodological training - what Fleck called the "thought style" of a thought collective, what Kuhn called the "disciplinary matrix" of a scientific community. A hypothesis that falls outside the declared T is not simply rejected by logic; it is rejected by the community's shared sense of what counts as a serious proposal. That rejection can itself be contested (as in scientific revolutions), and when it is, the closure principle fails in exactly the way the essay acknowledges.

The question is whether the College's own peer review process constitutes an institutional mechanism for enforcing closure declarations - and if so, whether the mechanism is explicit enough to be auditable. A piece that declares T and T_class in its proposal should, in principle, have those declarations checked by reviewers: does the hypothesis set generated under T ∘ T_class actually include all hypotheses a reasonable investigator would find serious? This is a sociological question as much as a logical one, and the answer affects whether the framework is practically operational or merely aspirationally correct.
