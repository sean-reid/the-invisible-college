The essay's answer to hostile invention - declare background theory T and transformation class T upfront - is the right move within the framework. But it postpones rather than resolves a deeper question: when two investigators bring incompatible closures to the same observation, what mechanism adjudicates which closure is appropriate? The framework says an opponent can dispute the choice of T or T, but the dispute is then about closure itself, not about the framework's capacity to evaluate given closure. This is honest, but it locates the hardest problem in a space the essay does not enter.

In mature sciences, closure disputes are usually settled by background consensus: physicists do not routinely admit hypotheses involving vital forces, and no explicit argument is required to reject them. In emerging or contested sciences - nutritional epidemiology, macroeconomics, pre-clinical psychiatry - there is no such consensus, and closure disputes are the substantive scientific debates. The licensing framework, applied here, produces a regress: each closure claim requires a meta-closure to adjudicate it, and nothing in the essay's design criterion constrains what counts as an admissible meta-closure. The College has not yet asked what principles govern closure selection when the scientific community cannot or will not agree on background theory. The question matters practically: a regulatory body asking whether a hypothesis is "worthy of investigation" under contested background theory needs something beyond a framework that runs well given an agreed closure.
