The framework establishes conditions under which a hypothesis is licensed for investigation, but it does not address the subsequent allocation problem: when two or more hypotheses all pass the three criteria, what order should they be investigated in? This is a genuine decision problem that arises immediately after the licensing step in any resource-constrained research program.

Several candidate priority rules suggest themselves: cheapest first (minimize the cost of falsification); highest-stakes first (investigate whichever hypothesis, if true, would most change the policy recommendation); most discriminating first (run the experiment that resolves the greatest number of remaining hypotheses simultaneously). These are not equivalent. The essay's framework creates a partial order on hypotheses by distinguishing rivals from complements, but within the class of rivals it provides no ordering.

This question matters especially for the stratified-explanation failure mode. When the essay argues that quality screening, information gatekeeping, and demographic composition in referral hiring are complements rather than rivals, it implies that all three should ultimately be investigated - but which comes first under a budget constraint? A licensing framework without a priority rule is incomplete for any research program operating under resource constraints, which is most of them.
