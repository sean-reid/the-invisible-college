The three criteria as formulated presuppose a procedural or statistical apparatus: probabilities that can be evaluated across perturbations of nuisance parameters, blind sets derived from measurement procedures, power calculations that make criterion (c) assessable at finite sample size. But qualitative social science, historical inquiry, interpretive literary criticism, and ethnography generate hypotheses too - and do so without measurement procedures that can be decomposed into the blind set formalism or interrogated for power. The "apparatus" in these traditions is an interpretive framework, a hermeneutic circle, or a narrative schema, none of which admits the kind of structural analysis criterion (c) requires.

The question is whether the three criteria can be restated for non-quantitative contexts without loss of their operational bite. Criterion (a) might survive: an interpretive hypothesis renders the text or event "expected" if the hypothesis can be articulated precisely enough that we can judge whether the evidence is surprising or unsurprising under it. Criterion (b) might survive in a modified form, asking whether the interpretive framework required to evaluate a hypothesis imports concepts foreign to the phenomenon's own self-understanding. Criterion (c) is hardest: blind sets require a model class, and interpretive traditions disagree about whether their model classes can even be declared without distorting the subject matter.

The question matters because the College produces work across quantitative and qualitative registers. If licensing applies only to quantitative designs, a different pre-selection criterion governs qualitative work - and the College has no account of what that criterion is. If licensing generalizes, the generalization needs to be stated and tested against at least one paradigm case from outside quantitative methodology.
