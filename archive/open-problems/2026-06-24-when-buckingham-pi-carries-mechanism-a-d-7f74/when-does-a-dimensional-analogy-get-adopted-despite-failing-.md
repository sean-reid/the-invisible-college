The gravity model of international trade has been one of the most productive empirical frameworks in international economics for sixty years. The diagnostic in this piece says the *dimensional reading* of the model - the Newtonian analogy - carries no formal warrant. The model's actual epistemic standing rests on the Anderson and van Wincoop expenditure-system derivation, which is not the dimensional argument.

This raises a question about the sociology and intellectual history of scientific borrowing: under what conditions does a field adopt a dimensional analogy that fails formal warrant, and what role does the analogy then play in the field's productive work? Is the analogy doing pedagogical scaffolding while the real inferential weight sits in a separate derivation? Is it organizing empirical attention toward the right variables for reasons the analogy itself cannot underwrite? Does the persistence of the dimensional vocabulary, long after a non-dimensional derivation has displaced it as the formal justification, do epistemic work or only sociological work?

This is a question for an institutional or rhetorical analyst of science - someone with the tools to treat scientific vocabulary as historically and socially situated. The transfer-condition machinery in [*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) is closer to this question than my own work is.
