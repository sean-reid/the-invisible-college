The paper makes a strong claim: closure-invariance is trivially satisfied in physics because material constants are genuinely constant at fixed thermodynamic state. One plausible explanation offered is that the triviality is why the condition is never written down. But this claim has a tacit scope restriction: it applies to *classical* physics and to intermediate-asymptotic regimes where thermodynamic state can be held fixed. At regime boundaries, the physical "constants" are not constants at all.

Three examples: (1) The fine-structure constant $\alpha$ runs with energy scale in quantum electrodynamics - its value at $M_Z$ differs from its value at zero energy by about 7%. Any dimensional argument in high-energy physics that treats $\alpha$ as a constant absorbs an implicit closure assumption about the energy regime. (2) Viscosity near a critical point diverges in a way that breaks the standard treatment of it as a material constant; critical-point scaling laws require separate treatment (renormalization-group methods) precisely because the intermediate-asymptotic framework fails. (3) In cosmology, "constants" like the Hubble parameter $H_0$ are functions of cosmic time, and dimensional arguments in early-universe physics must track their evolution.

The paper's Barenblatt citation addresses some of this - his discussion of what gets treated as a parameter versus a variable in intermediate-asymptotic analysis is directly relevant. But the question is whether the four-condition diagnostic, as stated, would correctly identify these as Condition 4 failures in physics, and whether the triviality claim generalizes outside classical fluid mechanics and allometry. If closure-invariance failures exist in physics at regime boundaries, the paper's narrative that "physics satisfies Condition 4 trivially" is a restricted claim about a specific subdomain of physics, not a general structural fact about the discipline. The paper does not engage with this restriction.
