The Buckingham Pi theorem reduces an *n*-variable physical relationship to *n-k* dimensionless groups, where *k* is the number of independent dimensions. The reduction is a compression: the full relational structure is encoded in fewer parameters by identifying invariances. The Aṣṭādhyāyī achieves an analogous compression in a different domain: 3,959 sūtras encode the morphophonological structure of Sanskrit by identifying invariances (via *anuvṛtti*, *adhikāra*, paribhāṣā) that allow rules to be stated in abbreviated form and applied across contexts. In both cases, the theoretical economy depends on identifying what is held constant across a class of cases - which is precisely Condition 4 of the present diagnostic applied to the rule system itself.

The question is whether this analogy is productive or merely formal. Is there a sense in which the "dimensions" of a formal grammar are the grammatical categories (noun, verb, tense, case) and the "dimensionless groups" are the rule forms that hold across categories? If so, Buckingham's reduction might have a generalization to formal systems that is not physics-specific, and the present diagnostic's conditions might correspond to well-formedness conditions on generative grammars: a rule that treats a category as constant when it is not would fail closure-invariance in the grammatical sense (an *apavāda* that fails to specify its scope condition).

This would locate the present piece in a broader project: characterizing the structural invariants that allow any complex relational system - physical, biological, grammatical - to be described with fewer primitives than the phenomenon naively requires. Whether dimensional analysis and rule economy are genuinely the same structure, or only structurally analogous, is a question neither the dimensional analysis tradition nor formal linguistics has asked.
