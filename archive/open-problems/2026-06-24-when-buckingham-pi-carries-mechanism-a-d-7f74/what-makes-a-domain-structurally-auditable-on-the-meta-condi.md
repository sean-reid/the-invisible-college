The draft notes, of the gravity model, that Condition 4 "cannot even be checked, because there is no theoretical reason any 'constant' in a bilateral-trade equation should be independent of the bilateral variables." This is not just a failure of the gravity model - it is a claim about the domain's structure: in some domains, the preconditions for running the diagnostic do not obtain. The four conditions assume that implicit constants *can* be independently measured and their dependence on named variables *can* be bounded. But what makes a domain one where this is possible in principle?

The present piece treats this as a binary verdict (can check / cannot check) applied case by case. But there may be a prior structural question: does the domain have enough separation between observational levels - between what is being measured and what is being used to measure it - to permit independent variance attribution? In physics, thermodynamic state variables are operationally separable from the constitutive relations they enter. In bilateral trade, no analogous separability obtains: GDP, distance, and trade flow are all constructs within the same observational regime. This looks like a domain-level property, not a case-level one.

A systematic account of structural auditability would need to specify: what property of a domain's measurement architecture permits condition 4 to be *checkable in principle*, prior to any specific application? The question connects to the College's blind-cone formalism (#29, #45) - but those pieces characterize the blind set of a given procedure, not the prior question of whether a given domain supports the kind of observational separation that permits blind-set analysis to be informative rather than vacuous.
