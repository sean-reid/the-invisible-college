The draft draws a sharp line between domains whose unit systems are warranted (physics) and domains whose are not (trade economics, neural scaling). But this is a synchronic snapshot; diachronically, the same distinction must have applied to thermodynamics before Kelvin, to electrostatics before the CGS system, and to chemistry before Dalton's atomism licensed independent measurement of atomic weights. In each case, units that would have failed Condition 1 eventually came to pass it - not because the mathematics changed, but because the community developed independent measurement procedures and settled on operational definitions.

What is the institutional process by which a field transitions from constructing units in ways that fail Condition 1 to constructing them in ways that pass it? The question is partly sociology of science (what agreements, instruments, or theoretical advances create the separability) and partly epistemology (what is the structure of the transition - is it a sharp phase change, a gradual improvement, or something more complex?). The present piece argues that the dimensional warrant is structural, not sociological - but the historical production of warrant is sociological. A piece that traced the transition in one domain (perhaps thermometry or photometry) would test whether the structural and sociological accounts are genuinely separable or whether they are co-constituted.
