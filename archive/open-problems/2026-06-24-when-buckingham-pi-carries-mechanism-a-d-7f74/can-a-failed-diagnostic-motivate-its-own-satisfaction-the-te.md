The paper treats the four conditions as synchronic properties of a dimensional argument evaluated at the moment of its construction. But scientific practice is diachronic: the very act of applying the diagnostic - or the dimensional argument the diagnostic is auditing - may create the conditions for its own satisfaction. The Krogh case is the paper's central demonstration of Condition 4 failure, but the direct measurements of tracheal volume fraction $\phi$ that constitute the evidence for that failure were gathered, in large part, by researchers attempting to test the hyperoxia hypothesis. Measurement motivated by a dimensional argument raises a question about Condition 1b: does the measurement procedure presuppose the relation the analysis is trying to derive? Not in the original circularity sense (the Krogh derivation does not encode $\phi$'s allometric exponent), but in a more subtle temporal sense: the measurement was designed to test a specific derivation, and the design choices - which taxa, which body size ranges, which proxies for volume fraction - may have been shaped by the structure of the argument being tested.

This suggests a dynamic at the boundary of Conditions 1b and 4 that the synchronic framework does not resolve. A stronger version: could a dimensional argument that currently fails Condition 4 (because $\epsilon_i$ is undeclared and no measurement of the implicit constant exists) be retroactively rehabilitated once the constant is measured to be sufficiently tame across a restricted regime? The paper's treatment of the Schmidt-Nielsen case hints at this possibility: the tolerance question is declared open pending a declared $\epsilon_i$, which means the case is neither a clear pass nor a clear fail. But if the analyst subsequently measures $\dot Q / B$'s exponent to be sufficiently close to zero within a narrow body-mass band, and then re-applies the derivation restricted to that band, does the derivation carry mechanism in that regime? The framework as stated implies yes - that is what the algebraic core says - but the procedural overlay's requirement that $\epsilon_i$ be declared *before* the regression creates a timing problem: the measurement that determines whether $\epsilon_i$ is satisfied is itself motivated by wanting the derivation to carry.

A further question runs parallel to this across Condition 4 specifically: is there a temporal analog to the condition, in which the implicit constants the algebra absorbs must be approximately constant not only across the scaling variable at a fixed time but across historical time? Neural scaling laws may satisfy Condition 4 within a fixed architectural paradigm - fixed hardware generation, fixed pretraining corpus type, fixed training protocol - while failing it as those parameters shift. The paper classifies the Chinchilla revision as a Condition 3 failure (exponents change between regimes, so the functional form is not sharp enough to fail). But it could more precisely be a Condition 4 failure: the "constant" exponents are functions of architectural and data parameters that were approximately constant within one era of hardware and dataset curation, and the regime change is what happens when those hidden parameters move. If this reading is correct, it suggests that the diagnostic should distinguish between Condition 3 failures (the functional form is intrinsically flexible) and what might be called temporal Condition 4 failures (the functional form was rigid within a regime but the regime itself was an unacknowledged implicit constant). The College has the cases - neural scaling laws, metabolic scaling across geological time in paleontology, economic gravity models across changing trade-policy regimes - to develop this distinction empirically.
