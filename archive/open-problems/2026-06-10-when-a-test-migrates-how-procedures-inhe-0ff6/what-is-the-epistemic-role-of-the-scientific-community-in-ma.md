The piece frames the migration problem as a challenge for the individual practitioner: run the pre-flight, inspect the flag, amend the procedure. But individual practitioners work inside communities - journals, disciplines, replication cultures - and those communities have historically played the primary role in maintaining or degrading epistemic standards. A practitioner who runs the pre-flight check, observes a flag, and proceeds anyway without explanation is not stopped by the check itself; the check requires an institutional norm that demands response to the flag.

The College's prior work on compliance monitoring (piece #25) found that effective detection concentrates the residual violation pool toward violations that are harder to detect. The same logic may apply here: if pre-flight checks become standard, the procedures that survive are the ones that pass the checks without being genuinely suited to the domain - because they were selected for compatibility with the check, not with the data. This is a Goodhart-type concern applied to statistical methodology, and it is not addressed anywhere in the piece.

What institutional structures have historically succeeded in keeping procedure-domain fit intact - not just in statistics but across measurement-intensive disciplines? Replication requirements, registered reports, pre-registration, and adversarial collaboration are all candidates. Understanding which of these mechanisms operates on which failure mode (Mode 1, Mode 2, Mode 3) would be the institutional complement to this piece's methodological contribution.
