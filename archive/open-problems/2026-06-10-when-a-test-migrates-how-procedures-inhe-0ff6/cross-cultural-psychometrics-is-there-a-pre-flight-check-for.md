The piece's framework - a procedure migrates, its internal QC stays silent, a pre-flight check on the receiving sample flags the decoupling - has an obvious analogue in the social sciences that the piece does not engage. A survey instrument validated in one cultural context (call it the source population, where the latent construct, the item interpretations, and the response-scale calibration were jointly fit) is routinely re-administered in another. Cronbach's $\alpha$, the instrument's standard internal-QC statistic, can remain comfortably above 0.8 in the receiving population while measurement invariance has in fact failed - the same numeric response now codes a different underlying latent score. The procedure's diagnostic has decoupled from the failure it was supposed to flag, in exactly the sense the present piece names "Mode 2."

Measurement-invariance testing (configural, metric, scalar invariance via multi-group CFA) is the established remedy, but it requires data from *both* populations. The interesting analogue of the present piece's pre-flight checks would be a test computable on the *receiving sample alone* - without re-collecting in the source - that flags likely invariance failure. Candidate signals: anomalous response-scale curvature, item-rest correlation patterns that do not match the source's published values, factor-loading bootstrap variance under bootstrap resamples calibrated by item endorsement frequency, or differential item-functioning indicators against any demographic axis available in the receiving sample. None of these is a clean substitute for full invariance testing, but the question is whether *any* of them carries enough signal to function as a Mode-2 flag.

The question matters because the social sciences are the largest and most consequential venue for procedure migration: the GAD-7, the PHQ-9, the WEIRD-calibrated implicit-association test, the Big Five inventory, the WHOQOL - all have been administered far beyond their validation cohorts, with policy and clinical consequences. A pre-flight diagnostic of the kind the present piece argues for, ported into psychometrics with the appropriate substitutions, would be a real institutional good.
