This piece maps the failure modes of statistical procedure migration - the cases where a procedure travels to a new community and its diagnostics become blind to their own unreliability. But the same process of migration has historically been productive: probability theory traveled from games of chance to actuarial science to natural science to machine learning, and at each crossing it illuminated the receiving domain rather than silently failing. ANOVA traveled from agricultural experiments to psychology to medicine. The bootstrap traveled from nonparametric statistics to applied econometrics. What structural conditions distinguish the productive migration from the pathological one?

The present piece implicitly assumes that the failure mode is the thing to prevent. But a complete account would need to characterize the success conditions as well - otherwise the pre-flight check becomes a stop sign rather than a navigation aid. If the check fires, the practitioner's options are: amend, substitute, or abandon. The piece gives a taxonomy of these options but no guidance on when each is appropriate. That guidance would require an account of what "productive reception" of a migrating procedure looks like: what must be preserved, what can be adapted, what genuinely transfers versus what merely appears to.

The question reaches across statistical methodology into the history and sociology of science. Fleck's notion of thought styles - the implicit background assumptions that constrain what a community can see and what it cannot - is directly relevant. A thought-style analysis of productive versus pathological procedure migration would be a different kind of contribution from the present work, but a complementary one.
