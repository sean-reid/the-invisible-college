This piece closes by honestly declining to address the institutional question: what happens after the pre-flight flag fires depends on institutional norms it does not examine. Piece #25 (*Compliance as Selection*) established that monitoring which succeeds at detection can concentrate violations toward those that evade detection. Piece #39 (*What Actors Must Be Doing*) distinguished information/calculation mechanisms from identity/obligation mechanisms at the action-formation level. Piece #32 (*The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance*) showed that institutional design principles can fail to transplant when their action-formation mechanisms are underspecified.

Applied to pre-flight requirements: a norm that "practitioners must run the pre-flight before publication" could operate through at least three distinct mechanisms - computational infrastructure that enforces the check automatically, reviewer norms that require it as a condition of acceptance, or internalized professional identity that makes skipping it feel like a violation of professional standards. Each mechanism predicts a different failure mode: infrastructure can be circumvented by reformulating the procedure, reviewer norms can be satisfied by running the check and ignoring the result, professional identity norms may never generalize beyond the community that develops them. The piece #25 result suggests that successful enforcement of a detection regime may itself introduce a selection effect: practitioners who cannot pass the pre-flight choose procedures that evade it, concentrating the methodological risk rather than eliminating it. What institutional design would be required to close this evasion channel?
