The essay's central contribution is a refashioning of Condition 2: a legitimate anachronistic reading is not one that "generates falsifiable claims" (Popper-flavored, too precise for interpretive work) but one that is "capable of surprise" — that has specific claims at stake when it encounters particular passages, such that the reading could be frustrated. The essay develops this diagnostically, applying it as a test case by case. But the condition is stated without a formal account of what "having something at stake" means for an interpretation, and without any account of what "frustration" looks like at scale — across a corpus rather than in close reading of three canonical texts.

This matters because if the condition has no principled content beyond case-by-case expert judgment, then the essay's framework is only as generalizable as the next skilled reader's application of it. The essay acknowledges this in the "Where the Conditions Fall Short" section ("The positive capacity... cannot be fully formalized"), but treats the limits as a known constraint. The question I am raising is not whether the framework can be fully formalized — it cannot — but whether there is a useful partial formalization, and what it would reveal if applied. Specifically: an interpretation that is "capable of surprise" is presumably one that commits to predictions about as-yet-unread texts, and those predictions can be checked. This is structurally similar to what a model does when it generalizes. The condition may be underspecified not because formalization is impossible but because the essay is not asking the right question of it.

The College's existing empirical apparatus — running repeated tests, pre-registering analysis paths, distinguishing what can and cannot be executed given data constraints — provides a natural home for a worked example. What would it look like to take the Hadot reading of the *Meditations* and ask, for a held-out set of passages: does the "spiritual exercise" frame make specific predictions about what those passages will contain, that a different frame (say, doctrinal treatise) would not? The "structural reading passes condition two" claim is currently defended by example; could it be defended by a small pre-committed study? This question is not calling for the humanities to become social science — it is asking whether a precision analog to condition two exists, and whether building it would reveal something about where the condition's content actually lives.
