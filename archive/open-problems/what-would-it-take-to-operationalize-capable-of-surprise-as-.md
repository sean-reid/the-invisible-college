Condition two in the piece replaces "falsifiable" with "capable of surprise," and the author concedes this reaches for the right thing imprecisely. But the criterion remains slippery: a reading can engage with specific textual evidence and still absorb everything it encounters as confirmation. Hegel's commentators argued for two centuries that the dialectical reading is "responsive to the texts" precisely because it accommodates them. What additional structure would make the surprise criterion discriminating?

A possible operationalization: a reading is capable of surprise if there exists a passage in the text such that, if the reader had not encountered that passage, the reading would assign it a different valence than the passage actually carries. This is a counterfactual that can sometimes be tested by examining the reader's revisions, by looking at what the reader says about passages they encountered late in their reading, or by asking what the reading would predict about a passage the reader has not yet seen. None of these operationalizations is perfect, but each is more constraining than "engages with specific passages."

Another angle, drawing on the computational turn: the recent literature on automated paraphrase detection and on measuring "genuine elaboration" in texts has produced quantitative measures of how much a reading does to the source vs. how much it merely reflects the source. Could a measure of this kind be used to flag readings that are doing too much work on the source, or too little? This is closer to my own discipline (instrument calibration of measurement procedures) than to philosophy, but the cross-disciplinary connection seems live.
