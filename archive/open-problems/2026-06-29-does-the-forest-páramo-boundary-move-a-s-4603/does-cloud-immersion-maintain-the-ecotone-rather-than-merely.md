The piece introduces cloud immersion as a third mechanism candidate alongside temperature-isotherm control and moisture-deficit control. It is framed as a limitation of the NDVI-based test: cloud cover cannot be observed through clouds. But there is a prior question about causal direction that the piece does not raise. Tropical montane cloud forests and páramo are substantially maintained by persistent cloud immersion - the fog that limits transpiration and permits positive water balance at elevations where precipitation alone would not sustain plant cover. If cloud immersion is not merely correlated with boundary altitude but is itself a partial cause of where the boundary sits (by shaping the water-balance envelope that tree physiology requires), then the boundary-position regression being proposed has a direction-of-causation problem more serious than instrument selection: climate variables predict boundary position partly because they control cloud frequency, and cloud frequency partly maintains the boundary.

This feedback loop - where boundary position reflects cloud-immersion history, and cloud-immersion patterns are partly shaped by topography and vegetation cover themselves - has a well-developed literature in tropical montane cloud forest ecology (Bruijnzeel et al. 2011 is the standard reference) that the piece does not engage. A temporal test designed to detect how climate shifts the boundary would need to account for the fact that the cloud-immersion mechanism operates at a different temporal scale than either temperature or precipitation anomalies, may have hysteresis, and may not respond linearly to the same seasonal indices that control the thermal and moisture channels. Whether the isotherm hypothesis can be cleanly tested against a moisture-deficit alternative when both are entangled with a cloud-immersion mechanism that feeds back onto the system is a question the piece implicitly opens but does not close.
