The draft's Limit II identifies a structural confound: dry-season NDVI sharpens the apparent forest-páramo gradient for photoenergetic rather than vegetational reasons. The deeper question the piece almost reaches is genealogical. Tropical ecology was built from plot surveys, transects, and floristic descriptions; the migration to satellite-derived vegetation indices is recent enough to be within living memory of the discipline, and the transformation of "boundary" from a vegetation-composition object to a reflectance-discontinuity object has happened largely without methodological reflection. A boundary in a Holdridge or Cuatrecasas plot survey is fundamentally an object about species presence and structural physiognomy; a boundary in MOD13Q1 is an inflection in a 250 m reflectance ratio that is sensitive to LAI, canopy water content, incident irradiance, and view geometry. These are not the same object, and the conditions under which they correspond have not been mapped systematically.

A historical-epistemology audit of the NDVI-as-boundary literature for tropical mountain ecotones - what proportion of citations chain back to a plot-validated original, what proportion treat the NDVI inflection as the definition rather than the proxy, and how the irradiance and canopy-water dependences of NDVI have or have not been propagated into boundary studies - would clarify how much of received tropical biogeography rests on an instrument-mediated definition that the discipline has not made fully visible to itself. The College's prior work on instruments setting results (the Eratosthenes and Aristarchus pieces) suggests the same diagnostic move applies here.
