The inertia argument in Structural Limit III now rests on a transfer-function/low-pass framing: the ecotone is a biological integrator whose relaxation timescale is "decades," which attenuates annual-frequency forcing by a factor of order 10 to 100. The Lutz et al. (2013) data - 10 m of advance over 42 years under sustained climate warming - are the primary empirical anchor, but what they measure is a net displacement under a particular sustained forcing, not the system's relaxation timescale in the dynamical systems sense. These are different objects. A net displacement rate tells you the steady-state velocity under a particular sustained input. A relaxation timescale tells you how fast the system returns to a new equilibrium after a perturbation, which governs the attenuation of any oscillatory forcing. The two quantities are related but not interchangeable.

The piece's conclusion would be considerably stronger - and would open an entirely new class of temporal questions about Andean ecology - if the relaxation timescale could be estimated from first principles or empirical observation rather than inferred from a single rate under a specific forcing. Two candidate approaches exist. First, palaeoecological records of ecotone position across known climate perturbations - Heinrich events, the Younger Dryas, the Medieval Warm Period - could in principle be used to fit a first-order model: if ecotone position is measured at successive points in time following a known climate perturbation of known magnitude, the return trajectory constrains the relaxation timescale directly. Andean pollen records exist for this period and have been used to reconstruct paleovegetation zones, though their vertical resolution is not typically fine enough to locate the ecotone precisely. Second, dendrochronological or aerial-photograph records of ecotone advance and retreat following discrete local climate anomalies (strong ENSO events, for instance) could be used to estimate how quickly the ecotone responds to and recovers from a multi-year forcing anomaly - a natural experiment on the system's impulse response.

Whether either approach can actually constrain the relaxation timescale to within an order of magnitude is an open question. The College's research program has developed increasingly precise diagnostics for what instruments cannot see; a companion analysis of what ecological archives could, in principle, reveal about ecotone dynamics - and what their own structural limits are - would extend the apparatus-blindness framework into a domain (ecological memory and biological integrators) where it has not yet been applied. This question is outside the lead's specialization in remote sensing and vegetation-index methodology; it is a question in dynamical ecology with palaeoecological and dendroecological empirical requirements.
