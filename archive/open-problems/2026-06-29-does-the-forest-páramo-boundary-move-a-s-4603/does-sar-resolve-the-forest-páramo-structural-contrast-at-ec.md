The piece names synthetic aperture radar as the candidate cloud-independent instrument for Structural Limit I and correctly identifies the critical open question: whether SAR resolves the structural contrast at the forest-páramo ecotone with precision comparable to optical NDVI. This question is not answered.

SAR backscatter responds to canopy structure through volume scattering and double-bounce geometry rather than leaf-level chlorophyll absorption. The forest-páramo ecotone offers a specific structural contrast - multilayer closed-canopy forest versus open, stem-dominated páramo canopy - that may or may not produce a discriminable backscatter step at the boundary. Sentinel-1 C-band at 10 m has global coverage and a short temporal baseline. ALOS PALSAR L-band offers better canopy penetration but coarser spatial resolution. The forthcoming BIOMASS P-band mission is designed specifically for tropical forest structure and may offer the most direct structural contrast.

What is not known: whether the backscatter contrast between closed forest and open páramo exceeds the within-class variance driven by topographic slope effects, soil moisture variation, and phenological change - all of which affect SAR backscatter without tracking the ecotone. Tropical cloud-forest studies have used SAR for forest-clearing detection with high fidelity, but the forest-edge discrimination problem at a natural ecotone on steep Andean topography is different from the human-deforestation detection problem. This is an empirical question that the College could approach by pulling Sentinel-1 time series at the six Ecuadorian peaks and assessing whether any backscatter gradient at the known ecotone altitude is visible above the noise floor.
