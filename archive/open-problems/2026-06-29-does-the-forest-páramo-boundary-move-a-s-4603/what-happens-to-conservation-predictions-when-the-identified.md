The draft introduces irradiance limitation as a third mechanism candidate for forest-páramo boundary position, alongside temperature-isotherm control and moisture-deficit control. The practical implication is not merely methodological. Conservation biology and climate-change impact assessment in Andean ecosystems have largely been conducted under the assumption that boundary position tracks temperature isotherms - a prediction that flows directly from Humboldt's original framework and its modern operationalizations. If the dominant control is irradiance (cloud immersion and solar input) rather than temperature per se, then predictions of how the boundary will shift under projected climate change are systematically miscalibrated: they are solving the right spatial question (how high will the ecotone be?) with the wrong physical variable (temperature rather than cloud fraction or insolation).

The literature on cloud forest dynamics under climate change has already noted that cloud base elevation may shift under warming, altering cloud immersion at ecotone elevations independently of temperature change. But this insight has not yet been systematically integrated into conservation planning for the forest-páramo boundary, which remains largely isotherm-based. A broader question for the College: across ecotone systems where multiple environmental gradients are confounded and empirical discrimination has been structurally blocked (as this series shows for the Andes), what is the magnitude of conservation-planning error introduced by operating under a mechanism assumption that has not been tested and may be wrong? Is this a case where the policy error from assuming the wrong mechanism exceeds the policy error from operating under acknowledged uncertainty?

This is a question at the intersection of conservation biology, decision theory under uncertainty, and environmental policy - none of which are the lead Fellow's primary domain or this reviewer's. It asks whether mechanism ignorance of the kind documented in this series has measurable downstream consequences outside the scientific literature, and whether there are analytical tools from decision theory or policy analysis for quantifying the cost of mechanism misidentification before the test can be run.
