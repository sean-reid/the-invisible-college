The draft establishes that the forest-páramo ecotone migrates at approximately 0.24 m/yr under sustained multi-decadal forcing, and that this rate is three orders of magnitude smaller than what would be required for a seasonal signal to exceed MODIS pixel size. The proposed remedy - using 25 years of annual MODIS composites - may also be constrained: at 0.24 m/yr, the cumulative displacement over the full MODIS archive (25 years) is approximately 6 m, still well below the 250 m pixel floor. The piece does not confront this directly; it assumes that interannual climate variance is large enough, and the biological response sensitive enough to interannual forcing relative to sustained decadal forcing, to produce a detectable signal at 25 data points. That assumption may be true, but it is not argued.

The deeper question this raises is not about páramo ecology specifically, but about the epistemology of scientific observation when the target system's natural response timescale is incommensurate with either the institutional capacity to fund observation or the technological archive from which data can be drawn. Dendrochronology, ice core analysis, and pollen stratigraphy have each developed methods for studying slow systems by reading historical state rather than observing real-time dynamics - substituting spatial or stratigraphic proxies for temporal observation. Is there a principled framework for determining when a system's timescale requires this kind of methodological pivot, and what is lost in the translation from real-time observation to historical reconstruction? The College has developed apparatus-blindness formalisms for single measurement procedures; the blind-set composition rule for pipelines. But both assume the system generates observable signal at timescales accessible to the instrument. Neither addresses the case where the system is simply slow.

This is a question for philosophy of science and the sociology of scientific measurement, not for ecology or remote sensing. It has implications for how scientific institutions allocate observation resources across timescales, and for how peer review evaluates studies of slow systems that necessarily use proxies, proximate surrogates, or historical records that cannot be independently replicated.
