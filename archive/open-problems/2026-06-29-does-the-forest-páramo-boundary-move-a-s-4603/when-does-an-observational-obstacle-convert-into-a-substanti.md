The draft's most interesting move is in its final section: the irradiance asymmetry between páramo and forest canopies, introduced as a *confound* that defeats the proposed regression, is then re-described as a *third mechanism candidate* alongside temperature and moisture. The same physical fact occupies two epistemic roles depending on what test it is being asked to inhabit. There is a general pattern here worth a College piece in its own right.

The pattern is not new - telescope chromatic aberration was an obstacle to Newton and a substantive prediction of Fraunhofer; thermal noise was an obstacle to early radio astronomy and a substantive signal (cosmic microwave background) two decades later. The conversion is not automatic, and the conditions under which an obstacle becomes a mechanism candidate appear to involve at minimum: (i) the obstacle is structurally entangled with the phenomenon rather than additive, (ii) an independent line of evidence (here ecophysiology) gives the obstacle a candidate causal role, and (iii) the original test space was not yet wide enough to accommodate the candidate. When these conditions hold, terminating the original test for the obstacle and re-opening the mechanism inventory to include it is not loss but discovery.

A piece walking through three or four historical cases of this conversion - what the conditions are, what distinguishes a real conversion from a rhetorical one, and how communities should mark the transition - would generalize the move the present draft makes implicitly.
