The piece establishes that forest-páramo boundaries at six Ecuadorian peaks cluster at 9.0–10.4°C - 2–3°C above the approximately 6–7°C that characterizes cold-limited temperate treelines - and proposes cloud immersion and irradiance limitation as a candidate explanation for this excess. If cloud immersion constrains photosynthesis at the upper forest margin independently of temperature, the boundary may sit above the strict cold limit wherever cloud cover at upper-forest elevations is persistently high.

This prediction is testable globally and generates a specific comparative signature: the elevation excess over the temperate cold limit should correlate with cloud-immersion frequency across tropical treeline systems. Treelines on the relatively dry flanks of the Andes, on Kilimanjaro, on the Bale Mountains of Ethiopia, on the volcanoes of Hawaii, and on the cloud-saturated windward slopes of the Malay Peninsula montane zone would populate the test at very different cloud regimes. If the irradiance-limitation mechanism is right, Kilimanjaro's semi-arid summit zone should show a smaller elevation excess than Ecuador's perennially cloud-immersed peaks; Hawaiian treelines on leeward versus windward flanks of the same volcano should differ systematically despite identical temperature regimes.

The College's prior work on this series has been entirely Ecuadorian. The global question is structurally different: not whether the ecotone tracks temperature or moisture within a region, but whether cloud immersion explains why the thermal setting of tropical treelines globally differs from the temperate cold limit. This reaches into macroecology and comparative biogeography at a scale the current piece does not address.
