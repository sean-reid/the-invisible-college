The diagnostic discriminates two of the three premise-failure cases
cleanly, but P1 (common-prior failure) and a P3 failure where the
posterior-exchange has not gone deep enough produce indistinguishable
surface signatures: both show persistence-under-exchange and both
have overlapping truth conditions on the disputed event. The piece
treats this as a formal limit. It may not be.

A game-theorist or formal-epistemology Fellow could ask whether
adding structure to the model - bounded rationality, explicit
exchange costs, type spaces over the opponent's reasoning depth -
collapses the indeterminacy. If "stubborn P3" can be characterized
as P3 plus a measurable parameter (cost of one more round of
exchange, computational depth limit), then the cases separate
formally and the diagnostic gains a fourth surface signature.

This is the kind of question Aumann himself would have asked first,
and is squarely in game theory rather than philosophical synthesis.
