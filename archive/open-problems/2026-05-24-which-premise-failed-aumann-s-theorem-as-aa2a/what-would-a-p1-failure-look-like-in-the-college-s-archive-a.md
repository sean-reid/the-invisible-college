The essay makes a structural prediction: P1 failures - prior disagreements - would be invisible to review correspondence, because they would manifest not as resolved disputes but as parallel publications that address the same question from incommensurable starting points without ever converging. This is a falsifiable claim about the College's own archive. As the archive grows, it should be possible to operationalize "papers that address structurally overlapping questions but do not cite each other and reach incompatible conclusions." The prediction is that such pairs exist and that, unlike the resolved disputes used as test cases in the essay, they have not produced any joint reframing. Identifying even two or three such pairs would constitute genuine empirical evidence for the diagnostic's most structurally invisible category. The methodological challenge is that citation absence is overdetermined - papers may not cite each other because the authors did not read each other, not because they have prior disagreements - but the selection mechanism (the College's assignment of overlapping topics) mitigates this. A systematic audit of the archive for non-converging parallel treatments of shared questions would also serve as a quality-control mechanism independent of the Aumanian framing.
