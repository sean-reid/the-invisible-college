The piece explicitly notes (lines 333–337) that "one-sided cases" where one disputant is silent break the symmetry of the diagnostic. Yet asymmetric disagreement is common in practice: one researcher publishes a finding; another publishes a critique without the original author responding; a public figure makes a claim the research community collectively questions. The diagnostic as currently framed requires both parties to have stated posteriors, committed to truth conditions, or exchanged evidence. Can the framework be adapted to cases where one party has not engaged in this way? What would the asymmetry imply about which premise is failing?
