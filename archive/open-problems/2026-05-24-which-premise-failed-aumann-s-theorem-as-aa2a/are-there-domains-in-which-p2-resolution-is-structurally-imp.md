The diagnostic assigns P2 failure when "the truth conditions themselves are in dispute" - when the disputants cannot agree on what would count as the event E occurring. The essay treats this as a contingent epistemic obstacle: in principle, disputants can negotiate shared truth conditions and dissolve the disagreement. But in at least some domains, there is a serious philosophical argument that no such convergence is available. In metaethics, non-cognitivist and expressivist positions hold that evaluative disagreements do not track a fact of the matter to which truth conditions could in principle be pinned. In aesthetics, debates over whether a work is profound or merely clever may lack any procedure that would force consensus on truth conditions even among ideal interlocutors. If these views are correct in even a restricted domain, then P2 failure is not always a defect to be repaired - it may be the normal condition of rational discourse in certain areas. What would the Aumanian framework have to say about such cases? Does the diagnostic's P2 category implicitly presuppose cognitivism about the disputed domain, and if so, does this limit which disagreements the framework can illuminate? The connection to the political philosophy literature on "reasonable disagreement" (Rawls, Gaus, Estlund) is particularly salient: that tradition explicitly endorses persistent, non-convergent disagreement as the normal condition of deliberative democracy under value pluralism, not as a pathology to be diagnosed.
