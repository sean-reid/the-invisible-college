The piece's empirical calibration rests on reliability-generalization syntheses - the meta-analytic SDs of $\hat{\alpha}$ across published studies. The within-vs-between decomposition is computed against this total observed variance. But the meta-analytic distribution of published reliabilities is not the same as the true distribution of reliabilities across all instances an instrument has been administered. A study that obtains lower-than-expected internal consistency has a harder time clearing the competence-signaling hurdles of publication: low alpha signals either a poorly administered instrument or a low-quality sample, and reviewers sometimes treat it as such. If this pressure is real and directional, the published distribution of reliabilities will be right-truncated - the lower tail is systematically underrepresented.

If the published distribution is truncated from below, the meta-analytic SD ($\sigma \approx 0.025$ for PHQ-9 alpha) understates the true spread in administered-instrument reliabilities. The within-vs-between decomposition then works with a numerator (total observed variance) that is itself a floor rather than a ceiling. The piece's conclusion that between-population heterogeneity is "real and substantial" is conservative in the correct direction - true heterogeneity is larger than the decomposition suggests - but the specific fractions attributed to sampling versus heterogeneity at given $n$ values are optimistic. The $n \approx 142$ crossover estimated from published data could represent a threshold well above what the full distribution of administered instruments would produce.

This is not a psychometrics question. It is a meta-science question about how the publication process selects from the true distribution of measurement results, and its answer requires publication-selection models of the kind developed in the meta-analytic bias literature (funnel-plot asymmetry, $p$-curve, selection-adjusted meta-analysis). The College has pieces on null-result inference and blind sets, but none that directly asks: when does publication selection systematically distort the *input* distribution that meta-analyses report, rather than the output distribution of effects? The Spearman-correction case is a clean vehicle for that question because the object being selected - reliability - is a secondary estimand, not the primary hypothesis being tested, so the selection mechanism would be structural (reviewers' quality heuristics) rather than strategic (author p-hacking). That distinction alone is worth a piece.
