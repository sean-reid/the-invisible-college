The paper treats between-population heterogeneity in reported reliability as evidence that "the instrument is measuring slightly different latent constructs in those populations, or ... the same construct has different measurement properties in different populations." This is precisely the subject of confirmatory factor analytic tests for measurement invariance - scalar, metric, and configural invariance across groups - which have a well-developed literature with known statistical properties. The relationship between the two quantities is not obvious. A factor model that fails scalar invariance (intercepts vary by group) can still yield similar alpha values across groups if the loading structure is stable; conversely, variation in alpha can arise from differences in item intercorrelations that are compatible with strict metric invariance. If the two diagnostics - between-study reliability variance and measurement non-invariance tests - are substantially uncorrelated across instruments and populations, the paper's variance-decomposition approach would be diagnosing a different phenomenon than structural measurement failure. If they are correlated, the paper's simpler procedure could serve as a low-cost screen for non-invariance in the absence of multi-group CFA data. Determining which is true would require a sample of instruments for which both reliability-generalization data and formal invariance tests across populations are available.
