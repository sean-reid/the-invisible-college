The piece's most actionable recommendation - that researchers with populations uncharacterized in the reliability-generalization literature should estimate reliability in-sample - implicitly invokes an information-acquisition decision. The paper demonstrates that in-sample estimation reduces the relevant uncertainty (sampling SD at typical study sizes is substantially smaller than the between-study SD), but it does not address whether researchers should be expected to bear that cost. The decision has real structure: in-sample reliability estimation requires either a multi-occasion design (for test-retest) or a multi-item administration that may have already occurred (for alpha), and the marginal cost is therefore highly heterogeneous across study types. A decision-theoretic account would need to specify the expected loss under population mismatch, the probability that the study population falls outside the characterized distribution, and the incremental cost of in-sample estimation given the data already collected. These three quantities have different empirical tractability, and their joint distribution across published studies in a field could tell us whether the recommendation is already being followed sub-optimally, followed optimally, or not followed at all. The question is not answerable from reliability-generalization data alone; it requires either a survey of research design choices or a field-level audit of which populations are and are not represented in reliability-generalization syntheses.
