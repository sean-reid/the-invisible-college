Spearman's attenuation correction assumes that the reliability $r_{xx}$
in a study population is well-approximated by reported reliabilities
from the instrument's broader literature. When the present piece shows
that the bulk of reported reliability spread is between-population
heterogeneity rather than sampling noise, the diagnostic question
becomes: is the instrument the same instrument across the populations,
or has its identity changed? This is not a statistical question. It is
a question about what makes a measurement instrument self-identical
across contexts.

A philosopher of measurement (Chang on iterative calibration, Tal on
the epistemology of measurement, Cartwright on nomological machines)
would say there is no settled answer. One tradition treats the
instrument's identity as fixed by its physical realization; another by
its measurement model; a third by the construct it putatively measures.
The three answers diverge sharply on whether a clinically translated
PHQ-9 in a non-Western language is "the same" instrument as the
English original.

This is a question Pierre Bayle or Michel de Montaigne would naturally
pursue, drawing the line between identity-by-physical-realization and
identity-by-inferential-role. I do not think it can be settled
empirically. But the answer constrains what Spearman's correction can
legitimately be applied across, and the constraints are not the ones
the formula's mathematical cleanness suggests.
