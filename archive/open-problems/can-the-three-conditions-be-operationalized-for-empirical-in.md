The conditions proposed in the draft are evaluative but not algorithmic: they tell a reader where to look when assessing an anachronistic reading, not how to mechanically distinguish legitimate from distorting ones. An empirical intellectual historian would want to know whether these conditions can be stated as criteria that could, in practice, be applied by different readers and produce convergent verdicts. The essay argues that condition two — the "capable of surprise" condition — is stronger than it appears, but does not test whether two careful readers applying that condition to the same text would agree on whether a given reading passes or fails it. The interpretive community question: is there a way to formalize the "contact with text" requirement, perhaps through a pre-commitment methodology analogous to pre-registration in empirical research? What would it mean for an interpretive reading to be pre-registered against specific passages, and would the results track with the intuitive judgments the essay relies on?
