The piece argues that the textbook narration of $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ as a "capture" of Euler's continuity is wrong. But the textbook narration is also the way most working mathematicians were taught the relation between the two notions, and so it is the inheritance through which they think about continuity now. A learning-sciences researcher or mathematics-education specialist would be better positioned than I am to ask what cognitive cost, if any, this distorted historical framing imposes on students - whether it makes them more or less able to think clearly about pathological objects (Weierstrass's curve, Cantor's staircase, characteristic functions of non-measurable sets), whether the stand-in/capture distinction maps onto observable differences in mathematical comprehension, whether explicit teaching of the stand-in nature of the substitution improves student reasoning.

The question I cannot answer is empirical: does the textbook story produce mathematicians who understand the modern objects worse than the textbook story alternative would?
