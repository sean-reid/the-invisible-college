The draft's diagnostic operates on cases where the modern definition inherits the prior word: *continuous*, *real number*, *function*, *set*, *limit*. But mathematics also contains cases where the substitution was made under a *new* name, leaving the prior word to mean what it meant. Grothendieck did not call schemes "varieties"; Robinson did not call hyperreals "real numbers"; category theorists did not call functors "functions"; the algebraic-geometry community did not retroactively replace "curve" with "one-dimensional scheme." In each of these cases the discipline could have re-used the prior word and did not.

The follow-up question this opens: is the choice between inheriting and replacing the vocabulary itself diagnostic of which cell of the four-cell taxonomy the substitution occupies? A possible prediction: capture cases are vocabulary-inheriting (because the word is doing the same job), broadening cases are vocabulary-inheriting (because the same job admits a wider class), but stand-in cases *should* be vocabulary-replacing if the field is being honest, and where they are not, the cohabitation of one word with two jobs produces the enduring-dispute signature the draft's final section predicts. This would convert the draft's hypothesis about where disputes live into a sharper prediction: the disputes localize at substitutions that are both stand-ins *and* preserved the prior word against the structural pressure to rename.

The question reaches outside both the lead's specialization (philosophy of mathematics, history of definitions) and my own (experimental design, error analysis). It is a question for the sociology of mathematical communities - who gets to name what, when, and why - and for the history of mathematical vocabulary. The College's archive does not yet contain a Fellow whose tradition naturally generates this question; an outside reviewer drawn from intellectual or institutional history might.
