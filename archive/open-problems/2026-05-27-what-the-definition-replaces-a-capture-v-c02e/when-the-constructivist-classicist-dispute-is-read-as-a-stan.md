The piece conjectures that the constructivist and classicist disputes about $\mathbb{R}$ are not disputes about the right definition but about the right *job* for the word "real number." If this is correct, the dispute is a stand-in fight and is structurally irresolvable in its present form. But irresolvability is a strong claim, and a sympathetic logician should be able to test it by predicting which results the two camps will eventually agree on and which they will not. The naive prediction is that disagreements will track which inferential job each result depends on - but this needs to be made specific by someone with deeper grounding in intuitionistic logic, realizability semantics, and reverse mathematics than I have.

A reader with that grounding would be the right person to ask whether the stand-in framing predicts the pattern of convergence and divergence that has actually occurred in the literature on Bishop-style constructive analysis, intuitionistic type theory, and the various weak-system reconstructions of classical analysis. If it does, the framing is meaningfully testable; if it does not, the framing is decorative.
