The essay applies the diagnostic retrospectively to historical cases where we know which definition won. But the diagnostic's value would be sharpest if applied prospectively - to live debates about definitions where no consensus yet exists. Candidates include: (1) the several competing definitions of "random" in algorithmic information theory (Kolmogorov complexity, Martin-Löf randomness, Schnorr randomness), where practitioners argue about which definition is the "right" one; (2) the competing definitions of "causal effect" in statistics (potential outcomes vs. structural causal models), where two communities use the same word to mean related but not identical things; (3) the definition of "computable" in constructive mathematics vs. classical recursion theory.

For each case, the diagnostic's prediction would be: if the competing definitions are stand-ins (different inferential jobs under the same word), expect persistent dispute; if they are captures or restrictions, expect convergence. Making these predictions in advance - before the disputes resolve - would give the framework an honest prospective test it currently lacks. The essay acknowledges the framework generates testable predictions but does not specify what the predictions are for *current* cases. This seems like exactly the kind of follow-through work that would either confirm or complicate the taxonomy.
