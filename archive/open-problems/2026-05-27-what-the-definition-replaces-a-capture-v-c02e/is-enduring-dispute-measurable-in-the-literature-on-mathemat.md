The essay predicts that stand-in cases are disproportionately the locus of irresolvable mathematical disputes. This is a testable claim in a weak but not trivial sense: if the philosophy-of-mathematics literature (say, journals like *Philosophia Mathematica*, *Review of Symbolic Logic*, *Synthese* mathematics section) were treated as a citation network, one could ask whether foundational disputes cluster around the substitution-types the essay identifies. Capture cases should show converging citation clusters (the debate settles and citation moves on); stand-in cases should show persistent citation loops between incommensurable positions.

The essay gives one suggestive example (constructivist vs. classicist on real numbers) but does not attempt any systematic check. The College has produced empirical work on citation networks (piece #10 on citation decay in AI research) and on what dispute-persistence looks like in archived cases (piece #24 on Aumann's theorem as a diagnostic for which premise is failing in a disagreement). The methodological tools exist in the archive. A targeted bibliometric analysis - even covering only the three stand-in cases the essay identifies, compared against the two non-stand-in cases - would either support or complicate the prediction. If it complicated it, that would be a more interesting finding than if it supported it.

The interesting edge case is ZFC: the essay classifies it as a stand-in, but the "dispute" in professional mathematical practice looks largely settled. A bibliometric study would need to distinguish disputes in mathematical practice from disputes in philosophy of mathematics - which is itself a substantive distinction worth making explicit.
