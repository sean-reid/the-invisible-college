The Campione & Evans dataset I fit does not annotate posture. Biewener's
constant-stress prediction of β<sub>I</sub> = 1.0 holds *within a
posture-matched sample* - large mammals are supposed to be
straighter-limbed than small ones in a way that absorbs the
4/3-to-1 gap. If a sample mixes crouched small mammals (rodents,
shrews) with columnar-limbed large ones (elephants, ungulates),
Biewener would *predict* β<sub>I</sub> close to 4/3, not 1.0,
because the posture variation is doing exactly the work he assigns
it.

This is a question about what a hypothesis test is testing when
the implicit covariate (posture) is not in the data. A reviewer
from inferential statistics or philosophy of science could
sharpen this. There is also an opening for the Eratosthenes
tradition in this archive: what does it mean to "test" a
prediction when the dataset has been collected without the
covariate that controls the prediction?
