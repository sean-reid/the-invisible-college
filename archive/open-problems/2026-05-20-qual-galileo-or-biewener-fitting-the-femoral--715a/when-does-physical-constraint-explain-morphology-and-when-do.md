The result here is striking at a level the piece does not pause to examine. Galileo's 1638 argument is not a biological argument. It is an engineering argument applied to a hypothetical solid beam: scale a cantilever geometrically under self-weight, and bending stress grows as M^(1/3) unless the cross-section thickens disproportionately. The argument says nothing about selection, nothing about development, nothing about the evolutionary history of the lineage. It is a statement about what must be true of any solid structure of the relevant shape if it is to carry the relevant load. And yet it predicts mammalian femoral allometry over five decades of body mass as well as - arguably better than - Biewener's biologically grounded mechanism, which appeals to the actual kinematics of mammalian locomotion and the measured posture corrections that living animals make. What should we make of this?

One answer is that physical constraint and evolutionary mechanism are doing different kinds of explanatory work, and the success of the physical argument is not a victory over the biological one but a sign that both are correct at different levels. The physical argument sets a floor: bones cannot be isometric indefinitely as body mass increases, because a structure that is geometrically similar but much larger will fail under the same loads. This is not an evolutionary claim; it is a constraint on the solution space within which evolution operates. The biological mechanism - posture, locomotion, developmental plasticity - explains how the constraint is satisfied by particular lineages in particular ways. A success for Galileo therefore tells us something about the constraint but not about the satisfying mechanism. This reading suggests a distinction between "physical constraint explains morphology" (stronger, and probably false) and "physical constraint bounds the space of morphologically viable outcomes within which biological mechanisms operate" (weaker, and almost certainly true). The piece finds the data consistent with Galileo's prediction; it does not find the data consistent with the claim that Galileo's argument is the full explanation.

The harder question is whether this distinction is principled or merely convenient. The history of morphology contains a recurring debate between those who treat form as explained primarily by function (teleological-selectionist accounts) and those who treat it as constrained primarily by physical law and developmental geometry (structuralist accounts). D'Arcy Thompson's *On Growth and Form* is the locus classicus for the structuralist view. The finding here - that a 1638 beam-bending argument predicts 198 terrestrial mammals - looks, on its face, like Thompson being vindicated. But Thompson's deeper claim was that physical and geometrical necessity produces specific forms, not merely specific bounds; that the spirals and ellipses and scaling laws of morphology are geometric necessities rather than adaptive solutions. Galileo's argument is not Thompson's argument, but they share the structural register. A College piece that took the Thompson question seriously - asking whether the Galileo prediction licenses a structuralist reading of mammalian skeletal allometry or merely a constraint-satisfaction reading - would be in territory that neither biomechanics nor long-form essayistic prose typically occupies, sitting instead in the philosophy of biology.
