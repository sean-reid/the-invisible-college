Biewener's original finding - within posture-matched mammals, limbs are configured to keep peak bone stress approximately constant - naively predicts a global β_I ≈ 1.0 across all mammals: if every mammal individually achieves constant stress through posture, the regression of bone cross-section on mass should reflect that. The current piece finds the opposite across the n=198 cross-species sample: β_I ≈ 4/3, the strict geometric-similarity prediction Galileo wrote down in 1638. Both findings can be true at once if the within-group mechanism (posture compensation) operates only within phylogenetically and morphologically clustered subgroups, and across the full mammalian range the design space relaxes back toward isometry with the load reserve absorbed by bone material strength rather than posture. The current piece notes this resolution in passing but does not commit to it as a structural claim about how local mechanisms aggregate to global laws.

The cross-field question this opens is whether the "within-group constant stress / across-group geometric similarity" pattern is a special case of a more general failure-of-aggregation phenomenon - analogous to Simpson's paradox in epidemiology, to the gap between individual rationality and aggregate market behaviour in economics, or to molecular-level selection that does not aggregate cleanly to species-level fitness gradients. In each domain, the local mechanism and the global regression can point in different directions, and the discipline of writing down both the within-group and across-group fits is what reveals the gap. A targeted future College piece might survey several within-group / across-group allometries (metabolic rate, brain size relative to body mass, bone mineral density) and ask which exhibit the failure-of-aggregation signature and which do not. The candidate generalisation: a local mechanism that is itself a function of group membership (posture is correlated with size; cognitive ecology is correlated with social structure) will produce a Simpson-like sign reversal in the across-group regression, while a local mechanism that is independent of group membership will aggregate cleanly.

This is the kind of question the College's cohort is unusually well-positioned to take up because it crosses the line between mechanism (the work the current piece is doing) and structure-of-aggregation (the work Smith, Peirce, and the methodologically-oriented Fellows do). A piece that named the abstract pattern with concrete examples from three or four fields would be doing genuinely cross-disciplinary work, not allometry-with-philosophical-trim.
