The piece finds λ̂ = 0.68 under Pagel's λ model and interprets the departure from λ = 1 (strict Brownian motion) as evidence of convergent selection pressure - multiple mammalian lineages independently tracking a mechanical optimum for limb-bone cross-section under similar gravitational and locomotor constraints. The Hansen 1997 Ornstein-Uhlenbeck framework is cited as theoretical motivation. But the piece stops short of the obvious next question: if OU dynamics with a known strength of attraction toward an optimum are the correct generating process, what value of λ should we *expect* to observe at n = 200 taxa on a mammalian supertree, as a function of the OU selection parameter α and the time depth of the tree? This is answerable in closed form - the expected covariance under OU as a function of branch lengths and α is well-known - and the answer would tell us whether λ̂ = 0.68 is consistent with the moderate-selection regime the piece implicitly invokes, with a very-strong-selection regime that would produce λ ≈ 0, or with something in between.

The practical payoff is a prior distribution over λ for this question, derived from the biology rather than from the data. If the OU framework predicts λ in the range [0.5, 0.8] for moderate selection on skeletal traits across mammals, then λ̂ = 0.68 is confirming a biologically motivated prior, not just a statistical artefact. If the OU framework predicts λ ≈ 0.1 for the selection strengths the limb-loading literature implies, then λ̂ = 0.68 is anomalously high and the convergent-selection interpretation is strained. The piece cannot distinguish these cases without the calibration, and the current reading is therefore somewhat underdetermined.

This connects to a broader methodological question the College has not yet addressed: most phylogenetic comparative analyses treat λ as a nuisance parameter to be estimated from the data, rather than as a quantity with a prior derivable from the evolutionary model. A piece that (a) derived the expected λ under OU dynamics calibrated to published α estimates for mammalian skeletal traits, (b) compared the predicted λ range to the empirical λ̂ distribution across a sample of published allometry studies, and (c) used this comparison to assess whether Brownian motion's widespread use as a default is defensible for this class of trait, would be a substantive methodological contribution to phylogenetic comparative biology - and would close the loop this piece opens.
