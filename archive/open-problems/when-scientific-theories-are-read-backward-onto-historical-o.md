The essay's framework was developed for reading historical *texts* through later *concepts*. But an analogous problem arises in the sciences: Babylonian astronomical records are regularly fit to modern orbital mechanics; Newtonian mechanics is read back through relativistic corrections to explain Mercury's precession; Mendel's breeding data are re-examined through modern population genetics. In each case, a historical practice or observation is described using conceptual resources the original practitioner did not have. The essay's three conditions apply: Is there a genuine structural feature being picked out? Is the reading capable of surprise? Does the reading require attributing the later theory's presuppositions to the historical actor?

The interesting question is whether the conditions apply differently when the historical material is quantitative. Scientific measurements have explicit formal structure — they are reproducible, they have error bars, they can in principle be checked against modern instruments. This makes the "independently identifiable structural feature" (Condition 1) much easier to establish and much harder to dispute: the shadow angle Eratosthenes measured is a physical fact that modern optics and geodesy can characterize independently of any historical reading. But it also creates a new risk: the formal structure of scientific data may make "structural readings" feel more objective than they are, concealing selection effects analogous to the ones the Butler analysis exposes. The choice of which features of a historical observation to treat as "structural" (the angle itself) versus "noise" (the measurement method, the unit choice) is governed by the later theory in ways that mirror Butler's framework selecting which structural features of Antigone to analyze.

The College has published work on Eratosthenes (#08), on claim decay in scientific citation (#10), and on the history of specific mathematical objects. Whether the humanities-derived framework extends cleanly to the sciences — or requires modification, or fails in instructive ways — has not been examined. The answer would shed light on whether the conditions are specific to the reading of texts or apply to empirical inquiry generally.
