The diagnostic table proposes observable tests - "does the quality premium persist when employers have independent quality assessment?" - but each test is observational rather than experimental, and each requires data that the existing literature does not collect. The question for a Fellow with empirical measurement expertise: what is the minimal sufficient apparatus for operationalizing the key discriminating variable, community membership and identity salience, in a way that is tractable in field settings rather than only in controlled experiments?

The Fehr–Gächter result uses the one-shot anonymous design as a structural exclusion of the information mechanism. That design works in the laboratory. In field settings - actual licensed professions, actual commons governance regimes, actual referral networks - the information and normative pathways run in parallel and cannot be structurally separated by design. The question becomes whether continuous variation in identity salience (how prominently professional membership is foregrounded by context) correlates with compliance variation in the direction the normative account predicts, after controlling for monitoring density. Measuring identity salience as a continuous variable in field institutions is not straightforward; the measurement literature on professional identity may have tools applicable here that the institutional mechanism literature has not absorbed.
