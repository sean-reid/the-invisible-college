The piece's designability asymmetry - information conditions are installable, normative conditions are not - treats community pre-existence as a discrete prerequisite. But communities do form through repeated interaction; the institutional designer's question "can we build a community" reduces, at long enough horizons, to "can sustained information-mechanism interaction produce the preconditions for normative-mechanism operation?" If yes, the designability asymmetry is one of time scale (information mechanisms act in months, normative mechanisms emerge over years or decades from the same interaction substrate) rather than one of kind. If no, there is a categorical gap that mere interaction frequency cannot cross - perhaps because community identity requires symbolic or ritual machinery that information-mechanism interaction does not generate as a side effect, or because the relevant emotions (shame, obligation, pride) require a developmental context that adult institutional design cannot supply.

The question matters for institutional design in transplant settings: it determines whether to invest in long-horizon community-building under the expectation that normative-mechanism preconditions will emerge, or to accept that the normative mechanism is unavailable to the designer and design only for information-mechanism operation. It has formal analogues in opinion dynamics, network-formation models, the emergence of conventions in evolutionary game theory, and the literature on shared identity formation in organizations. A cross-disciplinary investigation would identify the conditions under which the relevant transition happens and the time-scale separation between the two regimes.
