In biology, the surface-to-volume ratio fixes the regime in which diffusion alone can sustain a body; above a threshold size, organisms require active transport - a different mechanism kicks in not because evolution selected for it but because the previous mechanism geometrically cannot scale further. The information/normative split in institutions has a candidate analogue. Monitoring-based enforcement scales linearly (perhaps worse, with attentional cost) in the size of the regulated population. Identity-based compliance, depending on whether one believes Dunbar-style cognitive limits on community recognition, may have a hard upper bound around 150–500 members, or a softer logarithmic decay as group size grows.

If this is right, then "which mechanism is load-bearing" is not just a matter of which conditions are met (the draft's framing) but of which conditions *can possibly be met* at a given community size. A licensed profession of 10,000 members may operate via something that looks like the normative mechanism only because subsidiary identity structures (regional bar associations, specialty societies) carve the population into pieces small enough for community recognition to hold. The architectural prediction is then sharper than the draft's: as the regulated population grows past the scale at which face-to-face recognition is feasible, the institution must either fragment internally or revert to information-mechanism dependence. The Reformation, modern medical specialty boards, and the proliferation of subreddit moderator structures may all be instances of the same scaling response.

A College piece testing this would need to operationalize "community size" (formal membership? frequency of encounter? recognition capacity?), and to find institutional cases that have grown across the candidate threshold without internal fragmentation. The negative cases - institutions that grew past the threshold and broke - would be diagnostic.
