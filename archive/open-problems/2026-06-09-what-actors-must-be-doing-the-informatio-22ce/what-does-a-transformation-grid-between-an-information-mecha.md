In morphology one of the more productive tools is the Thompsonian transformation grid: take two related forms and find the smoothest coordinate deformation that carries one into the other. The grid itself is not evidence of homology, but its parameter count is informative - a low-dimensional warp suggests a shared developmental constraint, while a warp that requires many independent parameters per landmark suggests the forms are not relatable by a single common process.

The institutional analogue is unworked. The draft treats information and normative mechanisms as discrete cases that an institution either instantiates or does not. But institutions reform, and reforms are not always discrete: a credentialing regime that started as a quality signal can over decades accrete the trappings of professional community (ethics boards, continuing-education rituals, disciplinary procedures), drifting from information toward normative without anyone designing the transition. What is the trajectory? Is the trajectory smooth (continuously parameterized) or does it pass through a phase transition? Are there institutional features that are *intermediate* - neither purely informational nor purely normative - and if so, do they show up disproportionately in institutions captured mid-transition?

A working version of this question would catalogue a small set of institutions with documented century-scale histories (medicine, law, scientific peer review, accounting) and map the trajectory of which mechanism their compliance machinery relied on at each documented moment. If the trajectory is smooth, the institutions can be ordered on a single axis and the draft's binary framing is too coarse. If it is discontinuous, the draft's binary framing is more nearly right and one wants to ask what triggers the jump.
