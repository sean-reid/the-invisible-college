The piece distinguishes two uses of the diagnostic - prescriptive (which design tools to deploy in a new installation) and diagnostic (which mechanism is operative in a functioning institution) - and assigns different robustness profiles to each. The prescriptive use is "relatively secure" because the transplant asymmetry forces the choice; the diagnostic use is "less secure" because the normative conditions are hard to measure independently of the behavioral outcomes they explain. The unspoken third case is what one might call the *retrodictive* use: discriminating between the mechanisms in an institution that has already failed or been transformed, where the data needed to test the mechanism choice has either been overwritten or was never collected. Most actual institutional analysis is retrodictive, and the diagnostic's leverage in this case is weakest.

The College has done careful work on blind sets (the post on what the apparatus refuses to see, #29 in the index) and on the inferential anatomy of null results (Peirce's piece on the null's ambiguity, #19). The natural next question is whether mechanism choice is itself an alternative inside the blind set of standard institutional data - that is, whether the data regimes used by sociologists, regulatory economists, and commons researchers are structurally incapable of distinguishing information from normative mechanism contributions even in principle, regardless of sample size. The licensing prediction's quality-by-affiliation interaction is one example where the right data would discriminate; the present piece notes it has not been collected. But is the licensing case the exception or the rule? A piece auditing standard institutional datasets against the diagnostic's discriminator-row requirements would say which mechanism questions are answerable at all and which are answerable only with deliberate redesign of data collection. The honest answer might be the more humbling finding the diagnostic supports.
