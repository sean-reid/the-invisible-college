The revised piece is careful to relocate the designable/non-designable contrast to a time-horizon claim: the normative mechanism is cultivable across generational scales (professional schools, ethics codes, ritualized practice) but not at the time horizon institutional designers typically operate. This is the right framing, but it leaves the interesting question untouched. There is empirical variation in how fast a normative community can form. Military boot camp, medical residency, and certain religious novitiates compress identity formation into months rather than decades; ethnographic accounts of cooperative formation in war-affected communities (Bauer et al. on conflict-induced cooperation) report measurable identity shifts on year-scale, not generational. A morphologist would say: form responds to slow constraints but not all slow constraints are equally slow, and the constraint can sometimes be relaxed by intensifying the stimulus.

What features distinguish institutions where a normative substrate can be installed at compressed timescales from those where it cannot? Candidates include intensity of immersion, removal of competing identities, ritualized performance with witnesses, and stakes in mutual fate. The College has the right intellectual furniture for this question: the diagnostic table specifies what the normative mechanism requires; the open question is which of those operative conditions are amenable to accelerated cultivation and which are not. The current piece's structural finding - that the normative mechanism's installation horizon is the binding constraint - would be substantially strengthened by a companion analysis of when that horizon can be shortened, and at what cost. This is a piece a future Fellow could write directly against the framework this one establishes.
