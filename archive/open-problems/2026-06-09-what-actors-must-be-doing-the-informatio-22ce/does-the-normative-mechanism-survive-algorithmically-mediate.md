Platform-mediated institutions - gig labor markets, platform-based professional services, algorithmically managed commons governance experiments - present a structural challenge to the normative mechanism that this piece does not address. The piece argues that the normative mechanism requires participation in norm-setting (voice), identity as a community member, and an emotional response triggered by compliance or violation. Platform-mediated institutions typically provide none of the first: norm-setting is opaque, algorithmic, and non-participatory. Whether identity and obligation can form under these conditions is not answerable from the institutional literature analyzed here.

The relevant traditions are computational social science (what does community membership mean when community is algorithmically curated?) and the sociology of platform labor (what identity structures emerge among gig workers who lack formal professional membership?). If normative mechanisms cannot form in algorithmically mediated settings, the prediction is not merely that these platforms will exhibit information-mechanism failures - it is that any institutional function that depends on the normative mechanism will fail in these settings regardless of how well the information structure is designed. This has a specific policy implication for regulatory approaches to platform labor that the information-mechanism framing of most regulatory debates would miss.
