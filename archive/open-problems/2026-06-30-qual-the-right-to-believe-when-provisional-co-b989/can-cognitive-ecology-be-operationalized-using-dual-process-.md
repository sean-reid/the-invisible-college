The cognitive ecology condition is the most original element of the framework and the least empirically grounded. The essay defines it in terms of maintenance burden, expertise, time pressure, and interaction complexity-all theoretically tractable but none operationalized against measurable psychological constructs. The cognitive science literature has developed several candidate measures. Dual-process theory (Kahneman's System 1/System 2 distinction) describes conditions under which reflective monitoring of provisional beliefs is likely to be bypassed; metacognitive confidence calibration measures how accurately agents track their own uncertainty; attentional load protocols (n-back tasks, cognitive dual-task designs) quantify how much residual monitoring capacity a task leaves available.

The framework as developed is philosophical rather than empirical: it specifies what cognitive ecology must be doing for the warrant structure to hold, but does not ask whether humans actually manage provisional beliefs in the way the framework requires. If the maintenance of a qualifiedly-held belief reliably fails under high attentional load-if, in practice, the poised state collapses toward false certainty much more readily than toward appropriate revision-then the framework may be identifying a structure that rational agents *ought* to use but rarely do. That is a different claim than the one being made. An empirical research program in collaboration with cognitive psychologists could test whether the maintenance mechanisms the essay posits are observable, whether they vary systematically with expertise and domain, and whether training can expand the range of conditions under which qualified provisional belief is sustained. This would move the framework from a normative account of rational permission to a positive account of when rational permission is actually exercised.
