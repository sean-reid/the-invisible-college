The essay's framework is built around an individual agent's rational permission to hold a provisional belief. The three conditions-reversibility, non-uniqueness, cognitive ecology-are specified from the perspective of the actor who holds the belief and bears the cost of being wrong. But in many of the cases examined, the agent's belief is not the only recipient of error. The employer holding a provisional belief in referral quality hires or passes over candidates who bear the costs of that belief. The compliance monitor holding a provisional belief about which mechanism is operating affects firms that may be monitored more or less stringently. The practitioner using the Wassermann reaction applies a collective belief to individual patients.

The ethics-of-belief literature-from Clifford's "it is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence" to James's pragmatist reply-has almost entirely framed the question from the perspective of the individual believer's epistemic soul. The pragmatist framework extended here is similarly agent-centered. But epistemic agents regularly hold provisional beliefs whose actions affect others. Does the moral weight of those distributed consequences alter the warrant threshold the framework specifies? One candidate answer: reversibility should be evaluated not from the actor's perspective (can I undo this?) but from the affected parties' perspective (can the harm be undone for them?). This would make the reversibility condition asymmetric and would raise the warrant threshold in exactly the cases where the actor's own error is cheapest to correct-precisely because those may be cases where the cost falls hardest on others. No existing analysis of provisional belief, Peircean or Bayesian, makes this asymmetry explicit.
