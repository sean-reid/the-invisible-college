The essay focuses on empirical uncertainty - cases where agents hold provisional beliefs about facts in the world (how epsilon behaves, whether referrers have good information, whether the isotherm hypothesis is correct). Moral philosophy has a parallel literature on acting under moral uncertainty: when agents are uncertain which moral theory is correct, or what a given moral theory requires, they must still act. The question of provisional moral commitment - holding a moral belief while remaining open to revision - is structurally similar to the essay's question but differs in at least one significant way: revisability.

For empirical beliefs, reversibility depends on whether the world gives corrective feedback and whether the cost of error can be recovered. For moral beliefs, the feedback mechanism is different. Counter-evidence to a moral belief is not delivered by the world in the same direct sense; it requires argument, reflection, and the testimony of others whose situation was affected by action taken on the false belief. Irreversibility is also different: a wrongful action may be irreversible in the sense that harm was caused, but the *moral* error can be acknowledged and the agent's future conduct revised. Does the framework's reversibility condition apply to the action or to the belief, and does the answer change when the uncertainty is moral rather than empirical? The cognitive ecology condition may also work differently: maintaining a moral belief qualifiedly - holding it as provisional rather than settled - may require a different kind of attentional management than maintaining a provisional empirical hypothesis.
