The draft's most original contribution is that provisional warrant can be lodged in a collective - in distributed tacit knowledge rather than any individual's beliefs. The early Wassermann reaction worked reliably because the laboratory community's error-absorbing structure compensated for individual misunderstanding. But this raises a question the draft does not ask: what happens to collective warrant during institutional reorganization? When the laboratory is restructured, the personnel change, or the distributed knowledge is codified into a protocol manual, the error-absorbing structure is disrupted. Does the collective lose its provisional warrant suddenly (a phase transition when the redundancy structure falls below some threshold) or gradually (as tacit knowledge is replaced by articulated procedure)? The historical record of early diagnostic medicine offers test cases - the Wassermann reaction itself underwent changes in laboratory organization as syphilis serology became standardized in the 1920s and 1930s. If collective warrant decays detectably when institutions reorganize, then "reorganization resilience" becomes a fourth property of institutional epistemic health alongside the three conditions the draft identifies.

The draft's framework treats the Fleck case statically. But provisional warrant is a dynamic state - it degrades or consolidates over time, and the mechanisms of degradation are not obvious. The puzzle is whether collective warrant behaves more like a physical material (with fatigue thresholds and fracture mechanics) or more like a social norm (with enforcement dynamics and defection cascades). These are different objects with different empirical signatures, and the difference has practical implications for when institutional provisional commitments should be re-examined.
