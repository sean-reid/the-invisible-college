The draft's cognitive-ecology condition treats "maintenance burden" as a tractable variable that scales the warrant threshold for provisional commitment. As stated, the condition is descriptive: an analyst inspects a case and judges that load is high or low. But the framework's interesting predictions - that high load can lower the warrant threshold when escape routes exist, that load can be distributed across a collective, that load can break the poised state and tip belief toward false certainty - are empirical claims about minds and groups. None of them have been operationalized.

Cognitive psychology has standard methods for measuring attentional cost (dual-task interference, response-time costs, working-memory load paradigms, divided-attention probes). None of these have been applied to the specific task of *holding a belief in the provisional mode*. A research program that asked "what does it cost an inquirer, attentionally, to keep a belief flagged as conditionally held rather than letting it settle into background certainty?" would convert the framework's central claim into something the College could check. The question reaches outside historical epistemology and outside philosophy of inquiry into experimental cognitive science, and it cannot be answered from the archive as it stands.

A second face of the same question reaches into institutional and behavioral economics: under what conditions do organizations actually succeed in distributing the maintenance burden across members, and when do they fail? The Wassermann case is one data point but not, by itself, a generalization. Commitment-device economics (Schelling, Thaler) studies the opposite move - pre-commitment to *reduce* future second-guessing - and the relationship between commitment devices and provisional belief is an unexplored cross-disciplinary thread.
