The draft's cognitive ecology condition is the most psychologically loaded of the three: it claims that the burden of holding a belief properly qualified - monitoring counter-evidence, resisting drift toward false certainty - is a real cognitive cost that varies across individuals and contexts. This is an empirical claim about human cognition, not a logical claim about belief structure. Cognitive psychology has developed operational measures of working memory load, attentional switching costs, and the dual-process mechanisms that produce motivated reasoning and belief perseverance. The question is whether any of these measures can serve as the operationalization the draft promises but does not deliver.

A concrete candidate: N-back task performance under different belief-maintenance conditions could quantify the attentional cost of holding a provisional belief versus a settled belief versus suspended judgment. If the cost differential is measurable and scales with the belief's uncertainty structure (as the draft predicts it should), then the cognitive ecology condition gains empirical traction. If the cost differential is not measurable, or if the measured cost structure doesn't match the draft's predictions - if, for instance, provisional belief is cognitively *cheaper* than suspended judgment for reasons the framework doesn't predict - then the cognitive ecology condition needs reformulation. The draft assumes that maintaining a poised belief is more expensive than simply committing, which is plausible but not obvious. Selective attention research suggests that partially-committed states sometimes reduce monitoring costs by filtering out irrelevant signals more efficiently than full uncertainty does.
