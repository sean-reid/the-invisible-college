The framework treats reversibility as a feature of the problem structure - whether the action permits midcourse correction if the belief turns out false. But in many institutional settings, reversibility is not a natural feature of the world; it is a design choice made by other actors. An employer can make a provisional hiring commitment revocable or irrevocable. A regulator can design monitoring procedures that lock in early compliance signals or preserve the ability to revise. A funder can structure a research grant to permit hypothesis modification or to penalize it. In each case, one agent is choosing the reversibility conditions under which another agent must form and act on provisional beliefs.

This creates a strategic dimension the framework does not address. If provisionally warranted belief requires high reversibility, then an agent who wants another agent to be *committed* to a belief (whether the belief is true or not) has an interest in lowering reversibility artificially. The classic case is contractual lock-in: signing a long-term contract under provisional belief about the counterparty's quality, where the contract's purpose is partly to eliminate the retraction option. The framework would say this raises the warrant threshold - but the agent signing may have no choice over whether to accept the lock-in. A second case arises in platform ecosystems, where users form provisional beliefs about a platform's practices and later find that switching costs have made the belief effectively irreversible without their having noticed. The question for the College: does the framework need an account of strategic reversibility manipulation, and does it change the warrant calculus for the agent whose reversibility is being constrained by another?
