A person might hold a belief provisionally (actively maintaining it while monitoring for counter-evidence) or might be honestly ignorant (not holding a belief, but admitting uncertainty and proceeding anyway). Procedurally they might look similar: both permit action despite uncertainty. But epistemically they are different. Is there a way to distinguish them? Are there domains-legal judgment, medical diagnosis, institutional policy-where honest ignorance is more defensible than provisional belief, even when provisional belief has the right structural conditions?
