The piece establishes that BA networks at empirical sizes (N = 500 to 50,000) pass the CSN test 90–98% of the time. Broido and Clauset (2019) applied the same test to roughly 1,000 real-world networks and found fewer than 4% showed strong evidence for power-law degree distributions. The paper draws the correct implication - even the canonical generative model is not strictly scale-free by the CSN criterion - but it leaves unanswered what to do with the enormous gap between those two numbers.

Two explanations are compatible with the results so far. The first: most real-world networks are not generated by preferential-attachment-like processes, so the BA model's high pass rate is the wrong baseline for them. The second: real-world networks have structural features that produce more severe deviations from a power-law degree distribution than BA does at the same size - community structure, heterogeneous local attachment rules, bounded degree, degree-degree correlations of a different character than BA's. On the first explanation, the 4% finding is a statement about generative processes in the empirical world. On the second, it is a statement about statistical procedure: real networks have more deviation than BA, and the test is detecting that deviation, but the 4% boundary has no principled meaning beyond "the test has more power against these networks than against BA."

The College's toolkit is already suited to test this. The same pipeline - CSN test, same x_min scan, same bootstrap count - could be run on a curated set of real-world networks at matched sizes, comparing pass rates against the BA benchmark at each N. If real networks fail at higher rates than BA networks of the same size, that is evidence for the second explanation and raises the further question of which structural features drive the additional failures. If pass rates are comparable, the explanation must be distributional - real network generating processes are not BA-like, and the ones that pass happen to look like BA anyway. The Broido-Clauset dataset, or the SNAP network repository (Stanford Network Analysis Project), would provide the raw material. A natural control is the configuration model with the same degree sequence, which would separate the contribution of the marginal degree distribution from the correlation structure.
