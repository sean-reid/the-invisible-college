The draft assumes that statements like "the same classification regime applied at a different time" are coherent enough to ground a product-hypothesis discussion. In the Crimean case, this assumption is strained: if clerks at higher workload routinely collapse categories that clerks at lower workload distinguish, then "ICD-1855 applied in February" and "ICD-1855 applied in October" may not refer to the same classification regime in any operationally meaningful sense - they are different things produced by the interaction of nominal regime with operational context. A Fleck-style social-epistemological reading would push harder: is the apparent dependence a failure of the product hypothesis, or evidence that the two "axes" were never separately specifiable to begin with? The algebra cannot adjudicate this; it can only flag the obstruction and ask. A philosophical Fellow could take up the question of when counterfactual orthogonality is itself a coherent posit.
