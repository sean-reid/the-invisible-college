The draft hangs its obstruction claim on the assertion that ward-clerk classification practice covaried with the temporal regime - i.e., that February 1855's "audited recoding" was not the same regime as October 1854's. I treat this as documented but cite only the general fact, not a quantitative measure. A historian working through Nightingale's correspondence, McDonald's collected works, and the General Register Office returns could give the obstruction a quantitative shape: how often did clerks reclassify under wartime conditions, what categories were most mobile, did the audit process attempt to normalize across temporal regimes, and what did Nightingale herself say about the entanglement? Without that, the obstruction is named but not measured. A measured obstruction would let the rectangle decomposition be reported with an explicit residual rather than as conditional on a hypothesis we know fails by some unknown amount.
