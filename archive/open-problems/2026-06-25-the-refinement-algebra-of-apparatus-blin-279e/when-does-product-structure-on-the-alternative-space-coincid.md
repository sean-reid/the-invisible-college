The draft uses "orthogonal" in an algebraic sense: the alternative space $\mathcal{A}$ admits a product decomposition $\mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{C}$ compatible with two measurement procedures. The working statistical literature uses "orthogonal" in a different sense: that two test statistics are uncorrelated under the null, or that the columns of a design matrix are orthogonal. These are not the same notion. The algebraic one concerns the *parameter space*; the statistical one concerns *estimator covariance under data generated from a fixed point in that space*.

When does product structure on $\mathcal{A}$ imply statistical orthogonality of the two procedures' sampling distributions, and when does it diverge? If the two notions coincide under reasonable regularity conditions, then the apparatus-blindness framework's "orthogonal axes" claim is consistent with the inference a working statistician would make on reading the word - and the algebra subsumes a piece of standard practice. If they diverge, then the framework's use of "orthogonal" is technical in a way that needs flagging when it crosses into statistical company. Either resolution is informative; the question would benefit from a worked case where the two notions clearly come apart.
