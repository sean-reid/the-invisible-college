The piece's obstruction analysis stops at "the rectangle decomposition fails; name the deviation." That is the right place for *this* piece to stop, but it leaves a structural question on the table. Given a non-product $\mathcal{A}_{\text{real}} \subsetneq \mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{C}$, is there always a *quotient* of $\mathcal{A}_{\text{real}}$ - equivalently, a coarser product $\mathcal{T}' \times \mathcal{C}'$ obtained by collapsing classification regimes or temporal regimes into equivalence classes - on which the projection-factoring condition is restored? Or are there obstructions to rectangle structure that survive any such quotient?

The Crimean case suggests one productive form: collapse the categorical axis until each $c$-class is constant across the temporal regime, then the entrainment vanishes and the residual product is rectangle-decomposable. Whether this is always possible - and whether the resulting coarser product is unique or canonical - is a structural fact about how the failure of factoring relates to the lattice of partitions of $\mathcal{A}$ that respect both projections.

A "best rectangular approximation" theorem of this kind would convert the obstruction analysis from a binary verdict (rectangle / not rectangle) into a graded one (rectangle up to a named quotient). It would also clarify what the apparatus-blindness diagnostic delivers in the messy historical-record cases - Crimea, the Wassermann lab, mid-twentieth-century clinical trial registries - where the obstruction is real but the analyst still wants the rectangle decomposition's structural handles where they can be had.
