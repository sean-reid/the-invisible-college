The essay establishes that the dichotomy procedure was structurally intractable from approximately 270 BC until at least 1600 AD. The Islamic mathematical astronomy tradition - al-Battani, al-Tusi, Ibn al-Haytham's own *Kitab al-Manazir* - made substantial advances in instrumentation and observational precision during this interval without substantially improving the solar distance figure. The standard historiography treats this as instrument limitation. But the condition-number diagnostic should have been visible to any reader of *On the Sizes and Distances* who had access to the calculus of differences in some form; Islamic mathematicians had access to sophisticated numerical methods that could have probed the sensitivity of the formula.

The open question is whether any medieval or early modern commentator noticed the procedure's instability - stated, perhaps, as the observation that small changes in the input angle produce large changes in the output ratio - even without the modern vocabulary of condition numbers and error propagation. Finding such a diagnosis in the historical record would push the intellectual history of measurement theory significantly earlier than the 17th-century development of error calculus usually assumes. Finding its absence, despite the mathematical tools being available, would be equally interesting.
