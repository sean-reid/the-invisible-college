The disclosure standard in §6 requires that every measurement-bearing piece declare the alternative class 𝒜 the procedure is responsible for adjudicating. The Eratosthenes case shows this is a principled choice, not a mechanical derivation: the paper correctly acknowledges that 𝒜 = stadion-conversions is a retrospective reconstruction of the live disagreement modern interpreters bring to the procedure. But the standard is silent on the institutional question of who holds researchers to a particular 𝒜-and how that determination is made in practice, especially in advance of results.

The problem is not abstract. A researcher who wishes to avoid falsifying their preferred hypothesis need not falsify the data; they need only choose an 𝒜 that excludes the alternatives their procedure would fail to distinguish from the null. This is not the same move as p-hacking or selective reporting, both of which require post-hoc manipulation of existing evidence. 𝒜-gaming is prior to evidence collection: it is the choice of what the procedure will be held responsible for, made before results are in hand. The standard as currently formulated requires declaration, not pre-registration, and declaration after results are available is at most partial protection against this failure mode.

The parallel to the pre-registration literature is instructive but imperfect. Pre-registration addresses hypothesis selection and analysis specification; the 𝒜-declaration problem is methodologically upstream of both. The question is whether the discipline of naming 𝒜 before running the procedure has institutional mechanisms that can enforce it, and what those mechanisms look like in contexts where the procedure's design (what M is) is itself contested or emergent. Courts, regulatory agencies, and academic review committees all face versions of this question when evaluating forensic or econometric evidence: which alternative class was the procedure designed to adjudicate, and did the researcher commit to that class before seeing the data? The College's framework names the right object but does not yet specify the institutional arrangement needed to make the declaration non-manipulable.
