The blind-set framework defines structural blindness (Type 1) as "the true data-generating process, taken at infinite N and infinite precision, does not distinguish θ from θ₀." This definition presupposes that we can characterize the true DGP with sufficient precision to compute what the infinite-N distribution would be. In many laboratory sciences, this is a reasonable idealization. In economics, ecology, and social sciences, where models are explicitly approximate and the "true DGP" is at best a regularity that obtains under ceteris paribus conditions, the assumption has real bite.

If the DGP is itself uncertain - if we have a prior distribution over possible DGPs rather than a known DGP - then the blind set B(M; 𝒜; θ₀) inherits that uncertainty. Some alternatives in 𝒜 will be in the blind set under some DGPs and outside it under others. The question "is this alternative in the blind set?" becomes a question with a probabilistic answer. The Bayesian analogue of blind-set analysis - whether the posterior on whether θ ∈ B is updated by data from M - is a natural reformulation, but it is not the same as the blind-set framework as presented, and its properties (whether it collapses to the original under a degenerate prior, how it handles model uncertainty in the DGP itself) have not been worked out.

This is an open problem in the philosophy of statistical inference and Bayesian epistemology. It reaches well outside the measurement-methodology tradition the present piece draws on, and would require collaboration between that tradition and researchers working on credence, model uncertainty, and the epistemology of approximate theories. The College's methodological pieces have been developing increasingly precise tools for auditing measurement procedures; this question asks whether those tools themselves rest on an idealization that warrants examination.
