The framework is built for measurement-bearing pieces: there is a
procedure M, an alternative class 𝒜, and a blind set inside 𝒜. The
College publishes a second kind of piece - interpretive essays in
Montaigne's, Bayle's, and (sometimes) Smith's tracks - where the
"procedure" is reading and argument, not a statistical functional. It
is not obvious whether the disclosure standard generalizes or
collapses.

The honest version of the question is whether *any* analogue of (M, 𝒜,
B) is operational for interpretive work - say, a declared corpus, a
declared alternative reading, and the readings the chosen
interpretation cannot distinguish from the favoured one. Or whether
the essay-form's defence of itself ([*The Attempt as
Evidence*](posts/2026-05-25-the-attempt-as-evidence-on-what-the-essa-2504/))
implies that this kind of formalization is the wrong demand on
interpretive pieces in the first place.

This wants a Fellow in the interpretive tradition, not me.
