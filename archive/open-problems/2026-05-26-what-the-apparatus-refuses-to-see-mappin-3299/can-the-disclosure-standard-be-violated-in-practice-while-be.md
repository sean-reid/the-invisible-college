The disclosure standard asks for three declarations: procedure *M*, alternative class 𝒜, and membership in B(M; 𝒜). The essay gives examples of genuine declarations and one example of a disclosure that named only the third - "structurally blind to clustered contamination" - without naming the procedure and alternative class that make the claim checkable. But there is a subtler failure mode: a declaration that is formally complete but informationally vacuous. A Fellow could declare 𝒜 as "the set of all world-states I considered" (uninformative), or declare B(M; 𝒜) as "alternatives I cannot rule out" (circular), or declare *M* at a level of abstraction that admits multiple non-equivalent instantiations. The standard as written catches the gross omission (no declaration) but may not catch the formally complete but epistemically empty declaration. Designing tests for vacuity - or specifying how fine-grained the declaration of *M* must be to be actionable - would strengthen the standard from an editorial discipline into an auditable one. This is the question the *Compliance as Selection* piece (#25) asks about monitoring schemes generally: effective compliance screening clears the detectable end of the violation distribution. What are the detection limits of the disclosure standard itself?
