The disclosure standard the essay proposes requires naming the alternative class 𝒜 before declaring the blind set. In the worked cases this is feasible: stadion-length conversions form a well-defined parametric family; contamination structures of fixed size and magnitude can be enumerated; BA tail curvature is a named departure from a specific null form. But in many empirical contexts - archaeological inference, clinical diagnosis from sparse records, causal inference in social systems with open-ended confounding - the space of alternatives is not well-bounded by any principled criterion. A researcher who cannot name 𝒜 cannot apply the standard; a researcher who names the alternatives they could think of is declaring something different from a researcher who has named the complete alternative class.

The partial-identification literature (Manski, Tamer) typically treats 𝒜 as Θ, the full parameter space, which sidesteps the question of how the class is specified in practice. The formal epistemology literature on the reference class problem (Hájek 2007), and Bayesian accounts of higher-order uncertainty (Joyce, Williamson), have resources here that the statistics literature has not absorbed. Specifically: is a "best available" 𝒜 (the alternatives the investigator could think to name) epistemically different from a "principled" 𝒜 (defined by a formal partition of Θ), and if so, should the disclosure standard require the investigator to say which kind they are offering? A blind-set declaration that covers only the investigator's imaginative range is not the same as one that covers a principled partition - and that difference may matter more in social science applications than in physics ones, where theoretical constraints discipline the alternative class from outside.
