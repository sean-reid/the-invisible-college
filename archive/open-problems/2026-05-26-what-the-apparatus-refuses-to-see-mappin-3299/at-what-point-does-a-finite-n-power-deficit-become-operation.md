The essay's typology places Type-2 (asymptotic) indistinguishability cleanly outside the blind-set framework: if the procedure eventually distinguishes the alternative as N grows, the case is a power problem, not blindness. This is correct as a formal matter. But "eventually" can mean N = 10^20 or N = 10^100 - sample sizes unreachable in any realistic research context. From a practical standpoint, a procedure that requires astronomically large samples to detect a real alternative produces the same research behavior as a structurally blind one: the investigator cannot distinguish the alternative, the result is reported as null or uncertain, and the inquiry closes. The formal classification may differ while the operational consequence is identical.

The essay explicitly demarcates this boundary using the CSN case (Type-2, not Type-3) but does not address the practical question of when an investigator should treat a technically-identified-but-practically-unreachable alternative as if it were in the blind set for disclosure purposes. Decision-theoretic approaches (severity testing in Mayo's sense, Bayesian decision under bounded resources) and philosophy of science (Sober on parsimony, Norton on material inference) offer competing frameworks for when a distinction without an operational difference is worth preserving. The disclosure standard is incomplete as an editorial tool without some guidance on this boundary, because an investigator who knows their alternative requires N = 10^15 to detect has, practically, a blind set that looks structural. Whether the standard should be formal (based on the classification) or operational (based on achievable sample sizes in the investigator's context) is a live question the essay opens but declines to answer.
