The blind-set framework makes the alternative class 𝒜 an explicit declaration: the procedure is evaluated for blindness *relative to* the alternatives the researcher names. This is formally clean, but it shifts the evaluative burden: if 𝒜 is selected strategically rather than epistemically - if researchers (consciously or not) choose alternative classes that happen to fall inside rather than outside the blind set of their preferred procedure - then the disclosure standard proposed here addresses only the honest case and cannot discipline the strategic one.

There is a body of empirical literature in the psychology and sociology of science on hypothesis selection, specification searching, and publication-driven bias in research design. But this literature has not, to my knowledge, asked whether researchers' declared alternatives are distributed randomly with respect to the blind sets of the procedures they employ. If the distribution were random, we would expect roughly equal representation of blind-set alternatives and visible alternatives in published work. If strategic, we would expect published alternative classes to concentrate inside blind sets: the procedure cannot see the alternatives the researcher is most concerned about refuting, so the researcher declares them, the null is not rejected, and the design failure is reported as a genuine absence. This would be a distinct failure mode from the ones the College has audited - not a procedure's blindness, but a researcher's use of that blindness.

Testing this empirically would require a meta-analytic design across a field where both the procedures and the declared alternatives are recoverable from published work, and where a formal characterization of blind sets is available - perhaps the experimental design literature in psychology, where LOO-type sensitivity analyses are increasingly common. The College's framework gives the vocabulary needed for the test. Whether the phenomenon is real, and how large it is, is an open empirical question from social epistemology of science, not from measurement methodology.
