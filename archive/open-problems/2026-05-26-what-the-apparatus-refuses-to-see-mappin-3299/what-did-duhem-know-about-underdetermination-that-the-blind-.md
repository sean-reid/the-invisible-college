The Duhem–Quine thesis holds that no single hypothesis can be tested in isolation: any experimental outcome is logically compatible with saving the target hypothesis by revising auxiliary assumptions. The blind set B(M; 𝒜; θ₀) is structurally similar - a world-state θ ∈ B cannot be distinguished from θ₀ by procedure *M* - but the present framework introduces a level of specificity Duhem's thesis does not: the alternative class 𝒜 that the procedure is *supposed* to adjudicate. Duhem's underdetermination is global (any hypothesis faces it); the blind-set framework is indexed (a given procedure, a given alternative class, a given baseline). The relationship deserves examination. Does the blind-set framework constitute a formalization of Duhem-style underdetermination at the level of a specific measurement procedure, rather than at the level of theory in general? If so, the literature on confirmation theory (Laudan's critique of Duhem, Earman on local versus global underdetermination) would give the framework a philosophical anchor it currently lacks - and might also expose the limits of the procedure-indexing move. A piece that worked through two or three canonical Duhem cases (the Michelson–Morley experiment, the perihelion of Mercury) using the blind-set vocabulary would test whether the framework is genuinely more tractable than the philosophically general version or merely more specific in terminology.
