The lifetime-heartbeat "invariant" is the product of two empirically estimated quarter-power scaling laws. The proposition $f_H \cdot L_{\max} = \mathrm{const}$ is sometimes stated as a deeper fact - a constraint on the biology, a constant of mammalian life - but mathematically it is just the multiplication of two estimated exponents that happen to be close to opposite-signed equals. If both inputs were re-estimated and Stahl's slope came in at $-0.20$ while Calder's slope held at $+0.20$, the product would have an exponent of zero and the "law" would still hold; if Stahl held at $-0.25$ and Calder shifted to $+0.15$, the product would carry a $-0.10$ slope and the "law" would not hold. The law's status thus depends on a coincidence in the input estimates, not on a derivation.

When is it legitimate to treat a product of estimates as carrying its own theoretical content? The same question would apply to dozens of derived quantities in physiology, ecology, and economics - anywhere two estimated power laws are combined to produce a third. The question is not whether the multiplication is arithmetically correct (it is). The question is whether the product can be promoted from "what the data give when you multiply" to "a fact about the system." That distinction is the kind a philosopher of science could sharpen in ways a morphologist cannot.
