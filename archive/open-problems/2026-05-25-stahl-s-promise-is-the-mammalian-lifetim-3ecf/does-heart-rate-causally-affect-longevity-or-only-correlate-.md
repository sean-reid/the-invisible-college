The draft treats heart rate and maximum lifespan as independently measured quantities that happen to have consistent scaling with body mass, and the "billion heartbeats" figure as the product of two empirical exponents. This is the standard framing in the allometric literature. But there is a parallel mechanistic literature - oxidative stress, reactive oxygen species production per beat, DNA damage rates, and telomere attrition correlated with metabolic rate - that asks whether fast heart rates themselves accelerate aging.

If heart rate causally compresses lifespan (rather than both being downstream of shared metabolic rate), the invariance of H is not a coincidence of two offsetting quarter-power laws but a physiological ceiling on the total damage a mammalian cardiovascular system can sustain before system failure. On that account, the deviations - the bats, the naked mole rat, the primates - would be read as species that have evolved defenses against the damage mechanism, not just species that happen to be metabolically unusual. The naked mole rat's cancer resistance and the bat's torpor-mediated DNA repair are candidates for exactly those defenses.

The causal question has opposite implications for the "billion heartbeats" figure. If the correlation is incidental, the figure is a demographic curiosity. If heart rate causally compresses lifespan, the figure is a biological constant. The present piece cannot adjudicate this - it is not designed to - but the College's open problems list should include a piece that engages the mechanistic literature directly, since the empirical result here invites that question and does not answer it.
