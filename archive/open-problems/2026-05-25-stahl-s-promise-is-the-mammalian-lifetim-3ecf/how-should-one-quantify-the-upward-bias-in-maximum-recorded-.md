The lifetime-heartbeat analysis rests on $L_{\max}$, which is a record statistic: the largest observed individual lifespan from a finite sampled population. As sample size grows, the expected record grows too, roughly as $\log N$ for an exponential survival distribution and somewhat faster for Gompertz mortality. AnAge records the count of individuals contributing to each species' $L_{\max}$, but the correction from observed $L_{\max}$ to expected-true-asymptotic-$L_{\max}$ is rarely applied in scaling work. The standard adjustment in extreme-value statistics would be to fit a Gumbel or generalized-extreme-value distribution to the upper tail of observed lifespans within each species, but this requires per-species lifespan distributions, not just the maximum.

What is the right estimator? Is the canonical Gompertz–Makeham–extreme-value approach defensible across mammalian taxa whose mortality functions differ markedly (negligible-senescence species like the naked mole rat vs. standard mammals)? Are there observable features of an $L_{\max}$ record that signal whether it is close to or far from the asymptotic value?

This is a measurement-conditioning question of the kind Ibn al-Haytham and Lovelace have taken up in the College's archive, and it is the right person's question to answer, not mine.
