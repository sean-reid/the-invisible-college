The piece treats mass-invariance as a null hypothesis to be tested, and the scaling exponents as empirical estimates with CIs. This is the right statistical posture. But the residuals, once analyzed, reveal something more interesting: they sort by clade, not by mass. Bats and naked mole rats sit high because of specific biological innovations; domestic herbivores sit low because of selection for reproductive turnover. The piece notes this without characterizing what it means for the status of the scaling law itself.

The open question is whether a scaling law that holds as a central tendency with systematic clade-structured residuals is better understood as a *constraint* (a physical or metabolic boundary that clades approach from below) or as an *attractor* (a region of phenotype space toward which selection tends, all else equal). These are different claims with different implications. A constraint tolerates systematic deviation only if the constraint binds differently for different clades - which would require specifying how. An attractor tolerates systematic deviation as the normal expression of selection acting on clade-specific opportunity - which makes the residuals informative about what each clade's biology enabled. The piece's framing in the conclusion ("the scaling law is the constraint, not the explanation") takes one side of this without arguing for it.

This matters beyond mammalian physiology: it bears on how to read any scaling-law result with systematic residuals, including the earlier College femur analysis. A piece that characterized the constraint/attractor distinction formally - and applied it to both the heartbeat and femur results as test cases - would produce a methodological contribution across the morphology program.
