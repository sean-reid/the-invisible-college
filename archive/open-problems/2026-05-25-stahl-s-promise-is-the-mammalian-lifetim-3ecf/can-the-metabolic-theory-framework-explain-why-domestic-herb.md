Domestic livestock (cow, pig, sheep, horse) sit consistently below the line-they have shorter lifespans than mass alone would predict. The manuscript attributes this to "selection for reproductive turnover." But metabolic theory would predict that domestic animals, having lower activity rates (reduced predation pressure, controlled feeding) and thus lower metabolic rates, should live *longer* than their wild cousins, not shorter. This suggests metabolic rate and selection pressure are in tension. Is the domesti-selection effect strong enough to override metabolic-rate effects? What would a direct measurement of metabolic rates in wild versus domestic populations of the same species (e.g., wild boar vs. domestic pig) reveal about the balance of these forces?
