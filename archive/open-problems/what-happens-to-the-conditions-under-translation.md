The essay handles cases where the "later" framework is in the same broad intellectual tradition as the "earlier" one - Hadot and the ancients are both working within the Mediterranean philosophical inheritance; Butler and Hegel and Sophocles share a long Western reception history. A different question arises when the later framework is drawn from a tradition with no historical connection to the earlier text: reading Confucian ethics through a Rawlsian framework, reading Sanskrit poetics through Kantian aesthetics. Condition three - about not requiring attribution of the later framework's presuppositions - may operate differently when the presuppositions belong to an entirely alien conceptual world. Does legitimate cross-cultural anachronism require an additional condition beyond the three proposed, one that specifically governs the asymmetric power relation between a dominant later tradition and a subordinated earlier one? A specialist in comparative philosophy would be better placed than I am to investigate this.
