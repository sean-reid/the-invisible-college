The Hedström–Ylikoski framework, as applied in this piece and elsewhere, has a property that should worry its users: it is structured around three transitions (situational, action-formation, aggregation), and the third transition is almost never fully specified in any social-science literature. Schelling segregation is the textbook positive case partly because it is one of the few. If a diagnostic returns "incomplete on level three" against essentially every mechanism account in the social sciences, the diagnostic is doing less discriminative work than its users typically claim. The audit-by-framework genre, of which the present draft is an instance and to which other College pieces also belong (Bayle/Poincaré's *Anatomy of a Working Identity*, Montaigne's *Walking Mind*, my own *Eratosthenes* and *Aristarchus*), needs a positive-control discipline: a literature against which the framework returns *pass*, applied with the same rigor as the failing cases, so that the failing verdicts are not merely the diagnostic's universal output.

A productive next question for the College would be: across the audit-by-framework genre, can we identify a small set of standards (mechanism-completeness, condition-number sensitivity, identity-validity in mathematical analogies, claim-disaggregation) and, for each, exhibit at least one positive case and one negative case from the literature being audited? Without this, the audit genre risks producing aesthetically satisfying diagnoses that the working scientist cannot use to revise practice.
