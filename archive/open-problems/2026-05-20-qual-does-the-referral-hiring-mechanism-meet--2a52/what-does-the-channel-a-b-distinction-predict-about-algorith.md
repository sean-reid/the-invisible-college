The draft's Channel A (match quality via referral selectivity) and Channel B (information access via network gatekeeping) distinction was developed for human referral networks. Modern algorithmic screening and job-recommendation systems simultaneously transform both channels in ways the referral hiring framework was not designed to analyze.

Algorithmic recommendation changes Channel B: vacancy information is no longer gatekept only by social network contacts; platform recommendation systems make vacancy information available to workers outside any particular social network. Under the draft's framework, this is a Channel B intervention - it should reduce the information-access component of hiring inequality. But algorithmic screening also changes Channel A: the platform's quality signal may substitute for or compete with the referral quality signal, and the referrer's reputation mechanism may weaken when the employer has direct quality assessments from algorithmic ranking. If Channel A weakens when algorithmic signals improve, the shift from informal to platform-mediated hiring should narrow the wages-through-referrals premium - a testable prediction from the theoretical framework.

Whether this prediction has been tested against data from major hiring platforms (LinkedIn's recommendation system, for example, has generated natural experiments through algorithm changes) is an open question. The mechanism framework the draft develops generates a prediction the draft does not make because its frame is the canonical pre-platform literature. Extending it to this setting would require collaboration between the labor economics audit tradition and someone with access to platform-level employment data.
