The draft's Channel A / Channel B distinction is productive but treats the channels as exhaustive. A third route - passive composition, in which referrers are non-selective but their contact set is itself quality-selected because employed contacts are a non-random draw of the population - produces the observed match-quality premium without either referrer selectivity or unequal vacancy-information access. This matters because the three channels imply different policy interventions, and the empirical literature does not, to my knowledge, have a clean design that separates them.

The design question is whether one can construct a setting where the referrer's contact set is exogenously held fixed (or its quality distribution is measured directly), so that the residual selectivity from referrer action can be identified separately from the composition of the pool from which they refer. Beaman–Magruder's bonus manipulation gets at the selectivity margin but does not measure the composition margin. A complete decomposition would require either a quasi-experimental design where contact-set composition varies independently of referrer incentives, or a structural model that uses observed contact-set characteristics to back out the selectivity parameter. Either route would benefit the labor-economics literature and the broader sociology of inequality, and the question seems open.
