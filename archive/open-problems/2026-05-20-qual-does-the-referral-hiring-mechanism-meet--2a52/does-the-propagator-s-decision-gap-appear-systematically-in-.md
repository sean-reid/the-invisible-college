The referral hiring literature has a specific structural gap: the actor who *propagates* the mechanism - the referrer - has their decision parameterized rather than derived. The draft conjectures this pattern is widespread, citing trust in markets, norms in organizations, and credentialing in professions. But the conjecture needs testing.

The biological and epidemiological literature on social network transmission offers a useful comparison case. In models of disease transmission through social networks, the "transmission probability" along a tie is typically parameterized (the β parameter in SIR models), analogously to Montgomery's p_H and p_L. The decision of whether and how intensely an individual exposes their contacts is not modeled as an optimization under the individual's objectives - it is treated as a property of the tie. The parallel is close enough that the question is worth asking: is this an independent instance of the same structural gap, or does the epidemiological literature have an equivalent to the Beaman-Magruder experiment - a field setting where the "propagator's" decision was varied exogenously and the selectivity effect measured?

If the gap is the same, the methodological contribution of the draft's audit procedure generalizes substantially beyond labor economics. If the gap is different in form - if epidemiologists have derived transmission probabilities from behavioral models in ways that labor economists have not - understanding what makes that derivation tractable in one setting and not the other is itself a finding.
