The essay argues that Aristarchus may have been producing a geometric machine for converting an empirical input - the true angular elongation at dichotomy - into an output that would follow if the input could be obtained, without claiming to have measured the input. Berggren and Sidoli (2007) favor this reading, and the essay argues that the condition-number diagnostic sharpens it: a thinker who understood the procedure well would have recognized that the true angle was not measurable by his century's instruments, and would have stipulated a worked example rather than reporting a measurement.

If this pattern is genuine - geometric machinery produced separately from, and as a precondition for, empirical determination of inputs - it raises a deeper question about the epistemological structure of Hellenistic mathematical science as a genre. The *Sand Reckoner* of Archimedes stipulates the angular diameter of the Sun (roughly 1/200 of a radian) with an explicit note that the actual value has not been precisely determined, then proceeds with the formal calculation anyway. Eratosthenes's procedure is structurally different: it reports specific measured inputs and a specific output. The contrast between these two cases looks, from the condition-number frame, like a contrast between a thinker who recognized his inputs were uncertain and bracketed them (Archimedes, Aristarchus) and a thinker whose inputs were tractable enough to measure (Eratosthenes, whose procedure has condition number one).

Whether Hellenistic mathematicians had an explicit awareness of this distinction - between formal tractability and empirical tractability - is a question the manuscript tradition might or might not support. O. Neugebauer's *A History of Ancient Mathematical Astronomy* and Reviel Netz's work on the *Archimedes Codex* and the cultural practice of Hellenistic mathematics both bear on it, but neither (to my knowledge) asks the question using the conditioning frame. A piece that worked through the relevant texts - Aristarchus, Archimedes's *Sand Reckoner*, Hipparchus on precession - asking in each case whether the author was separating geometric machinery from empirical determination, and whether there is a pattern that reveals something about Hellenistic mathematical epistemology, would sit at the intersection of history of mathematics and philosophy of science in a way that the College has not yet attempted.
