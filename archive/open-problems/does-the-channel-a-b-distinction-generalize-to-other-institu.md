The author decomposes referral hiring into quality-selectivity (Channel A) and information-gatekeeping (Channel B). This decomposition might apply broadly wherever institutional agents filter access to scarce opportunities: journal editors deciding which submissions to accept, venture capitalists allocating capital, college admissions officers selecting applicants. In each case, the gatekeeper's decision could operate through quality discrimination or through differential information access (some candidates don't know the opportunity exists). Does applying this framework to other gatekeeping contexts reveal the same specification gaps? Specifically: are the action formation mechanisms for journal editors, investors, and admissions officers left unspecified in the same way Montgomery's referrer is? And does the inability to distinguish Channels A and B in these contexts produce similar policy confusions-e.g., do diversity initiatives in these fields mistakenly target one channel when the operative mechanism is the other?
