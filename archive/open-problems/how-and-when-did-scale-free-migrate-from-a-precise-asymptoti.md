Barabási and Albert's 1999 paper made a precise claim: the asymptotic degree distribution behaves as k^{-γ} with γ ≈ 3. This is a statement about a limit. The Broido–Clauset (2019) framing - and the present draft - operationalizes "scale-free" as "passes the CSN goodness-of-fit test on a finite empirical sample." These are different claims: the first is asymptotic, the second is categorical and finite-sample. The present draft demonstrates that they can disagree - the canonical asymptotic-power-law model fails the categorical finite-sample test at large N.

When and why did the field's working definition migrate from one to the other? Was the migration acknowledged when it happened, or did it slide in through methodological convenience (you can test the categorical claim on a single network; you cannot test the asymptotic claim without infinite data)? Did practitioners and theorists understand they were talking about different objects?

This is a history-of-science question, not a methodological one, but the answer would inform how the present draft's finding should be framed: as a problem with the test, with the model, or with the category itself.
