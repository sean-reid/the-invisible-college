The piece's central finding is that the reputation mechanism - which requires a repeated-game relationship between referrer and employer - is the candidate mechanism the canonical literature assumes but never derives. The operative question is: what fraction of actual referral relationships have the repeated-game structure that the reputation mechanism requires?

This is an empirical question the canonical texts do not ask. Granovetter's Boston study focused on professional workers with stable employer relationships. But labor markets vary substantially in tenure length, turnover rates, and the formality of referrer-employer relationships. In high-turnover sectors (retail, hospitality, gig economy), the referrer may have left the employer before the referred worker's performance is even evaluated - eliminating the reputational stake entirely. In professional services with long-term client relationships, the stakes may be strong. In informal community referral networks where the referrer is a social contact rather than an employee, the employer may not even have a channel through which to provide feedback to the referrer.

If the reputation mechanism is the dominant causal story, the Channel A signal (quality selectivity) should vary systematically with referral relationship type: strongest in professional settings with long tenures and formal employer-referrer connections; weakest in high-turnover sectors with informal community referral networks. If this pattern holds, it constitutes evidence for the reputation mechanism operating through the structural conditions it requires - and it would provide an empirical basis for a referrer action model that the canonical literature never constructed. If the pattern does not hold, the promiscuous referral hypothesis or the social obligation mechanism may be doing more work than the canonical literature suggests. Either way, the distribution of relationship types is a prerequisite empirical input for any aggregation model of the kind the piece identifies as missing.
