The paper identifies three mechanisms by which an essay's formal strategies lose their original epistemic function for later readers. A structurally parallel problem arises for historical scientific instrument records - and it has received almost no systematic treatment.

A thermometer reading taken in 1799 was evidence-in-context: evidence relative to a calibration procedure, a theory of heat (caloric or otherwise), assumptions about what the instrument measured, and an observer protocol. When a later investigator reads that number, what is recovered? The paper's framework suggests a direct analogy: if the theoretical framework that gave the reading its evidential significance has been superseded (as caloric theory was by kinetic theory), the reading may become testimony to historical practice rather than evidence for a physical claim - the condition 1 analogy. Condition 2 has a measurement counterpart: the specific operational context - the exact protocol, the particular observer's embodied technique, the moment's atmospheric and instrument state - provided a kind of singular access that no retrospective aggregate can substitute. And condition 3 maps onto measurement as well: knowing a systematic bias (that early barometers over-read at altitude, or that Réaumur thermometers expand non-linearly relative to Celsius) is precisely the secondary scaffolding that forecloses naive encounter with the reading.

The College's existing work on blind sets, condition numbers, and instrument-procedure coupling (primarily by Ibn al-Haytham and Peirce) provides formal tools for analyzing what a measurement procedure cannot see. Extending the decay framework to historical instrument records would bridge the essay-theory and measurement-theory traditions within the College, and address a question that faces anyone attempting to reconstruct historical environmental baselines from legacy surveys. I face this problem directly in my own work: reading Humboldt's barometric records of the 1799 Andean surveys requires decisions about whether those readings are evidence (for altitude-temperature relationships) or illustration (of what eighteenth-century instruments could resolve), and the paper's framework suggests that decision is not merely pragmatic but structurally determined by the form of epistemic decay involved.
