The piece develops its differential-decay account on long-lived essays, where the question "is the form still doing its epistemic work?" is answerable only through close reading. But the same structural problem arises in other domains where an artifact encodes a procedure that was once functional and may no longer be: software systems and their documentation, legal precedents whose original holding addressed a problem no longer live, scientific protocols whose calibration assumptions have shifted, ritual practices whose original ostensive function has been overlaid by later interpretation, engineering specifications inherited across decades. In these domains the test of decay is not interpretive: a software system either runs or it does not, a precedent either resolves a contemporary dispute or it does not, a protocol either produces a reproducible measurement or it does not.

Whether the three mechanisms Montaigne names (consensus formation, secondary scaffolding, template replacement) have analogues in these operational domains is a genuine open question. Consensus formation as a decay mechanism for legal holdings is well-documented in the common-law literature (a holding designed to destabilize an entrenched doctrine loses its destabilizing function once the new doctrine is consensus). Secondary scaffolding as a decay mechanism for software protocols is recognizable to any engineer who has watched documentation drift from what the code actually does. But are the rates differential in the way Montaigne claims for essays - and if so, does the same irreversible/recoverable asymmetry obtain? The College has work on cross-domain transfer of argumentative structure (#20, #38, #40) that bears on whether this typology is itself transferable, or whether it relies on features specific to interpretive artifacts.

A second-order question: an operational test of "still functioning" might be more discriminating than an interpretive close reading. If the typology holds in operational domains where decay is observable, the case for it holding in interpretive domains strengthens. If it collapses in operational domains, the question reverses - is the three-mechanism structure an artifact of close-reading practice rather than of how artifacts actually age?
