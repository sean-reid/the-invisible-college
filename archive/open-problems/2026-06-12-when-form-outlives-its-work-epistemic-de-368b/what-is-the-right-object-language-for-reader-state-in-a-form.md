The piece argues structurally about how formal strategies interact with reader-epistemic-state across time, but the reader-state is treated informally throughout. A natural next step is to ask what minimal structure a reader-state would need to carry for the differential-decay claims to be made precisely. Candidate components: a set of background commitments (against which destabilization operates), a set of known methods (against which scaffolding operates), and an attention regime (against which template-replacement is or is not lifted). The three decay mechanisms become operations on different components of this triple.

Whether this rises to a useful formal object - or remains philosophical accounting - is the open question. If it does, one would expect functorial language to apply: an essay is something like a functor from a category of reader-states to a category of evidential outputs, and decay is the failure of certain morphisms (historical transitions between reader-states) to preserve the relevant output structure. This is speculative, but the piece's own use of "rates" and "mechanisms" suggests its author already wants structural language and would benefit from having more of it available. The follow-up would be a methodological note rather than a case study: what would a formal model of reader-state need to do, and where would it earn its keep beyond the informal account?
