The essay develops its mechanisms entirely along the temporal axis: a formal strategy that was epistemically functional at composition may cease to be so for a later reader because the shared conceptual environment has shifted (environmental decay) or because the individual reader's prior configuration has changed (reader-state decay). Both cases are framed as diachronic - the reading culture now versus then, the reader's preparation now versus at the designed-for moment of first encounter.

But there is a structurally parallel question about synchronic cultural distance that the essay does not address and that its taxonomy might illuminate in a different direction. A reader from a tradition in which the category of "barbarism" was constructed differently - or never constructed in the European framework's terms - encounters Montaigne's "Des Cannibales" in a different epistemic situation from a twenty-first-century Western reader, not because a shared framework has been superseded by consensus but because the relevant framework was never part of their conceptual inheritance. Is this condition 1 decay, or something else? The framework cannot be destabilized if it was never held, but the reason for its absence is synchronic (different cultural formation) rather than diachronic (consensus formation across a shared culture). Whether these are the same mechanism operating through different causes or structurally distinct mechanisms with different recovery profiles is not obvious.

A similar question applies to condition 2. If phenomenological singularity depends on the reader having contextual preparation that allows them to recognize the specific coordinates of the testimony - this father, this diner, this riot - rather than just registering it as foreign, then a reader who lacks that contextual preparation is in a different epistemic situation from one who holds the template-replacement failure the essay describes. Template replacement is a form of over-preparation that redirects reception toward recognition; contextual unfamiliarity is a form of under-preparation that may prevent access to the singular phenomenological claim in a different way. Are these symmetric failure modes, or does one of them foreclose access more fundamentally?

The spatial analog from my own field is perhaps useful as a pointer. Isotherms describe temperature contours that organize biological communities across both a spatial axis (latitude, altitude) and a temporal axis (seasonal, geological). The same mathematical object - the isotherm - applies in both directions, but the mechanisms by which organisms track temperature boundaries differ between the spatial and temporal cases, and the same boundary may be more or less permeable depending on which axis you are moving along. Whether the essay's decay mechanisms have the same structure-preserving dual-axis applicability - where the same mechanism (consensus formation, template replacement, secondary scaffolding) operates equivalently across temporal and cultural distance - or whether the cross-cultural case introduces genuinely new mechanisms, seems like a question the College is in a position to pursue.
