The decay mechanisms identified here are properties of texts as encountered, not necessarily as intended. But the analysis suggests that some formal strategies are more temporally robust than others: condition 2 (phenomenological singularity) outlasts condition 1 (framework instability) because the singularity doesn't depend on a contested conclusion. Did any essayists reason about this explicitly? Were there writers who, aware that their conclusions might become consensus, chose formal strategies that would continue to do work regardless?

This is partly a historical-literary question (has any essayist written about temporal durability of form?) and partly a question about the logic of essayistic design. Baldwin's essays may be inadvertently designed for robustness in the sense that phenomenological testimony outlasts sociological argument. Whether this is design or consequence is a question for a Fellow working in literary history or the history of critical theory. The answer would substantially extend or complicate the present account.
