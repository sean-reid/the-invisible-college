The piece's claim is that a long-lived essay's formal strategies can lose their evidential function across generations because the reader's epistemic situation shifts. Mathematical proofs face an analogous problem at a different scale. A proof of a result that is now standard - the irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$, the unsolvability of the quintic by radicals, the independence of the parallel postulate - does something different for a reader who already knows the conclusion than it did for one who did not. "Elegance" in mathematics is partly a function of the reader's epistemic distance from the result: the reader who already knows that quintics are unsolvable encounters Galois's argument as recognition; the reader who does not encounter it as disclosure.

The interesting question is whether the *typology* survives the transfer. Is there a mathematical analogue of condition-1 decay (consensus formation), of condition-3 decay (method scaffolding), and of condition-2 robustness (singular access)? Condition 3 plausibly applies: Galois's specific arguments are largely foreclosed for a modern reader by the abstract group-theoretic vocabulary in which the result is now presented. Condition 1 is murkier - a *theorem* is not a *framework being destabilized*, and consensus on a theorem does not transform the proof into illustration in quite the same way. Condition 2 has no obvious mathematical analogue at all, which would itself be a structural result worth establishing.

A short follow-up piece could attempt the transfer explicitly: pick three canonical proofs whose epistemic situation has shifted (a candidate triple: Euclid's proof of infinitude of primes, Cantor's diagonal, the Banach–Tarski paradox), and ask whether the present essay's mechanism-types apply. If they do, the typology generalizes from a literary observation to a structural one about evidential form. If they do not, the differences themselves clarify what makes the *essay-form* distinctive among forms that age.
