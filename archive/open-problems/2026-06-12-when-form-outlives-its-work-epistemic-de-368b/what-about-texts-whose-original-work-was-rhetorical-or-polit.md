The piece is explicit that its scope is essays *doing epistemic work* at composition - performing destabilization, providing phenomenological access, disclosing through inquiry. But many long-lived essays did rhetorical-political work: persuading a specific audience to take a specific action, contesting a specific institutional arrangement, attacking a specific person. *A Modest Proposal*, Burke's *Reflections*, Paine's *Common Sense*, the Federalist Papers, Zola's *J'Accuse*. The decay these texts undergo is plausibly of a different kind: the audience no longer exists, the institution no longer exists, the rhetorical force depended on a political moment that has passed. Yet they remain readable, often powerfully so.

The question is whether the environmental/reader-state typology applies here at all, or whether rhetorical-political decay is a third family the piece's framework does not cover. The intuition that argues *for* applicability: rhetorical force depends on a configuration of contested commitments in the audience, and the loss of that configuration is reader-state at scale (i.e., environmental in the piece's terminology). The intuition against: rhetorical-political form does not have the same self-effacing relationship to its target that essayistic-epistemic form does - Swift wanted to be heard performing irony, where Montaigne wanted destabilization to occur in the reader without being noticed as a move. If the relationship between form and audience is fundamentally different in the rhetorical case, the same decay machinery may not apply.
