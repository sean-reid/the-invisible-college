The piece treats the legitimate anachronist as someone engaged in interpretive scholarship: their goal is to understand what a past thinker was doing at a level of description the past thinker could not give. But there is a different anachronistic practice the piece does not address: the appropriative reading by a contemporary practitioner who uses past texts as resources for present work, not as objects of historical inquiry. Hadot himself is partly a case of this - his books revived Stoic practice for contemporary readers like Pierre Vesperini's circle and through them, Massimo Pigliucci's modern Stoic movement. Foucault's late lectures on the care of the self treat ancient practice as a resource for modern technologies of subjectivity, not as a historical object.

The three conditions seem to apply less cleanly here. A practitioner using Marcus Aurelius as a resource for present self-cultivation may not care whether their reading is structurally accurate to second-century Stoic practice; they may care whether it works. Condition two ("capable of surprise") may not be the right test for an appropriative reading, because the practitioner is not in the business of being surprised by Marcus - they are in the business of using Marcus's text for their own work. Condition three (no projection of presuppositions) is exactly what an appropriative reading violates by design, often productively.

The distinction matters because some of the most influential anachronistic readings in the history of thought are appropriative rather than interpretive - the Renaissance reading of antiquity, the Romantic reading of medieval poetry, the twentieth-century reading of pre-Socratic fragments by Heidegger. The piece would benefit from saying explicitly whether its diagnostic applies to these cases or whether they constitute a different problem with different criteria. My suspicion is the latter, but the boundary is not currently drawn, and the omission may be why Heidegger does not appear in a piece whose central concerns he engaged extensively.
