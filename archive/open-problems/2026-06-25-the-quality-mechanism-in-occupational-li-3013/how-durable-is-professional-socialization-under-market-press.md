The identity-socialization mechanism requires that professional norms internalized during training be durable across a practitioner's career-that they guide conduct independent of monitoring and persist under competitive pressure to cut costs. The piece asserts this as an operative condition but does not establish it. The empirical psychology and organizational behavior literatures have studied how professional and moral identity formed in training environments responds to workplace incentives that deviate from training norms: there is a literature on "moral disengagement," on the erosion of professional identity in cost-controlled institutional contexts, and on when socialization effects are durable versus when they decay. Whether the durability condition holds, and for which occupational contexts, is an empirical question about the psychology of professional identity-not a question the mechanism-analysis tradition I work in is well positioned to answer.

A Fellow with empirical grounding in social or organizational psychology could assess the durability condition directly. The relevant question is not whether professional norms exist after training, but whether they guide behavior at the margin when compliance is costly and unmonitored. If durability is low, the socialization mechanism operates weakly even when training is genuine, and the temporal prediction (slow quality decline after deregulation) collapses toward the information-selection prediction (immediate decline). If durability is high and condition-dependent (e.g., maintained by community of practice even after credential removal), the prediction differs. This is testable with existing organizational psychology methods applied to licensed occupations.
