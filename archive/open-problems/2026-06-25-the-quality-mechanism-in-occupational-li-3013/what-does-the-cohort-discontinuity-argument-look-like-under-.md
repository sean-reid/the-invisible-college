The piece's central structural claim - that licensing deregulation is unique among institutional mechanism changes because the credential is removed administratively while incumbents remain in place - assumes a clean binary policy event. Most actual deregulatory changes are partial: scope-of-practice expansions that let unlicensed practitioners perform a subset of tasks; interstate reciprocity that imports practitioners credentialed under foreign-state rules; certification-instead-of-licensure substitutions that retain a credential signal but remove the entry restriction; sunset clauses that grandfather existing licensees while disclaiming new enforcement. Each is structurally different, and each smears the cohort discontinuity the discrimination tests rely on across multiple practitioner types rather than separating "pre" from "post" cleanly.

The methodological question is: under what taxonomy of partial deregulation events does the temporal-signature argument still apply, and under what taxonomy does it break? A scope-of-practice expansion, for instance, may preserve the entry-screening function for the original scope while removing it at the margin; the predicted temporal signature is then mixture-dependent in a way the binary tests cannot read. Reciprocity events create a new cohort - practitioners credentialed under another state's regime - whose socialization is neither "pre-deregulation" nor "post-deregulation" in the home state's sense.

This is partly a regulatory-history question (what is the empirical distribution of partial-deregulation event types?), partly a political-economy question (which partial forms are politically more available, and why?), and partly a methodological question (when does the framework's binary discrimination structure survive partial events, and when does it require reformulation as a continuous-treatment design?). The piece's three internal predecessors - referral hiring, commons governance, and the abstract information-normative split - do not face this question because none of those institutional changes typically occurs as a single administrative event in the first place. The licensing case opens the question precisely because its events are usually cleaner; the realistic instances are messier than the piece's framing acknowledges.
