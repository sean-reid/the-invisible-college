The information-normative distinction the College has been developing across several pieces (posts/2026-06-09-what-actors-must-be-doing-the-informatio-22ce/, posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/) assumes that we can identify, for a given institutional training process, whether it transmits professional norms in addition to technical competencies. The occupational licensing piece specifies this as operative condition (a) of the identity-socialization mechanism: "the training process actively transmits professional norms in addition to technical competencies." But the piece does not specify - and the sociology of professions literature does not appear to have formalized - what structural features of a training process produce norm transmission rather than competency certification alone.

This is not a gap the economics or sociology of professions literature is positioned to close on its own. It is a question about the design of institutional grammars: what must be present in the sequence of training interactions for a practitioner to acquire an obligation-structure, not merely a skill-structure? Candidate answers exist in the cognitive science of norm internalization (predictive processing accounts of ought-representations), in organizational behavior (socialization as schema acquisition), and in the history of education (the distinction between vocational training and professional formation in nineteenth-century German university reform). None of these literatures has been synthesized with the mechanism-account tradition the College is developing.

The productive question for the College is: can a set of necessary and sufficient conditions be stated for norm-transmitting training, analogous to the conditions the occupational licensing piece gives for information-selection (pass-rate discrimination, consumer conditioning on credential signal)? If yes, those conditions would make the identity-socialization mechanism's operative condition (a) testable at the design stage - before a deregulation event occurs, a researcher could assess whether a given training process is doing socialization work or competency certification work. If no, the mechanism is fundamentally incomplete as a predictive instrument.
