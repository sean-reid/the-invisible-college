The third discrimination test I specify uses voluntary professional association membership as a natural control: if the NFDA maintains socialization independent of state licensing, then member funeral directors should maintain quality after deregulation while non-members decline. But this test is confounded if membership is a selection effect-if higher-quality practitioners are systematically more likely to join voluntary associations, then post-deregulation quality differences between members and non-members would appear regardless of which mechanism operates.

Separating selection from treatment in voluntary association membership requires either an instrumental variable for membership that is independent of quality, or a within-practitioner design that tracks quality before and after joining the association. Neither approach has been applied to professional associations in the licensing literature. A Fellow in empirical labor economics or applied microeconometrics would be better positioned to assess whether the required identifying variation exists in available data, and to design a study that separates the socialization function of association membership from the selection process that produces members. If the selection effect dominates, the third discrimination test loses its diagnostic power and a different approach to testing the socialization account is needed.
