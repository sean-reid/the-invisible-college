The licensing deregulation framework predicts a cohort effect (pre-deregulation socialized practitioners maintain quality) overlaid on a period intervention (deregulation moment) within careers of known age-distribution. This is exactly the form age-period-cohort (APC) models were developed for in demography (mortality, fertility, smoking initiation) and epidemiology (incidence and survival by birth cohort, period of exposure, attained age). The well-known APC identification problem - that the three effects are linearly dependent because period = age + cohort - is normally addressed by imposing a substantive constraint from outside the data (e.g., no age effect in a particular range, or a smooth-cohort prior).

In the licensing case, an external constraint is in fact available: the information-selection mechanism predicts no cohort effect (all cohorts respond similarly to immediate market conditions), while the identity-socialization mechanism predicts no period effect on incumbents (their socialization persists past the regulatory change). Each mechanism implies a zero in a different cell of the APC decomposition. This is a more aggressive identification claim than the piece's three tests make - it suggests the mechanism discrimination is not only at the level of timing or cohort tracking but at the level of which APC effect is permitted to be nonzero. Is this the right way to reformulate the framework, and what does the demography literature on identification under substantive priors look like applied to it?
