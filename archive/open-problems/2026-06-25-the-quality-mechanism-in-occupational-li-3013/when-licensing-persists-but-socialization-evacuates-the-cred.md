The piece analyzes deregulation - the nominal removal of the credential requirement - and asks which mechanism accounts for the subsequent quality trajectory. But the identity-socialization mechanism's operative condition (b) surfaces a structurally distinct scenario: what if the credential requirement persists while the training process gradually ceases to transmit professional norms? Under this scenario, the formal mechanism is in place - licensing board, examination, renewal - but the training pipeline has shifted from normative socialization to pure technical certification. The pieces of the credential regime are all present; only the socialization function has quietly evacuated.

This "credential shell" scenario would produce a quality trajectory that mimics the deregulation prediction under information-selection without any deregulation event. Aggregate quality declines over time as incoming cohorts complete training that certifies technical competence without forming professional identity; incumbent cohorts maintain quality for reasons unrelated to current training norms; and the aggregate quality signal exhibits the intermediate temporal signature the piece attributes to the drift variant of identity-socialization - gradual decline preceding cohort replacement. Absent a specific deregulation event, this trajectory is undetectable by the piece's three tests, which are designed around the discrete treatment created by the removal of the credential requirement.

The policy implication runs in the opposite direction from the piece's main argument. The piece shows that deregulation studies systematically underestimate the quality cost of removal by ignoring long-run socialization effects. The credential-shell problem shows that the quality function of an apparently intact licensing regime may already be compromised without any policy change, if the training pipeline has been quietly reoriented toward technical throughput. The College has developed instruments for apparatus-blindness (what a measurement system cannot see at any sample size) but not for mechanism-blindness within nominally functioning institutions (what an evaluation framework cannot see when a mechanism's formal structure persists while its operative conditions have failed). Is there a specifiable diagnostic - running parallel to the deregulation tests but designed for detection of internal mechanism decay - that would allow a researcher to determine whether a surviving credential regime still performs its declared socialization function?
