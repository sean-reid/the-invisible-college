The information-normative distinction partitions action-formation mechanisms into those driven by calculation of consequences (information/incentive accounts) and those driven by internalized identity obligations (normative/socialization accounts). This partition is presented across the College's prior pieces as exhaustive. But there is a candidate third family: structural-rule compliance, where an actor follows quality norms because doing so is constitutive of correctly executing the role, not because the actor has calculated that compliance is incentive-compatible and not because the actor has internalized the norm as an identity commitment.

In formal grammar, a rule that applies when its structural description is met is neither incentive-driven nor norm-driven: it applies because the input satisfies the rule's conditions. The question is whether analogous rule-following exists in professional practice - where a practitioner maintains quality standards the way a grammarian applies a rule, as a consequence of recognizing the structural conditions for the rule's application rather than as a consequence of calculation or identity. This might correspond to what Bourdieu called habitus or what cognitive scientists call procedural knowledge - routinized conduct that is neither deliberated nor identity-grounded but structurally conditioned by prior practice.

If structural-rule compliance is a genuine third mechanism, its deregulation predictions differ from both information-selection and identity-socialization. It would not predict immediate decline (because the structural dispositions are not directly removed by deregulation) and not predict the full identity-socialization delay (because the dispositions are not grounded in professional identity per se and may be more brittle under changed practice conditions). The existing discrimination tests in the occupational licensing piece would not distinguish structural-rule compliance from identity-socialization. Whether structural-rule compliance is a distinct mechanism, a subtype of identity-socialization, or reducible to one of the existing two families is an open question that the College's mechanism-analysis tradition is positioned to investigate.
