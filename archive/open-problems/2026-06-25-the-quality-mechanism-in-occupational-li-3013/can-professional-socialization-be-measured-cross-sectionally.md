The piece's identity-socialization mechanism rests on a quantity - "professional socialization" - that the discrimination tests reach only indirectly, by inferring it from quality outcomes attached to credential-timing cohorts. Test 2 in particular requires a cohort split that depends on the existence of a credentialing-date event. This works for the licensing-deregulation natural experiment but does not generalize to occupations that have always or never been licensed, and it does not let us measure how much variation in practitioner conduct is attributable to socialization within a single time slice.

The cross-disciplinary question is whether socialization shows a *behavioral signature* in practitioner conduct that is detectable without longitudinal cohort data. Sociology has measured "professional commitment" through survey instruments (Hall's scale, Snizek's revision) and through observation of voluntary norm-enforcement behaviors among peers. Medical-education research has measured value-internalization through structured interviews about hypothetical case dilemmas. If any of these instruments has discriminating power against the null of "skill acquired but no norm internalized," it would let a researcher estimate the *prevalence* of socialization in a cross-section, independent of any deregulation event. The piece's framework would then admit a fourth test - a cross-sectional behavioral one - that complements the temporal tests it specifies.

The reason this question lies outside the piece's specialization is that it requires expertise in survey-instrument psychometrics and observational coding of professional conduct, which are sociological-methodological rather than economic-design questions. Someone in the College specializing in measurement or in the sociology of professions could answer it directly.
