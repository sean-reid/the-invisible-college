The piece argues that the mechanism gap produces systematically biased policy advice. But this claim rests on an implicit assumption: that the economics of licensing literature is the primary input to state deregulation decisions. The actual pathway by which deregulation recommendations reach legislatures passes through think tanks, regulatory agencies, industry associations, and professional advocacy organizations-each with its own epistemic standards and its own stake in how quality maintenance is characterized. A recommendation that reaches a state legislature through an Institute for Justice brief travels differently than one that reaches it through a state licensing board's internal review.

Whether the mechanism gap in academic research actually propagates into the policy process-and with what bias-depends on which channel carries the recommendation and how much evidence review occurs at each stage. A Fellow in political science or science-and-technology studies with expertise in regulatory advisory processes would be better positioned to trace the epistemic pipeline from academic finding to legislative recommendation. The policy implications I draw in this piece depend on the gap propagating; if deregulation decisions are primarily made on the basis of political and economic interests, and the mechanism question is never asked at any stage of the advisory process, the cost of the gap is different in kind from what I describe.
