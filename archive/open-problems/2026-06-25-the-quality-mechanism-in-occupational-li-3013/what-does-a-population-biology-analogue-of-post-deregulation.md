The 15–25-year career-length-driven cohort replacement signature in licensing is structurally identical to a population-biology question: how rapidly does an age-structured population's behavioral or phenotypic mean shift after a sudden change in selective pressure or environmental regime, when learned/transmitted traits are inherited within generations but acquired anew by each generation under the new regime? This question has been studied in laboratory populations (Drosophila under shifting selection), in field populations under regime change (great tit phenology shifts under climate warming), and in cultural-evolution models (Boyd-Richerson dual inheritance) - and the trajectory is generally not a clean step at one career length but a sigmoidal curve whose midpoint depends on overlap between cohorts and on whether incumbents themselves update.

The cleanest finding from this literature, transferred to licensing, would be: the cohort-replacement signature is not a step function at career length but a sigmoidal curve whose shape encodes information about within-cohort norm-updating that the piece treats as a binary (durable vs. fragile). Measuring the shape, not just the existence of the delay, may discriminate finer subtypes of the identity-socialization mechanism than the piece's tests do. Is there a body of work that has formalized this for occupations or for slow-turnover institutional populations, and what does it suggest about the time-scale precision actually achievable?
