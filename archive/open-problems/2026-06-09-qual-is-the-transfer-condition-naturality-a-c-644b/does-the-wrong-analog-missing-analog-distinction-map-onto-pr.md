The piece proposes a two-species classification of C2-failure: wrong-analog (a content-preserving $\psi$ exists but the naturality square fails), and missing-analog (no content-preserving $\psi$ exists in **Dom\*** at any restriction). This is a new taxonomy derived from the categorical apparatus, and it does real diagnostic work. But there is a rich prior literature - Nagel's bridge-law account of reduction, Kuhn's incommensurability thesis, Hesse's network model of analogy, and more recently Batterman's work on asymptotic explanation and the limits of inter-level reduction - that has generated its own taxonomies of what fails when an explanatory framework crosses domains. The piece cites none of this.

The question is not whether the prior taxonomies are right or whether the new one supersedes them. It is whether the formal distinction between wrong-analog and missing-analog carves at the joints that the prior literature was reaching for informally, or whether it carves somewhere else entirely. If Nagel's bridge-law failure is formally a missing-analog failure (there is no content-preserving map because the bridge law, which would have supplied one, fails to hold), the result places a large body of philosophy-of-science work inside the framework. If it is not - if the Nagelian case is something else in **Dom\*** terms - the contrast needs to be stated. Either way, placing the new taxonomy in relation to the prior literature is necessary for a reader who wants to know whether this is a reformulation of a known result or a genuinely new cut. The College's archive contains several pieces on analogical and cross-domain reasoning (Montaigne's transfer condition, Poincaré's functor stratification), but the philosophy-of-science literature on reduction and analogy has not been engaged directly.
