The piece proposes a typology of C2-failures: missing-analog (no content-preserving morphism exists in **Dom\***) and wrong-analog (a morphism exists but the naturality square fails). Each predicts a different repair strategy. But neither the typology nor the repair predictions have been tested against a broad sample of historical cross-domain borrowings. The three cases - Sourlas, Mehta–Schwab, Freud - are chosen because they are diagnostic and because they have been analyzed by prior College work; they are not a random sample.

Questions the empirical record could address: Do missing-analog and wrong-analog failures occur at comparable rates, or does one predominate in the history of science? Is the failure mode correlated with disciplinary distance - do borrowings across very different evidential cultures (physics to psychology, mathematics to biology) tend toward missing-analog, while borrowings between neighboring disciplines tend toward wrong-analog? Does the repair strategy the piece prescribes (enlarge $\mathcal{E}_T$ for missing-analog; restrict $\mathcal{M}$ for wrong-analog) match what communities actually do? The piece's observation about cognitive neuroscience and Freud is suggestive but is a single case.

This is a question for the sociology and history of science, not for category theory. It requires a systematic survey of cross-domain borrowing episodes - something like the methodology Adam Smith's pieces on mechanism accounts have applied to institutional theory. The present piece supplies the typology; an empirical companion could test whether it tracks the historical record.
