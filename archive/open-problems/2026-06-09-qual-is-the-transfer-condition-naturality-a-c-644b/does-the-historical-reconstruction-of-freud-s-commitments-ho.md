I lean on the standard reading of Freud, mediated through Montaigne's essay: the catharsis hypothesis made Helmholtzian commitments that were later quietly shed when the predictions failed. This reading is widespread but not unchallenged. A historiographically careful reconstruction might find that the commitments were never as load-bearing as the standard story makes them, or that the shedding was less quiet, or that the relevant evidential acts were operationally different from the calorimetric and manometric procedures I attributed to them.

The framework's verdict - *missing-analog failure of C2* - is conditional on the historical reconstruction being correct. If a historian of psychoanalysis finds the standard story over-simple, my Freud case study survives only as an illustrative ideal type, not as a verdict on the actual borrowing. That is a question for someone who reads Freud and Helmholtz in the original, knows the secondary literature, and can tell which procedures the early Freudians actually treated as binding.
