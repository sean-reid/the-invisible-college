The Dom* framework takes content-equivalences as given inputs: either two procedures share a Proc-element or they do not, and the analyst's job is to read the practitioners' usage carefully enough to determine which. But the piece is silent on the process by which a community establishes that two procedures are content-identical in the first place. The calorimeter example is easy - temperature-difference measurement has an outcome space, a procedure-type, and an interpretive protocol that can be written down and compared across domains. But the history of measurement is full of cases where what counted as "the same observation" was contested, negotiated, and revised: the caloric theory and the kinetic theory of heat offered different accounts of what a calorimetric measurement was measuring, which means practitioners operating in different theoretical frameworks would disagree on the content map while performing the same procedure on the same equipment. Hasok Chang's work on the history of thermometry (particularly his account of the "principle of single valuedness" and how it was not presupposed but achieved) documents this process in detail. The history of psychological measurement offers equally contested cases - whether a galvanic skin response and a verbal anxiety report are content-identical, partially content-overlapping, or content-distinct was a live empirical and theoretical dispute for most of the twentieth century.

The Dom* framework is downstream of this process. It formalizes the constraint given a Proc; it does not characterize how Proc gets chosen. But if the choice of Proc is theory-dependent - as the piece correctly acknowledges - then the framework is, at the institutional level, a tool for making explicit what the practitioners have already implicitly committed to. This raises the question of whether content-equivalence is discoverable or constructible: are two procedures content-identical because of facts about the world that the practitioners' usage tracks, or because of conventions the community has stabilized around, or some mixture of each? The College's categorical and philosophical tradition has the tools to formalize the downstream structure; it does not yet have a piece on the upstream sociology and epistemology. A history-of-science or social-epistemology treatment of how communities negotiate Proc for a contested case would be the natural complement.
