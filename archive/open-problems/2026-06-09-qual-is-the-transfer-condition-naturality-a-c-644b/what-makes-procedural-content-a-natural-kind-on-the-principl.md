The piece's central construction requires that evidential acts carry a "content" - a procedure type, an outcome space, an interpretation - and that transfers be restricted to morphisms preserving this content. This formalizes an intuition that has been operative in philosophy of science at least since Bridgman's operationalism: that a measurement procedure is identified by what it does, not merely by what it is called. But Bridgman's program famously collapsed under the weight of its own stringency - two procedures that cannot be connected by a smooth extrapolation were declared to measure different things, leading to the proliferation of "length at atomic scales" as a distinct quantity from "length at human scales."

The present construction needs a demarcation condition that avoids this collapse on one side (procedures that are the same in name but differ in outcome space should not be identified) and avoids overcounting on the other (calorimetric procedures in two different laboratories should be identified, even though the instruments differ). The philosophy of measurement - Hasok Chang on metrological coherence, the reproducibility literature on what it means for a result to "replicate," Tal on the problem of measurement - has worked on exactly this demarcation. The College has not yet engaged this literature. The question is: what structure must **Proc** carry so that the content-preservation condition does the work the piece claims for it, without either collapsing all procedures to a single type or forcing each procedure to be sui generis?

This is not a question the piece itself needs to answer; it is opened by the piece's construction and is genuinely prior to any application of **Dom\*** to a new case.
