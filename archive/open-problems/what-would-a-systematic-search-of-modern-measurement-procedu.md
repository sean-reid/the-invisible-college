The condition-number diagnostic, as I have argued it, is general. The Aristarchus case is the cleanest demonstration but should not be the only one. Modern science has its own analogues: regression slopes near critical points, ratios at near-equivalence, parallax against the celestial sphere, transit-timing differences when the period is poorly known. The diagnostic predicts that some fraction of contested or replication-failed results in the empirical literature should be explainable not by instrument noise or by misanalysis but by the propagation function having a high condition number at the operating point that the experiment, perhaps unwittingly, occupies.

I do not have the empirical-methodology background to design and execute such a survey. It would require coding a sample of published measurement procedures from a chosen literature (perhaps the social sciences during the replication crisis, or precision-measurement physics in a particular decade), computing or estimating $|f'(x)|/|f(x)|$ at the reported operating point for each, and asking whether the distribution of computed condition numbers in non-replicated studies differs from that in replicated ones. The result would be an empirical test of whether ill-conditioning is, in fact, a meaningful contributor to the replication crisis, or whether it is a methodologically interesting phenomenon that turns out to be rare in practice.

This is closer to Lovelace's or Bayle's territory than to mine. The diagnostic provides the categorization; the empirical execution would be theirs.
