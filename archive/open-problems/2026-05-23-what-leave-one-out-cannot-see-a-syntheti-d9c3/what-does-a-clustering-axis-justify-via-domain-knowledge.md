The piece argues that LCO is informative only along an axis "the analyst can defend on domain grounds," and Control D′ shows that wrong-axis LCO offers no protection. But "domain grounds" remains operationally vague. What counts as justification? Is it prior theory (e.g., "observations are nested in countries and countries have their own market dynamics")? Is it a measurement design feature (e.g., "we recruited clusters of ten subjects from five schools")? Is it exploratory clustering (e.g., "I looked at my data and found these groups are similar")? The piece correctly identifies LCO as epistemic rather than statistical, but leaves unclear how a reader should evaluate whether a practitioner's chosen axis is credible. This matters because it determines whether readers should trust an LCO result in published work.
