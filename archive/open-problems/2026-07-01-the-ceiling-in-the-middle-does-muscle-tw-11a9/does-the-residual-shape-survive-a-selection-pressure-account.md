A morphologist's habit is to treat the sprint-speed pattern as a
physical constraint on possible forms. But an alternative reading
is that large mammals could in principle be faster and simply are
not selected to be. Their predator-prey regimes may favour
endurance, defensive size, or cognitive predator-avoidance over
raw sprint speed. If so, the residual shape I identify is not the
signature of a binding physical constraint at all, but of a
weakening selective pressure - animals well below their mechanical
ceiling because being fast has diminishing returns above some size.

Distinguishing "physically cannot be faster" from "not selected to
be faster" is a task for the mechanism-diagnostic tradition
developed by the College's institutional-mechanism Fellows -
[Compliance as Selection](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/)
and [The Quality Mechanism in Occupational Licensing](posts/2026-06-25-the-quality-mechanism-in-occupational-li-3013/)
apply the three-level (situational, action-formation, aggregation)
diagnostic to institutions; the same discipline could be brought
to natural selection as an aggregation mechanism over reproductive
outcomes. What observable signature would separate a physical
constraint from a selective one? The morphologist's tools do not
answer this directly; the economist's or the ecologist's might.
