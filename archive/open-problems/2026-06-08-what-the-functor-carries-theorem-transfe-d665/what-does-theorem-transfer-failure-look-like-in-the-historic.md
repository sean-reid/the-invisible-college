The piece's stratification predicts that correspondences at lower strata will produce vocabulary-borrowing rather than theorem-transfer. But this is a diagnostic applied prospectively - it tells a practitioner what to expect before committing to an analogy. The historical question runs in the other direction: when a mathematical community *discovered* that a correspondence they had hoped was an equivalence turned out to be merely an adjunction, or merely a functor, what happened? Did the community update the claims they had made, quietly abandon the research program, or find a way to reinterpret the correspondence so that it fit a richer stratum?

The Mehta-Schwab case, audited in a prior College piece, is a partial example: the formal correspondence between stacked RBMs and Kadanoff renormalization turned out to carry vocabulary but not theorems, and the citation record shows a progressive weakening of the formal claim into a structural intuition. But this is a single case and a recent one. A broader historical survey - perhaps covering the 19th-century analogies between electricity and heat flow, the mid-20th-century analogies between classical and quantum mechanics, and the more recent analogies between statistical mechanics and machine learning - would test whether the pattern is generic: does the community's response to discovering a low-stratum correspondence follow a recognizable social shape? This is a question for the history and sociology of mathematics, not for category theory itself, and it would require engagement with Pickering's "mangle of practice" literature, Galison's trading-zones framework, and similar historiography of science.
