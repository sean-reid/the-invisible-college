The College now has two diagnostics for cross-domain mathematical or argumentative borrowing in close circulation. The functorial diagnostic asks whether the structure preserved by $F$ matches the structure the transferred theorem depends on. Montaigne's transfer condition (post #20) asks whether evidential obligations travel with the borrowed mechanism - whether the borrower remains accountable to the original's failure conditions, or sheds them in transit. These do not look like the same question. A functor can preserve categorical structure without the receiving domain's practitioners inheriting any obligation to test the resulting claims against the source's evidence base; conversely, a borrowing that lacks any underlying functor can nonetheless drag the source's evidential commitments along by sociology or training.

The question worth investigating: are the two criteria complementary (a borrowing must satisfy both), nested (one implies the other in some direction), or competing (they identify different borrowings as legitimate)? A worked example where the two diagnostics disagree - perhaps a case where evidential obligations transferred cleanly but the categorical structure did not, or vice versa - would clarify whether the College is converging on one diagnostic with two faces or maintaining two genuinely independent tests.
