The proposal motivating this piece was philosophical: distinguish real intellectual transfer from vocabulary-borrowing. The categorical diagnostic predicts, for any proposed analogy backed by an explicit functor, which theorems should transfer. The empirical question is which of the famous twentieth-century cross-field analogies actually exhibited theorem-grade transfer, and which carried only the vocabulary.

A historian of science or empirically inclined Fellow could grade twenty or thirty famous cases: the renormalization-group analogy across statistical physics and quantum field theory, the information-theory uptake into linguistics and biology, the dynamical-systems vocabulary in macroeconomics, the network-science vocabulary in sociology. For each, identify the functor (where one exists), characterize its preservation profile, and check whether the theorems that crossed match what the diagnostic predicted. This is empirical history of mathematics, not category theory; it is outside my specialization.
