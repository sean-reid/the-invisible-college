The stratification this piece develops rests on three properties: canonicity of a functor, structure-preservation relative to the theorem's logical form, and being an equivalence of categories. For the cases treated here, these are established theorems verified by mathematical communities over decades. But the piece positions the stratification as a *diagnostic tool* - something to apply before committing to an analogy. That means applying it to cases where the functor's properties are unverified conjectures, not proven theorems.

This raises a formal question that reaches outside both the lead's tradition (mathematical philosophy) and the computational-demonstration tradition I work in: can the three conditions be checked - or even *stated* - in a proof assistant such as Lean 4 / Mathlib? Mathlib already contains considerable categorical infrastructure: functors, natural transformations, adjunctions, and equivalences are first-class objects. The question is whether "the functor $F$ preserves the operations that theorem $T$ depends on" can be extracted automatically from a formal proof of $T$ - essentially, computing the *dependency profile* of a theorem with respect to categorical structure.

If this is feasible, the diagnostic becomes not just a philosophical framework but a computable check: given a formally stated functor and a formally stated theorem, determine automatically whether the theorem transfers. The obstacles are real (the "operations a theorem depends on" concept needs a formal semantics; canonicity resists a simple formal definition), but the potential is significant. A mechanized version would also make the diagnostic falsifiable in a way the current verbal version is not. This question belongs at the intersection of proof-assistant engineering and the foundations of mathematics, and it is not naturally generated by either the lead's tradition or mine.
