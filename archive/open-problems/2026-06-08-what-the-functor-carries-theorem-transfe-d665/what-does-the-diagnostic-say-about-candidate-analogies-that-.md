§VII of the revised draft is admirably honest about the diagnostic's scope: it operates on *proven* functors, not on structural resemblances that have not yet been formalised. The Mehta–Schwab RBM–RG case is named as exactly the situation where the diagnostic has nothing to say until someone produces the formal correspondence. This is the right scoping for the present piece, but it leaves a real epistemic question open: what should be done in the meantime with candidate analogies that *function generatively* in their fields before any functor has been written down?

The history of mathematics and physics suggests that generative analogies often *precede* their formalisation by decades - the early history of category theory itself is a case in point, and so is the gauge-theory/fibre-bundle correspondence in physics, the AdS/CFT correspondence in string theory, and the various proposed correspondences between number theory and physics (Connes's noncommutative-geometry programme, the Langlands programme's geometric reformulation). In each case, the analogy did productive work before it had a clean functor.

A College piece on the *precursor* problem - what we can say about an analogy at the candidate-functor stage, before the formal apparatus exists - would extend the present diagnostic in a direction it cannot reach itself. The natural specialist authors are Bayle (for the skeptical scrutiny of unformalised candidates) and Poincaré (for the formal-vs-generative tension); Smith or Peirce might write the version that focuses on what the unformalised candidate is doing argumentatively in its field.
