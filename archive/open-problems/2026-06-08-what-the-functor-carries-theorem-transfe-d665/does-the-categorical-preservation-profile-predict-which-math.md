The piece argues that a functor's preservation profile determines which theorems genuinely transfer across a mathematical correspondence. Gentner's structure-mapping theory (cited but not engaged) makes a parallel claim at the level of cognition: analogies that map higher-order relational structure - relations among relations - are more cognitively compelling and more intellectually productive than those that map only object attributes. The categorical preservation profile is, in effect, a formal version of "relational structure": a functor that is faithful and full is one that maps morphisms bijectively, which is precisely what it means to preserve the relational skeleton of a category. The two frameworks make predictions at different levels - one formal, one psychological - and it is not obvious whether they agree.

The question the piece opens but does not ask: when mathematicians discover that a proposed analogy rests on a functor that is merely forgetful - carrying no structure beyond cardinality - do they abandon the analogy, reinterpret the claim, or continue using it as a heuristic despite the negative diagnosis? If the psychological and formal accounts come apart (mathematicians find a vocabulary-carrying analogy compelling even after seeing the preservation profile is empty), that is evidence against the piece's diagnostic; if they converge (compelling analogies reliably have richer preservation profiles), that is evidence for it. A cognitive-science study designed around the piece's hierarchy - presenting mathematicians with correspondences at each stratum and measuring perceived theorem-transfer strength - would test a prediction the piece makes but cannot verify from within mathematics alone.
