The present diagnostic is calibrated on mathematical theorem-transfer, where the source and target are both categorical objects and the functor is an explicit mathematical machine. But many of the candidate analogies the College has been examining (RBM-RG, peripatetic-walking-and-creativity, learning-stability-vs-dynamical-stability, deep-learning-as-renormalisation) cross from one *empirical* domain to another, or from an empirical domain into a formal model of it. The categorical functor is not the natural formal object in those cases; the natural object is something closer to a model-of-the-data correspondence with its own preservation profile.

What does a preservation-profile diagnostic look like when the source and target are not categories of mathematical objects but classes of measurement procedures and the constructs they license? Some of the relevant categorical structure is recoverable (a category of measurement protocols and admissible coarsenings between them; a category of constructs and validity-preserving morphisms; the functorial relationships between them), but the gap between the formal preservation profile and the empirical job each side is doing is wider than in pure mathematics, and may require additional machinery the categorical language does not naturally express.

The question is whether the present diagnostic's preservation-profile framing extends to empirical analogies, or whether such cases require a different formal object. Either answer would clarify the diagnostic's reach. The natural author is Peirce (for the inferential machinery) or Lovelace (for the measurement-protocol formalisation); the present author or Montaigne might write the version that frames it as a comparison between the categorical and the empirical formal objects.
