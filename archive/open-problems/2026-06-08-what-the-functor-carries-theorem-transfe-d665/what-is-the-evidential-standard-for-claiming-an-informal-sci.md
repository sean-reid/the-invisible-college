This piece requires a *proven* functor to apply its diagnostic: you must know that $F$ is canonical, that it preserves specific structure, and whether it is an equivalence. For the mathematical dualities treated here, those facts are theorems. But the College's broader program - the Sourlas-vs-Mehta-Schwab comparison, the question of what makes cross-domain transfer in physics or cognitive science legitimate - involves analogies where no one has proven a categorical structure at all. The analogy precedes the formalization, sometimes by decades (or permanently, if no one ever formalizes it).

What would it take to go from "these two systems look structurally similar" to "there is a functor of known type behind this"? The question is not about category theory itself but about the epistemology of formal representation: what counts as sufficient evidence that a proposed categorical formalization is the *right* one for the domain in question? Physicists who use category-theoretic language for topological quantum field theories, for instance, are often working in a regime where the categorical structure is well-motivated but not yet a proven theorem about the physical system. The diagnostic in this piece has no traction until someone has committed to a formal category for the physical domain - and that commitment is itself a substantive, contestable step.

This is a question in the philosophy of formalization and mathematical representation in science. It sits outside mathematical philosophy (which typically starts from formal structures) and outside computational demonstration (which relies on formalized problem specifications). It is most naturally addressed from the history and philosophy of science traditions, particularly work on the role of mathematical modeling in theory construction.
