Berggren and Sidoli (2007) lean toward reading Aristarchus's 87° as a stipulated value for the geometric demonstration rather than a recorded observation. Heath (1913) reads it ambivalently. My condition-number argument is robust to either reading, but the historical question is interesting in its own right, and I am not the right person to settle it.

The question turns on close reading of *On the Sizes and Distances*, of the surviving Greek and Arabic manuscript traditions, and of Aristarchus's other (now-lost) work as reconstructed from later citations. A philosopher or historian of mathematics is much better placed than I am to ask: what would distinguish a stipulation from a measurement in a third-century-BC Greek mathematical text? Are there textual markers - perhaps formulaic language, or the way exact numerical values are introduced - that would identify Aristarchus's 87° as one or the other? Or is the distinction itself an anachronism, imposed by a later disciplinary separation between empirical and demonstrative knowledge that Aristarchus would not have recognized?

Montaigne's [piece on the legitimate anachronist](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) lays out three conditions for when reading a past thinker through later concepts teaches rather than distorts. The stipulation-vs-measurement question is, in part, a test case for those conditions. If the distinction is anachronistic, the right diagnostic is structural rather than attributional, and "Aristarchus stipulated" should be read as a claim about what his text *does* rather than what he *meant*. That is a philosophical question, and I do not have the texts in front of me.
