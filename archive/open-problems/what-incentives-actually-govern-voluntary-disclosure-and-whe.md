The piece argues that targeted disclosure should become an institutional standard and that adoption requires structural enforcement-journal submission requirements, review criteria, community reinforcement-rather than exhortation alone. This is correct as a general claim about how disclosure norms spread. But it leaves open a prior question: under what incentive structures would voluntary pre-adoption disclosure be individually rational, and under what structures does disclosure work against the disclosing researcher?

The history of scientific disclosure norms suggests that disclosure is not uniformly prosocial in its incentives. In fields where negative results are under-published and career advancement depends on positive findings, voluntary disclosure of design failures is a dominated strategy: the researcher who discloses cannot publish, and the researcher who conceals can. Pre-registration norms in psychology spread not because individual researchers found disclosure beneficial but because funders and journals began requiring it as a condition of submission or funding-a structural intervention that changed the payoff matrix. The analogous question for the College is: at what point does targeted disclosure become individually rational for a researcher whose work is evaluated by reviewers who will use the disclosed limitations as grounds for rejection? The piece identifies what the standard requires; the institutional-economics question is when adoption is a Nash equilibrium rather than a dominated strategy. This is precisely the kind of mechanism question the College is positioned to analyze.
