The audit of sūtra 1.4.2 raises a structural question that recurs across rule-systems wherever multiple potentially-applicable rules conflict and the system specifies a tiebreaker. The Pāṇinian three-tier architecture is one such system; Talmudic halakhic interpretation specifies its own conflict-resolution principles (*halakhah ke-batra'i*, "the law follows the later authority," and the *kelal u-perat* general-and-specific principles); common-law adjudication uses *lex posterior derogat priori* and *lex specialis derogat legi generali*; programming languages encode operator precedence and associativity tables; theorem-proving search procedures rely on heuristics like unit-preference resolution. In each, the question this audit poses can be asked of the system's named principles: does the principle do empirical work, in the sense that its presence determines outputs the prior-tier or default-tier mechanisms would not, or is it a citation convention covering work the prior mechanisms already settle?

Some of these systems will be easy to audit (operator precedence in formal languages is determined by the parser's grammar and can be checked computationally on a synthetic corpus). Others will be hard for the same reasons the Pāṇinian audit is hard: the corpus of "invoked cases" is biased toward discriminative ones, the structural descriptions of the competing rules are sometimes not preserved separately from the conflict-resolution outcome, and the named principle and the underlying mechanism may have grown together in the tradition such that disentangling them requires structural criteria that the tradition itself does not supply. The Pāṇinian case sits in the middle of this spectrum: the rules are formally stated, but the citation corpus is small and the commentarial tradition has co-evolved with the principle under audit.

The cross-disciplinary value lies in the structural typology that would emerge from two or three parallel audits. A small number of underlying patterns may govern when a conflict-resolution rule does work and when it is a name for work others do. A piece auditing the operative role of *halakhah ke-batra'i* in a defined corpus of responsa-tradition cases, or *lex posterior* across a sample of common-law adjudication, against the parallel role of Pāṇini's 1.4.2, would expose what is generic about the audit's machinery (the diagnostic/blind-set distinction, the citation-corpus selection bias) and what is specific to grammars whose rules have positional ordering. The work is genuinely outside both the lead's specialization (Sanskrit grammar) and mine (historical epistemology of medicine).
