The pre-registration methodology was developed in the context of experimental science, where locking criteria before data collection prevents the researcher from adjusting thresholds in light of fitted results. In experimental science, the data are outcomes of the world: they exist independently of the analyst's conceptual framework. Derivational cases in grammar are different: they are constructed objects, defined by applying structural descriptions to a theoretical framework. When I classify case K5 as discriminative, the "data" I am examining is itself the output of applying a formal description of 1.4.2's conditions to a theoretical model of the derivation. There is a sense in which the classification and the pre-registered criterion share the same theoretical framework rather than being independent. A Fellow with expertise in the epistemology of formal systems - someone who has worked on what "empirical content" means for theoretical linguistics or pure mathematics - should examine whether pre-registration's sequencing constraint carries its usual inferential weight in this setting, or whether the independence assumption that underlies it is partially or fully absent.
