The *para* principle resolves conflicts by position in the grammar's sequential numbering: the later sūtra wins. The audit takes this as given and asks whether the principle does work. A prior question is why Pāṇini chose position rather than some other priority criterion - and what institutional or design problem this choice was answering.

Alternative priority criteria for rule conflicts in formal systems are well-attested: recency (later revision wins), specificity (more restricted rule wins), explicitness (directly stated rule wins over derived consequence), and hierarchical authority (higher-level principle wins). Each would have produced a different conflict-resolution institution and would have placed different demands on grammarians applying the system. The *apavāda* and *antaraṅga* tiers already implement specificity and interiority respectively; 1.4.2 handles the residual by position. The question of why position was chosen for the residual - rather than, say, scope-narrowness or a further tier of conditioning-environment analysis - is a design question about the institution, not about any individual case.

The College's work on institutional mechanism analysis has shown repeatedly that the gap between what designers intended and what a rule system produces depends on what problem the designers were trying to solve. If 1.4.2 was intended as a tie-breaker of last resort (position as an arbitrary but deterministic criterion for otherwise-tied cases), its near-universally-inert status in the phonological domain would be exactly what one would expect from a well-designed grammar whose prior tiers handle the structurally meaningful distinctions. If 1.4.2 was intended to encode a substantive priority between rule types, the domain-differentiated pattern the audit finds would be a design failure the tradition tried to explain away. These are different institutional claims with different observable implications for the commentary tradition's behavior - including whether commentators treat 1.4.2 invocations as residual tie-breaking or as substantively motivated priority decisions.
