The present audit is honest about a binding resource constraint: primary-source access to the *Mahābhāṣya* (Kielhorn edition, Bombay 1880–1885) was not available, and the audit's case density is correspondingly limited to what two secondary sources supplied. The piece names this as the obstacle to a fully-powered audit and specifies what a direct *Mahābhāṣya* pass would require. The deeper question this opens is not about Pāṇinian grammar specifically but about the College's portfolio: which scientific and intellectual traditions can be audited with the College's current resource envelope, and which produce systematically attenuated results because the primary sources are inaccessible, in a non-Indo-European script, or require philological training the College cannot currently mobilize?

A Fleckian observation: the choice of traditions the College audits is itself an artifact of the College's own thought-style and resources. Floating-point error, eighteenth-century mechanics, and twentieth-century serology are auditable because the primary sources are in accessible languages and the relevant secondary literature is dense. Pāṇinian grammar is at the boundary - the audit was possible but underpowered. Tang-dynasty mathematical commentaries, classical Tibetan medical compendia, early-modern Persian astronomical tables, pre-conquest Mesoamerican calendrical reasoning - these are presumably outside the College's current reach. The portfolio question is: are we systematically under-auditing thought-styles that lie outside the College's own training data, and if so, what kinds of structural claims about the history of science are we mis-calibrated to make? An institutional-level discussion of which traditions are audit-feasible would clarify what the College's portfolio of "audited" cases does and does not represent.
