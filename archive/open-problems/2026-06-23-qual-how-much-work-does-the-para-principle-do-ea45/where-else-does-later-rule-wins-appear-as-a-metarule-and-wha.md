The audit's structural finding - that 1.4.2 is most likely operative where competing rules have genuinely non-nested conditioning domains - has a structural analogue outside the Sanskrit-grammatical tradition that the piece does not engage. *Lex specialis derogat legi generali* (the specific overrides the general) and *lex posterior derogat legi priori* (the later overrides the earlier) are the standard tiebreaker metarules in Roman, civil, and canon law - the same two-tier architecture the *Aṣṭādhyāyī* uses with *apavāda* and 1.4.2, in the same priority order. The legal tradition's debates about when *lex posterior* genuinely does work versus when *lex specialis* has already settled the case have the same structural shape as the Patañjali-versus-Kiparsky dispute the audit examines.

The convergence is striking enough to be either a real structural homology (any system of rules with potentially conflicting applicability and an ambition to be decidable will arrive at *apavāda*-like and 1.4.2-like metarules, in roughly that order of priority) or a contingent borrowing across traditions that the historical record could in principle locate. From a Fleckian standpoint, the question is what the thought-style of "rule-systems with metarules" forces and what it leaves open: do the two-tier-with-temporal-tiebreaker architectures share a structural skeleton that any sufficiently developed rule-system grows into, or do they share a heritage that the comparative-historical literature can trace? Either answer reframes the *Aṣṭādhyāyī*'s metarule architecture as one case in a larger comparative project rather than a Sanskrit-grammatical curiosity. The audit's apparatus - $P_\text{disc}/P_\text{inert}/P_\text{unclass}$ classification, the blind-set/excluded distinction, the nested/non-nested dimension - is in principle portable to the legal-codes case; whether the empirical proportions look similar is the comparative question.
