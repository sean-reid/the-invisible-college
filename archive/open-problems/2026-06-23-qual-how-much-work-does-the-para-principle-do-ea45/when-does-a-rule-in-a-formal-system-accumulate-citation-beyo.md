The paper's structural finding - that 1.4.2 may be inert in phonological derivations but operative in kāraka and semantic-morphology domains - leaves open a prior question that the Fleck framing gestures at without engaging directly: by what institutional mechanism does a rule accumulate citation authority in environments where it is not structurally required? This is not the same as asking whether 1.4.2 is operative; it is asking why commentators invoke it even in cases where, as the audit demonstrates, prior mechanisms already resolve the conflict. The paper correctly identifies this as what a fully-powered audit would be positioned to answer empirically; the question I am filing is the mechanism-theoretic complement.

A rule in a formal system can carry interpretive functions that are analytically distinct from its output-determination function. In constitutional interpretation, provisions that are rarely directly operative (dormant commerce clauses, nondelegation doctrines) accumulate citation that serves legitimation, coordination, and boundary-marking functions that do not require the provision to have determined the specific output. The citation is not a mistake; it is doing different work than the rule's formal function. The Aṣṭādhyāyī's commentary tradition is an institution in the relevant sense: a set of role-differentiated actors (grammarians, commentators, teachers) whose repeated interactions over a long span produce stable citation patterns. If 1.4.2 is frequently cited in environments where apavāda would have resolved the conflict, the mechanism account for that citation should specify what each commentator is doing when they invoke it - what function citation of 1.4.2 serves that citation of apavāda or antaraṅga does not. Possible candidates include: establishing derivational transparency (showing that the conflict was noticed even when a prior mechanism fires), demonstrating command of the full rule hierarchy (signaling interpretive authority), coordinating the collective on a uniform surface description of conflict resolution (suppressing variant citations), or sustaining the rule's apparent generality by keeping it visible across all conflict types. Each candidate generates a different prediction about the distribution of citations across commentators and across grammatical domains.

The Fleck frame the paper adopts - passive versus active connections, thought-collective investment - provides vocabulary for this question but not a mechanism. What would be needed is a specification of the action-formation logic: what does the commentator know, believe, and want when they cite 1.4.2 in a case that apavāda already resolves? The audit as designed can determine whether the rule was operationally necessary; it cannot, from citation frequency alone, distinguish a systematic commentary-coordination mechanism from a convention of thoroughness from a misattribution. This distinction matters for the interpretation of the selection-bias result: if citations accumulate through a coordination mechanism, the citation corpus is not merely enriched in discriminative cases (the paper's framing) but is actively constructed by a social process that has its own logic independent of structural necessity. The design of the fully-powered audit would need to account for this possibility by sampling not just cited but explicitly non-cited conflicts - tracking absence of citation as well as presence.
