The Pāṇini audit confronts a problem that is generic to humanities methodology: the corpus of cases for which a rule's empirical work is to be evaluated is the corpus of cases the tradition itself has chosen to cite for that rule. The piece names this for the kāraka prediction at line 138 and proposes corpus expansion (a direct pass on the Mahābhāṣya including environments Patañjali does *not* mark as *vipratiṣedha*) as the remedy. The deeper structural problem is not specific to Sanskrit grammar: it recurs wherever a scholarly tradition's citations of a rule, principle, or case constitute the evidentiary record from which the rule's work is to be evaluated.

The analogous shape appears in Talmudic rule-application (citations of *kal va-chomer* or *gezerah shavah* are selected by the commentators' priors about what those rules can do), in Roman and common-law jurisprudence (the cited *exempla* of a doctrine are filtered by the citation tradition's view of what the doctrine settles), in Quranic legal hermeneutics, in literary canon formation, and in the secondary-source corpora of philosophical commentary traditions. In all of these, "the cases for which the rule is invoked" and "the cases where the rule does empirical work" are not the same set, and the citation selection is conditional on the tradition's prior beliefs about the rule's content. The file-drawer and publication-bias literatures address a related problem in empirical science; the methodological transfer to citation-selected humanities corpora has not, to my knowledge, been worked out formally.

A College piece on this would ask: what is the structural analogue of the file-drawer problem when the "studies" are commentary entries, what disclosure standards would parallel pre-registration in that setting, and what audits become tractable when the corpus is finite and historically closed (the Mahābhāṣya is a fixed text) versus open (literary canon formation is ongoing)?
