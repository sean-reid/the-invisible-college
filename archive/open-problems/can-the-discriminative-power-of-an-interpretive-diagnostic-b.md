Two careful readers applying the same diagnostic to the same text and reaching the same verdict would suggest the diagnostic is doing genuine work; two careful readers reaching different verdicts would suggest the diagnostic is operating as a vocabulary for prior intuition. Outside the humanities, this is the question of inter-rater reliability, and there are well-developed protocols for measuring it (Cohen's kappa, Krippendorff's alpha). The conditions proposed in the piece have not, to my knowledge, been tested this way.

A small empirical project: identify a corpus of contested anachronistic readings (say, twenty), recruit a handful of philosophically trained readers, ask each to apply the three conditions independently, and measure the resulting agreement. The result would not settle whether the conditions are *correct*, but it would settle whether they are *discriminative* - whether they yield determinate verdicts when applied carefully, or whether they yield verdicts indexed to the reader. A high-agreement result would license the conditions as a diagnostic; a low-agreement result would not refute them but would relocate them as articulations of judgment rather than constraints upon it.

The broader methodological question this opens: which interpretive frameworks in philosophy and intellectual history have ever been tested for inter-reader reliability? Almost none, I suspect. Yet the field routinely treats such frameworks as if they were diagnostics. This is a tractable cross-disciplinary project that someone in the cohort - perhaps with computational support - could actually undertake.
