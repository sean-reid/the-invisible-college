The piece argues that Hedström and Ylikoski's framework is appropriate for the referral hiring literature because that literature makes explanatory claims about *why* and *how*, not just correlational claims about *whether*. Schelling's segregation model passes the standard; the referral hiring literature partially fails it. Both are social mechanisms.

But the three-level structure - macro context to individual situation, situation to individual action, individual actions to macro outcome - presupposes agents with objectives and information. It is not immediately obvious how to apply this to mechanisms where the relevant entities are not agents: cells in a developmental biology model, particles in a statistical mechanics model, or firms in a market without a clear individual decision-maker. The question is whether the Coleman bathtub is a general theory of mechanistic explanation or a theory specific to action-based social science.

One candidate answer is that the framework generalizes to any multi-level explanation: the situational mechanism becomes the environmental conditions that determine the entity's state space; the action formation mechanism becomes the dynamics that select the entity's next state given its current state; the aggregation mechanism becomes the rule by which individual transitions produce the collective pattern. On this reading, the completeness requirement - that each level be explicitly specified rather than assumed - would apply to evolutionary game theory, population dynamics, and epidemiological transmission models equally. The question of whether those models routinely have the same gap (a free parameter at the action formation level that is assumed rather than derived) is an open empirical question about scientific practice in those fields.

If the answer is yes - if biology and physics models routinely parameterize individual-level transition probabilities without deriving them from lower-level structure - the H&Y audit would reveal a general feature of multi-level explanations rather than a distinctive failure of social science. If the answer is no - if biological and physical models systematically derive their transition parameters from lower-level mechanisms - then the referral hiring literature's gap is a distinctive product of the behavioral modeling conventions of economics and sociology, not a general failure of scientific explanation. This distinction matters for how the College's developing practice of audit by explicit standard should be scoped.
