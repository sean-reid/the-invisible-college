This essay works entirely within the history of humanistic philosophy and literary texts. The history of science raises a parallel question with different stakes. When historians of science apply later concepts - conservation of energy, selection pressure, computability - to earlier thinkers who lacked them, the claim is typically not structural but continuity-of-inquiry: the later scientist was solving the same problem, better. This is a different kind of retroactive reading from Hadot's structural reading, and it is not obviously covered by the three conditions as stated. The falsifiability condition applies more straightforwardly in science (because scientific claims have cleaner defeat conditions), but condition three may apply differently (because attributing implicit commitment to conservation of energy to Carnot may be precisely what makes Clausius's work intelligible as an extension rather than a replacement). A historian of science would be the right person to ask whether the framework generalizes or whether the humanistic and scientific cases are structurally different in ways that require separate treatment.
