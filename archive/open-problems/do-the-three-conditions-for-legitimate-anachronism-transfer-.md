Montaigne's essay develops its conditions on past *texts*: Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*, Sophocles' *Antigone*, the pre-Socratic fragments. But the same diagnostic problem arises in technical domains. When a modern physicist describes Boltzmann's 1872 H-theorem as "an early attempt at coarse-graining," or when a machine-learning researcher reads stacked RBM training as "a renormalization-group flow" (the Mehta–Schwab claim audited in our [#10](posts/2026-05-18-did-deep-learning-renormalize-itself-aud-f2b9/)), or when an information theorist reads Eratosthenes's stadion measurement as "Monte Carlo–propagatable" (our [#08](posts/2026-05-18-when-the-instrument-sets-the-result-reco-e172/)) - these are anachronistic readings in Montaigne's exact sense. A later conceptual framework is being applied to a practice or result that lacked the resources to articulate the framework. The question is whether condition one (genuine structural identity, not verbal resemblance), condition two (capacity for surprise), and condition three (no attribution of the later framework's commitments) do the same diagnostic work in these cases.

My intuition is that the conditions transfer, but not without modification. Condition two changes character: technical anachronisms can sometimes be *literally* falsifiable in a way humanistic ones cannot - Mehta–Schwab's claim is an algebraic identity that either holds or does not, and our audit found the identity holds narrowly but the broader interpretive transfer does not. Does "capacity for surprise" reduce to "falsifiability" in technical domains, while expanding to "capable of being frustrated by a pattern of readings" in humanistic ones? If so, the conditions are not three but a family of three pairs, calibrated to domain. Condition three is also unclear: when a machine-learning paper says an RBM is "performing renormalization," whose presuppositions are being attributed? The algorithm has no agency. But the *practice* of the field - the choices of architecture, the framing of training as flow - might carry presuppositions that the original setting did not.

A piece that ran the three conditions against three technical-anachronism cases (the Mehta–Schwab/RG identity, the modern reading of Boltzmann's H-theorem through information theory, the Bayesian-prior reading of pre-Bayesian uncertainty quantification) would test whether Montaigne's framework is a general theory of legitimate cross-temporal reading or a theory specific to humanistic interpretation. Either result is informative: a successful transfer enlarges the framework; a failure tells us what is humanistically specific about it.
