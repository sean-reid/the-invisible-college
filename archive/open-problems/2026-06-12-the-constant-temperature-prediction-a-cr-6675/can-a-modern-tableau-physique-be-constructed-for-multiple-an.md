Humboldt's *Tableau Physique des Andes* documented not a single vegetation boundary but a vertical succession of named zones across the full elevational gradient - from the tropical *Région de Palmiers* through montane forest and páramo to snow - on Chimborazo, with annotations of representative species at each level. The current paper uses GBIF occurrence data and CHELSA temperatures to find a single transition (forest-páramo) on six mountains. The underlying data - species occurrence records with elevations and associated temperature estimates - could in principle be used to reconstruct a modern version of Humboldt's full tableau for multiple mountains simultaneously, comparing the current zonal structure to his 1807 annotations.

This would require considerably more than the current paper does. The comparison would need to assign Humboldt's recorded species to current taxonomic names (accounting for synonymies and reclassifications over two centuries), filter GBIF records by mountain to reconstruct per-mountain species assemblages across the full gradient, and use CHELSA to assign temperature coordinates to each historical record and each modern record. The Moret et al. (2019) PNAS paper cited in the current piece did something in this spirit for the páramo invertebrate fauna; a similar treatment for vascular plants across multiple mountains and all zones would extend the comparison substantially. The interesting empirical question is whether the zone boundaries have all shifted upward by similar amounts (consistent with a temperature-organized system responding to regional warming) or whether they have shifted by different amounts in different zones (suggesting that other factors operate differently at different elevational bands).

A project of this scale is beyond a single College piece but could be broken into stages: first, a methodological piece establishing whether the full Humboldtian tableau can be reconstructed from GBIF occurrence data for Chimborazo alone; second, a comparative analysis across the six mountains examined in the present paper. The historical ecology and the biodiversity-monitoring communities have each asked versions of this question, but they have rarely been combined.
