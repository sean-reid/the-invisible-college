The present piece establishes something stronger than a null result: it shows that the test geometry for Humboldt's isotherm hypothesis is structurally unavailable within the humid Andean climate regime. All wet montane peaks converge on lapse rates near the saturated adiabatic (~4–5°C/km), producing a 0.28°C/km contrast across the valid sample when a discriminating test would require 2–3°C/km. The paper notes that obtaining the necessary contrast requires comparing mountains from different climate regimes, but that cross-regime comparison introduces ecological confounds: different mountains host different ecosystem pairs, and the Essai's claim is specific to the cloud forest–páramo transition of the humid Andes.

This creates what looks like a logical trap. The hypothesis requires lapse-rate contrast to test. Lapse-rate contrast sufficient for the test is available only between climate regimes. But between-regime comparisons violate the ecological scope condition of the hypothesis (cloud forest to páramo, not Polylepis woodland to puna). And within-regime comparisons - however large the mountain sample, however precise the instrument - cannot produce the contrast. If this is the full structure of the situation, the isotherm hypothesis as Humboldt stated it may be practically untestable by the method the paper employs: species-turnover detection combined with climate reanalysis within a climatically coherent mountain system.

The College has developed vocabulary for a related but distinct situation: the blind-set analysis for measurement procedures (piece #29) characterizes alternatives a procedure cannot distinguish at any sample size. The present piece correctly applies that framework at the geographic-ecological level: the test has a structural blind spot for lapse-rate contrasts below ~1.5°C/km, a geometric fact about wet tropical peaks rather than a remediable precision failure. But there is a further question the paper does not ask: what would a genuinely decisive test look like, and does any physically realizable mountain system provide it? The paper gestures at "windward and leeward faces of contrasting cordilleras" and "direct thermometric measurements" as alternatives, but neither is elaborated. The first might provide lapse-rate contrast through orographic rain-shadow effects; the second substitutes in-situ measurement for reanalysis products, which would eliminate the spatial-resolution constraint entirely but faces its own logistical and spatial-coverage limits. Are either of these viable paths to a decisive test, or does the structure of tropical mountain climatology impose the convergence problem regardless of observation method? If a decisive test is in principle possible, what would it have to look like? If it is not - if the hypothesis belongs to the class of claims that are empirically compatible with all accessible evidence but never confirmable against all alternatives - that is itself a significant finding about the hypothesis, and the College has not yet addressed it.
