The paper detects forest-páramo boundaries at 9.0–10.4°C across four Ecuadorian mountains. This range emerges from statistical aggregation: Jaccard dissimilarity over species-composition turnover in 100 m elevation bands. The temperature at maximum dissimilarity is the thermal coordinate of the zone where assemblage identity shifts most rapidly, but the method cannot say *why* it shifts at that temperature. Two quite different mechanisms are consistent with the observed pattern. In the first, a core set of montane tree taxa - *Gynoxys*, *Polylepis reticulata*, *Buddleja incana*, or their associates - have growth or reproduction performance curves that decline sharply below approximately 9–10°C, and these taxa are the actual agents of the boundary. In the second, temperature is a proxy for something else (cumulative frost-day exposure, nighttime minima, growing-season vapor-pressure deficit, soil nitrogen mineralization rates at low temperature) that is the actual constraint, and the thermal reading of the boundary happens to sit where it does because temperature correlates with the operative variable at these elevations and latitudes.

The distinction matters for prediction under climate change and for the theoretical claim at stake. If the boundary is organized directly by a mean annual temperature threshold, the isotherm hypothesis holds as a causal claim and upward boundary shifts under warming should track the isotherm precisely. If temperature is a proxy, then the rate and direction of boundary shift under warming will depend on how the actual constraint variable responds to the warming - which may not be proportional to mean annual temperature change, particularly if nighttime warming outpaces daytime warming (a common asymmetry in mountain regions). Occurrence data aggregated from GBIF cannot resolve this: it gives the statistical boundary location but not the physiological mechanism producing it. What would be needed is either experimental growth and germination temperature response curves for the key tree taxa, or long-term monitoring data showing how the boundary elevation has actually moved in relation to temperature changes at the sites.

A College piece on this would need to draw on plant ecophysiology literature (where per-species thermal performance curves exist for some montane taxa) and would likely find that the observed 9–10°C boundary temperature is consistent with, but not uniquely explained by, the mean-annual-temperature threshold. It would also need to grapple with the concept of "thermal limit" at the population level - which is not a hard threshold but a probability density on performance.
