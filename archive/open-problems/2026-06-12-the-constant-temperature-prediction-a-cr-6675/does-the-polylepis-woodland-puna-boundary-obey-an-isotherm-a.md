This paper excludes Sajama (Bolivia) on the grounds that its Polylepis woodland–puna transition is a different ecosystem type from the cloud forest–páramo system that Humboldt's Essai describes. The exclusion is methodologically correct. But it implicitly leaves open a question the Essai does not address: is the Polylepis–puna boundary itself isotherm-organized, or does some other environmental variable (frost frequency, moisture deficit, soil freeze-thaw regime) do the organizing work that temperature does for the cloud forest–páramo system?

The Polylepis woodland is one of the highest tree lines on Earth, reaching 5,200 m on sheltered Andean slopes. If it too tracks an isotherm, the constant-temperature prediction generalizes beyond the humid Andes into the semi-arid puna, which would be a significant extension of Humboldt's claim and would require a different isothermal value than the 9–10°C detected here. If it does not track an isotherm - if instead frost duration or soil moisture is the organizing variable - that would be informative about when the isotherm mechanism applies and when it breaks down, which is a question neither Humboldt's Essai nor the current paper addresses.

Testing this requires the same design as the current paper (well-characterized GBIF coverage for Polylepis species, lapse-rate readings from CHELSA, a cross-mountain regression), applied to Bolivian and Peruvian peaks where Polylepis woodland is well-documented. The ecological constraint is milder in one respect - Polylepis woodland is more spatially discrete than the broad ecotone the current paper detects, so boundary sharpness may be higher - but harder in another, since Polylepis coverage in GBIF is historically thinner than cloud-forest vascular plants. The question reaches outside the Humboldtian program, into comparative questions about what kinds of tree lines are thermal-threshold phenomena and what kinds are not.
