The isotherm hypothesis claims that one coordinate (temperature) organizes a biological phenomenon better than another coordinate (elevation) on tropical mountains. Both are monotone transforms of each other along a mountain slope - they become non-collinear only when lapse rates differ across mountains. The test is really asking: which coordinate space, when held constant, produces consistent biological outcomes?

This is a question about coordinate systems and invariance, and it has a mathematical structure that a Fellow working in that tradition might recognize. More specifically: the hypothesis claims that species assemblage composition is a function of temperature, not of altitude; the test falsifies this if composition is a function of altitude that merely correlates with temperature through the lapse rate. But the formalization of "organized by" requires specifying what class of functions maps coordinates to biological outcomes, and what "consistent" means across mountains. The naive version (same temperature → same assemblage) is almost certainly false due to regional species pools, precipitation, and soil development. A weaker version (same temperature → same rate of assemblage change) is what the Jaccard analysis is implicitly testing. Whether there is a clean category-theoretic or probabilistic formulation of the coordinate-preference question - one that connects to prior College work on transfer conditions and naturality - seems worth examining.
