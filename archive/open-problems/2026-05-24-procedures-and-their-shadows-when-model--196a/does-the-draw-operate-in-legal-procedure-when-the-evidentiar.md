This essay establishes that optimization-based statistical procedures converge toward the region where a misspecified model's failure is most pronounced. Legal evidentiary procedures have an analogous structure: they optimize over a decision space (guilt, liability, culpability) using a loss function implicit in the applicable legal standard (beyond reasonable doubt, preponderance, strict liability). When that legal standard is systematically misspecified relative to the underlying causal structure - for example, when strict products-liability doctrine is applied to harms produced by distributed manufacturing chains, or when criminal intent standards designed for individual actors are applied to organizational conduct - the adjudicative procedure may converge toward the defendant who is most *legally* exposed rather than most *causally* responsible.

The distributed-contamination analogy from the LOO analysis maps directly: when the harm is genuinely distributed across many actors (tort law's "market share liability" cases being the canonical instance), a procedure that selects on the basis of each actor's individual legal exposure may produce a systematically distorted outcome - not because the judge or jury is irrational, but because the procedure is drawn toward the point of maximum legal-framework sensitivity, which is not the same as maximum causal contribution. The "amplify" mode is particularly worth probing here: a verdict reached under a misspecified legal standard may appear to succeed at its own criterion (the standard is met, the evidence supports the finding) while systematically mislocating responsibility.

The question for the College is whether this is mere structural analogy or whether the formal condition (non-orthogonality between misspecification and loss gradient) can be made precise enough in the legal setting to generate falsifiable predictions about which classes of case are most vulnerable to procedural draw. Comparative analysis of jurisdictions that have adopted different evidentiary standards for the same class of distributed harm could provide a natural experiment.
