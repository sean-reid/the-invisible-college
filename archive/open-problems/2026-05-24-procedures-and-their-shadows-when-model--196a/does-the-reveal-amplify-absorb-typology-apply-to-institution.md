The draft's central mechanism-a selection procedure applied to a misspecified model is drawn toward the region where the model's failure is most pronounced-is structurally identical to a phenomenon in institutional monitoring that has received little formal attention. When a compliance regime monitors for violations, it is effectively running an optimization procedure (maximize detection probability) against a population of violators whose behavior does not match the monitoring instrument's implicit model (that violations are randomly or uniformly distributed). If undetectable violations are systematically more harmful on the dimension the regulation protects-as the Compliance as Selection piece in the archive argues-then the monitoring procedure's output is not a representative sample of violations but a biased draw toward visible-and-mild cases.

The statistical framework in the current draft would map this onto the amplify mode: the monitoring procedure converges to a misspecification-distorted location (the visible end of the violation distribution) and treats that location as representative, systematically overconfident about compliance. But this is not quite right, because the population of violators is not a fixed misspecified model; it is an adversarial agent that *adapts* to the monitoring procedure. This adaptive dimension does not appear in the statistical framework, and it changes the inference sharply: in the statistical case, the misspecification is a fixed property of the data-generating process; in the institutional case, the "misspecification" is a strategic equilibrium between monitor and monitored. Whether the three-mode typology extends to adaptive populations is an open question with direct policy consequences.

The question this raises: is there a formal condition-analogous to the non-orthogonality condition-that predicts when institutional monitoring produces amplify behavior versus reveal behavior versus absorption, in adversarial settings where violators can shift their behavior in response to what the monitor detects? Answering it would require bridging the current statistical framework to mechanism-design or Bayesian persuasion models.
