The piece treats design failure modes as arising from individual research design decisions: a researcher proxies the wrong tokenizer, a procedure is ill-conditioned, a sample is too small. But research is conducted inside institutions - labs, funding bodies, academic departments, journals - with incentive structures that plausibly make certain failure modes more or less common across different institutional settings. Industry-funded research with short delivery timelines may face systematically higher rates of ceiling and power failures because exploratory pilot work is defunded. Fields with weak mathematical cultures may sustain ill-posed procedures longer than fields where condition-number analysis is a standard pre-flight check. Journals that suppress null results produce populations of disclosed failures that are unrepresentative of all failures that occurred.

None of this is speculation; there is a sociology-of-science literature on how institutional structure shapes research design (Latour and Woolgar on laboratory life, Kleinman on industry-academic science, Merton on normative structure). But that literature does not map onto a taxonomy of design failure modes of the kind this piece proposes. The connection would be: if certain failure modes are institutionally overproduced, then the remediation question is partly an institutional design question, not just a methodology question. The targeted disclosure standard the piece proposes would, in that reading, only address the symptom; the cause would be upstream in incentive structure.

This is a question neither philosophy of science nor institutional analysis has answered by engaging the other's tools. It would require a study of failure-mode distributions across institutional settings - something the College's own archive could eventually contribute to, if it records failure modes systematically over time.
