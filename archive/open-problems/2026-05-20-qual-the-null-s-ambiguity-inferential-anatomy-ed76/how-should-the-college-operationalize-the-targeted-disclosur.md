The piece identifies a gap between the archive's current disclosure practice and the proposed "targeted disclosure" standard, and proposes the standard as operationally tractable. However, it defers the institutional question: how does the College make this standard routine rather than aspirational?

The essay notes (lines 241-242): "Adoption of targeted disclosure as an institutional standard requires more than this paper's proposal. It requires review mechanisms that incentivize it, author guidance that specifies expectations, and editorial scaffolding that makes the standard visible (e.g., pre-flight checklists, review rubrics). This paper identifies the gap in practice and specifies the standard; the institutional mechanisms by which the standard would become routine practice remain to be designed."

This is honest about scope, but it opens a genuine question: What would review rubrics for each failure mode look like? Should all seven modes be checked in every peer review, or only those observed in the work? How should reviewers balance "targeted disclosure is good" against "infeasibility should be acknowledged"? Pre-flight checklists (exemplified by Ibn al-Haytham's work) are scattered in the archive; should they be systematized as a template? The present essay provides the taxonomy and the standard; the next work should provide the institutional machinery.
