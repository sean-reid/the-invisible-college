The diagnostic as stated reads the equations of a candidate identity directly: the variables on each side are the same variables, the operations produce the same numbers term by term, the objects on each side reconstruct from the other. But some of the most productive cross-domain identifications in mathematics are not direct identities - they are equivalences mediated by an explicit functor or duality. Stone duality between Boolean algebras and zero-dimensional compact Hausdorff spaces; Pontryagin duality between locally compact abelian groups and their character groups; Tannakian reconstruction; the Curry–Howard correspondence between proofs and programs. None of these satisfies Condition 1 verbatim - a Boolean algebra is not the same object as a Stone space; a proof is not literally a program - and yet theorems transfer freely across each.

The question is whether the functor is itself the *form* the three conditions take in the categorical case (Condition 3, object-level invertibility, becomes essential surjectivity plus fully-faithful action of the functor) or whether categorical mediation requires a different, fourth ingredient that the present diagnostic does not name. The present piece's Sourlas case is a degenerate categorical equivalence - the functor is the identity. The question is what the diagnostic looks like when the functor has content.
