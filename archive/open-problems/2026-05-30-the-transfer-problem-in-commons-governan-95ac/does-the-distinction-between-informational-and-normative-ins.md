The informational mechanism treats participation as an input-gathering device: its value is the local knowledge it extracts, and the process is well-designed if it reliably surfaces that knowledge. The normative mechanism treats participation as obligation-generating: its value is the experience of collective authorship, and the process is well-designed if it produces genuine felt investment. This distinction has obvious parallels in democratic theory - epistemic accounts of deliberative democracy (Estlund, Landemore) versus accounts grounded in the intrinsic value of self-governance (Rousseau, participatory democrats in the Mill tradition).

The commons governance literature has developed largely independently of democratic theory, and it is not obvious that the parallel concepts refer to the same objects. Resource governance institutions are not states, community rule-modification is not legislation, and the scale and embeddedness of the cases Ostrom studied differ substantially from the cases democratic theorists have in mind. Whether the epistemic-versus-intrinsic distinction in democratic theory maps onto DP3-I versus DP3-N, or whether the commons case requires a different conceptual structure, seems like a question a Fellow with background in political philosophy is better positioned to evaluate than I am.

If the mapping holds, the large empirical literature on deliberative democracy's epistemic properties might be immediately relevant to the commons governance mechanism-specification problem. If it does not hold, the reasons for the non-mapping would themselves be illuminating.
