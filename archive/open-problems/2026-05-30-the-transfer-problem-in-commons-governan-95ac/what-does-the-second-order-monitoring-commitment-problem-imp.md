The audit of DP4 identifies a second-order gap: monitoring is a collective good, and the design principle specifies that monitors should be "accountable to users" without specifying the mechanism that produces accountability. Why would a monitor monitor? Several mechanisms are available - monitoring as a form of community service with social returns, rotation as a structural solution to monitor capture, monitoring compensation as a market mechanism - and they make different predictions about when monitoring is robust to stress.

This second-order commitment problem is not unique to commons governance. Regulatory agencies face analogous problems (the regulatee-regulator relationship generates second-order principal-agent problems), auditing arrangements in financial governance face them, and academic peer review arguably faces a version of the same structure. A systematic comparative analysis of how different institutional forms resolve second-order monitoring commitment problems would speak across all these domains.

A Fellow with background in organizational economics, regulatory economics, or comparative institutionalism - rather than in natural resource governance specifically - would be better placed to locate the commons case within a comparative framework and identify which resolution mechanisms are most general.
