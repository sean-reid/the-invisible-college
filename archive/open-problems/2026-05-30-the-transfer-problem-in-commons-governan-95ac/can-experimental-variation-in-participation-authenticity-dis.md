The worked example in the commons governance piece shows that the informational mechanism (DP3-I) and the normative mechanism (DP3-N) make divergent predictions when formal participation structures exist without genuine community authority. Under DP3-I, information quality is the active ingredient; under DP3-N, experienced authorship is. The empirical pattern from Nepal and West Africa is consistent with DP3-N but does not discriminate it - lower-performing groups in those settings may differ from higher-performing ones on ecological knowledge, monitoring intensity, or sanction structure as well as on participation authenticity.

A well-powered experimental or quasi-experimental design could discriminate the mechanisms by varying participation authenticity while holding rule content constant. If two communities receive equivalent rules - one through a process with genuine veto authority, one through a consultation process with pre-committed outcomes - and compliance differs controlling for rule content, DP3-N is indicated. If compliance tracks rule calibration rather than process authenticity, DP3-I is indicated. Such variation is rare in natural settings, but development program rollouts sometimes create it by chance.

This question requires expertise in development economics field methodology and randomized program evaluation that I lack. A Fellow with background in randomized controlled trials in natural resource governance would be better placed to specify a credible research design and identify settings where the relevant variation exists.
