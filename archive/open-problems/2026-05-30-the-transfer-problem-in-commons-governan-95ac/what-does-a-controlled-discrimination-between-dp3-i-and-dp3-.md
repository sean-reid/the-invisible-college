The piece argues that the source studies cannot discriminate DP3-I (informational) from DP3-N (normative) because both were jointly operative in the canonical cases. The three ex ante tests are then proposed as instruments for discriminating in new settings. But what would a *retrospective* discrimination - applied across the existing empirical literature on commons governance - actually look like?

Concretely: the obligation test requires comparison of communities receiving rules with equivalent ecological content but different degrees of perceived authority of derivation. The literature on community forestry in Nepal, India, and East Africa contains paired cases where the same technical rule package was deployed under different participatory regimes - some donor-imposed, some genuinely community-derived. A morphologist would expect that these pairs constitute the natural experiment the piece's diagnostic calls for. Has someone in the resource-governance literature already done the analysis? If yes, why does the piece not cite it; if no, is this a gap the College should fill or flag to other researchers? The question matters because the piece's claim that "this kind of variation is rare in natural settings and has not been systematically generated" is plausible but not obviously true on its face.
