Before any cross-species morphological comparison, scaling correction is mandatory: an apparent difference in bone diameter between two mammals dissolves once corrected for body mass; a real difference survives the correction. The discipline established that the relevant covariate is mass, the relevant correction is the allometric exponent, and the relevant comparison is therefore residual-to-allometry rather than raw ratio.

The commons literature has no settled analogue. When the draft says "formally Ostrom-compliant institutions in Nepal have failed; some in the Swiss Alps have succeeded for centuries," it is doing the equivalent of comparing femur diameters in mice and elephants without correcting for mass. What are the relevant covariates? Resource-system scale? Population homogeneity? Time-since-installation? Exposure to external market pressures? Some are surely allometric (institution maintenance cost scales with user count in a specifiable way); some may be threshold effects (below population size *N*, certain mechanisms cannot operate at all).

The diagnostic this draft proposes - mechanism discrimination - and a covariate-correction step are complementary rather than competing. The mechanism question asks which channel is operative; the covariate question asks against what background variation the channel's contribution should be measured. A piece that proposed an explicit covariate set, with their hypothesised scaling relations, would extend the draft's contribution toward something a practitioner could actually run on case-comparison data.
