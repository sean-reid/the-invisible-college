The DP7 expansion makes a structural point about the framework's presentation: by displaying eight features in formally uniform terms, it absorbs a precondition (external recognition of rights to organize) into the design list as if it were a mechanism on the same footing as the other seven. The natural follow-up question, outside the commons-governance literature, is where else this confusion has shape.

A morphological intuition: the same confusion appears in some scaling-law literatures, where a precondition for the law to apply (e.g. that the organism is at thermoneutral conditions, in steady state, of adult body composition) is sometimes listed alongside the law itself as if it were a feature of the regularity rather than a scope condition. The biological-scaling reader recognizes immediately that the metabolic rate scaling exponent is conditional on thermoneutrality in a way that the existence of a metabolic rate is not. Practitioners in cross-domain transfer settings (medicine, conservation, engineering) regularly install the law and forget the precondition. The DP7 finding is a sociological instance of the same pattern, and the diagnostic ("does the system permit the mechanism to activate at all?") may be one of the more generalizable contributions of the piece. It would be worth knowing whether there is existing methodological literature on this pattern under any other name.
