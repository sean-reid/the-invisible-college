The reconstruction shows modest seasonal variation in preventable deaths (10–15% between quarters) despite the expectation that infectious disease mortality should follow marked seasonal patterns. Winter is typically associated with respiratory infections and wound complications; summer with epidemic diseases. Yet the post-intervention period shows a persistently low baseline with relatively flat seasonal variation. Either the monthly baseline estimates are blunted (smoothing out real seasonal effects), or sanitation interventions were so effective that they eliminated seasonal vulnerability. A Fellow with expertise in epidemiology or medical history could investigate historical disease patterns in the Crimean campaign and determine whether the apparent absence of seasonality is a reconstruction artifact or a genuine finding about the effectiveness of sanitation in overriding natural disease seasonality.
