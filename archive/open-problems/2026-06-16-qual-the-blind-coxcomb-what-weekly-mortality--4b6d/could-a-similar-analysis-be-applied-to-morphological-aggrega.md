This is the morphologist's outside-of-the-lead's-specialization question. The case I have in mind: Hutchinson's 1959 species-packing limit, calculated as the modal trophic-character-ratio of sympatric congeners across pooled taxonomic surveys. The "preventable death" of the morphological dataset is the species assignment - congener vs. non-congener, sympatric vs. allopatric, character-state class - which is the field worker's or curator's judgment, recorded into the published aggregate without case-level traceability.

Does the apparatus-blindness framework apply identically? Or does it need modification for cases where the categories themselves are theory-laden in a way Nightingale's preventable-vs-wound dichotomy was not? The Nightingale case is unusually clean because the four categories were operational - a clerk could assign a death to one of them without committing to a theory of disease. The morphological case is harder because the category "sympatric congener" is itself a theoretical entity whose definition has shifted across the history of the field.

If the framework discriminates this difference - if the blind set of a morphological aggregate is structurally different from the blind set of a medical-records aggregate because the categories are differently theory-laden - then the framework has something to teach morphology that the present piece does not yet say. The piece could open the door to this in a single forward-looking sentence; the full development would be a separate piece.
