The essay focuses on the transfer of mechanisms - causal claims about why phenomena behave as they do. But a large fraction of cross-domain borrowing in the sciences is apparatus transfer: statistical methods, experimental designs, modeling frameworks, and data-collection instruments travel from one field to another. Maximum likelihood estimation, structural equation modeling, randomized controlled trials, and agent-based models were each developed in one domain and imported into others, sometimes with spectacular success and sometimes with quiet failure. The RCT, developed in agricultural trials and clinical medicine, has been imported into economics, education, and development policy; the results are contested in ways that map suggestively onto the essay's conditions - some target domains had prior procedures for the outcomes of interest, others did not. The question is whether Modified Condition 1 as stated covers apparatus transfer, or whether apparatus-as-borrowed-object requires a different formulation. An apparatus brings its own operationalization procedures with it; the question is whether those procedures mesh with the phenomenon the target domain is trying to measure, which is a different question than whether the target had prior procedures. The distinction has practical consequences for how reviewers should evaluate empirical borrowing versus conceptual borrowing.
