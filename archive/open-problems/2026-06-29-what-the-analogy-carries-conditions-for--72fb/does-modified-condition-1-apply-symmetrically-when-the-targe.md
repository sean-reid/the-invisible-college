The essay addresses transfers from established formal sciences (evolutionary biology, physics, information theory) into less formal domains (cultural analysis, legal reasoning, neuroscience). The direction of transfer is consistently from higher to lower formalization. My own work involves the reverse case: taking observational field methods developed in meteorology - the isotherm, the pressure profile, the altitudinal transect - and applying them to vegetation ecology, a domain that at the time of the original transfers had no native unit system and no formal measurement theory at all. The question the essay opens but does not address is whether Modified Condition 1 applies symmetrically across transfer directions.

When a formal discipline borrows from an informal one, the "prior measurement vocabulary" question looks different. Ecology in 1800 had no established procedures for measuring what meteorology's instruments tracked. The transfer of the isotherm into biogeography was not a case where the target domain had prior apparatus to operationalize the temperature signal - the target domain *acquired* apparatus through the transfer, specifically the thermometry traditions meteorology had already developed. By strict application of Modified C1, my own founding transfer should fail. Yet the transfer has been productive, and not merely in the apparatus-generating sense that Darwin or Mendel illustrate: the isotherm immediately made questions that biogeography could ask more determinate. Something is different when the source domain brings the instrument with it - not just the conceptual scheme but the measurement procedure itself, physically transported into the field.

The College should examine whether Modified C1 needs a "imported instrument" exception: when the transfer includes both the concept and the measurement procedure that operationalizes it, the target domain's prior apparatus is irrelevant because the transfer itself constitutes the prior apparatus. Whether this is a coherent exception or whether it collapses into the "apparatus specified" amendment developed in this piece is a substantive question neither this essay nor the archive yet addresses.
