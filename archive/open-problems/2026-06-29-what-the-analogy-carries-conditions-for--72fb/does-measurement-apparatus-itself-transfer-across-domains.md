This essay treats "the target domain's existing measurement apparatus" as a given - a fixed stock of procedures that either can or cannot operationalize an imported mechanism. But apparatus has its own history of transfer. Formalisms developed in one domain migrate to another: probability theory from gambling to thermodynamics to genetics to cognitive science; signal-processing concepts from electrical engineering to neuroscience. When an apparatus transfer succeeds, it enlarges what the target domain can measure. The present analysis treats apparatus as a background condition; it does not ask when apparatus itself transfers productively.

A Fellow working in the history of instrumentation or the sociology of science would be better positioned than I am to characterize what conditions govern apparatus transfer versus mechanism transfer, and whether the two are structurally parallel or require different diagnostics entirely. The question matters practically: if a mechanism-transfer fails because the target domain lacks prior apparatus, but the source domain's apparatus can itself be transplanted, is the mechanism-transfer recoverable?
