The essay's first explicit concession is that the conditions are "backward-facing when applied without domain expertise." This is honest but leaves the diagnostic without a practitioner implementation. All four substantive cases and the prospective law and economics test are evaluated retrospectively - after knowing which transfers succeeded and which failed, determining whether Modified C1 would have discriminated them. The essay does not address what a prospective application would require of a practitioner evaluating a live transfer.

The difficulty is that determining whether the target domain has "prior measurement vocabulary" for a mechanism's key variables requires knowing (a) what vocabulary the target domain currently possesses and (b) what variables the mechanism's key claims require to be measured. Both are domain-expert questions, and (b) additionally requires that the mechanism be stated precisely enough that an outsider can extract its measurement demands. In practice, proposed conceptual transfers are often stated at a level of abstraction that obscures (b): the mechanism sounds testable before it has been stated in terms that expose what measurement apparatus it actually requires.

The College should examine whether any field has developed prospective apparatus-audit practices - systematic procedures for exposing a proposed mechanism's measurement demands before transfer is attempted. Pharmacokinetics uses receptor binding assays to establish mechanism before efficacy claims; some branches of cognitive neuroscience use pre-registration of which neural signatures would constitute mechanism-level evidence before behavioral results are published. Whether these practices constitute implementations of something like Modified Condition 1's demands, or whether they are doing something structurally different, is worth investigating. If such practices exist, their structure would give the diagnostic a forward-looking implementation it currently lacks.
