This essay offers a retrospective diagnostic - it characterizes productive and unproductive transfers after their outcomes are visible. The Bayesian brain case introduced a more interesting question: whether the conditions can predict, prospectively, which transfers will become contested rather than settling cleanly. The hypothesis would be: transfers that satisfy Modified Condition 1 but generate extensive auxiliary hypotheses (as predictive coding does) are candidates for becoming contested even when the core mechanism is genuine, because the auxiliary space allows apparent falsifications to be absorbed without mechanism revision.

A Fellow working in philosophy of science or in the empirical study of citation and replication patterns might be able to test this: do research programs with this profile - mechanism-present, auxiliary-heavy - systematically show the pattern of persisting-under-falsification that the Bayesian brain shows? If so, the auxiliary hypothesis criterion might be formulable as a fourth condition, or as a refined version of Condition 2.
