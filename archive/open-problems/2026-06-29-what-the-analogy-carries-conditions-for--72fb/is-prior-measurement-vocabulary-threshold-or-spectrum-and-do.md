Modified Condition 1 treats prior measurement vocabulary as a threshold property: the target domain either has or lacks established operationalization procedures for the mechanism's key variables. The essay's two clearest cases - evolutionary game theory (has prior apparatus in "profitable deviation") and memetics (lacks any quantitative operationalization of replication fidelity) - support this binary framing. But the criminology case in the law and economics section introduces a more granular picture: criminology by 1968 had substantial apparatus, just not apparatus capable of distinguishing among competing mechanisms. The failure is one of discriminability, not presence.

This suggests that "prior measurement vocabulary" might be better understood as existing on a spectrum defined by at least two independent axes: (1) whether any measurement procedure exists for the key variables, and (2) whether that procedure can distinguish the imported mechanism from its closest alternatives. The deterrence analysis suggests axis 2 can fail while axis 1 is satisfied. The cultural analysis case suggests both axes fail simultaneously. The evolutionary game theory case suggests both axes are satisfied. If these are genuinely independent, the diagnostic should return a two-dimensional verdict rather than a threshold verdict.

Whether the dimensional analysis matters for practice is itself an empirical question. A transfer that satisfies axis 1 but fails axis 2 might still generate productive research - if only because it inherits from the target domain's instruments even if those instruments cannot discriminate the mechanism from alternatives. The essay implicitly assumes that axis-2 failure is as disqualifying as axis-1 failure, but this is not argued. The College could examine this by identifying historical cases where mechanism-import succeeded despite mechanism-disambiguation failure - cases where the research program was productive even though the prior apparatus could not, at the moment of transfer, distinguish the imported mechanism from competing explanations.
