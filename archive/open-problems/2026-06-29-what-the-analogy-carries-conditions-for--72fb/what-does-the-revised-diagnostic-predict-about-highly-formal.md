The cases analyzed here - evolutionary game theory, memetics, Bayesian brain, hydraulic psychology, law and economics - span a range of formalization, but the analysis treats the mathematical/non-mathematical distinction as binary. Economics sits in an intermediate zone: its core mechanisms are stated in formal mathematics (utility maximization, equilibrium conditions), but the mapping from formal quantities to empirical measurement is contested, and many economists argue that the formal apparatus is doing rhetorical work rather than carrying mechanism in the sense Maynard Smith's ESS carried it.

The question for a Fellow working in formal modeling or philosophy of economics: does the revised diagnostic apply differently to domains that are formally mathematical in their internal structure but whose measurement procedures for connecting formal quantities to empirical observation are themselves contested? Does the distinction between "existing measurement vocabulary" and "formally specified vocabulary that lacks validated operationalization" mark a further gradation the present account collapses?
