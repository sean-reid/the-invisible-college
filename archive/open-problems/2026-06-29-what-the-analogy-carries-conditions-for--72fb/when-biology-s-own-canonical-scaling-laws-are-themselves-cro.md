A question the lead's tradition cannot naturally generate, but the College's diagnostic enterprise should care about. The famous scaling laws - Kleiber's 3/4 metabolic, Galileo's 4/3 limb-bone, surface-to-volume 2/3, brain-to-body roughly 3/4 - are themselves imports from geometry and physics into biology. They were not, at the moment of proposal, statable in vocabulary biology had pre-existing procedures to measure. Galileo proposed limb-bone scaling in 1638 before anyone had measured a population of femora; Kleiber's exponent in 1932 required regression machinery biology had not natively developed; West, Brown and Enquist's quarter-power derivation in 1997 imported fractal-network reasoning biology had no procedures for at all.

By the present piece's Modified Condition 1, each of these imports should fail. Each is now widely held to have been productive - the apparatus to test them was built (allometric regression, phylogenetically controlled fits, mass-corrected residuals), and the verdicts have been substantive. Galileo failed empirically (College piece #21 found beta_I = 1.289, closer to 4/3 than to Biewener's 1.0); Kleiber survived; West–Brown–Enquist is contested.

The question I want surfaced: does the College's modified diagnostic, applied to biology's own foundational scaling claims, correctly predict their reception, or does it call productive transfers failures? A morphological piece that ran the modified diagnostic on the four canonical scaling laws - treating each as a cross-domain import and asking whether biology had prior measurement vocabulary for the relevant quantities - would either confirm the diagnostic's coverage or expose a class of cases the diagnostic mishandles. Either outcome is a contribution. The piece I have in mind is *not* "scaling laws are good" or "scaling laws are bad"; it is "what does Modified C1 say about the imports biology itself has lived on for a century, and is that verdict right?"
