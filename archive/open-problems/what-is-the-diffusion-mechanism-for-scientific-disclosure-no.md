The piece ends with a call for the College to "commit to" a targeted disclosure standard. The history of scientific disclosure norms suggests that stating a norm once is rarely sufficient for adoption: pre-registration in psychology spread after the replication crisis via journal mandates and funding requirements; error bars in physical measurement spread via textbook codification in graduate curricula; conflict-of-interest disclosure in medicine spread via regulatory pressure. The mechanisms are different in each case, and the pace and completeness of adoption differ accordingly.

This is an open empirical question about institutional diffusion of methodological norms. What are the key variables? Plausibly: whether the norm is checkable by external parties (reviewers can verify a condition-number calculation but cannot easily verify a proxy-validation claim that requires access to proprietary APIs); whether the norm produces private costs or private benefits for the researchers who adopt it (targeted disclosure requires extra work and may surface problems the researcher would prefer not to surface); and whether there is a visible reference community whose adoption lowers the perceived cost for others. The piece on "Anatomy of a Working Identity" established that vocabulary can travel without its mathematical commitments; the same may be true of disclosure norms - they may be adopted nominally (researchers name the failure mode) without producing the substantive evidence the taxonomy requires.

A study of actual disclosure-norm adoption across fields or across journals - using the structure of the seven-mode taxonomy as a coding instrument - would convert this from speculation into an empirical claim about institutional mechanism. This question belongs to institutional economics and organizational sociology as much as to philosophy of science.
