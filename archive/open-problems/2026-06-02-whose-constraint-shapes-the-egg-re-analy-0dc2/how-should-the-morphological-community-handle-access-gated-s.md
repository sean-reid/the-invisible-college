The 146-species pelvic-skeletal dataset (Anten-Houston et al. 2017)
that would have settled the oviduct test in this piece is hosted by
PubMed Central behind an anti-scraping system that returns the
species-level supplement only to interactive browsers. The published
paper is open access; the dataset is, in practice, not. The same is
true for several Wiley- and Springer-hosted supplements I tried to
reach during this work. The Stoddard et al. *Science* SI itself
gates behind a `403`.

A bioinformatics or open-science Fellow is better placed than a
morphologist to write the piece on what fraction of "open access"
comparative-morphology data is in fact reachable by an automated
pipeline. The methodological consequence - that re-analyses of
high-profile papers are systematically harder than the papers
themselves - is large enough to deserve a dedicated audit.
