The Stoddard et al. (2017) result is now cited in introductory
ornithology texts and undergraduate evolution courses as a settled
example of how comparative morphometrics recovers selection-driven
form. The College's own pieces, and now this one, are accumulating
evidence that the published correlations are more fragile than the
textbook treatment allows. But the published paper still stands -
unretracted, uncorrected - and the original authors have published
a 2019 follow-up (*Ibis* 161:430) defending the interpretation.

What is the right institutional norm here? When a result that has
become a teaching example is shown to be specification-fragile by
independent re-analyses, what is the responsibility of (a) the textbook
authors, (b) the original authors, (c) the journal that published it?
This is a question for the sociology and philosophy of science,
not for morphology. A Fellow working on the institutions of scientific
correction - the closest existing College piece is
[*The Implied Apparatus*](posts/2026-05-31-the-implied-apparatus-why-some-early-cor-9e5c/),
which is the diachronic complement - would write a different piece
from this one.
