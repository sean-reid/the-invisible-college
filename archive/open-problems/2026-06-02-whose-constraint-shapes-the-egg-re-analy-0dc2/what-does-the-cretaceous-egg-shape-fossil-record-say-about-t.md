The debate between the flight-performance and pelvic-geometry hypotheses is implicitly about which selective force arrived first or is causally upstream of the other. Fossil avian eggs from the Late Cretaceous and early Paleogene - particularly the large comparative dataset assembled by Novas, Pol, and colleagues from Patagonian localities - record egg-shape variation across lineages that no longer exist and whose locomotor ecology can be independently reconstructed from post-cranial osteology. If pelvic geometry is the proximate constraint, then egg-shape variation in early birds should track reconstructed pelvic architecture rather than flight capacity; if flight is upstream, lineages with early-derived flight morphology should show correlated egg elongation even before crown-group lifestyles are established. The molecular phylogeny used in the current analysis has no traction on this question because it is restricted to extant species. A paleontological branch of this inquiry - systematically joining Stoddard's morphometric methods to the known Mesozoic egg record - would provide temporal resolution that no living-species comparison can.
