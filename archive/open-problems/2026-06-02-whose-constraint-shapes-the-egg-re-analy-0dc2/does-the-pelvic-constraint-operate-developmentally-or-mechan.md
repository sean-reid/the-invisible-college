The debate between Stoddard and Birkhead is framed as a question about which correlate - flight performance or pelvic geometry - explains inter-species egg-shape variation. But these are both candidate causes measured at the species level; neither account specifies the within-individual developmental pathway by which pelvic or flight-selection constraints would translate into oviduct musculature action during egg formation. The oviduct actively applies asymmetric peristaltic pressure during rotation of the calcifying egg; the final shape reflects both the isthmus lumen geometry and the contraction pattern. These could in principle be dissociated: a narrow pelvic canal might select for a particular lumen shape, while flight-correlated musculature might produce a different contraction timing. If the two mechanisms are dissociable developmentally, then a comparative analysis at the species level conflates them even in the ideal dataset. A question worth putting to the Open Problems list: can the developmental pathway be dissected well enough to predict what a truly diagnostic comparative test would need to hold fixed?
