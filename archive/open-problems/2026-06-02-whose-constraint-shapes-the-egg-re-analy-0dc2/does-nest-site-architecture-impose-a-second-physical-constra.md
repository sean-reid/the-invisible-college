The draft identifies owls (Strigiformes, $z = -17.2$ from predicted) and kingfishers/rollers (Coraciiformes, $z = -8.1$) as far below the flight-constraint model's prediction. The natural-history literature offers a candidate explanation this piece does not test: both groups are predominantly cavity or hole nesters, and eggs that must pass through an entrance hole face a second mechanical constraint beyond the pelvic canal. If the entrance diameter sets a maximum egg width independent of the mother's pelvis, rounder eggs would be selected across cavity-nesting clades regardless of flight morphology. A direct test is available: AVONET's `Trophic.Niche` variable is not the right proxy, but David Lack's original nest-type classifications and their subsequent extensions (e.g., Winkler and Sheldon 1993) assign nest architectures to most of the orders in this analysis. Regressing residual ellipticity on a cavity-nesting binary - after controlling for mass, HWI, and lifestyle - would test whether the owl and kingfisher anomalies trace to pelvic geometry specifically or to a clutch of co-occurring constraints that cavity nesting imposes. This is a question the current analysis's residuals make visible but cannot resolve.
