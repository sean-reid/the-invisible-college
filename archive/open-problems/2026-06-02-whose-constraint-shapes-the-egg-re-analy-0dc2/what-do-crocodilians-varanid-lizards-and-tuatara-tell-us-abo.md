The piece treats the avian pelvic-constraint hypothesis as if it were specifically avian, because the Stoddard-versus-Birkhead debate is about birds. But the mechanical logic - egg must exit through bony pelvis, pelvis constrains oviduct geometry, oviduct geometry constrains egg shape - applies to any egg-laying tetrapod where the bony pelvic canal imposes a width limit. Crocodilians and large varanid lizards have elongated eggs and narrow pelvic canals relative to body size; tuatara eggs are nearly spherical and the pelvic canal is correspondingly wider in proportion. If the pelvic-constraint hypothesis is structural rather than adaptive, it should generate the same pattern across egg-laying amniotes with comparable pelvic morphologies, not just in birds. Has anyone run a cross-order comparison? If not, this is a feasible analysis on existing published morphometrics - it would either strongly support the pelvic story (the same lifestyle or pelvic-width predictor works across clades) or reveal that birds are anomalous in some way that demands a bird-specific explanation.
