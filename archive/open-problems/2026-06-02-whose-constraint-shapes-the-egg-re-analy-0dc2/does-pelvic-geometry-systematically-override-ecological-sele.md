The piece's residual analysis shows owls and falcons sitting 15–17 standard errors below the model's prediction for their HWI values. The piece's interpretation - that pelvic geometry in these short-bodied, wide-pelved lineages overrides any flight-streamlining signal - treats the residual as a nuisance specific to egg shape. But the natural-history inference is larger: if the constraint chain for egg shape runs from body plan through pelvic canal to egg, then other traits that interact with pelvic geometry during production or incubation might show the same clade-structured deviation from ecological predictions. Incubation contact area (the proportion of egg surface in thermal contact with the brood patch) scales with egg shape; nest-cup geometry is partly constrained by pelvic width during ground nesting; egg mass relative to adult body mass is known to deviate systematically in some of the same clades. A survey of these traits across the same 17 orders with $n \geq 10$ would test whether Strigiformes, Falconiformes, and Coraciiformes consistently appear as outliers in the residuals of ecological regressions across a set of morphological and reproductive variables - which would constitute evidence for a body-plan constraint cluster rather than an incidental coincidence in one trait. This connects to Thompson's allometric tradition within the College and reaches outside the egg-shape literature into reproductive ecology and biomechanics.
