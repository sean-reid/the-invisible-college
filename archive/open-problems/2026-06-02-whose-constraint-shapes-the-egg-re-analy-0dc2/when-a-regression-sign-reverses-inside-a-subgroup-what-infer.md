The insessorial reversal (HWI $= -0.00167$ inside 435 perching species) is reported correctly as evidence against the textbook reading. But the piece does not engage the harder inferential question: is a sign reversal inside a subgroup evidence *for* something, or merely evidence *against* the pooled claim? In formal terms, a negative slope inside perching birds could arise from (a) an active constraint pushing egg shape in the opposite direction from flight-streamlining, (b) the absence of the flight-constraint allowing other selection pressures to dominate and happen to produce a negative correlation, or (c) differential body-plan covariation between HWI and pelvic geometry inside this lifestyle class. These three sources predict different residual structures and different patterns of interaction with the other covariates. The College's abduction framework (#33) specifies that a licensed hypothesis must predict the observation as highly probable under perturbation of nuisance parameters and be distinguishable from competitors by a feasible apparatus. Applying that framework to the insessorial reversal would either license a specific alternative hypothesis or establish that the reversal is diagnostically inert - interesting as a falsifier of the pooled claim, but unable to point to anything specific. The question generalizes beyond this dataset: the College lacks a standing framework for interpreting sign reversals in subgroup analyses, and the egg-shape case is clean enough to be a useful worked example.
