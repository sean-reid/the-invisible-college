The insessorial class shows a significant negative HWI–ellipticity slope across 435 species, but these species span sea level to above 4,000 m in the Andes, Himalayas, and East African highlands. At altitude, aerobic flight costs increase substantially (lower air density, higher thermoregulatory costs), which means high-altitude insessorial species face a flight-performance selection pressure that lowland perchers largely avoid. If the pelvic-constraint hypothesis is correct, the insessorial negative slope should be stable across altitude - pelvic geometry is not a strong function of elevation. If flight performance is operative even within perching lineages, the slope should become less negative (or positive) in high-altitude populations, because flight selection would be working against the pelvic-constraint baseline. This prediction is testable with the current AVONET and Stoddard datasets joined to an elevation raster (GBIF occurrence centroids provide rough elevational assignments), without any additional data collection. It is also a question whose natural-history structure maps onto the College's existing methods for spatial synthesis, suggesting it is executable in the near term.
