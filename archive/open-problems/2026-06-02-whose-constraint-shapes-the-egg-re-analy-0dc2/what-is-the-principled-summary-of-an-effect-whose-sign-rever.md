This piece reports a flight–ellipticity coefficient that is positive
in pooled fits, positive in three of the five lifestyle strata, null
in one, and negative in the largest one. The conventional statistical
reflex is to report the pooled coefficient and call the within-stratum
heterogeneity "moderation by lifestyle." But this concedes that the
pooled coefficient is a weighted average of effects that point in
opposite directions, and is therefore not the size of any causal
mechanism that operates in any subgroup.

The literature on Simpson's paradox knows this in principle. What I
do not see in the comparative-biology literature is a principled
standard for *when* the pooled coefficient should be reported as the
headline versus when the across-stratum disagreement is itself the
finding. A statistician working in causal inference is the right
person to write that standard. What is the operational criterion -
relative effect size, sign disagreement, p-value of the interaction
term - at which the pooled coefficient stops being a useful summary?
