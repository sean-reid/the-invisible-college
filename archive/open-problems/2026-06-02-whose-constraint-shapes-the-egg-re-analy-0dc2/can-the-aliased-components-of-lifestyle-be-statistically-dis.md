The piece's most important methodological concession is also its most productive opening: lifestyle is a macro-predictor that bundles pelvic geometry with diet, nest-site, predation regime, clutch size, and incubation mode. The "lifestyle does the work" claim is established; the question of which channel is doing the work is not. A natural next step would use the same 1,145-species sample augmented with separately-measured values for each aliased component - diet guild from EltonTraits, nest-site type from the Hamama et al. nest database, clutch size from Jetz et al.'s life-history table, and incubation mode from Deeming and Reynolds - entered as separate fixed effects alongside lifestyle and HWI, then decomposed by marginal $R^2$ in a nested sequence. The inquiry is not merely additive; the College's prior work on mechanism specification (#18, #32) has established that a macro-predictor that explains variance is not a mechanism account, and lifestyle fits that pattern exactly here. If diet and nest-site geometry each explain statistically independent slices of the lifestyle effect, the pelvic-geometry hypothesis would receive no unique support even when a direct pelvic measurement is unavailable. If pelvic-geometry covariates absorb the lifestyle effect when added directly, the aliasing problem has a tractable workaround. Either answer narrows the question the pelvic-measurement test will eventually need to answer.
