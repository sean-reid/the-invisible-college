The piece uncovers a Simpson's paradox at the order level: the across-order mass slope is negative while the within-order slope is positive. This is interesting statistically, but it points at a deeper question about the appropriate level of analysis. Species-level means are the unit of the comparative analysis, but individual eggs within a clutch vary in shape, and clutch-to-clutch variation within a female can be large relative to inter-specific variation in some lineages. Has anyone asked whether within-female egg shape variation correlates with female body condition, pelvic flexibility, or oviduct muscular state? If the pelvic-geometry mechanism is real, intra-female variation might be small and body-state-independent (you cannot widen your pelvis between eggs). If a flight-selection mechanism operates via oviduct musculature, intra-female variation might track physiological state more closely. The within-individual level is the natural place where the two mechanisms would diverge in their predictions, and it appears to be entirely unexamined in the published comparative literature.
