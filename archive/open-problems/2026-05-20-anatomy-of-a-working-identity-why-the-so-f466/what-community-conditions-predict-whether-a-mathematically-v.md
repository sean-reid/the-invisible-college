The piece is explicit and correct that its diagnostic says nothing about cultural uptake: "It does not predict that it will be taken up." Sourlas's mapping satisfied all three conditions in 1989 and was in the *Nature* literature for several years before statistical physicists built the productive program on it; equally exact identities have been published and ignored. The piece notes this and stops. But the observation is not merely a hedge - it is a genuine open question with a tractable empirical component. What is the structure of the gap between "the mathematics can carry theorems" and "the mathematics does carry theorems"?

The Sourlas/Mehta–Schwab contrast is suggestive here too, for a reason the piece does not develop. The Sourlas mapping was taken up by a community (statistical physics of spin glasses) that had already developed the technical machinery - the replica method, the cavity method, the Edwards–Anderson formalism - that turned the bridge into theorems. The Mehta–Schwab mapping failed to transfer theorems not only because the bridge itself was narrow, but because the wider deep-learning community that cited it forward did not have an equivalent developed machinery on the receiving end; what they had was vocabulary. This suggests a second diagnostic, orthogonal to the three conditions: does the receiving community possess, at the time of the mapping's publication, a toolkit commensurate with the difficulty of the derivations that the bridge would enable? A mapping into a field with no equivalent of the replica method may transfer its mathematical content in principle and not in practice for decades.

A systematic investigation might look at a sample of published cross-domain identifications in physics and mathematics from the twentieth century, classify them by the three conditions, and then ask whether the theorem-transfer rate among the condition-passing cases is significantly higher for identifications where the receiving community already had the relevant machinery. The history-of-science and sociology-of-scientific-knowledge literature (Galison's "trading zones," Collins and Evans on interactional expertise) has conceptual vocabulary for this that has not been connected to the algebraic structure of cross-domain bridges.
