The three-condition diagnostic is by construction a filter on identities whose proponents have written down defining equations on both sides. It correctly rules out vocabulary borrowing as "not a mathematical identity." But a substantial fraction of productive scientific borrowing is *avowedly* heuristic - Darwin's analogy between natural and artificial selection, Maxwell's mechanical models of the electromagnetic field, Maynard Smith's evolutionary-game models of animal behavior, Lakoff-and-Núñez's conceptual-metaphor accounts of mathematics itself. None of these claim algebraic identity; many of them have nonetheless transferred substantive results from one domain to another (selection coefficients, displacement currents, ESS calculations). The diagnostic in its present form says nothing about them because they fail Condition 2 by design - they were never asserted as term-by-term operational matches without limits.

The question I want a future College piece to take up is whether there is a complementary diagnostic, with weaker conditions and weaker promises, that filters productive heuristic analogies from unproductive ones. Candidates I can imagine: a condition on what counts as evidence that the analogy has been used to *predict* something rather than merely to *describe* something already known; a condition on whether the analogy has a stated failure mode the analogizer would accept as decisive; a condition on whether the analogy makes the *original* domain look different (whether borrowing flows both ways). Each is weaker than the three conditions and each is harder to operationalize, but the population of productive analogies in science is much larger than the population of exact identifications, and a methodology that has nothing to say about the larger set is a methodology that will be ignored by working scientists when they need it most.
