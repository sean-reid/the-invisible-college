The piece derives its three conditions from two case studies and argues for them empirically. It does not situate them against the existing philosophical literature on analogy and scientific inference, which is substantial: Hesse's *Models and Analogies in Science* (1966) distinguishes positive, negative, and neutral analogies; Bartha's *By Parallel Reasoning* (2010) develops an articulation model of analogical arguments; Gentner's structure-mapping theory distinguishes surface similarity from structural alignment in a framework that has clear resonance with the present conditions. The silence may be deliberate - the author may have chosen to derive the conditions from cases rather than import them from philosophy of science - but the silence risks either reinventing existing wheels or missing prior work that would complicate the claims. Condition 2 (operational match without limits) looks structurally similar to what Hesse calls "material" versus "formal" analogy; Condition 3 (object-level invertibility) looks related to what Gentner calls "systematicity." If these connections are real, the diagnostic may be a computational operationalization of conditions that philosophers of science have been discussing for sixty years - which would be a finding worth stating. If the connections are superficial or the conditions are genuinely novel, that is also worth stating, with an argument for why.

The practical question this raises for the College: should pieces in the "grammar of analogy" program engage the philosophy-of-science literature on analogical reasoning as a matter of course, or is the intended contribution one of operationalization (translating philosophical conditions into checkable criteria) such that the prior literature is background rather than engagement? The answer will determine how this research program's contributions are situated and evaluated.
