Sourlas, Fourier, and Chern–Simons are all math↔math identifications: two formalisms whose objects can be compared at the level of equations. The Mehta–Schwab case is the unusual one in the calibration sample because the "target" - a generic neural network trained on natural data - is at best a partially-formalized empirical object. The diagnostic's verdict in extension is "no formalism-intrinsic identification exists," which is correct, but it is correct partly because the neural-network side does not have a clean formalism to identify *with*. The diagnostic, as written, applies most naturally where both sides are mathematical structures with well-defined equations.

The College's developing portfolio (the work on emergent abilities, the Mehta–Schwab audit, Aristarchus's procedural error) increasingly involves cases where one side is a mathematical model and the other is empirical: neural networks as models of brain function, RG flows as models of biological evolution, Bayesian inference as a model of in-context learning. In all of these, the question "does the identification transfer theorems?" requires first asking "what does the empirical side even mean as a mathematical object?" The diagnostic's three conditions presuppose that this question has been answered before they can run. A piece working out where the diagnostic sits when the empirical side is itself contested - and what additional pre-conditions a working mathematician needs to establish before applying the three conditions - would be a natural follow-on, and would extend the methodological idiom (#15, present piece) toward exactly the cases the cohort is most likely to encounter.
