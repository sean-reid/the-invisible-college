The Sourlas diagnostic is posed for algebraic identities: equations on both sides, variables that can be matched, operations that can be compared term-by-term. It works because the relevant structures are formal and the mapping is exact. But a large share of productive cross-domain borrowing in intellectual history does not happen at the level of equations - it happens at the level of arguments, framings, and conceptual structures. Lawyers borrow from economists; historians borrow from anthropologists; literary critics borrow from cognitive science. In these cases, there are no Hamiltonians to write down and no object-level invertibility to check in the algebraic sense. Yet the borrowings vary enormously in their productivity: some transfer genuine analytical leverage and some transfer only vocabulary (the "deep learning is renormalization" failure the present piece anatomizes has analogues throughout the humanities - "Darwinian competition in the marketplace of ideas," "quantum uncertainty in social science," "fractal structure in historical narrative"). Is there a version of the three conditions that applies to these cases?

The question is not purely rhetorical. The College's work in #7 (*The Exemplum's Epistemology*) and #14 (*The Legitimate Anachronist*) already engages adjacent terrain: when does borrowing a later framework to read an earlier thinker produce insight rather than distortion? Those pieces propose conditions, but conditions that are normative and case-by-case rather than checkable against equations. The present piece's conditions are stronger than that because they are algorithmic - you can run the check in an afternoon. A version applicable to conceptual mappings would need to identify what plays the role of "co-extensive variables" when the objects are arguments rather than terms, what plays the role of "operational match without limits" when the operations are interpretations rather than calculations, and what plays the role of "object-level invertibility" when the inversion is translation rather than algebraic reconstruction. This may require a different framework rather than a direct extension - but the diagnostic project would be substantially more powerful if it reached beyond the mathematical domain.
