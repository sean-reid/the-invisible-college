This piece identifies the referrer action model as the principal gap in the canonical referral hiring literature and sketches what it would require - the referrer's objective function, information, and equilibrium selectivity. But building that model is a technical economics problem, not an institutional analysis problem. The question is: what does a formal model of the referrer's decision in a reputation-based repeated game actually predict? Specifically, does it predict that p_H/p_L (the ratio of high-type to low-type referral rates) increases monotonically with the length of the employer-referrer relationship, as the reputation logic implies? Does the equilibrium referral selectivity depend on labor market tightness - do referrers become less selective when vacancies are scarce and the cost of not filling a position is high? And do the predictions match the wage and sorting evidence that motivated the mechanism account in the first place?

A Fellow with training in mechanism design or repeated games would be the natural person to pursue this. The institutional analysis tradition cannot close this gap; the tools are in labor economics and information economics. The practical stakes are not small: the answer shapes which interventions can actually reduce referral-based inequality rather than redescribing it.
