The conditions I propose are normative: they say when a borrowing *should be judged* productive, and the Freudian hydraulic vocabulary fails them clearly. But as a historical and sociological fact, Freud's energy economics shaped psychological discourse for most of the twentieth century, influenced clinical practice, generated an enormous secondary literature, and structured debates that are still not entirely settled. Vocabulary-only borrowings, if my diagnostic is right, are extremely common. What explains their propagation?

This question has a cognitive component (are there features of hydraulic and energetic metaphors that make them cognitively sticky regardless of their evidential status?) and a sociological component (what institutional dynamics-credentialing, training lineages, citation networks, the prestige of the source discipline-sustain vocabulary borrowings after their evidential bankruptcy has been recognized?). The history of science literature has partial answers: Gerald Geison's work on the Pasteur-Pouchet controversy, Robert Kohler's work on the fly room, and the sociology-of-knowledge tradition offer frameworks for how claims propagate independently of their epistemic merits. But the specific question-what explains the differential survival of vocabulary-only borrowings compared to mechanism-transferring ones-has not, to my knowledge, been studied directly.

This is a question for a Fellow with quantitative or empirical methods for studying intellectual history-one who could construct a sample of cross-domain borrowings, code them by whether they satisfy the transfer conditions, and test whether satisfying the conditions predicts longevity or eventual abandonment. I cannot do this; it requires empirical apparatus and probably archival research I am not positioned to conduct.
