The essay produces three conditions as qualitative diagnostics and applies them by close reading of four canonical cases. The AI/ML extension in the Assessment section gestures at a broader population - the vocabulary of cognitive science applied to transformer architectures - but applies the diagnostic impressionistically rather than systematically. This opens a question that is not primarily philosophical: can the three conditions be given operational definitions precise enough to apply to a corpus of papers without expert judgment on each case?

Condition 1 has a natural operational form: the vocabulary-free translation test. If a framework has been genuinely transposed, it should be possible to describe what it does without source-domain vocabulary. This could, in principle, be operationalized as an ablation experiment on a paper's text: remove the borrowed vocabulary, replace it with paraphrases, and ask whether the resulting claims are weaker, equivalent, or dependent on the vocabulary for their specificity. Human raters could make this judgment; with a sufficient annotation corpus, an automated classifier might approximate it. The test is not trivial - determining whether a paraphrase "preserves the specific causal commitments" requires understanding those commitments - but it is not obviously harder than other annotation tasks in scientometrics.

Condition 2 is harder. It requires knowing (a) what predictions the mechanism generated, (b) whether those predictions were tested by means motivated by the mechanism, and (c) how the field responded to predictive failures. This is partly an empirical question about citation patterns: papers that operationalize hydraulic constructs, test them, find negative results, and are then cited by papers that retain the vocabulary but loosen its physical commitments leave a detectable trace in the literature. The catharsis research tradition is exactly this kind of trace. A bibliometric investigation could, in principle, identify vocabulary-persistence-despite-predictive-failure in a corpus of interdisciplinary borrowings and ask whether the three conditions predict which borrowings exhibit this pattern.

Whether this is a tractable research program is genuinely uncertain. The annotation requirements are demanding, and the cases the essay uses as anchors are canonical precisely because they are exceptionally well-documented. A large-scale survey would need to work with far less well-documented borrowings. But the essay's shift from "generates no testable predictions" to "shed evidential obligations under the pressure of its own predictions' failure" is exactly the kind of precise behavioral definition that opens this door. The difference between "untestable" and "tested, failed, survived by loosening commitments" is, in principle, detectable in the publication record. The College should investigate whether it is detectable in practice.
