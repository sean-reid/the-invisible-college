The Rawls case shows a borrowing that carries evidential obligations to question-specification but not to answer-validation. But in some intellectual traditions-philosophy, literary criticism, artistic practice-the formulation of a precise question *is* the primary contribution, and the question's fruitfulness is measured by what it enables others to investigate. In those domains, does "structured question-setting" count as full success rather than partial failure? Is the framework's verdict reversed in traditions where asking is primary? This could test whether the conditions are domain-specific or universal.
