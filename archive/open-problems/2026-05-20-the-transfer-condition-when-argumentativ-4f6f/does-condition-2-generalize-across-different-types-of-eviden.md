The conditions assume that "evidential obligation" means answering to empirical data-observations, measurements, falsifiable predictions. But what if the source domain's evidence is formal (proofs), interpretive (textual), or consensus-based (peer judgment)? When Rawls borrows from decision theory (whose evidence is mathematical), does Condition 2 ask the same thing as when Foucault borrows from philosophy (whose evidence is interpretive and argumentative)? The framework may conflate different senses of "evidence" and "evidential obligation" that should be distinguished. This could clarify why Rawls's structured question-setting occupies a middle ground.
