The essay's closing paragraph names the etiological question it does not answer: why do vocabulary-only borrowings propagate, survive, and sometimes dominate their target domains even after the evidential failure has been documented? The essay offers a brief list - institutional prestige transfer, in-group signaling, the laundering of one field's authority into another - and then steps back, correctly treating this as a complementary inquiry rather than a competing one. The normative question has to be answered first, and the essay answers it.

But the etiological question is now open and the essay has given it better-than-usual diagnostic resolution. We now know, more precisely than before, what "failure" means: the framework shed its evidential obligations under the pressure of its own predictions' failure, demonstrating that those obligations were severable. This is a behavioral definition that has empirical purchase: the hydraulic model generated predictions, those predictions were tested (Bushman 2002 and the catharsis research program) and refuted, and the vocabulary survived the refutation by loosening the physical commitments that had generated the predictions. We know this happened; the essay explains why it constitutes failure. The question "why does it persist?" is now a well-formed sociological research question, not a vague gesture at intellectual pathology.

The sociology of scientific knowledge (SSK) and the sociology of scientific error have tools for this question that the current essay does not deploy. Sociologists working in the tradition of Bloor, Collins, and Latour have studied how scientific communities stabilize beliefs, negotiate anomalies, and maintain research programs through boundary work, credentialing, and social network dynamics. The specific mechanism by which a framework survives the falsification of its central predictions - by retreating to a metaphorical rather than literal reading - is arguably a special case of what Lakatos called a "degenerating research program." But the institutional conditions under which a degenerating program maintains adherents, funding, and citation flow (psychoanalysis did so for decades after the behavioral evidence against catharsis had accumulated) is a question about social structure, not epistemic logic. That question is answerable by different methods: citation network analysis, institutional history, sociology of professions and therapy markets. The College should ask it.

One concrete entry point: the essay's three cases of failure or partial failure (Freud, and arguably Rawls's Condition 2 in its answer-validation function) differ substantially in how well their originating communities recognized and responded to evidential challenges. Psychoanalysis resisted; rational choice theory in political philosophy has had a genuine internal debate about the status of reflective equilibrium vs. formal derivation. These different institutional trajectories presumably track different sociological conditions. Mapping those conditions would produce a result the current essay cannot: a theory of when evidential failure propagates and when it is corrected, which is more actionable than a diagnostic of whether failure occurred.
