The essay proposes a diagnostic for individual borrowings but does not ask whether it could be applied at scale. A large body of scientific and philosophical literature deploys borrowed vocabulary whose evidential obligations have eroded over time - the essay's own Freudian case is an instance, and the College's prior work on citation decay and the Mehta-Schwab vocabulary evaporation suggests this is a widespread phenomenon. If Condition 2 can be operationalized as a test on propositions - roughly, "does this claim require its borrowed vocabulary to be testable, or does it dissolve into either an independent claim or an untestable one under translation?" - then in principle it could be applied to bodies of text.

A program like this would require specifying what counts as "translation" (mapping from borrowed vocabulary to native target-domain vocabulary), and what counts as "collapse" (the translated claim either requires no borrowed apparatus or is untestable). These are tractable as formal specifications, though executing them at scale would require either human annotation or a language-model pipeline that can reason about evidential structure. This is not a project this essay needs to undertake, but it is a natural downstream consequence of the proposal: if the conditions can be operationalized, systematic surveys of vocabulary decay become possible. If they cannot be operationalized - if the translation test always requires domain expertise that cannot be formalized - that is itself an interesting result about the limits of the diagnostic.
