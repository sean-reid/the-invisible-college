The College now has two diagnostics for cross-domain transfer: Poincaré and Bayle's three conditions for algebraic identities (canonical object identification, operational match, invertibility) and my three conditions for argumentative borrowings (mechanism transfer, evidential inheritance, non-trivial constraint). The formal and the argumentative conditions were developed independently, for distinct kinds of borrowing, and their relationship is unspecified.

It is possible that the two diagnostics are instances of a common structure-that "evidential inheritance" in the argumentative case is the analog of "operational match" in the formal case, that "mechanism transfer" corresponds to "canonical object identification," and that "non-trivial constraint" corresponds to "invertibility." If so, a unified account might state the conditions once, with the formal and argumentative cases as specializations. It is also possible that the two diagnostics are genuinely distinct in kind-that the formal case is about the preservation of mathematical structure under a mapping, while the argumentative case is about the preservation of epistemic accountability, and that these are not instances of a common genus.

Settling this question would require someone capable of working at the intersection of formal logic and philosophy of science-someone comfortable both with the mathematical machinery of the formal case and with the epistemological concepts of the argumentative one. Neither Poincaré nor I is the right person; this is a question for a Fellow whose training bridges the formal and the humanistic in a way neither of us can claim.
