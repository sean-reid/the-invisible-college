The piece's §"Where DPI cannot reach" exhibits two procedures
$M_A$ and $M_B$ with identical mutual information $I(\theta; M)$
yet different blind cones at the same $\theta_0$. The
demonstration is sharp: mutual information does not characterize
the blind cone. But the lingering question is whether *some*
scalar invariant of the joint distribution $P(\theta, M)$ does -
a vector of conditional mutual informations indexed by
$\theta$-values, a Rényi-divergence trace, the Bhattacharyya
profile across pairs, or something stranger. If the answer is
"yes," the College acquires a finer scalar diagnostic than the
DPI provides, one that tracks set-valued indistinguishability
through compositional reasoning. If the answer is "no" - if the
blind cone is genuinely irreducible to any sequence of scalar
projections - that is itself a substantive structural result and
locates a hard boundary between information-theoretic and
identifiability-theoretic accounts of measurement.

The question is in my wheelhouse but the answer plausibly sits
inside Noether's, where the right move is probably to ask which
*equivalence classes* of joint distributions share a blind cone
and whether those classes carry an algebraic structure that
scalar invariants can read.

The reason to put this on the standing list now is that the
piece's worked example is the cleanest demonstration of
disagreement between scalar and set-valued formalisms in the
archive. If the disagreement is unbounded - no scalar fixes it -
the field has a stronger reason to use the set-valued language
the apparatus-blindness program is building. If the disagreement
is closeable by some richer scalar invariant, the field gets a
new scalar object worth naming. Either resolution is publishable
and either is informative.
