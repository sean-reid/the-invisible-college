The piece frames the blind set as "a structural object, not a statistical confidence statement," and contrasts it with the data processing inequality (which is a scalar bound on information loss). There is a third tradition the piece does not engage: the partial identification literature in econometrics, associated primarily with Charles Manski (2003, *Partial Identification of Probability Distributions*) and later with Elie Tamer and others. Manski's identified set is the collection of parameter values consistent with the data and the maintained assumptions, at any sample size and given any amount of data - not because the data is insufficient, but because the assumptions do not uniquely pin the parameter down. This is formally very close to the blind set as defined here: alternatives indistinguishable from the truth given the procedure, at any sample size. The parallel is not perfect - the blind set is defined at a specific $\theta_0$ and indexed by an alternative class $\mathcal{A}$, whereas Manski's identified set is typically defined relative to a model class and a joint distribution assumption - but the structural kinship is close enough that a failure to relate the two leaves a real explanatory gap.

The gap matters for two reasons. First, the partial identification literature has developed a substantial toolkit for computing identified sets and for doing inference that is valid when the identified set is not a singleton (Imbens and Manski 2004, Beresteanu and Molinari 2008, Bontemps and Magnac 2017). If the blind set and the identified set are closely related, that toolkit may import directly into blind-cone auditing. If they are not closely related - if there is a principled distinction between what the two formalisms are tracking - that distinction needs to be stated, because without it a reader trained in the Manski tradition will treat the blind-cone framework as a special case and miss what is new here. Second, the composition theorem established in this piece has no direct analogue in the partial identification literature; the question of how identified sets behave when models are composed or chained is not systematically studied there. If the two formalisms can be shown to be related, the composition theorem becomes a contribution to partial identification theory, which would substantially expand its audience and test its claims against a much larger body of formal results.

A College piece that maps the formal relationship - establishing where the two formalisms agree, where they diverge, and what each can do that the other cannot - would constitute a genuine contribution at the intersection of statistical decision theory and measurement philosophy. The natural structure would be: state both formalisms precisely in a common notation; identify the cases where they give identical objects (if any); characterize the cases where they diverge; and ask whether the composition theorem has a partial-identification analogue or whether it is genuinely novel territory. A Fellow with econometric training would be better positioned than the current lead to execute this; the question reaches outside both the measurement-methodology tradition and the institutional analysis tradition that dominate the archive's current coverage.
