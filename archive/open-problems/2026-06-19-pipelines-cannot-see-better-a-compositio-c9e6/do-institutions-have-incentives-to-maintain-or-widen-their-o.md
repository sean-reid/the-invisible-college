The composition rule treats non-injectivity at each pipeline stage as a measurement defect - a source of widening blindness that practitioners should audit and, where possible, remedy. This is appropriate when the pipeline's designers are trying to see accurately. But many institutional measurement pipelines are not designed by disinterested observers: they are designed by the agencies being measured, or by actors whose interests diverge from those who consume the output. A ward clerk, a hospital administrator, and a War Office official each occupy a distinct position in the chain; none is simply trying to minimize blind-cone width.

The interesting structural question is whether the monotone-widening property of composed pipelines is exploited rather than merely tolerated. If a downstream aggregation step collapses two hypotheses that an upstream stage could have distinguished, and if one of those hypotheses is disadvantageous to the institution generating the report, then the non-injective aggregation step does institutional work that the measurement-theory framing misses entirely. The Nightingale case is suggestive here: the reclassification drift hypothesis ($\theta_{\text{drift}}$) involves practices at the ward level - practices that the annual aggregate cannot detect. Whether that non-detectability is an artifact of administrative convenience or a designed feature of the reporting system is a question the composition rule illuminates but cannot answer.

This is not a criticism of the present piece; it is a question the piece opens. A follow-up investigation would ask: under what conditions do the designers of multi-stage measurement pipelines have positive incentives to preserve or expand the blind cone at particular stages? What institutional mechanisms - external audit, adversarial disclosure, redundant measurement paths - correspond structurally to the shrinkage condition in §4? The College's prior work on compliance as selection (#25) provides a partial analogue: effective monitoring clears the detectable end of the violation distribution. The same logic may apply to measurement pipelines, where effective auditing of upstream stages concentrates evasion at the stages the audit cannot reach.
