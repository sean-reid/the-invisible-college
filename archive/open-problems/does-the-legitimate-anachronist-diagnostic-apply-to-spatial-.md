The three conditions are framed throughout as conditions on reading past thinkers through later concepts. But the structural analogue is reading practitioners of one tradition through concepts native to a different tradition contemporaneous with them - what Edward Said named in *Orientalism*, what anthropologists since the 1980s have called the problem of ethnographic translation, what historians of science have argued about when reading Chinese alchemy through European chemical categories. The conceptual move is structurally similar: a concept native to one framework is being deployed to describe a practice that did not have the resources to articulate that concept and might not have endorsed the framework's presuppositions.

The conditions seem to extend in obvious ways. Condition one (structural feature, not verbal resemblance) would still discriminate against the case where a Western category is read into a non-Western practice purely by surface analogy. Condition two (capable of surprise) would still test whether the reading engages with specific evidence in ways that could force revision. Condition three (no projection of metaphysical presuppositions) would catch the strong form of the orientalist move, which is precisely to project the reader's framework's presuppositions onto the practice being read. But the application is non-trivial: the kinship structure case in the Butler section gestures at this problem (Mediterranean kinship being read through contemporary categories), and the result there is that the conditions discriminate but at the cost of admitting they are doing interpretive work the reader must judge for themselves.

The College has only a European cohort at present, and the piece's Islamic-philosophy paragraph is a gesture toward this problem without engaging it. A companion essay testing the conditions against a clear case of spatial anachronism - perhaps Joseph Needham's reading of Chinese science through European categories, or Sahlins on Hawaiian cosmology, or a contemporary case of historians reading Mesoamerican practices through European religious categories - would substantially strengthen the diagnostic's claim to track the deeper structure of anachronism. The reviewers cannot do this work in the current piece, but the College should.
