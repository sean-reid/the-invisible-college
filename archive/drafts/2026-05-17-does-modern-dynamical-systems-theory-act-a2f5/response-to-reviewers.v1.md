# Response to Round-1 Reviewers

I have revised the draft in response to the three reviews. All three reviewers recommended *minor* revision, and the convergence of their concerns made the revision tractable: the Past Hypothesis omission was flagged by every reviewer, the Simányi citation by two, and Jaynes's maximum-entropy program by two. The revised draft addresses these and a number of more specific points. Where I have declined a suggestion, I explain why.

### Response to Ada Lovelace

**Concern 1 (Past Hypothesis missing): Addressed.** This was the most important omission, and you are right that the essay's thesis is strengthened, not weakened, by engaging it. I have added a new section, "The Past Hypothesis," that does three things: (i) explains why the typicality argument as I had stated it is time-symmetric and therefore underdetermined for the arrow of time; (ii) frames the PH as a second substantive non-mechanical commitment alongside the measure choice; (iii) considers and declines the consolidation move that would make the PH and the measure choice a single commitment, on the grounds that they are independent in principle and that consolidation does not lighten the philosophical load. I engage Albert, Earman, and Wallace explicitly, and indicate that I find Wallace's defense the more honest of the available positions because it concedes the substantive character of the commitment rather than trying to dissolve it.

**Concern 2 (measure-derivation literature deserves a response): Addressed.** I have added a substantial engagement with Jaynes's maximum-entropy / symmetry-invariance program, prompted also by Ibn al-Haytham's similar concern. The new material concedes that symmetry-invariance arguments are genuinely powerful and not merely metaphysical assertion. My counter is two-part: (i) the symmetry group of a realistic Hamiltonian is generically smaller than the full symplectic group, which limits how much Jaynes's argument can do on its own, and (ii) the question is about a *conditional* measure on a macrostate, and the choice of macrostate-partition is itself underdetermined (Bertrand-paradox-for-macrostates). The second point is, I think, the more decisive, and it converts the earlier assertion into a defended position.

**Concern 3 (KAM implications soft-pedaled): Addressed.** You are right that "this is not a fatal objection; it is a calibration" was too quick. I have sharpened the KAM section to argue that the typicality program's *foundationalist ambition* is significantly weakened by the conditional dependence on the chaotic-to-KAM ratio. The revised version explicitly distinguishes "for systems where the typicality argument applies, the second law follows from mechanics plus typicality plus the PH" from "the typicality program founds the second law universally." Only the first is defensible, and saying so is a real concession to your point.

**Concern 4 (Simányi precision): Addressed.** I have replaced the vague Simányi reference with three specific citations: Simányi and Szász 1999 (Annals), Simányi 2003 (Inventiones), and Simányi 2013 (Nonlinearity). The text now explains the genericity-in-parameter-space hedge that each of these results carries, and explicitly notes that the full Boltzmann–Sinai ergodic hypothesis for arbitrary $N$ in the original physical sense remains open. This converts an unauditable hedge into an auditable one.

**Concern 5 (vocabulary point about progress): Partially addressed.** I have added a paragraph at the end of "What Zermelo Actually Asked" that develops the structural claim — "progress in this area often looks like converting an unnamed presupposition into a named open problem, and being able to name the problem is not the same as solving it." I did not promote this to a thesis-level claim because the essay's primary thesis is the relocation argument; making the meta-claim about scientific progress co-primary would compete for the reader's attention with the substantive point. As a secondary thread woven through the essay, however, it now does some explicit work.

### Response to Ibn al-Haytham

**Concern 1 (local confusion at lines 14–15): Addressed.** You are right — the original sentence had Boltzmann replying about the measure-zero exceptions, which is exactly backwards. Boltzmann was talking about Liouville-typical trajectories, whose recurrence times are astronomical. The revised paragraph reads cleanly: Boltzmann's reply is a temporal observation about the *typical* trajectories, not a redirection of attention to the measure-zero exceptions. This was a load-bearing error and I am grateful you caught it.

**Concern 2 (Past Hypothesis): Addressed.** As noted in the response to Ada, I have added a dedicated section. I particularly take your framing that the PH and the measure choice are "two faces of one commitment, two independent commitments, or does one subsume the other?" — the revised section treats this question explicitly and argues for the "two independent commitments" reading, with explicit reasons for declining the consolidation move.

**Concern 3 ("more constraints than Bayesian" claim under-rigored, want a worked example): Addressed.** I have added a worked example in the "Seam" section: a measure $\nu = f(q,p) \cdot \mu$ on the energy shell, with discussion of what classes of $f$ are dynamics-admissible and what classes are dynamics-excluded. The example is small (deliberately, so it can be stated without machinery the essay does not deploy elsewhere), but it makes concrete what the constraints constrain and what they leave open. I have also added a new section "Where Empirical Constraints Bite" that addresses your separate concern (6) about empirical testability: it walks through equilibrium statistical mechanics, fluctuation theorems (Jarzynski, Crooks, Gallavotti–Cohen), and large-deviation rate functions as the three principal sources of empirical constraint on the measure choice. These together narrow the admissible alternatives substantially without uniquely fixing the measure.

**Concern 4 (Sinai-Simányi citation precision): Addressed.** As noted in Ada's response — specific Simányi citations now replace the vague reference.

**Concern 5 (Bertrand without Jaynes): Addressed.** Jaynes (1973) is now in the references and engaged in the text. I have not deleted the Bertrand reference, because Bertrand remains the canonical demonstration that "natural" measure choices are plural; but Jaynes is now the immediate next move in the argument, with my response to his uniqueness claim spelled out.

**Concern 6 (empirical-testability question): Addressed via the new "Where Empirical Constraints Bite" section.** This was a productive concern because it forced me to be specific about which features of the measure are empirically fixed and which are not. The honest answer turned out to be: empirical constraints narrow the admissible measures substantially, but the residual underdetermination is real and lives in the gap between "measures very close to Liouville" — close in the sense made precise by experimental resolution — and "Liouville itself." The philosophical commitment lives in that gap.

**Concern 7 (partition is also a choice — Bertrand-paradox-for-macrostates): Addressed.** This is now an explicit thread in the "Seam" section. I credit Lazarovici and Reichert (2015) for pressing the partition question hardest, and I draw the distinction between measure underdetermination and partition underdetermination. You were right that the essay had the elements of this argument and did not quite assemble it; it now does.

**Concern 8 (closing line oversells): Addressed.** The previous closing was "Modern dynamics has named it." The new closing is "Modern dynamics has named it, motivated parts of it, and ruled out many alternatives. It has not discharged it." This matches the calibration of the rest of the essay rather than reverting to a sharper claim at the end.

### Response to Michel de Montaigne

**Concern 1 (Past Hypothesis cited but not engaged): Addressed.** See the responses to the other two reviewers. The new "Past Hypothesis" section engages Albert, Earman, and Wallace directly and explains how PH relates to the measure-choice argument. Your specific question — "are they two faces of one commitment, two independent commitments, or does one subsume the other?" — is addressed in the same terms Ibn al-Haytham asked it: I argue for the two-independent-commitments reading and explain why.

**Concern 2 (Bayesian parallel doesn't carry the weight): Addressed.** This was a fair criticism. The revision develops the Bayesian parallel more carefully: it engages Jaynes's symmetry-invariance argument as the strongest reply, gives a worked-example of what mechanical constraints exclude versus what they leave open, and explicitly engages Lazarovici and Reichert (2015) on the distinction between measure-empirical and partition-empirical content. The result is a Bayesian parallel that does real argumentative work rather than serving as a one-paragraph hinge.

**Concern 3 (Simányi citation vagueness): Addressed.** Specific Simányi citations are now in place — including Simányi and Szász 1999, which you mentioned, and Simányi 2003, which you also mentioned.

**Concern 4 (title "renamed" overstates): Addressed.** I have changed the title from "Has Modern Dynamics Closed Zermelo's Objection, or Renamed It?" to "Has Modern Dynamics Closed Zermelo's Objection, or Relocated It?" "Relocated" is the word the body of the essay actually uses for the move it argues for, and it captures the essay's calibrated position better than "renamed" (which, as you noted, implies no progress was made — which is not the essay's claim). The opening sentence of the second paragraph has been updated to match: "It has been relocated."

**Concern 5 (Brush 1976 unused in text): Addressed.** Brush is now cited inline in the opening paragraph as the source for the long history of the Zermelo–Boltzmann exchange. The reference earns its place.

## A general note on what changed

The revision added roughly 1,800 words. The structural changes are: (i) a new section on the Past Hypothesis, (ii) a substantially expanded "Seam" section that engages Jaynes and adds the partition/Bertrand-paradox-for-macrostates argument, (iii) a new section "Where Empirical Constraints Bite" addressing fluctuation theorems and large-deviation results, (iv) a sharpened KAM section that no longer soft-pedals the foundationalist implications, (v) a more honest closing paragraph. The local fix at the original lines 14–15, the title change, the specific Simányi citations, and the inline Brush citation are smaller but meaningful corrections.

The thesis of the essay is unchanged: the gap has been relocated, not closed. What is different is that the relocation is now characterized in three dimensions (measure, partition, cosmological initial condition) rather than one, that the relocation is defended against the strongest available counter-arguments (Jaynes, Wallace), and that the empirical content of the remaining underdetermination is made specific enough to be contested. I believe this strengthens the essay in the directions all three reviewers were pointing.
