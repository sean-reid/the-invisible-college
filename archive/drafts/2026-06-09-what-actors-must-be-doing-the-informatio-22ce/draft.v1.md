# What Actors Must Be Doing: A Diagnostic for the Information-Normative Mechanism Split

The gap in institutional mechanism accounts recurs at a predictable level: action formation. The situational mechanism - what the institutional structure places in front of actors - is typically specified with reasonable care. The aggregation step - what the macro-level consequence of actor behavior is - is often asserted rather than derived. But the action-formation level, the question of why individual actors respond to their situation in the way that generates the aggregate pattern, is where structural ambiguity most reliably enters. And the particular ambiguity that enters, across a range of institutional domains, has a consistent shape: two analytically distinct mechanism families are being used to explain the same phenomenon without acknowledgment that they are different, and without specification of which is actually operative.

One family works through *information and calculation*. Actors observe something - the behavior of others, the state of an enforcement regime, the record of past compliance - update their beliefs about payoffs or probabilities, and alter their behavior to serve their interests given revised expectations. The other family works through *identity and obligation*. Actors comply because compliance is constitutive of who they take themselves to be: compliance expresses membership in a community whose norms they have internalized, and violation triggers shame or guilt independently of any calculation about detection or consequence.

These are not terminological variants. They make different predictions about when an institution will fail, what interventions will preserve it, and whether the mechanism can function in a transplant setting where the social context differs from the one in which it was developed. Treating them as equivalent is not just analytically imprecise; it generates systematic policy error.

## The Two Mechanisms Specified

Applying Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level framework to the action-formation level forces a precise statement of what each mechanism requires.

The **information mechanism** requires three conditions. First, a structural arrangement that makes some actors' behavior observable to others - either through direct monitoring, public disclosure, or reputational transmission. Second, a calculation by which observed behavior is informationally relevant to the observer's decision: the observation updates a belief about payoffs, probabilities, or available options. Third, a cost structure that makes behavioral updating the individually rational response to the revised belief. The mechanism can be modeled within standard rational choice; its parameters are observation opportunities, belief revision rules, and cost-benefit calculations. Critically, it can in principle be gamed: an actor who understands the observation structure can manipulate what others see and produce compliance-looking behavior without the underlying behavioral change.

The **normative mechanism** requires structurally different conditions. First, participation in a norm-setting process - not merely exposure to the norm's content, but voice in its formation. Hechter makes this condition explicit: it is not sufficient to know a rule exists; one must have been part of the community whose authority it expresses. Second, an identity structure in which the actor takes themselves to be a member of the norm-governed community, such that the norm's demands are addressed to them as a member rather than imposed as an external constraint. Third, an emotional response - obligation, pride, shame - triggered by compliance or violation independently of detection probability. The actor who would comply even when unmonitored, and who would feel ashamed of a violation in the absence of any audience, is exhibiting the normative mechanism's signature.

These emotional responses are what distinguish the families most sharply in practice. Fehr and Gächter demonstrated in 2000 that experimental subjects punish defectors at personal cost in one-shot anonymous games - behavior that is strictly irrational under the information mechanism but consistent with the normative account if subjects have internalized a compliance identity. The one-shot anonymous setting matters: it eliminates every pathway by which rational expectation of future interaction could sustain costly punishment. What remains is the normative mechanism's distinctive mark.

### The Reductive Challenge

The game-theoretic response to this distinction is worth confronting directly. The challenge runs: normative compliance is just information-mechanism compliance with a longer socialization loop. Actors who appear to comply from identity are actually complying because prior enforcement experience has recalibrated their expected-payoff calculation. The norm is not an independent mechanism; it is an internalized probability distribution over punishment.

This response does not fail on its face. It does, however, make different empirical commitments than the normative account. If compliance is internalized enforcement expectation, then: compliance should track enforcement history rather than community membership; actors who exit the community but remain monitored should maintain compliance; identity-salience manipulations - contexts that make professional membership salient - should add no predictive power after controlling for enforcement exposure; and the Fehr–Gächter result should either not occur or should be restricted to actors with documented enforcement exposure histories.

The normative account predicts the reverse of each. Where the predictions diverge, the distinction is real and empirically decidable. Claiming the mechanisms are equivalent requires committing to the reductive predictions - which is a substantive empirical position, not a definitional escape.

## Retrospective Application: Three Cases

The following table presents the retrospective application of the diagnostic to three prior mechanism audits. Each row is a case; the columns identify the dominant mechanism assumption in the existing literature, what each mechanism family predicts about failure and appropriate intervention, and the observable test that would discriminate between them.

| | **Referral Hiring** | **Compliance Monitoring** | **Commons Governance** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dominant assumption** | Information (match-quality signal) | Information (deterrence calculation) | Both invoked; neither specified |
| **Info: operative condition** | Observability of worker quality signals; employer belief-updating | Credible detection probability; cost-benefit rational for compliance | Observability of resource withdrawals; monitoring infrastructure |
| **Norm: operative condition** | Network embeddedness; obligation to co-community members | Professional identity among regulated actors; participation in standard-setting | Community pre-existence; participation in collective-choice processes |
| **Info: failure mode** | Employer gains direct quality information; referral premium disappears | Detection probability falls; rational basis for compliance erodes | Observability breaks down; free-riding becomes individually rational |
| **Norm: failure mode** | Network community dissolves; obligation to refer within group erodes | Non-normative actors enter regulated population; violators are those outside the compliance community | Community pre-existence is absent at installation; participation fails to generate membership identity |
| **Info: transplant condition** | Requires observable matching outcomes and employer belief-updating capacity | Requires credible enforcement program perceived as operative by new participants | Requires observable commons; monitoring infrastructure in place at installation |
| **Norm: transplant condition** | Requires established social network with ongoing reciprocity; cannot be installed | Requires pre-existing normative community among regulated actors; cannot be designed in | Requires existing community with collective identity; cannot be created at design time |
| **Info: policy lever** | Improve information channels; referral bonuses; independent quality verification | Increase detection probability; escalate sanctions; publish violation rates | Strengthen observability infrastructure; graduated sanctions tied to observable harm |
| **Norm: policy lever** | Develop network diversity upstream; address community segregation at its source | Build professional identity; participation in standard-setting; community belonging mechanisms | Sequence installation so community development precedes formal governance |
| **Observable discriminator** | Does the referral quality premium persist when employers have independent quality assessment? | Do violations cluster in the hard-to-detect region, or in the population outside the normative community? | Do transplant failures correlate with absent observability infrastructure, or with absent community preconditions? |

Three observations follow from this application.

**First**, in referral hiring, the literature defaults to the information mechanism without fully acknowledging it. In [*Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/), Channels A and B - quality screening and information gatekeeping - operate through the information mechanism. The policy instruments they suggest (referral bonuses, accountability mechanisms, information diffusion programs) are appropriate to that mechanism. Channel C, passive demographic composition through network embeddedness, is different in kind: what determines whom a referrer thinks to recommend is not primarily a quality calculation but network membership and informal obligation. The policy implications of Channel C point elsewhere - to upstream investment in network diversity - and they are recoverable only if the normative mechanism is identified as the operative channel. The literature has not made this identification, and conflates interventions appropriate to different mechanisms.

**Second**, in compliance monitoring, the selection analysis developed in [*Compliance as Selection*](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/) was constructed entirely within the information mechanism. That analysis is valid under its assumption: effective monitoring concentrates the residual violation pool toward violations that escape detection and are systematically more harmful on the dimension the norm protects. But the normative alternative predicts a qualitatively different distribution: violations concentrated not in the hard-to-detect region but in the population outside the normative community, regardless of detection probability. These are different patterns in the data, and standard monitoring datasets do not collect the information needed to distinguish them - who the violators are, not just where in the distribution they fall. The selection result and the normative prediction are undischarged alternatives; the existing evidence base does not settle which is operative.

**Third**, the commons governance audit in [*The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance*](posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/) found the information/normative split explicitly in the collective-choice principle (DP3) and noted that the two mechanisms generate divergent transplant predictions. The table makes this precise. The information mechanism's transplant condition - requires observable commons and monitoring infrastructure - sounds technically demanding. The normative mechanism's transplant condition - requires existing community with collective identity - sounds sociologically demanding. In practice, this ordering is reversed: observability infrastructure can be built at installation time, while community pre-existence cannot be designed in. The normative mechanism is more demanding precisely where it matters most - in transplant settings - and the symmetric presentation of all eight of Ostrom's design principles as equivalent design requirements obscures this asymmetry.

## A Prospective Application: Occupational Licensing

The diagnostic table has a natural prospective application in occupational licensing, and the application yields an institutional finding rather than merely a demonstration of framework generality.

The standard policy analysis treats licensing as an information mechanism. The license certifies quality to consumers who cannot assess it directly; the welfare question is whether the information value justifies the entry restriction. Kleiner (2006) and Kleiner and Krueger (2013) both frame the efficiency case in these terms. The deregulation prediction follows directly: remove the credential, reduce barriers to entry, allow alternative quality-assessment mechanisms (reputation systems, platform ratings, voluntary certification) to develop. Under the information mechanism, quality effects of deregulation should be distributed across the deregulated population in proportion to how dependent each practitioner was on the credential as a signal.

The normative mechanism generates a different prediction - and identifies an institutional dimension the information account ignores. Occupational licensing is not only an information structure; it is a community-forming institution. Medical licensing, bar admission, and professional engineering certification each embed the credential in an ongoing professional community: continuing education requirements, ethics codes, disciplinary boards, formal identity markers, and institutional belonging that the credential alone does not capture. Hechter's operative conditions - monitoring capacity and stakes in membership - are present in functioning licensed professions. The credential is the entry mechanism; the ongoing community is the operative social structure that generates self-enforcing quality norms.

If the normative mechanism is operative alongside the information mechanism, the deregulation prediction changes systemically. Removing the formal credential removes both the information signal and the community membership requirement. Practitioners who retain professional community membership through voluntary association, practice context, or continuing education will maintain quality through the normative mechanism even after deregulation. Practitioners who exit all professional structures will experience both the information removal and the identity structure removal simultaneously. The prediction: quality degradation under deregulation will be concentrated in the unaffiliated population, not distributed uniformly across the deregulated cohort.

This is an observable test that the standard deregulation literature does not design to answer. Post-deregulation quality studies measure aggregate outcomes; they do not track practitioners' professional association membership or community embeddedness before and after the policy change. This is a condition that can be changed - the test is feasible, the data do not yet exist to run it.

The institutional finding is this: if licensing institutionalizes professional identity - and the architectural features of major licensed professions suggest it does - then the deregulation policy debate has been misspecified in a particular direction. It predicts quality effects that are too uniform (ignoring the normative mechanism among the persistently affiliated) and it misattributes the mechanism of professional quality maintenance to information rather than identity in ways that make quality preservation in deregulated markets seem to require only alternative signaling infrastructure. These errors are durable because the information mechanism is tractable, its policy implications are specific, and the data to test the normative alternative has not been collected.

## The Diagnostic as a Practitioner Tool

The information/normative distinction is not specific to the three cases audited here. It is a structural feature of any mechanism claim in which compliance is the operative behavioral unit. Any institutional account that explains compliance through monitoring, detection, signaling, or incentive alignment is invoking the information mechanism. Any account that explains compliance through professional identity, community belonging, moral internalization, or obligation is invoking the normative mechanism. The ambiguity appears whenever both families of language are in use in the same body of work without specification of which is load-bearing.

The diagnostic table provides a checklist. The operative question for any new case is not which mechanism sounds more appropriate but which mechanism's conditions are met: Is there an observability structure and cost structure that makes information-channel updating individually rational? Is there a participation history and community membership that makes normative compliance identity-constitutive? In many real institutions, both conditions are partially met, and the analytical work is not to choose between the mechanisms but to specify which is load-bearing under which conditions and which is the residual.

The cases where this matters most are transplant settings: institutions being designed new, transferred across social contexts, or rebuilt after disruption. In these settings, the normative mechanism is not yet established even if the institutional rules are formally in place. The information mechanism can be activated at installation time by constructing the right observability infrastructure. The normative mechanism cannot; it requires community pre-existence or a deliberate sequence in which community development precedes formal rule installation. This asymmetry - that one mechanism is designable and the other is not, at the time horizon that institutional designers typically operate - is the diagnostic's most actionable implication.

Practitioners who fail to make this distinction will deploy information-mechanism policy tools in settings where the normative mechanism was the operative structure, and will encounter failures that no adjustment to monitoring intensity or incentive design can correct. The language of institutional design typically does not make this visible. Specifying which mechanism is being invoked, before rather than after the intervention is implemented, is the minimum discipline this distinction requires.
