# When Form Outlives Its Work: Epistemic Decay in the Long-Lived Essay

Every long-lived essay makes a tacit claim: that its formal strategies, which were epistemically functional at composition, remain so for later readers. This claim is rarely examined. We inherit the great essays, read them with care, and tend to assume that what the form was doing at composition is still available to us. The assumption deserves scrutiny.

By "epistemically functional" I mean something precise. [*The Attempt as Evidence*](posts/2026-05-25-the-attempt-as-evidence-on-what-the-essa-2504/) identified three conditions under which essay-form carries epistemic weight the argument-form cannot replicate. When a subject constitutively resists closure and the essay performs what argument can only assert - as Montaigne does in "Des Cannibales" - the form does evidential work unavailable by other means (condition 1). When a claim is phenomenological and the first-person case provides singular access no aggregate can supply - as Baldwin's testimony does in "Notes of a Native Son" - the form is not ornamental but necessary (condition 2). And when the movement of inquiry itself discloses something about the subject's resistance to systematic grasp - as in Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* - the journey cannot be separated from the destination (condition 3).

These conditions are synchronic: they describe what the form is doing for a reader in the moment of encounter. The temporal question is different. The reader's epistemic situation shifts across generations; the text does not. When that shift is large enough that a formal strategy can no longer do what it was designed to do, something has changed - but what, exactly, and by what mechanism? The claim developed here is that the three conditions decay at different rates through different mechanisms, and that this differential account is not merely a historical curiosity but has consequences for how old essays can be read.

## A Prior Distinction

Before the cases, a distinction is needed. Quentin Skinner's account of illocutionary force in the history of ideas offers tools for reconstructing what a text was performing at composition - what speech act it executed, what intellectual problems it addressed, what framework it was contesting. The Skinnerian reconstruction is valuable and in principle recoverable even across great temporal distances. But there is a gap between reconstructing what a text was doing and that thing being done to you. I can fully understand that Montaigne was destabilizing a framework without the destabilization being available to me. The historical reconstruction gives you the first without the second; the gap between them is where epistemic decay lives.

This is also different from what Hans-Georg Gadamer describes as the "fusion of horizons." For Gadamer, meaning emerges from the encounter between text-horizon and reader-horizon - understanding is always the product of that fusion, never a simple recovery of original meaning. What Gadamer's framework does not ask is whether the fusion produces the original epistemic function. The fusion always produces *meaning*; that is Gadamer's point. But meaning and evidential function are not the same thing. An essay can be productive of insight for a later reader without its formal strategies doing the evidential work they did at composition. Wolfgang Iser, working in reader-response theory, emphasizes the reader's active role in filling gaps in the text and activating its potential. His framework, too, does not distinguish between evidentially warranted activations and aesthetically produced ones. A later reader of "Des Cannibales" still activates the text - still finds it sharp, traces the argument, responds to the prose. Whether that activation constitutes the same epistemic function as Montaigne's 1580 reader's activation is the question both Gadamer and Iser leave open.

## Condition 1: "Des Cannibales"

Montaigne's essay on the Brazilians, written around 1578-80 and published in the first edition of the *Essais*, is the paradigm case for condition 1. The formal work of the essay is not to argue for cultural relativism but to perform the destabilization of the evaluative framework that would be needed to resist it. The catalog of Tupinambá practices - the warfare, the captive-taking, the ritual consumption of the dead - is presented in terms that make them intelligible rather than alien; the practices have reasons, the reasons are coherent, and the coherence undermines the category of "barbarism" before the word can be earned back. Then the Brazilian visitors to France observe the child-king, the gorged and the famished living side by side, and ask why the hungry do not seize the gorged by the throat. Montaigne does not argue that European civilization is barbarous. He presents the observation from outside the framework and lets the framework fail under its own weight.

This formal strategy requires something of the reader: that the framework of European cultural superiority be genuinely available to be destabilized. Not that the reader personally endorse it, but that it be live enough in the reader's conceptual environment to be susceptible to the pressure Montaigne applies. Frank Lestringant's contextual account of European representations of cannibalism establishes that this was not a settled question in 1580s France - the wars of religion had made the question of who was barbarous politically and morally urgent in a way that gave Montaigne's inversions their destabilizing charge. The formal strategy operated on a reader for whom the framework was under genuine pressure.

A contemporary reader who already holds something like cultural relativism as default - not necessarily as a sophisticated philosophical position but as the natural resting place of an educated sensibility - encounters a different situation. The destabilizing moves still occur in the text. The visitors' observation is still sharp. But the observation cannot perform destabilization of a framework the reader does not hold in the relevant way. The essay becomes illustration - evidence for a conclusion already reached - rather than the means by which the conclusion is constituted in the reader.

This is not a failure of the essay. It is a change in what the essay can do. The text is the same; the reader's epistemic situation has shifted enough that the formal strategy has changed its function.

What partially resists the decay: the specific texture of the examples. Even for a reader who holds relativism as default, the particular vividness of Montaigne's descriptions - the specific logic of Tupinambá warrior culture, the precision of the visitors' observation about French inequality - does something. Not framework destabilization in the strong sense, but what might be called the refreshment of a general principle through encounter with particular cases. [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) distinguished constraining, illustrative, and loading functions for examples; what survives condition 1 decay is roughly illustrative function, which is genuinely weaker than the original. The loss is real.

## Condition 2: "Notes of a Native Son"

The phenomenological case operates by a different logic entirely. Baldwin's essay does not offer a contestable conclusion that might become consensus. It offers access to a particular configuration of experience: the specific weight of his father's madness and death, the specific quality of simultaneous grief and rage, the specific phenomenology of navigating a restaurant in 1940s New Jersey as a Black man who can afford neither the bitterness he feels nor its suppression. The singular access does not depend on the conclusion being contested. Regardless of how extensively Black American experience has since been theorized, analyzed, and represented in subsequent decades, the specific conditions of access Baldwin provides remain unreachable by other means. The singularity is not defeated by taxonomic development.

This makes condition 2 more temporally robust than condition 1. The argument-form cannot supply what the essay provides here; no aggregate account, however comprehensive, can substitute for what is available through the particular case at the particular level of resolution Baldwin maintains. That remains as true now as in 1955. The formal strategy is not vulnerable to the mechanism that afflicts "Des Cannibales": there is no analogue to "cultural relativism has become consensus" that would make the phenomenological access structurally unavailable.

The formal structure of "Notes of a Native Son" is itself worth examining here. The essay moves between the personal - the father's death, the funeral, the kitchen where Baldwin and his father circled each other for years - and the political - the Harlem riot of 1943, the systematic violence of which his father's paranoia was a kind of pre-adaptation. The alternation between these registers is not illustrative; it enacts the argument that they cannot be separated. This is formal work of condition 2 type: the movement of the prose constitutes the phenomenological claim rather than merely stating it. And that formal work seems genuinely available to a later reader in a way that Montaigne's destabilizing move is not.

The failure mode of condition 2 is, however, worth naming. The accumulated scholarly and cultural production around Black American experience since 1955 - subsequent memoir, fiction, sociology, critical theory, oral history - provides an interpretive template that a contemporary reader is likely to bring to the text before encountering it. When the template is active, the reading operates by recognition rather than reception: the reader matches what Baldwin describes against what they already understand, confirming and elaborating the template rather than encountering the singular phenomenological testimony the essay enables. The template may be accurate; it may even be illuminating. But it redirects the reading: the essay becomes data rather than access.

This is not decay in the mechanism of condition 1. The epistemic capacity is still there; it is being overridden by prior knowledge rather than made structurally unavailable. A sufficiently attentive reader - one who notices when recognition has replaced reception, who slows down enough to encounter the specific rather than the type - can resist the override. The failure mode of condition 2 is avoidable in a way that the failure mode of condition 1 is not. No reading strategy can recreate the epistemic situation of 1580; no amount of effort can make cultural relativism unfamiliar. But a reader can, with deliberate effort, bracket the template. The singularity Baldwin provides access to is still there.

Whether many readers actually do bracket the template is an empirical question close reading cannot settle. It may be that the template replacement is so pervasive and so automatic that the condition 2 robustness I am claiming is largely theoretical. I cannot rule this out. What I can say is that the mechanism is different from consensus formation, the remedy (if there is one) is different, and the structure of the failure is different - and that these differences matter for understanding how the essay ages.

## Condition 3: *Philosophical Investigations*

The *Investigations* presents the sharpest case for differential decay, and its mechanism is distinct from both of the above. By any reckoning, the book has generated a secondary literature of extraordinary density: Kripke's controversial sceptical reading, the Baker and Hacker commentaries, an extensive tradition of analytic engagement, and generations of textbook summaries that have diffused Wittgenstein's characteristic moves into philosophical common knowledge. A reader encountering the *Investigations* today is unlikely to encounter it naively.

What this pre-knowledge does to the text's epistemic function is precisely what makes condition 3 the most vulnerable case. The *Investigations*' formal strategy is to work through philosophical problems - the nature of meaning, the possibility of private language, the grammar of psychological concepts - in a way whose movement is the argument. The fragmented, questioning, circling form performs the impossibility of settling these problems from outside language; the failure to reach a systematic theory is not a limitation of the inquiry but its disclosure. The reader who is genuinely moved by this - who experiences the circling as philosophically significant rather than inconclusive - has been shown something about the subject that no propositional argument could convey.

But the reader who already knows where the *Investigations* goes - who knows that the method is the point, that the fragmentation is intentional, that the book works by dissolving rather than solving - is not taking a journey. This reader is recognizing a journey. The inquiry cannot disclose what is already known; the form performs an encounter with unknowing that the reader's prior knowledge forecloses. The text is still doing something - still provoking, still demanding - but the epistemic disclosure is converted into a different kind of experience: appreciation of a method already understood, recognition of the moves as they appear.

A complication: the secondary literature on PI is not monolithic. Kripke's reading, Baker and Hacker's reading, the therapeutic and quietist traditions disagree deeply about what the book teaches. A reader who has absorbed Kripke before reading the text is in a different epistemic situation from a reader who has absorbed the later therapeutic tradition. Does this mean the decay of condition 3 is more variable than the general account suggests? Perhaps. What is not variable is that meta-knowledge of the method - knowing that the fragmented form is intentional, that the circling is inquiry rather than confusion - substantially changes the reader's relationship to the text regardless of which interpretive tradition they bring. The secondary scaffolding forecloses the naive encounter even when it does not settle the interpretation.

This distinguishes the mechanism from condition 1. The problem is not consensus about a conclusion (what Wittgenstein teaches remains contested) but familiarity with the formal structure. Condition 3 decays not through consensus about content but through pre-knowledge of method - a distinction worth holding.

## Three Mechanisms, Three Rates

The cases suggest a provisional taxonomy. Condition 1 is most vulnerable to consensus formation: when the conclusion the form was destabilizing toward has migrated from contested to default, the formal performance of instability becomes illustration of a point already held. Condition 3 is most vulnerable to secondary scaffolding: when the formal method is known before the encounter, disclosure becomes recognition. Condition 2 is most robust, because phenomenological singularity is not defeated by either mechanism - but has its own failure mode in interpretive template replacement, which is avoidable in a way the other two failures are not.

The mechanisms also suggest different responses, or the absence of them. For condition 1 decay, the honest reading practice is what might be called epistemic archaeology: using the Skinnerian reconstruction to understand what the form was doing, acknowledging that the destabilization cannot be performed on you, and reading the text for the historical testimony it provides to a moment of genuine instability. The essay becomes evidence of its own moment rather than evidence for its original claim. This is not diminishment; it is a different kind of reading.

For condition 3 decay, a form of strategic naïveté is in principle available - encountering the text before the secondary literature, or deliberately bracketing it. This is increasingly difficult to achieve as Wittgenstein's characteristic moves filter into philosophical common knowledge well beyond the specialist texts, but it is not structurally impossible in the way that recreating 1580 epistemics is. The disclosure is still there in the text; it requires only an unprepared reader.

For condition 2, the relevant discipline is attention to the singular rather than the type. The template can be set aside; the encounter is still available to a reader who notices when recognition has replaced reception.

These three responses - epistemic archaeology, strategic naïveté, attention to singularity - are not equivalent, and the difference between them maps onto the difference between irreversible and recoverable forms of decay. Consensus formation closes a door that cannot be reopened from inside the reading; secondary scaffolding blocks a passage that can in principle be cleared; template replacement is an override that attention can lift. The decay is real in all three cases, but it is not the same thing in each.

---

[*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) addressed anachronism as the interpreter's problem - the risk that later concepts contaminate the historical reading. What I have been describing is the reader's complementary problem: not the distortion of the historical text by later frameworks, but the historical transformation of the conditions under which the text can do its specific formal work. The anachronist risks misrepresenting the thinker; the reader facing epistemic decay risks encountering only illustration where the text was designed to provide evidence.

Whether this differential account survives examination is a provisional judgment. Three cases are suggestive but not conclusive; a properly developed account would need to test the mechanisms across a much wider range of essays and ask whether the rates of decay are as different as the present analysis claims. It is possible that consensus formation and secondary scaffolding are aspects of a single underlying process - both are forms of "already knowing the answer" - and that the distinction between them collapses under pressure. If so, the typology would simplify without being refuted: condition 2 would remain distinct by virtue of its different failure mode, while conditions 1 and 3 might prove two instances of a single decay type. That would be a result worth reporting.

The question the present essay has tried to hold throughout is whether the formal strategies of old essays are available to later readers in the same way they were at composition. The answer, developed through three cases, is: it depends on which condition the form was serving, and by what mechanism the reader's epistemic situation has shifted. This is less clean than a simple yes or no, and it is what the evidence supports.
