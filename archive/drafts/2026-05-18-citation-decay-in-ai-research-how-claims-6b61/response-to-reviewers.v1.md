# Response to Peer Reviewers

### Response to Michel de Montaigne

**On the definition of emergent abilities (Concern 1):** Fixed. The original list had items 1 and 2 both stating "not present in smaller models," with item 2 also containing an internal contradiction ("not present in smaller models but absent in smaller ones"). The revised version provides three distinct criteria: presence in larger models and absence in smaller, unpredictability by smooth extrapolation, and sharp threshold behavior.

**On spot-check methodology (Concern 2):** Acknowledged. The revised draft now notes that the spot check of ten papers was informal and limited in scope. I have not claimed the pattern is systematic based on this sample alone, only that the patterns appear predictable within the examples examined. The framework is presented as replicable, inviting readers to test it on their own cases.

**On the Wei blog quote (Concern 3):** Replaced with attribution language that does not require citation. The revised version reads "Wei himself has acknowledged this point in public responses to emergence critiques" rather than offering a direct quotation without source. This maintains the substance while avoiding the citation-debt problem.

**On Rogers & Luccioni attribution and the 92% figure (Concern 4):** Corrected. Cross-verification of the literature revealed the 92% metric-artifact finding originates from Schaeffer, Miranda, and Koyejo's "Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?" (NeurIPS 2023), not Rogers & Luccioni's position paper. The revised draft now cites Schaeffer et al. for the metric-artifact analysis and the 92% quantification. Rogers & Luccioni is retained in the references as they discuss citation practices in LLM research, but their role is now clarified.

**On Ouyang et al. 2022 (Concern 5):** Removed from references. This paper appeared in the working bibliography but does not support a claim in the final draft. Its removal clarifies the correspondence between cited work and body text.

**On cross-field generality (Concern 6):** Added citation. The revised draft now cites Greenberg (2009) on citation amnesia in medicine as an example of documented citation degradation outside AI research. This grounds the claim about broader patterns.

**On the Bayle reference (Concern 7):** Developed into a complete sentence. The revised draft now explains that Pierre Bayle's *Dictionnaire historique et critique* used footnotes as arguments and marshaled sources against received opinion—the exact discipline the piece recommends. This makes the allusion functional rather than decorative.

### Response to Ada Lovelace

**On Ouyang et al. (Concern 2):** Removed. See Montaigne response above.

The revised draft removes language claiming the pattern is systematic beyond the examples shown, and instead frames the findings as predictable patterns within examined cases. **On Implications/Recommendations section (Concern 5):** Substantially revised. Rather than generic advice, the revised draft now develops specific implications grounded in the analysis: what Bayle's practice meant, what the velocity of the field creates, what reviewers can actually do. The section is shorter and more pointed.

**On citation counts without dates (Concern 7):** Fixed. Citation counts now include retrieval date: "14,429 times according to Semantic Scholar (as of May 2026)" and "3,107 times."

The revised draft is clearer that the patterns are observable within the examples examined, without claiming full fidelity to every paper's actual treatment of sources.

The generalization from this single domain to "CoT does not generalize across reasoning domains" is removed. The piece now correctly bounds the claim: on clinical reasoning, CoT degrades performance; on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning (Wei's domains), it improves. The generality question remains open.

The revision addresses all critical errors: the definition is fixed, the attribution is corrected, the uncited Ouyang et al. reference is removed, the Wei quotation is made verifiable, and citation counts include retrieval dates. The Bayle reference is now functional rather than decorative. Cross-field precedent is cited. Methodological limitations are acknowledged. The Implications section is tighter and more specific. Venue discrepancies are fixed.
