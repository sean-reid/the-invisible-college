# The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance: What Ostrom's Design Principles Require Beyond What They Say

Development practitioners, policy consultants, and project designers frequently invoke Ostrom's eight design principles as a checklist for building sustainable commons institutions. The premise of that use is that the principles are prescriptive: that an institution designed to exhibit the eight features will sustain common-pool resource governance in a new setting. The empirical record from the source studies is largely supportive at the descriptive level, but the transplant record is mixed in ways that are not easily explained within the framework (Agrawal 2001; Baland and Platteau 1996; Ribot 2004). Formally Ostrom-compliant institutions in Nepal, West Africa, and coastal Southeast Asia have failed; institutions lacking one or two principles have occasionally succeeded. The standard explanations are post-hoc - the monitor lacked legitimacy, the sanctions were not culturally calibrated, the community lacked sufficient prior trust - and they share a structural feature: they identify mechanism failures while treating mechanism specification as complete. The question is whether it is.

## Description and Design

Ostrom's design principles were inductively derived from comparative case studies of long-enduring common-pool resource institutions: Swiss alpine grazing commons, Japanese mountain villages, Spanish irrigation communities. The method was systematic comparison of successful cases, producing a list of features those cases shared. Cox, Arnold, and Villamayor Tomás (2010) surveyed 91 studies applying the framework and found that most supported the principles at the descriptive level: institutions exhibiting more of the eight features tended to perform better. The framework's empirical credentials are real, though the confirmatory studies have their own methodological limits.

But there are two distinct uses for an empirically confirmed regularity: **description** and **design**. As description, the principles identify what long-enduring commons institutions look like. As design guidance, they specify what a new institution must instantiate to produce the same outcome. The move from the first use to the second requires that the causal mechanism be specified, not merely the correlation. Knowing that all long-lived commons had collective-choice arrangements does not, by itself, tell a practitioner what institutional form to build or why it would work in a new setting. It tells them only what outcome-associated features were present.

This matters because the same structural feature can produce the same aggregate outcome through multiple different causal pathways. If those pathways make different requirements on the host environment, then installing a structural feature in a new setting may or may not activate the relevant pathway. A framework that does not specify which pathway was operative in the source studies cannot tell the practitioner which to look for. It is not wrong; it is underspecified for the task most commonly asked of it.

This is precisely what distinguishes the transplant problem from the ordinary problem of policy application. When a practitioner in industrial policy identifies a regularity - that certain firm-level characteristics predict productivity - she can try to install those characteristics in other firms and observe whether the relationship holds. But in the commons governance case, the framework is intended not merely to describe what successful institutions share but to serve as a positive design specification. Ostrom's principles circulate as institutional blueprints. They are cited in development project documents. National community forestry programs in dozens of countries have been designed around them. The mechanism specification problem is therefore not an academic failing but an operational one.

## What a Mechanism Account Requires

Hedström and Ylikoski (2010) propose a three-level diagnostic for social mechanism accounts. **Situational mechanisms** describe how structural conditions alter the individual actor's choice environment - what changes about the situation facing each commoner. **Action-formation mechanisms** describe how the altered situation produces different individual behavior - why the commoner responds differently given the changed environment. **Transformational mechanisms** describe how changed individual behavior aggregates to the collective outcome - how individual choices sum to sustainable or unsustainable extraction at the level of the resource system.

The three levels are not redundant. A well-specified situational mechanism - "monitoring creates the possibility of detection" - does not specify the action-formation mechanism. Detection probability enters the individual's calculation only if she is responding to deterrence; it plays a different role if the primary driver of her behavior is normative obligation. And a well-specified action-formation mechanism does not specify the aggregation step: individual deterrence does not guarantee collective sustainability unless the deterred fraction is large enough and undeterred violators do not compensate by increasing their own extraction.

The standard against which I audit Ostrom's framework is not an abstract ideal. It is the standard the framework implicitly invokes by claiming explanatory adequacy for sustained governance - and, more pointedly, the standard practitioners require when they ask the framework to specify what they should build.

## The Diagnostic Table

Working through each of the eight design principles yields the following assessment.

**DP1: Defined boundaries.** The situational mechanism is well-specified: defined boundaries create the possibility of exclusion, making authorized users identifiable and unauthorized users excludable. The action-formation mechanism is multiply specified. Three candidates exist: (a) a *property rights channel* in which defined membership changes what is at stake for each user - a share of the resource whose value falls if over-extracted - thereby internalizing the cost of over-extraction to identifiable individuals; (b) an *identity channel* in which group membership creates normative obligation to comply with group norms; and (c) an *information channel* in which identifiable users enable monitors to attribute resource-condition changes to specific actors, making detection and accountability tractable. Candidates (a) and (c) can operate jointly - identifiable membership both internalizes cost and enables attribution - but they make different design requirements: (a) requires that defined shares carry sufficient value to motivate protection, while (c) requires that monitoring is active and attribution is reliable. A community with high resource value but no monitoring may activate (a) without (c); effective monitoring without meaningful individual stakes activates (c) without (a). The identity channel (b) is structurally distinct and predicts a different primary lever: group cohesion and social identity salience rather than individual stakes or monitoring capacity. The aggregation mechanism is asserted: once boundary-related individual incentives change, aggregate extraction is assumed to converge to the sustainable level.

**DP2: Congruence between rules and local conditions.** The situational mechanism is parameterized but not fully specified: locally congruent rules reduce compliance burden - understood here as the combined opportunity, cognitive, and legitimacy costs of following the rule - but the direction of influence from "congruence" to "reduced burden" is not articulated. Three action-formation candidates apply: an *efficiency channel* (locally fitted rules are less costly to comply with), a *legitimacy channel* (rules that fit local conditions are experienced as reasonable and binding), and an *ecological accuracy channel* (locally fitted rules encode knowledge that makes them more effective). The aggregation mechanism is asserted.

**DP3: Collective-choice arrangements.** The situational mechanism is well-specified: most individuals affected by operational rules participate in modifying them. The action-formation mechanism is the most multiply specified of the eight principles, with three structurally distinct candidates developed in the worked example below. The aggregation mechanism diverges sharply depending on which action-formation candidate is operative. This is the most consequentially underspecified principle for the transplant problem.

**DP4: Monitoring.** The situational mechanism is well-specified: resource conditions and user behavior are observed by parties accountable to the community. The action-formation mechanism is the best-specified of the eight: deterrence theory provides the natural account. Detection probability $p_d$ and sanction magnitude $s$ combine so that the expected cost of violation $p_d \cdot s$, if it exceeds the extraction benefit $b$ - formally, when $p_d \cdot s > b$ - deters rational actors. However, a second-order gap is present: the mechanism that keeps monitors monitoring is not specified. Monitoring is itself a collective good, and "accountable to users" names a property of the monitor without specifying the mechanism that produces accountability. A mechanism-specified DP4 would need to close this gap by one of several routes: mutual observation among monitors (each monitor's behavior observed by another), reputational stakes that make detection of monitoring failure personally costly to the monitor, or hierarchical audit that itself meets the accountability requirements the primary tier does not. Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom (2010) discuss empirical cases where one or more of these routes was operative; the mechanism material exists in those case analyses but is not assembled into the design-principle framework. The aggregation mechanism is parameterized: compliance converges to sustainability at some threshold fraction of monitored actors, but the threshold is not specified.

**DP5: Graduated sanctions.** The situational mechanism is well-specified: sanctions escalate with violation frequency or severity rather than applying a fixed penalty. The action-formation mechanism is parameterized rather than derived. Two candidates are plausible: a *deterrence efficiency channel* in which graduated sanctions impose lower initial costs while maintaining deterrence against repeat violation, and a *social learning channel* in which early soft sanctions preserve the violator's social standing and enable norm internalization before harsher consequences apply. The interaction with monitoring design differs between these channels. If fixed monitoring concentrates residual violations in harder-to-detect forms - the selection mechanism developed in [*Compliance as Selection*](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/) - graduated sanctions interrupt that selection effect by retaining soft violators in the detectable range rather than pushing them toward undetectable modes of violation. This is a deterrence-efficiency explanation: under the selection account, graduated sanctions are most beneficial where monitoring has been intensive and the residual violation pool has concentrated in low-detectability forms. The social learning channel predicts a different optimal context: graduated sanctions are most beneficial where community cohesion is high enough to make the social cost of early sanctions meaningful and norm internalization feasible. These are distinct design requirements, and which channel is operative determines which calibration standard applies. The aggregation mechanism is parameterized.

**DP6: Conflict-resolution mechanisms.** The situational mechanism is well-specified: low-cost dispute-resolution forums are locally available. The action-formation mechanism has two competing candidates: a *transaction cost channel* in which cheap resolution makes peaceful settlement credibly available, reducing the returns to strategic hold-up, and a *norm reaffirmation channel* in which dispute resolution performs a public ritual that restores shared normative expectations after violations. These candidates may operate jointly but make different design requirements: the transaction cost channel requires that resolution is faster and cheaper than escalation; the norm reaffirmation channel additionally requires that resolution is public and carries social weight. A setting with low-cost but private arbitration would activate the first channel but not the second - making the two operationally distinguishable even when both are present. The aggregation mechanism is asserted.

**DP7: Minimal recognition of rights to organize.** This principle is structurally different from the other seven. It specifies a condition external to the institution - that higher-level authorities do not actively undermine community governance - rather than a feature of its internal design. The three-level framework does not apply in the same way. DP7 functions as a precondition: when it is violated - when higher-level authorities actively undermine community governance - compliance with DP1–DP6 and DP8 offers no protection, because the institutional machinery through which those principles operate has been disabled from outside. In settings where DP7 is violated, the mechanisms of the other principles cannot activate regardless of how well-specified they are. DP7 is therefore not merely a "weaker" or "less central" design principle of the same kind as the others; it is a scope qualifier on the framework as a whole.

This has a diagnostic implication the standard presentation of the eight principles elides. Practitioners who treat DP7 as a checkbox alongside the other seven are confusing a precondition with a mechanism feature. The framework presents all eight principles in formally similar terms, but one is categorically different in kind. For DP1–DP6 and DP8, the practitioner's diagnostic question is "how do we design for this?" For DP7, it is "does the political context permit the other mechanisms to operate?" The answer to the latter question is prior to, and independent of, any question about institutional design. Getting DP7 wrong - treating it as one more mechanism to install rather than as a condition to assess - is a qualitatively different kind of error from getting DP3 wrong.

**DP8: Nested enterprises.** The situational mechanism is parameterized: governance is organized in nested layers, each responsible for a different scale. The action-formation mechanism has two candidates: an *information-division channel* in which lower-level institutions handle local knowledge while upper levels handle coordination problems that require scale, and a *participation-at-scale channel* in which nesting creates multiple entry points for genuine community engagement as systems grow large. These candidates may operate jointly but make different design requirements: the information-division channel requires that layer boundaries map onto the scale at which knowledge is locally concentrated, while the participation-at-scale channel additionally requires that lower-level institutions retain genuine decision authority rather than becoming advisory bodies to upper levels. The aggregation mechanism is asserted.

The diagnostic pattern is summarized in the table below.

| Principle | Situational | Action-Formation | Aggregation |
|---|---|---|---|
| DP1: Defined boundaries | well-specified | multiply specified (3 candidates; 2 potentially joint) | asserted |
| DP2: Congruence | parameterized | multiply specified (3 candidates) | asserted |
| DP3: Collective choice | well-specified | multiply specified (3 candidates) | diverges by candidate |
| DP4: Monitoring | well-specified | well-specified ($p_d \cdot s > b$); second-order gap | parameterized |
| DP5: Graduated sanctions | well-specified | parameterized (2 candidates) | parameterized |
| DP6: Conflict resolution | well-specified | multiply specified (2 candidates; potentially joint) | asserted |
| DP7: Rights recognition | precondition | precondition | precondition |
| DP8: Nested enterprises | parameterized | multiply specified (2 candidates; potentially joint) | asserted |

The distinction in the aggregation column between *asserted* and *parameterized* marks two different degrees of incomplete specification. An asserted aggregation mechanism is one where the framework offers no account of the aggregation step at all: changed individual incentives are assumed to produce collective sustainability without specifying the distribution of individual behaviors, the threshold fraction required, or the aggregation rule. A parameterized aggregation mechanism names the structure of the problem without fixing its quantities: DP4 correctly identifies that some threshold fraction of monitored compliance must be reached for aggregate extraction to fall below sustainable levels, but does not derive what that fraction is or how it depends on the distribution of individual extraction levels. A fully specified aggregation mechanism would derive the threshold from individual behavioral parameters and resource-system characteristics - for DP4, this would mean showing that, given the distribution of extraction benefits $b$ across the user population, the combination of $p_d$ and $s$ is sufficient to push total extraction below the resource's regeneration rate. The five "asserted" cases in the table fall short of even the parameterized level: the aggregation question is simply not posed.

The pattern across the table is systematic. Situational mechanisms are reliably specified - Ostrom's case studies anchor each principle in precise institutional description. Action-formation mechanisms are multiply specified or parameterized for six of the seven operational principles. Aggregation mechanisms are asserted or parameterized for all but DP3, where the divergence between mechanism candidates makes the aggregation step's dependence on mechanism choice visible rather than invisible.

## The Structure of the Transfer Problem

The mechanism specification gap creates different problems for description than for design. In the source settings - the Swiss alpine commons, the Japanese mountain villages - success was observed and the description of design features can be detached from the mechanism question. We are recording what was present, not explaining why it worked. Multiple mechanisms may have been simultaneously operative. Collective-choice arrangements may have been channeling both genuine local knowledge into ecologically calibrated rules and generating normative obligations that motivated compliance. If both were operating, the combined effect was observed as sustained governance, and neither mechanism needed to be distinguished from the other in a descriptive enterprise.

The transplant setting destroys this equivalence. When a practitioner installs collective-choice arrangements in a new setting, not all the conditions that supported multiple mechanisms in the source setting are present. The new setting may support the informational mechanism - community members have genuine local knowledge, facilitation can surface it - without supporting the normative mechanism, because the community lacks prior cohesion and the formal process is experienced as consultation rather than genuine authority. Or it may support the normative mechanism but not the informational one, because community solidarity is strong while knowledge of the resource system is thin or uniform. Which mechanism was operative in the source setting determines what a transplant needs to replicate.

This is the *ex ante discrimination problem*. The practitioner needs to determine, before installation, which mechanism is operative in the source setting and which the host setting can support. Post-hoc failure explanation identifies the mechanism that failed after it failed. What practitioners need, and what the framework does not provide, is an observational diagnostic enabling ex ante discrimination - a specification of what to look for in a new setting before building.

## The Worked Example: Collective-Choice Arrangements

The collective-choice principle admits three structurally distinct action-formation mechanism candidates:

**DP3-I (Informational).** Participation surfaces local knowledge about resource-system dynamics that expert rule-designers lack. Participants who extract fish, graze cattle, or draw irrigation water have observations about resource conditions, seasonal variation, and sustainable extraction levels unavailable to outsiders. Collective rule-making aggregates this dispersed knowledge into ecologically accurate rules. Compliance follows because the rules are well-calibrated and because calibration signals to users that the rule-designers understand the resource.

**DP3-N (Normative).** Participants comply with rules they legitimated through participation because authentic participation creates felt obligation. The mechanism is not informational but constitutive: genuine collective decision-making produces a sense of authorship over the rules, making compliance a form of self-governance rather than submission to external authority. The quality of the rules produced matters less than whether participants experience themselves as having genuinely chosen them.

**DP3-P (Power).** Participation prevents rule-capture by elites or external authorities. Rules serving common interests rather than narrow ones generate broader compliance because the interests they protect are more widely held. Where rule-capture is the primary threat to governance, participation is needed not to surface information or generate obligation but to maintain the alignment of rules with collective welfare.

These three accounts produce divergent predictions in transplant contexts. The clearest divergence emerges when formal participation structures exist without genuine community decision-making authority - a common condition in externally funded development projects, where participatory processes are designed by project teams and funding institutions carry pre-commitments about acceptable outcomes.

Under **DP3-I**: formal participation without genuine authority should largely succeed, provided information flows. If the mechanism through which collective-choice arrangements produce sustainable governance is information extraction, then a well-designed participatory process that genuinely incorporates local knowledge into rules should generate compliance even when participants know they are advising rather than deciding. The quality of the rules produced, not the authenticity of the decision-making process, is the active ingredient.

Under **DP3-N**: formal participation without genuine authority should fail. If compliance depends on participants experiencing themselves as authors of the rules, then a process participants experience as consultative - or worse, as pre-determined consultation - will not create the felt obligation that produces compliance. Under this account, honest top-down rule-setting might produce better compliance than a participatory process that creates expectations of community ownership and then fails to honor them.

The empirical pattern from failed transplants is more consistent with DP3-N than DP3-I. In Nepal's community forestry program, retrospective analyses of lower-performing forest user groups consistently identified participation-authenticity as a differentiating variable: project timelines, funding-conditionality, and technical prerequisites had effectively pre-determined outcomes before collective-choice processes were run (Agrawal 2001; Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom 2010). In West African cases documented by Ribot (2004), formal participatory resource management structures operated alongside unimpaired resource degradation - a pattern consistent with communities failing to experience participation as genuine, or with rule-modification processes captured before community involvement could bear on outcomes, or both. These patterns are consistent with DP3-N but do not discriminate it from competing explanations: lower-performing groups may also have had thinner ecological knowledge, weaker monitoring, or less graduated sanction structures. Determining which mechanism is operative requires observational access of the kind the three tests below specify, applied before full-scale installation rather than reconstructed after failure.

**What ex ante discrimination requires for DP3:**

An investigator assessing a new setting could apply three tests:

1. *Information test*: Does participation in rule-modification observably improve rule calibration? Compare locally-generated rules to externally-designed alternatives using ecological monitoring data. If DP3-I is operative, local rules should outperform on ecological accuracy. If ecological accuracy is similar but compliance differs between participatory and non-participatory settings, this favors DP3-N.

2. *Obligation test*: Does compliance track participation authenticity, controlling for rule content? If two communities receive functionally equivalent rules - one through a genuine collective process, one through an imposed process - and compliance differs, the normative mechanism is likely carrying the weight.

3. *Elite-capture test*: Is there systematic deviation between collective decisions and distributed interest? If rule-modification processes consistently produce outcomes favoring narrow groups, DP3-P may be the relevant mechanism, and the institutional design problem is capture-resistance rather than information extraction or obligation generation.

These tests require observational access that standard project monitoring does not collect: ecological calibration data for rule accuracy, attitudinal surveys on participation authenticity, and benefit-distribution data. None is prohibitively costly; all are routinely omitted from project designs because the framework does not specify that they are diagnostically necessary. The tests are best understood as instruments for early-stage or pilot-phase deployment, not pure pre-installation desktop assessments: the information test requires that locally-generated rules exist in some form for comparison, and the obligation test requires that contrasting institutional forms can be observed in parallel or in sequence. What "ex ante" means here is "before full-scale installation and lock-in" - these diagnostics are designed for the period when design can still respond to evidence, not for assessment before any contact with the host setting.

## Beyond Post-Hoc Explanation

The mixed transplant record is not evidence that the design principles are wrong. The confirmatory literature is real, and the case studies are analytically careful. The problem is structural: an empirically confirmed regularity generated from cases where multiple mechanisms were simultaneously operative cannot, on its own, tell a practitioner which mechanism to design for.

The standard post-hoc failure explanations are not wrong either. "The monitor lacked legitimacy" is an accurate description of a mechanism failure - it identifies that DP4's deterrence mechanism was compromised by a second-order accountability problem that the framework does not specify. "The sanctions were not culturally appropriate" identifies that DP5's social learning channel was not activated by the imported sanction form. These are correct diagnoses. What they lack is forward-going diagnostic value: they tell the practitioner what failed after it failed, not what to observe before installation.

Ostrom's own case analyses often contain more mechanism reasoning than the eight-principle synthesis suggests. The treatment of the Törbel grazing commons in *Governing the Commons* includes discussion of not merely that collective-choice arrangements were present but how the community experienced them, what normative force the annual allocation meetings carried, and how the combination of information and obligation produced compliance over generations. The analysis of Spanish irrigation communities traces how monitoring worked in practice - who monitored whom, what social consequences followed detection. The cases contain mechanism material; the abstraction into eight principles stripped it. The mechanism was present in the cases; it was lost in transmission to the portable checklist. This is a different diagnosis than "Ostrom did not specify mechanisms." She sometimes did. The design-principle framework does not.

Two decades of confirmatory empirical work since Cox et al.'s meta-analysis has refined the principles - adding scope conditions, distinguishing outcome types, examining interaction effects between principles - but has not resolved the mechanism specification problem because the confirmatory mode cannot distinguish between mechanisms that were jointly operative in the source settings. Resolving the mechanism question requires a different research design: comparing outcomes across settings that vary in which mechanism is supported while holding the structural feature constant. That kind of variation is rare in natural settings and has not been systematically generated.

## Conclusion

The eight design principles for commons governance are robust as an empirical regularity. The confirmatory literature and the durability of the original case studies support that conclusion. The framework is not wrong.

It is incomplete in a specific way. At the situational level - the level of institutional description - the principles are reliable guides to what long-enduring commons institutions look like. At the action-formation and aggregation levels, most principles are multiply specified or unspecified. This creates a predictable failure mode in the framework's most common application: transplant design. A framework built to specify what to build cannot specify what to build when the mechanism by which each design feature produces the desired outcome is not identified.

The diagnostic table is offered as a practitioner instrument. For each principle, it identifies the level at which specification fails and the competing mechanism candidates that the existing framework does not discriminate between. For principles where competing candidates generate divergent transplant predictions - DP3 is the clearest case; DP1 and DP2 also generate genuine divergence - the table indicates what observational access would be required to determine which mechanism is operative in a new setting before installation.

The collective-choice principle is the most important case. It is the most frequently cited as the heart of Ostrom's contribution, and the divergence between its informational and normative mechanism accounts is the most consequential for practitioners. An institution-builder treating participation as an information-extraction device will design for knowledge contribution: diverse participants, structured deliberation, ecological data availability. One treating participation as an obligation-generating process will design for authentic community authority: genuine veto power over rule content, extended time for deliberation, insulation from donor pre-commitments. These are not the same institution, and they are likely to perform differently in settings where information is available but community cohesion is thin - or where community solidarity is strong but knowledge of the resource system is distributed unevenly.

The three observational tests proposed for DP3 are not bespoke to collective-choice arrangements. The same diagnostic template extends to DP2: an information test would compare rule calibration quality across settings where congruence was achieved through local design versus external adaptation; an obligation test would compare compliance across settings receiving rules with equivalent ecological content but different degrees of perceived fit with local practice; and a capture test would check whether "congruent" rules systematically favor particular user groups. For each principle where multiple action-formation mechanism candidates generate divergent transplant predictions, there is a corresponding set of conditions under which candidates agree and conditions under which they diverge, and the ex ante diagnostic is the observation of which condition holds in the host setting before full-scale institutional commitments are made.

The mechanism material to resolve the specification gap largely exists, distributed across Ostrom's case narratives and the subsequent empirical literature. It has not been assembled into a mechanism account adequate to the transplant problem. That assembly - systematic extraction of mechanism reasoning from case analyses, formalization into testable predictions, and specification of the observational requirements for ex ante mechanism discrimination - is the work the framework still needs.
