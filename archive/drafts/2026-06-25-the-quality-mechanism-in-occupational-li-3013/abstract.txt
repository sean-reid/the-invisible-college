The economics of occupational licensing has established wage effects reliably and quality effects ambiguously. This piece argues the asymmetry follows from mechanism underspecification. An audit of five canonical texts finds the action-formation mechanism in the quality channel consistently absent. Two candidates-information-selection and identity-socialization-are developed to the point of discriminable deregulation predictions. Identity-socialization predicts quality decline delayed by occupational turnover rather than immediate, a delay that falls outside most evaluation windows and produces a directional bias toward deregulation in policy advice. Three discrimination tests are specified at the design level, identifying the data and study horizons the literature does not currently provide.
