# The Quality Mechanism in Occupational Licensing: What Deregulation Analysis Assumes Without Specifying

Roughly a quarter of U.S. workers are in licensed occupations, up from under five percent in 1950. The economics literature has established that this expansion raises practitioner wages by ten to eighteen percent with considerable reliability. It has established with much less reliability that it raises consumer quality. The dominant finding-wages up, quality ambiguous-has been repeated across studies of dentistry, cosmetology, law, and medicine for thirty years. What it has not produced is an explanation of why quality effects are ambiguous when wage effects are not.

The explanation, in part, is that the mechanism connecting licensing to quality has not been specified. The literature knows that licensing raises wages through entry restriction: the supply of licensed practitioners is bounded by the credentialing requirement, and the wage premium reflects the scarcity rent this restriction creates. The quality story is less settled. Licensing could improve quality by screening out incompetent practitioners, concentrating higher-ability or better-motivated applicants in the credentialed pool. It could also improve quality by instilling professional norms through the training process required for credentialing, producing practitioners who follow quality standards as a professional obligation rather than a market incentive. These two accounts are not observationally equivalent. They make different predictions-specifically, they make different predictions at the moment of deregulation-and existing studies do not distinguish them.

This piece audits five canonical texts in the licensing literature against a standard three-level mechanism framework, develops both mechanism candidates to the point at which they generate discriminable predictions, and specifies the tests that would allow a researcher to determine which mechanism operates in a specific deregulation event. The tests are specified at the design level, not run empirically here. The piece's contribution is to show that the tests are feasible, that existing deregulation studies do not run them, and that the gap produces false precision in policy advice-specifically, advice about whether and how quickly quality will change when a licensing requirement is removed.

## The Mechanism Audit

Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level framework distinguishes: the *situational mechanism* (what institutional arrangement creates conditions for individual action?), the *action-formation mechanism* (what process transforms those conditions into behavior?), and the *aggregation mechanism* (how do individual behaviors produce the collective outcome?). A causal explanation that specifies all three supports design inference and transplant prediction. Situational mechanisms without action-formation accounts describe institutional conditions without explaining how actors respond to them; aggregation mechanisms without individual-level behavioral derivations produce numerical outputs that are uninterpretable for design.

Five canonical texts populate the audit:

| Text | Situational Mechanism | Action-Formation (quality channel) | Aggregation |
|------|----------------------|-------------------------------------|-------------|
| Kleiner (2006), *JEP* | Specified: licensing creates credentialed pool; state boards enforce entry; renewal requirements sustain monitoring | Underspecified: "ensuring minimum competence" stated as rationale; process connecting credential to practitioner conduct not given | Asserted: market quality assumed to track quality of credentialed practitioners |
| Kleiner & Krueger (2013), *Economic Journal* | Specified: licensing concentrated in occupations with significant consumer-protection rationale and high human-capital requirements | Underspecified: quality "supported by credential requirements"; entry screen's mechanism left unanalyzed | Asserted: supply restriction derives wage premium; quality mechanism is stated purpose, not derived consequence |
| Kleiner & Kudrle (2000), *JLE* | Specified: state dental boards require licensure, administer examinations, enforce scope-of-practice restrictions | Underspecified: quality measured by dental health outcomes across states with different licensing stringency; no mechanism specified for how stringency connects to individual dentist behavior | Asserted: aggregate dental outcomes track individual dental quality through unspecified mapping |
| Gittleman & Kleiner (2016), *ILR Review* | Specified: licensing and union membership as parallel supply-restriction institutions | Not specified: quality is not the paper's object; wage mechanism (supply restriction) is developed; quality mechanism is treated as background justification | Not applicable to quality channel |
| Representative deregulation studies | Specified: state-level removal or reduction of credential requirements creates variation in licensing status | Underspecified: quality effects measured pre/post without specifying the mechanism by which the change produces the measured effect | Asserted: aggregate quality outcomes track licensing status; direction of mechanism unspecified |

The pattern is consistent. Situational mechanisms are specified clearly: licensing creates institutional conditions, boards exist, requirements are enforceable. Aggregation mechanisms are asserted without derivation: market quality tracks licensee quality, or tracks licensing stringency, without specifying the mapping from individual behavior to collective outcome. The action-formation mechanisms in the quality channel are the consistent gap: no text specifies what process connects the credential to individual practitioner behavior.

This is not an oversight by individual authors. It reflects a structural feature of how the economics of licensing literature is organized. Wage effects suit the supply-restriction account, which specifies an action-formation mechanism: practitioners respond to price incentives at the margin of entry and exit. Quality effects require a different account, because quality is not directly observable to practitioners and consumers in the way that wages are. The literature has tracked quality through outcomes (dental health, consumer complaints, mortality rates) without deriving the pathway from credential to conduct that would explain why those outcomes should move-or, at deregulation, stop moving.

## Two Mechanism Candidates

The gap in the literature can be filled by either of two mechanism candidates. They are not competitors in the sense that one is right and one is wrong everywhere; they may operate in different proportions in different occupational contexts. The existing literature has not determined those proportions for any specific occupation.

### Mechanism 1: Information-Selection

Under the information-selection account, licensing raises quality through two linked processes. First, the credential requirement screens applicants: passing the examination or completing the required training is more difficult for less competent applicants, so the credentialed pool is higher-quality than an uncredentialed pool would be. Second, the credential provides a consumer signal: clients who cannot evaluate service quality directly use the credential as a proxy, seeking credentialed practitioners and allowing the market to sustain a quality premium.

The operative conditions are: (a) clients cannot evaluate quality ex ante or ex post without prohibitive cost-licensing is most justified for experience goods and credence goods, and least justified where quality is observable through reputation or outcome; (b) the credential requirement discriminates across the quality distribution-a pass-all examination provides no selection; (c) clients attend to the credential signal when choosing practitioners.

The failure modes are well-established. If examination pass rates approach one hundred percent, condition (b) fails and the screening mechanism collapses into a tax on entry without quality benefit. If consumers cannot identify credential status or do not condition purchases on it, condition (c) fails. If quality is observable through peer review or repeated purchase independent of credentials, the signal function is redundant.

The deregulation prediction under information-selection is immediate. Removing the credential removes both the entry screen and the consumer signal simultaneously. Average quality in the deregulated market declines promptly as unlicensed practitioners enter, and the consumer-information function that allowed the market to sustain the quality premium disappears. Quality effects are concentrated in the lower tail of the practitioner distribution and appear within the first years of deregulation.

### Mechanism 2: Identity-Socialization

Under the identity-socialization account, licensing raises quality through professional socialization. The training, apprenticeship, and examination processes required to obtain licensure transmit professional norms that practitioners internalize as a component of professional identity. Quality maintenance is not primarily a response to monitoring or market incentives; it is an expression of who practitioners understand themselves to be as professionals.

The operative conditions are: (a) the training process actively transmits professional norms in addition to technical competencies-practitioners acquire values, not just skills; (b) professional self-understanding creates obligations that practitioners follow independent of monitoring; (c) the socialization is durable-professional identity is stable through a practitioner's career once formed.

The failure modes are different. If training is purely technical certification with no normative transmission, condition (a) fails. If professional identity is fragile and dissolves under market pressure to cut costs, condition (b) fails. If post-credential market pressures override socialized norms at the margin, the mechanism operates but with less force than the pure account implies.

The deregulation prediction under identity-socialization is delayed. Removing the credential removes the entry requirement but leaves incumbent practitioners' professional socialization intact. Quality maintained by socialization does not decline when the credential is removed; it declines as pre-deregulation cohorts age out and are replaced by practitioners who entered under no mandatory socialization requirement. The timing of quality decline is proportional to occupational turnover rates. At turnover rates typical in licensed professions-average career lengths of twenty-five to thirty-five years-this delay may be fifteen to twenty-five years, a span within which most deregulation studies terminate their observation.

## What the Sociology of Professions Already Knows

The mechanism distinction I have been proposing has a history in sociology that the economics of licensing does not engage. This is itself an institutional finding, but it is worth establishing directly rather than presuming.

Eliot Freidson's *Professionalism: The Third Logic* (2001) argues that professionalism constitutes a distinct mode of organizing expert work, irreducible to market competition or bureaucratic control. The organizing force under the professional logic is internalized values: a commitment to service as an ideal and to an ideology that justifies the particular privileges the occupation has been granted. This is the identity-socialization mechanism stated in plain terms. Practitioners maintain quality because professional identity requires it, not because monitoring detects failures or competition punishes defection.

Freidson is explicit that this mechanism requires the training process to transmit values, not merely technical competencies. Professional schools, apprenticeships, and licensing examinations are socialization institutions. They do not merely certify that a candidate knows enough; they produce practitioners who understand themselves as members of a profession with obligations. Remove mandatory training, and you remove the mechanism-not immediately, because incumbents' socialization persists, but gradually, as the cohort that underwent mandatory socialization retires.

Andrew Abbott's *The System of Professions* (1988) adds a complication useful for the discrimination tests. For Abbott, quality maintenance is partly constitutive of the profession's claim to exclusive jurisdiction over a domain of work. Professions sustain quality not only because practitioners have internalized norms but because the jurisdictional claim is what the profession needs to maintain its monopoly on practice. Under this account, quality maintenance is partly strategic: it is part of what professions do to defend their territory. The credential is the institutional expression of the jurisdictional claim, and removing it threatens not only the signal to consumers but the claim itself.

Abbott's account implies a mixed mechanism. Professional identity (Freidson's socialization) and jurisdictional strategy (Abbott's boundary work) are both operative, and the mix may vary by occupation. An occupation with strong voluntary professional associations can maintain jurisdictional claims independent of state licensing; one whose jurisdictional claim is constituted entirely through the state credential may see quality effects appear more quickly at deregulation than Freidson's pure socialization account would predict.

Magali Larson's *The Rise of Professionalism* (1977) is the skeptical counterpoint. On Larson's account, professional ideology-the belief that practitioners follow norms as a professional obligation-is created by the credentialing system and serves the professional project of market closure. Whether practitioners genuinely internalize the ideology, or merely perform it while the credential system reinforces it, is not established. If the ideology is purely instrumental, quality maintenance is conditional on the monitoring and reputation mechanisms the credential system sustains; remove the credential, and quality declines immediately.

The economics of licensing literature, where it reads the sociological tradition at all, reads it through Larson: as ideological cover for wage extraction. This reading is available in the texts but it is not what Freidson argues, and it leads the economics literature to ignore exactly the mechanism claim that would make its deregulation predictions more precise. The more important observation is that neither subdiscipline has derived the testable prediction that the distinction implies. Freidson articulates the socialization mechanism without asking what an observable deregulation event would reveal about whether it is genuine. The economics literature uses deregulation events as natural experiments without specifying which mechanism they test.

## The Temporal Signature of Deregulation

What makes the occupational licensing case analytically useful-and what distinguishes it from the referral hiring and commons governance analyses in [*Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/) and [*The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance*](posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/)-is structural, not merely that the diagnostic applies to a new domain.

Neither referral networks nor commons institutions face a selective removal event that eliminates the formal requirement while leaving incumbent practitioners in place. When a commons institution fails through privatization or government takeover, the entire institutional context changes simultaneously; the mechanism change is confounded with the context change. When referral hiring practices shift, the social network that produces referrals transforms with the practices; there is no moment at which the mechanism is removed while the practitioners who embody it remain.

Occupational licensing deregulation is structurally different. The credential requirement is administrative. Remove it, and incumbents-whoever obtained credentials under the previous regime-remain in place with whatever training and socialization they acquired. New entrants after the deregulation event face no mandatory socialization requirement. The event creates a natural cohort division: pre-deregulation practitioners (potentially socialized) and post-deregulation entrants (not mandatorily socialized). This division is observable in principle if individual-level quality data are linked to credential timing.

Professional association infrastructure creates an additional observable unique to the licensing context. Licensed occupations often maintain voluntary professional associations that provide continuing education, accreditation, and peer review independent of state licensing requirements. Under the identity-socialization mechanism, practitioners affiliated with such associations should maintain quality after deregulation because the association continues the socialization function. Under the information-selection mechanism, association membership should have no independent quality effect, because voluntary associations do not replicate the entry-screening function that mandatory licensing performed-a voluntary association that any practitioner can join does not restrict the supply of practitioners in the way a mandatory credential does.

The domain-specific contribution is this: the deregulation event creates a temporal experiment-and the professional association infrastructure creates a natural control-that are unavailable in the other institutional domains where the information-normative distinction has been applied. The abstract mechanism distinction from [*What Actors Must Be Doing*](posts/2026-06-09-what-actors-must-be-doing-the-informatio-22ce/) becomes empirically testable in licensing precisely because these structural features are present.

## Discrimination Tests for a Concrete Case

Funeral director licensing provides the running case. All fifty states license funeral directors; requirements vary considerably in their stringency across states and have changed within states over time, creating natural variation. The National Funeral Directors Association maintains a voluntary accreditation and membership structure independent of state licensing, providing exactly the professional association infrastructure the third test requires.

Three tests would allow a researcher to determine which mechanism dominates in a specific funeral director deregulation event.

**Test 1: Timing of aggregate quality decline.**

*Data required:* Quality measures-consumer complaints to state boards, health code violation rates, inspection failure rates-at annual or semi-annual intervals before and after the deregulation event. At minimum, five years of pre-event data and ten years of post-event data, with sufficient power to detect changes of the relevant magnitude within two years of deregulation.

*Observable implication under information-selection:* Statistically detectable decline in average quality within two to three years of deregulation. The mechanism predicts immediate degradation as the entry screen is removed and unlicensed entrants reach market share.

*Observable implication under identity-socialization:* No statistically detectable change within two to three years. Decline emerges over a longer horizon, correlated with the rate at which pre-deregulation cohorts retire.

*Current status:* Existing deregulation studies report pre/post quality comparisons but do not report the temporal pattern of change within the post period at the granularity this test requires. A study that found no immediate effect and concluded licensing produced no quality benefit would not rule out socialization; the null result is uninformative without the timing.

**Test 2: Cohort-level quality tracking.**

*Data required:* Individual-level quality outcomes linked to credential timing-specifically, whether the practitioner obtained credentials before or after the deregulation event. Sufficient panel length to compare both cohorts at similar career stages: five to fifteen years of outcome data on each cohort.

*Observable implication under information-selection:* No systematic quality difference between pre- and post-deregulation cohorts at the same career stage; if any premium exists in the pre-deregulation cohort, it should reflect selection differences at entry, not training differences, and need not persist across the career.

*Observable implication under identity-socialization:* Pre-deregulation cohorts maintain higher quality at the same career stage than post-deregulation cohorts; the quality difference persists through the career because it reflects internalized norms rather than temporary advantages at entry.

*Current status:* No existing deregulation study in the economics of licensing literature reports individual-level quality data linked to credential timing. Consumer complaints and violation rates are collected at the business level-the funeral home, the dental practice-rather than at the individual practitioner level, making cohort tracking structurally impossible with existing datasets. This is a data-collection constraint, not merely an analysis constraint.

**Test 3: Professional association membership as natural control.**

*Data required:* Quality outcomes cross-referenced with voluntary professional association membership (NFDA membership for funeral directors) before and after deregulation events, with sufficient variation in membership rates within the deregulated market to support comparison.

*Observable implication under information-selection:* Association membership has no independent quality effect after deregulation; voluntary associations do not replicate the entry-screening function that mandatory licensing performed. Pre/post quality trajectories for members should not diverge from those for non-members after deregulation.

*Observable implication under identity-socialization:* Association membership predicts quality maintenance after deregulation; the association continues the socialization function after mandatory licensing is lifted. The quality gap between members and non-members should widen in the years after deregulation, as non-member new entrants without mandatory socialization enter the market while member practitioners maintain association-enforced professional norms.

*Current status:* Voluntary professional association membership is not collected in existing deregulation studies. The distinction between NFDA members and non-members is structurally invisible in the data, which means the most diagnostic natural control the deregulation event makes available is not being used.

## What the Gap Costs

The policy cost of the mechanism gap is specific and has a direction. When a deregulation study finds no immediate quality decline after a licensing requirement is removed, the standard interpretation is that licensing produced no quality benefit-and therefore that deregulation can proceed without quality cost. This inference is correct under information-selection, where immediate quality effects are exactly what the mechanism predicts. But it is incorrect under identity-socialization: if quality is maintained by professional socialization that survives credential removal, the immediate null finding is precisely what the mechanism predicts. The quality cost of deregulation, under the socialization account, arrives on a delay proportional to occupational turnover.

This delay is not a minor qualification. A state that deregulates funeral director licensing and finds no quality decline in the first four years may be observing a quality lag that will become visible fifteen years later, as the pre-deregulation cohort retires. The policy decision and its quality consequence are separated by a span that exceeds most regulatory impact assessments and most political careers. The standard practice of recommending deregulation on the basis of near-term null results in quality is systematically biased toward deregulation relative to what the evidentiary base warrants-and the bias is directional because the null in the near term is consistent with both mechanisms, while the longer-term prediction is not.

The mechanism gap also matters for where quality effects concentrate. Under information-selection, quality decline is distributed across the practitioner pool as unlicensed entrants reach market share-it is visible in average quality measures. Under identity-socialization, quality decline is concentrated in new entrants and firms that are not affiliated with voluntary professional associations. Aggregate averages will decline more slowly than the new-entrant distribution, and an evaluation that tracks aggregate outcomes will underestimate the quality exposure of consumers who patronize the least-socialized segment of the market.

The three discrimination tests specified above are not exotic research designs. They require individual-level quality data linked to credential timing, temporal granularity in quality measurement, and voluntary association membership records-none of which requires new instrumentation, only new data collection protocols in regulatory impact assessments and in state licensing board records. The reason these data are not collected is that the question motivating their collection-which mechanism accounts for quality maintenance-has not been asked by the literature that is best positioned to answer it.

Specifying the mechanism is not a methodological nicety added to a literature that already reaches correct conclusions. It is the difference between a policy recommendation that accounts for the full time horizon of deregulation's quality consequences and one that mistakes a temporal lag for the absence of an effect.
