# The Quality Mechanism in Occupational Licensing: What Deregulation Analysis Assumes Without Specifying

Roughly a quarter of U.S. workers are in licensed occupations, up from under five percent in 1950. The economics literature has established that this expansion raises practitioner wages by ten to eighteen percent with considerable reliability. Its record on quality effects is more mixed: across major studies of dentistry, cosmetology, law, and medicine, point estimates of quality effects cluster near zero or modestly positive, the sign reverses across occupations and deregulation contexts, and only a minority of studies report improvements large enough to justify the wage premium on consumer-protection grounds alone. The dominant pattern-wages up reliably, quality effects near zero and sign-variable-has replicated for thirty years. What it has not produced is an explanation of why quality effects are ambiguous when wage effects are not.

The explanation, in part, is that the mechanism connecting licensing to quality has not been specified. The literature knows that licensing raises wages through entry restriction: the supply of licensed practitioners is bounded by the credentialing requirement, and the wage premium reflects the scarcity rent this restriction creates. The quality story is less settled. Licensing could improve quality by screening out incompetent practitioners, concentrating higher-ability applicants in the credentialed pool, or by providing consumers who cannot evaluate service directly with a quality proxy. It could also improve quality by instilling professional norms through the training process required for credentialing, producing practitioners who follow quality standards as a professional obligation rather than a market incentive. These two accounts-information-selection and identity-socialization-are not observationally equivalent. They make different predictions, specifically at the moment of deregulation, and existing studies do not distinguish them.

This piece audits five canonical texts in the licensing literature against a standard three-level mechanism framework, develops both mechanism candidates to the point at which they generate discriminable predictions, and specifies the tests that would allow a researcher to determine which mechanism operates in a specific deregulation event. The tests are specified at the design level, not run empirically here. The piece's contribution is to show that the tests are specifiable at this level, that they require data and study horizons existing deregulation studies do not provide, and that this gap produces false precision in policy advice-specifically, advice about whether and how quickly quality will change when a licensing requirement is removed.

## The Mechanism Audit

Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level framework distinguishes: the *situational mechanism* (what institutional arrangement creates conditions for individual action?), the *action-formation mechanism* (what process transforms those conditions into behavior?), and the *aggregation mechanism* (how do individual behaviors produce the collective outcome?). A causal explanation that specifies all three supports design inference and transplant prediction. Situational mechanisms without action-formation accounts describe institutional conditions without explaining how actors respond to them; aggregation mechanisms without individual-level behavioral derivations produce numerical outputs that are uninterpretable for design.

Five canonical texts populate the audit:

| Text | Situational Mechanism | Action-Formation (quality channel) | Aggregation |
|------|----------------------|-------------------------------------|-------------|
| Kleiner (2006), *JEP* | Specified: licensing creates credentialed pool; state boards enforce entry; renewal requirements sustain monitoring | Underspecified: "ensuring minimum competence" stated as rationale; process connecting credential to practitioner conduct not given | Asserted: market quality assumed to track quality of credentialed practitioners |
| Kleiner & Krueger (2013), *Economic Journal* | Specified: licensing concentrated in occupations with significant consumer-protection rationale and high human-capital requirements | Underspecified: quality "supported by credential requirements"; entry screen's mechanism left unanalyzed | Asserted: supply restriction derives wage premium; quality mechanism is stated purpose, not derived consequence |
| Kleiner & Kudrle (2000), *JLE* | Specified: state dental boards require licensure, administer examinations, enforce scope-of-practice restrictions | Underspecified: quality measured by dental health outcomes across states with different licensing stringency; no mechanism specified for how stringency connects to individual dentist behavior | Asserted: aggregate dental outcomes track individual dental quality through unspecified mapping |
| Gittleman & Kleiner (2016), *ILR Review* | Specified: licensing and union membership as parallel supply-restriction institutions | Not specified: quality is not the paper's object; wage mechanism (supply restriction) is developed; quality mechanism is treated as background justification | Not applicable to quality channel |
| Representative deregulation studies | Specified: state-level removal or reduction of credential requirements creates variation in licensing status | Underspecified: quality effects measured pre/post without specifying the mechanism by which the change produces the measured effect | Asserted: aggregate quality outcomes track licensing status; direction of mechanism unspecified |

The pattern is consistent. Situational mechanisms are specified clearly: licensing creates institutional conditions, boards exist, requirements are enforceable. Aggregation mechanisms are asserted without derivation: market quality tracks licensee quality, or tracks licensing stringency, without specifying the mapping from individual behavior to collective outcome. The action-formation mechanisms in the quality channel are the consistent gap: no text specifies what process connects the credential to individual practitioner behavior.

This is not an oversight by individual authors. It reflects a structural feature of how the economics of licensing literature is organized. Wage effects suit the supply-restriction account, which specifies an action-formation mechanism: practitioners respond to price incentives at the margin of entry and exit. Quality effects require a different account, because quality is not directly observable to practitioners and consumers in the way that wages are. The literature has tracked quality through outcomes (dental health, consumer complaints, mortality rates) without deriving the pathway from credential to conduct that would explain why those outcomes should move-or, at deregulation, stop moving.

## Two Mechanism Candidates

The gap in the literature can be filled by either of two mechanism candidates. They are not competitors in the sense that one is right and one is wrong everywhere; they may operate in different proportions in different occupational contexts. The existing literature has not determined those proportions for any specific occupation.

### Mechanism 1: Information-Selection

Under the information-selection account, licensing raises quality through two linked processes that the literature tends to treat as a single mechanism but that have distinct operative conditions and fail through different channels.

The first is **entry screening**: the credential requirement filters the practitioner pool, with examination or training requirements more difficult for less competent applicants, so the credentialed pool is higher-quality than an uncredentialed pool would be. The second is **consumer signaling**: the credential provides consumers who cannot evaluate quality directly with a proxy for service quality, allowing the market to sustain a quality premium for credentialed practitioners.

Entry screening requires: (a) the examination or training requirement to genuinely discriminate across the quality distribution-a pass-all examination provides no selection benefit; (b) quality to be substantially a function of trainable competencies. Consumer signaling requires: (c) clients to be unable to evaluate quality ex ante or ex post without prohibitive cost-the mechanism applies most forcefully to experience goods and credence goods; (d) clients to attend to the credential signal when selecting practitioners.

The failure modes are independent. Screening fails when examination pass rates approach one hundred percent, condition (a). Signaling fails when consumers cannot identify credential status or do not condition purchases on it, condition (d). A credential can maintain high discriminatory power at entry while being invisible to consumers; a credential can provide a strong consumer signal while discriminating negligibly at entry. Empirically, high examination pass rates are documented across many licensed occupations, suggesting that the entry-screening sub-mechanism has frequently already collapsed in those occupations while the consumer-signaling function persists-a combination that changes the deregulation prediction.

When both sub-mechanisms are operative, the deregulation prediction is prompt: removing the credential simultaneously removes the entry screen and the consumer signal, and quality effects appear within two to five years as unlicensed practitioners reach market share and the quality information function for consumers disappears. When only signaling was operative (screening having collapsed via near-universal pass rates), the immediate loss at deregulation is the consumer-information function rather than average pool quality; the quality effects concentrate in consumer access to quality identification rather than in average service quality directly, but they still appear promptly.

### Mechanism 2: Identity-Socialization

Under the identity-socialization account, licensing raises quality through professional socialization. The training, apprenticeship, and examination processes required to obtain licensure transmit professional norms that practitioners internalize as a component of professional identity. Quality maintenance is not primarily a response to monitoring or market incentives; it is an expression of who practitioners understand themselves to be as professionals.

The operative conditions are: (a) the training process actively transmits professional norms in addition to technical competencies-practitioners acquire values, not just skills; (b) mandatory training constitutes the *dominant* vehicle for this socialization, rather than one of several pathways that persist independently of the credential requirement; (c) professional self-understanding creates obligations that practitioners follow independent of monitoring; (d) the socialization is durable-professional identity remains stable through a practitioner's career once formed.

Condition (b) requires explicit statement because the deregulation prediction depends on it. If practitioners can acquire professional identity through voluntary association participation, informal peer networks, or apprenticeship structures that persist after state licensing is removed, then the credential removal eliminates one socialization pathway without eliminating the opportunity for socialization. The expected timing of quality decline would depend not on occupational turnover but on whether and how quickly alternative pathways compensate.

The failure modes are distinct from information-selection's. If training is purely technical certification with no normative transmission, condition (a) fails and the mechanism reduces to information-selection's screening function. If professional identity can form through channels independent of mandatory training, condition (b) fails and the delay prediction is weakened. If professional identity is fragile and erodes under competitive market pressure to cut costs, condition (c) fails.

**The deregulation prediction under identity-socialization is delayed.** Removing the credential removes the mandatory entry requirement but leaves incumbent practitioners' professional socialization intact. Quality maintained by internalized norms does not decline when the credential is removed; it declines as pre-deregulation cohorts age out and are replaced by practitioners who entered under no mandatory socialization requirement. If mean career length in the occupation is $L$ years and deregulation occurs at time $t_0$, the transition window under full cohort replacement spans approximately $[t_0,\; t_0 + L]$, with the midpoint of the expected aggregate quality decline near $t_0 + L/2$. At career lengths typical in licensed professions-twenty-five to thirty-five years-this places the midpoint of the quality transition at twelve to eighteen years post-deregulation, well beyond the five-to-seven year horizon of most regulatory impact assessments.

**Within-career drift as a distinct variant.** The timing prediction above assumes durable socialization: professional identity formed at entry persists through a practitioner's career. An alternative variant allows identity to erode within individuals as post-deregulation market norms diffuse. If new entrants without mandatory socialization progressively change the professional culture of the market-and if incumbent practitioners update their conduct in response-quality declines not only as the pre-deregulation cohort retires but *within* individual careers. This within-career drift produces a temporal signature intermediate between information-selection's sharp near-term decline and the cohort-replacement delay: quality begins declining early but accumulates gradually across years rather than holding steady through each incumbent's career. The discrimination tests below identify the durable-socialization form; a pattern of gradual within-period decline that precedes cohort replacement would support the drift variant instead.

## What the Sociology of Professions Already Knows

The mechanism distinction I have been proposing has a history in sociology that the economics of licensing does not engage. This is itself an institutional finding, but it is worth establishing directly rather than presuming.

Eliot Freidson's *Professionalism: The Third Logic* (2001) argues that professionalism constitutes a distinct mode of organizing expert work, irreducible to market competition or bureaucratic control. The organizing force under the professional logic is internalized values: a commitment to service as an ideal and to an ideology that justifies the particular privileges the occupation has been granted. This is the identity-socialization mechanism stated in plain terms. Practitioners maintain quality because professional identity requires it, not because monitoring detects failures or competition punishes defection.

Freidson is explicit that this mechanism requires the training process to transmit values, not merely technical competencies. Professional schools, apprenticeships, and licensing examinations are socialization institutions. They do not merely certify that a candidate knows enough; they produce practitioners who understand themselves as members of a profession with obligations. Remove mandatory training, and you remove the mechanism-not immediately, because incumbents' socialization persists, but gradually, as the cohort that underwent mandatory socialization retires.

Andrew Abbott's *The System of Professions* (1988) adds a complication useful for the discrimination tests. For Abbott, quality maintenance is partly constitutive of the profession's claim to exclusive jurisdiction over a domain of work. Professions sustain quality not only because practitioners have internalized norms but because the jurisdictional claim is what the profession needs to maintain its monopoly on practice. Under this account, quality maintenance is partly strategic: it is part of what professions do to defend their territory. The credential is the institutional expression of the jurisdictional claim, and removing it threatens not only the signal to consumers but the claim itself.

Abbott's account implies a mixed mechanism. Professional identity (Freidson's socialization) and jurisdictional strategy (Abbott's boundary work) are both operative, and the mix may vary by occupation. An occupation with strong voluntary professional associations can maintain jurisdictional claims independent of state licensing; one whose jurisdictional claim is constituted entirely through the state credential may see quality effects appear more quickly at deregulation than Freidson's pure socialization account would predict.

Magali Larson's *The Rise of Professionalism* (1977) is the skeptical counterpoint. On Larson's account, professional ideology-the belief that practitioners follow norms as a professional obligation-is created by the credentialing system and serves the professional project of market closure. Whether practitioners genuinely internalize the ideology, or merely perform it while the credential system reinforces it, is not established. If the ideology is purely instrumental, quality maintenance is conditional on the monitoring and reputation mechanisms the credential system sustains; remove the credential, and quality declines immediately.

The economics of licensing literature, where it reads the sociological tradition at all, reads it through Larson: as ideological cover for wage extraction. This reading is available in the texts but it is not what Freidson argues, and it leads the economics literature to ignore exactly the mechanism claim that would make its deregulation predictions more precise. The more important observation is that neither subdiscipline has derived the testable prediction that the distinction implies. Freidson articulates the socialization mechanism without asking what an observable deregulation event would reveal about whether it is genuine. The economics literature uses deregulation events as natural experiments without specifying which mechanism they test.

Abbott's account does not straightforwardly generate a separate set of discrimination tests for the deregulation setting. The jurisdictional-maintenance mechanism predicts that an occupation with strong voluntary associations can sustain quality norms independent of state licensing-a pattern that would appear in the data similarly to identity-socialization's delayed decline. Separating jurisdictional maintenance from internalized professional identity requires measuring the organizational strength of the voluntary association, not merely its existence. The discrimination tests specified below are therefore designed to distinguish information-selection from the identity-normative family broadly-encompassing both Freidson's practitioner-level socialization and Abbott's organization-level jurisdictional maintenance-rather than to disaggregate that family internally.

## The Temporal Signature of Deregulation

What makes the occupational licensing case analytically useful-and what distinguishes it from the referral hiring and commons governance analyses in [*Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/) and [*The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance*](posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/)-is structural, not merely that the diagnostic applies to a new domain.

Neither referral networks nor commons institutions face a selective removal event that eliminates the formal requirement while leaving incumbent practitioners in place. When a commons institution fails through privatization or government takeover, the entire institutional context changes simultaneously; the mechanism change is confounded with the context change. When referral hiring practices shift, the social network that produces referrals transforms with the practices; there is no moment at which the mechanism is removed while the practitioners who embody it remain.

Occupational licensing deregulation is structurally different. The credential requirement is administrative. Remove it, and incumbents-whoever obtained credentials under the previous regime-remain in place with whatever training and socialization they acquired. New entrants after the deregulation event face no mandatory socialization requirement. The event creates a natural cohort division: pre-deregulation practitioners (potentially socialized) and post-deregulation entrants (not mandatorily socialized). This division is observable in principle if individual-level quality data are linked to credential timing.

Professional association infrastructure creates an additional observable unique to the licensing context. Licensed occupations often maintain voluntary professional associations that provide continuing education, accreditation, and peer review independent of state licensing requirements. Under the identity-socialization mechanism, practitioners affiliated with such associations should maintain quality after deregulation because the association continues the socialization function. Under the information-selection mechanism, association membership should have no independent quality effect, because voluntary associations do not replicate the entry-screening function that mandatory licensing performed-a voluntary association that any practitioner can join does not restrict the supply of practitioners in the way a mandatory credential does.

The domain-specific contribution is this: the deregulation event creates a temporal experiment-and the professional association infrastructure creates a natural control-that are unavailable in the other institutional domains where the information-normative distinction has been applied. The abstract mechanism distinction from [*What Actors Must Be Doing*](posts/2026-06-09-what-actors-must-be-doing-the-informatio-22ce/) becomes empirically testable in licensing precisely because these structural features are present.

## Discrimination Tests for a Concrete Case

Funeral director licensing provides the running case. All fifty states license funeral directors; requirements vary considerably in stringency across states and have changed within states over time, creating natural treatment candidates. The National Funeral Directors Association maintains a voluntary accreditation and membership structure independent of state licensing, providing exactly the professional association infrastructure the third test requires. The case is chosen for this infrastructure rather than for a specific documented deregulation event; the tests are designed for application to a discrete treatment-a state that eliminated or significantly modified a credentialing requirement in a named year-and identifying that event is a prior design step. The specifications that follow are general enough to transfer to any licensed occupation that combines a discrete deregulation event with voluntary professional association infrastructure.

One structural feature of the funeral director case deserves explicit note. Funeral services are infrequent purchases; most consumers experience them at most a handful of times in a lifetime. This makes consumer-side reputation mechanisms and repeat-purchase discipline particularly weak and the credential signal particularly load-bearing relative to occupations with richer consumer feedback. Funeral directing is therefore a case where information-selection's consumer-signaling sub-mechanism should be strongest-not a representative licensed occupation, but one that provides a bound on the signal mechanism's performance under favorable conditions. Findings from this setting may not transfer to occupations where consumers can exercise frequent quality judgments.

Three tests would allow a researcher to determine which mechanism dominates in a specific funeral director deregulation event.

**Test 1: Timing of aggregate quality decline.**

*Data required:* Quality measures-consumer complaints to state boards, health code violation rates, inspection failure rates-at annual or semi-annual intervals before and after the deregulation event. At minimum, five years of pre-event data and ten years of post-event data.

*Observable implication under information-selection:* Statistically detectable decline in average quality within two to three years of deregulation. The mechanism predicts immediate degradation as the entry screen is removed or the consumer signal disappears.

*Observable implication under identity-socialization (durable form):* No statistically detectable change within two to three years. Decline emerges over a longer horizon, correlated with the rate at which pre-deregulation cohorts retire, with the midpoint of the expected transition near $t_0 + L/2$.

*On interpreting a near-term null.* A finding of no detectable quality change within two to three years of deregulation is not, on its own, evidence for identity-socialization. At least three other accounts produce the same observation. First, insufficient statistical power: consumer complaint rates and health-code violation rates are highly variable across states and years; if the plausible effect size is small relative to the noise floor, neither mechanism will be detectable in the early post-period. The near-term null is informative only if the statistical design has documented power to detect effects of plausible magnitude, which requires an ex ante estimate of both effect size and the outcome's variance in the pre-period. Second, slow market entry: even under pure information-selection, unlicensed entrants require time to build market share; if entry is slow, quality decline may be genuine but not yet large enough to detect within two to three years. Third, Larson's instrumental reading: if the credential's quality function was negligible in this occupation to begin with-because pass rates were near-universal, quality is not substantially trainable, or other mechanisms disciplined quality independently of licensing-the near-term null reflects a null mechanism, not a delayed one. Identifying which account explains the null is a precondition for drawing policy inferences from it.

*On measurement-apparatus changes.* A further confound is specific to licensing deregulation and is rarely named. Consumer complaint rates and health-code violation rates are heavily censored outcomes whose measurement depends on inspection frequency, complaint infrastructure, and reporting incentives, all of which can shift when a licensing regime is removed. When a state eliminates a licensing requirement, it frequently also reduces or eliminates the state board's budget, inspection schedules, and complaint-processing capacity that generated the measured outcome. If post-deregulation inspection frequency drops, measured violation rates may decline mechanically even as actual quality declines. Test 1 cannot distinguish a genuine quality null from an apparatus-level change without documenting the inspection infrastructure before and after the event. This is the licensing-domain instance of the apparatus-blindness problem developed in [*What the Apparatus Refuses to See*](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) and formalized as a composition rule in [*Pipelines Cannot See Better*](posts/2026-06-19-pipelines-cannot-see-better-a-compositio-c9e6/): the pipeline from actual quality to measured complaint rate passes through inspection infrastructure, and removing the licensing regime can narrow or sever that pipeline independently of changes in the underlying quality it measures.

*Current status:* Existing deregulation studies report pre/post quality comparisons but do not report the temporal pattern of change within the post period at the granularity this test requires, do not document changes in the inspection infrastructure at the event, and do not establish that the design had power to detect effects of the relevant magnitude.

**Test 2: Cohort-level quality tracking.**

*Data required:* Individual-level quality outcomes linked to credential timing-specifically, whether the practitioner obtained credentials before or after the deregulation event. Sufficient panel length to compare both cohorts at similar career stages: five to fifteen years of outcome data on each cohort.

*Observable implication under information-selection:* No systematic quality difference between pre- and post-deregulation cohorts at the same career stage; if any premium exists in the pre-deregulation cohort, it reflects selection differences at entry and need not persist across the career.

*Observable implication under identity-socialization:* Pre-deregulation cohorts maintain higher quality at the same career stage than post-deregulation cohorts; the quality difference persists through the career because it reflects internalized norms rather than temporary entry advantages.

*Current status:* No existing deregulation study in the economics of licensing literature reports individual-level quality data linked to credential timing. Consumer complaints and violation rates are collected at the business level-the funeral home, the dental practice-rather than at the individual practitioner level, making cohort tracking structurally impossible with existing datasets. This is a data-collection constraint, not merely an analysis constraint: the test requires regulatory protocols to begin collecting individual-practitioner outcomes, not only analysis of existing data.

**Test 3: Professional association membership as natural control.**

*Data required:* Quality outcomes cross-referenced with voluntary professional association membership (NFDA membership for funeral directors) before and after deregulation events, with sufficient variation in membership rates within the deregulated market to support comparison.

*Observable implication under information-selection:* Association membership has no independent quality effect after deregulation; voluntary associations do not replicate the entry-screening function that mandatory licensing performed, and a voluntary association that any practitioner can join does not restrict the practitioner supply the way a mandatory credential does. Pre/post quality trajectories for members should not diverge from those for non-members after deregulation.

*Observable implication under identity-socialization:* Association membership predicts quality maintenance after deregulation; the association continues the socialization function after mandatory licensing is lifted. The quality gap between members and non-members should widen in the years after deregulation, as non-member new entrants without mandatory socialization enter the market while member practitioners maintain association-enforced professional norms.

*On selection into membership.* Practitioners who join the NFDA may differ from non-joiners on quality-relevant unobservable dimensions-motivation, professional commitment, prior training quality-in ways unrelated to any socialization the association provides. A simple post-deregulation comparison of member and non-member outcomes conflates these selection differences with the causal effect of membership. The appropriate design is a difference-in-differences on the member/non-member quality gap: comparing the gap before and after the deregulation event, under the parallel-trends assumption that, absent deregulation, member and non-member quality trajectories would have evolved similarly. The relevant test statistic is the *change* in the quality gap at the deregulation event relative to its pre-event trend, not its post-event level.

*Current status:* Voluntary professional association membership is not collected in existing deregulation studies. The NFDA member/non-member distinction is structurally invisible in the data, which means the most diagnostic natural control the deregulation event makes available is not being used.

**On the scope of these tests.** The three designs distinguish information-selection from the identity-normative family. They are not designed to discriminate against additional quality-maintenance mechanisms that could substitute for state licensing after deregulation: reputation effects operating through consumer review platforms, liability and tort exposure that disciplines practitioner quality independent of licensing, or third-party credentialing requirements from hospitals, insurers, or employers that continue after the state requirement is removed. If any of these substitutes for state licensing after deregulation, the near-term null in Test 1 and the member/non-member pattern in Test 3 could reflect substitution rather than socialization. A complete mechanism discrimination would require tests targeting these alternatives. The present tests identify the information-normative split specifically; whether that split accounts for the dominant quality-maintenance mechanism operating in a specific occupation is a prior empirical question the tests cannot settle on their own.

## What the Gap Costs

The policy cost of the mechanism gap is specific and has a direction. When a deregulation study finds no immediate quality decline after a licensing requirement is removed, the standard interpretation is that licensing produced no quality benefit-and therefore that deregulation can proceed without quality cost. This inference is correct under information-selection, where immediate quality effects are exactly what the mechanism predicts. But it is incorrect under identity-socialization: if quality is maintained by professional socialization that survives credential removal, the immediate null finding is precisely what the mechanism predicts. The quality cost of deregulation, under the socialization account, arrives on a delay proportional to occupational turnover.

This delay is not a minor qualification. A state that deregulates funeral director licensing and finds no quality decline in the first four years may be observing a quality lag that will become visible fifteen years later, as the pre-deregulation cohort retires. The policy decision and its quality consequence are separated by a span that exceeds most regulatory impact assessments and most political careers. The standard practice of recommending deregulation on the basis of near-term null results is systematically biased toward deregulation relative to what the evidentiary base warrants-and the bias is directional because the null in the near term is consistent with both mechanisms, while the longer-term predictions diverge.

The mechanism gap also matters for where quality effects concentrate. Under information-selection, quality decline is distributed across the practitioner pool as unlicensed entrants reach market share-it is visible in average quality measures. Under identity-socialization, quality decline is concentrated in new entrants and firms not affiliated with voluntary professional associations. Aggregate averages will decline more slowly than the new-entrant distribution, and an evaluation that tracks aggregate outcomes will underestimate the quality exposure of consumers who patronize the least-socialized segment of the market.

**A structural note on which deregulations are studied.** States do not select occupations for deregulation at random. Deregulation events are more likely where policymakers perceive the credential's quality function as low-where examination pass rates are near-universal, where quality outcomes are visible to consumers through reputation, or where the occupation has low consumer-protection salience. If the occupations actually deregulated are systematically those where the credential was not load-bearing to begin with, null findings in those events generalize poorly to occupations where the credential performed genuine quality work. The natural-experiment interpretation of deregulation events requires not only that the event be plausibly exogenous to individual practitioner quality but that the *sample* of events not be selected on the outcome the study intends to measure.

**On mechanism dominance and mixture.** The discrimination tests are designed to identify which mechanism dominates, not to estimate mixture proportions. The two mechanisms may operate simultaneously in the same occupation, and the tests as specified will yield contradictory signals if both are substantially operative. A finding of both an immediate quality decline and a further delayed decline proportional to cohort turnover is a mixture result, not a contradiction. What the tests establish is which mechanism is *primary*-which is sufficient for the policy conclusion about the timing of quality effects. If identity-socialization dominates, the near-term null is uninformative and deregulation advice based on it is biased. If information-selection dominates, near-term evidence is informative. Mixture dominance, not mechanism purity, is the policy-relevant finding.

**Interaction with the monitoring infrastructure.** An additional effect of licensing deregulation is worth naming even though the present tests do not capture it. [*Compliance as Selection*](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/) developed the argument that effective monitoring concentrates the residual violation pool toward violations that escape detection and are systematically more harmful. Occupational licensing deregulation typically removes not only the entry-screening and consumer-signaling mechanisms but also the monitoring infrastructure the licensing system sustains-state board oversight, inspection schedules, complaint-processing capacity. Removal of this infrastructure eliminates the harm-concentration effect that effective monitoring produced while simultaneously removing the discipline that monitoring imposed on average quality. These effects interact with both named mechanisms in ways the present discrimination tests do not address. A complete account of deregulation's quality consequences would require specifying what the pre-deregulation monitoring was doing to the violation distribution, not only what the entry requirement was doing to the practitioner pool.

The three discrimination tests specified above are not exotic research designs. They require individual-level quality data linked to credential timing, temporal granularity in quality measurement, documentation of the inspection infrastructure at the treatment event, and voluntary association membership records-none of which requires new instrumentation, only new data-collection protocols in regulatory impact assessments and in state licensing board records. The reason these data are not collected is that the question motivating their collection-which mechanism accounts for quality maintenance-has not been asked by the literature that is best positioned to answer it.

Specifying the mechanism is not a methodological nicety added to a literature that already reaches correct conclusions. It is the difference between a policy recommendation that accounts for the full time horizon of deregulation's quality consequences and one that mistakes a temporal lag for the absence of an effect.
