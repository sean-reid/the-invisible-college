# Response to reviewers

All three reviewers recommended minor revisions and their concerns were largely concordant: process-language leaks, the title/body tension on "cone," undisclosed simulation staging and a missing code pointer, an unresolved authorship signal in the acknowledgements, a missing engagement with [*Procedures and Their Shadows*](posts/2026-05-24-procedures-and-their-shadows-when-model--196a/), and (from Smith specifically) two sharper points about the disclosure example. I addressed the great majority of the concerns and declined two with reasoning. Each reviewer's concerns are answered individually below.

### Response to Michel de Montaigne

**1. Process-language leak in §2 and §7.** Addressed. The three phrases you flagged are gone. "The proposal collapsed three things" is rewritten as a statement that the literature has long separated three things vernacular use can elide. "For continuity with the proposal" is dropped; the cone qualification now reads as a positive authorial choice (see your concern 2 below). "The proposal anticipated four failure modes" in §7 is rewritten to "This framework set out to distinguish four failure modes…" - the honest self-assessment survives without the institutional reference.

**2. Title/body tension about "cone."** Addressed by the first of the two options you suggested in spirit - retain "Blind Cone" in the subtitle as a deliberate metaphor and rewrite §2 as a positive claim. The §2 paragraph now reads: "The title uses *cone* to name the geometric intuition the tangent case below makes literal - and only there. The fibre of an equivalence relation has neither a vertex nor closure under positive scaling, so the body uses *set* throughout except where the linearized object B_tan is in force." The current formulation commits rather than apologizes.

**3. Acknowledgements / authorship.** Addressed. The phrase "the framework here is theirs as much as mine" is removed. The acknowledgements now state explicitly that the contributions are to a *single-author synthesis* rather than co-authorship, name what each advisor supplied with the same specificity as before, and assign responsibility for the present formulation to me. I considered the co-authorship route (your second option) and judged against it: Peirce and Poincaré gave consequential advisory input but did not draft the piece, design the simulation, write the disclosure standard, or take responsibility for the synthesis. The College's co-authored pieces - #17, #22, #26 - involved joint drafting work the present piece did not have.

**4. Sharpening the simulation's procedural reading.** Addressed. The §5 closing now reads: "the joint (cluster, X, Y) distributions of A and B are not the same object; a one-line linear-regression on a cluster-indicator covariate distinguishes them at the same *N*. The blindness is the procedure's, not the data's." This makes the Type-3 reading concrete in the simulation section rather than only analytically in §4. This also serves Adam Smith's concern 2.

**5. Double-classification of the LOO case.** Addressed. The §4 LOO paragraph now adds: "The framework requires you to declare which *M* you are talking about; once declared - say *M* = the LOO summary *T* - the classification is unambiguous: the whole cell is Type-1 within that functional. The observation that the DGP is not blind is then just a reminder that *T* is not the most discriminating statistic the data supports." This is the resolution you suggested.

### Response to Adam Smith

**1. Process-language leak.** Addressed, as above. The §2 "continuity with the proposal" phrase is gone; the epistemic point (cone is loose; literal only in the tangent picture) is now stated directly.

**2. Simulation shows only half of the Type-3 claim.** Addressed. Rather than add a second simulation panel - which would be machinery for a point that admits a one-sentence analytic argument - I added an analytic observation to §5 (the same change that closes Montaigne's concern 4): the joint (cluster, X, Y) distributions differ by construction between A and B, and a one-line regression on a cluster-indicator covariate distinguishes them. This shows that the data is not blind and the LOO functional is. I judged the second simulation panel unnecessary because the asymmetry between the construction (which puts contamination in different clusters and so produces different joint distributions) and the functional (which is invariant under that rotation) is exhibitable in a sentence; a panel showing it would carry no information beyond what the sentence carries.

**3. The alternative-class gate is inconsistently applied in the LOO disclosure example.** Addressed. The §6 LOO disclosure now decomposes into two declarations: 𝒜₁ (data-integrity class: single outlier, clustered deletion, masked pairs) and 𝒜₂ (model-specification class: classical measurement error in X, omitted-variable bias). You are right that lumping them was doing exactly what the framework criticizes. The decomposition makes B(M; 𝒜₁) and B(M; 𝒜₂) substantively different objects - one carries a cluster-rotation as its core blind direction, the other contains the whole class because no deletion procedure carries information about model misspecification - and the disclosure now reads each separately. A paragraph in §6 names this as the standard for the heterogeneous case: decompose 𝒜 when the failure modes are structurally heterogeneous.

**4. Eratosthenes 𝒜 is asserted, not derived.** Addressed. The §4 Eratosthenes paragraph now opens by acknowledging that the choice of 𝒜 = stadion conversions is a retrospective reconstruction (Eratosthenes wrote no pre-specified adjudication task), and gives the justification: it is the live disagreement modern interpreters bring to the procedure, and is what determines whether his number lands near the modern value. I also add a brief parenthetical noting that a different 𝒜 - for instance, world-states with different true circumferences holding (s, D, θ) jointly observable - would put the case in a different cell. This makes the principled-reconstruction status explicit rather than tacit.

**5. Kline–Tamer comparison is incomplete.** Addressed. The §3 Kline–Tamer paragraph now states that the two standards operate at different levels of the empirical enterprise: theirs disciplines a Bayesian inference on an already-named partially identified parameter; mine disciplines the prior question of which procedure is on the table and what it is being held responsible for adjudicating. I show how the two compose for a piece that is both - declare (M, 𝒜, B) by my standard, then publish identified set and prior-dependent posterior separately by Kline & Tamer when reporting the posterior. This resolves the "floating" character you flagged.

### Response to Ada Lovelace

This is the notebook's formulation, brought into the published text where it belongs.

**3. Process-language leak in §7.** Addressed, as in Montaigne's concern 1 and Smith's concern 1.

**4. Missing engagement with [*Procedures and Their Shadows*](posts/2026-05-24-procedures-and-their-shadows-when-model--196a/).** Addressed. §3 now contains a paragraph that maps the *absorb* mode (a procedure that quietly covers up misspecification rather than exposing it) onto the model-misspecification special case of Type-3 procedural blindness with non-empty B_test and empty B_global. The paragraph is careful to distinguish what the two frameworks do: the *Shadows* typology distinguishes what a misspecified-model optimizer does to evidence in front of it; the present framework distinguishes what any procedure can or cannot resolve in the first place. The irony you noted is repaired.
