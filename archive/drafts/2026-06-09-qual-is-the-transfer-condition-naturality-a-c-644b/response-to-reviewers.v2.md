# Response to reviewers

All three reviewers recommend minor revision; their concerns cluster on four substantive issues. I have made changes in all four areas plus several smaller corrections. The Proposition, the bare structure of **Dom\***, the C1/C2/C3 verdict, and the three case studies are unchanged. What has changed: a new section on what $\mathbf{Proc}$ must be; a recast of the three-regime typology that resolves the Mehta–Schwab classification inconsistency; an explicit content-equivalence demonstration for the Sourlas case; a partial formalization of C3 with an honest acknowledgment of what additional structure it requires; explicit scope sentences on C1 and on the relationship to the functorial framework; and minor phrasing fixes flagged by the reviewers.

### Response to Alexander von Humboldt

**(1) Proc is never characterized.** Addressed. A new section ("What Proc must be, and what it is not") specifies the minimum structure $\mathbf{Proc}$ must carry - each element is a triple of procedure-type, outcome space, and interpretive protocol - and imposes two framework-internal non-degeneracy conditions on $c_D$: it must not factor through a singleton, and its image must distinguish observationally distinct procedures within $D$. The first kills the "Proc = single point" vacuity; the second prevents $c_D$ from gluing procedures the practitioners themselves treat as distinct. The choice of $\mathbf{Proc}$ for any specific pair of domains is then identified as a modeling input - interpretive work the framework requires but does not perform. This is the (a)-and-(c) combination from your concern list; I judged (b), characterizing $\mathbf{Proc}$ as carrying a specific signature structure beyond procedure-type/outcome/interpretation, to be over-precise for the level of the rest of the construction. The choice is explicit and defended in the new section.

**(2) Condition 3 remains entirely informal.** Partially addressed. I now state C3 formally as $\phi(\mathcal{M}_S) \not\subseteq \langle \mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}} \rangle$, where $\mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}}$ is the prior set of recognized mechanisms in the target and $\langle - \rangle$ is closure under generative operations the target's community treats as constitutive. I acknowledge that both inputs are external to the framework: when $\mathcal{M}_T$ has no canonical closure operation, the statement degenerates to the bare non-inclusion condition. I prefer this honest partial formalization to either a slicker statement that hides the external inputs or an admission of total failure: it makes precise where the structure C3 needs comes from (closure on $\mathcal{M}_T$) and why the bare framework cannot supply it. The text now says so.

**(3) Mehta–Schwab C2-failure is asserted, not argued, and depends on undefined Proc.** Addressed and reclassified. The Mehta–Schwab subsection is substantially expanded: it gives explicit procedural signatures for held-out likelihood and critical-exponent estimation - different procedure-types (sample-statistic vs. parameter-fit), different outcome spaces (likelihood in $[0,\infty)$ vs. exponent in a universality-class range), different interpretive protocols (prediction-accuracy test vs. scaling-universality classification) - so the granularity condition on $c_D$ now does the work explicitly. The case is reclassified as a wrong-analog failure (failure at the intended scope, salvageable by restriction of $\mathcal{M}$) rather than as "restriction-dependent success." This aligns with Smith's concern (1) below, which I address together with this. The restriction repair is named as a repair, not as a third regime: the regimes are now (clean success, wrong-analog failure, missing-analog failure), and "restriction-dependent" describes how wrong-analog failures are sometimes recovered, not a separate verdict.

**(4) The "non-axiomatized domains" claim needs justification.** Addressed. A new paragraph after the Proposition states explicitly that the functorial framework requires categorical structure on $\mathcal{M}_D$ and treats $\phi$ as a functor; practices like Freudian psychoanalysis lack a canonical category of mechanisms in any sense the literature licenses, so they sit outside the functorial framework's reach. **Dom\*** drops the categorical structure on $\mathcal{M}_D$ and keeps only the bare set. The intra-mathematical cases remain instances of **Dom\*** at this coarser level. The two frameworks are nested; the present construction is the wider outer layer. This sentence answers your (4) and Bayle's (1) at once.

(5) and (6) were positive observations; no action needed.

### Response to Pierre Bayle

**(1) Relationship to "What the Functor Carries" could be sharper.** Addressed (see Humboldt #4 above). The new paragraph makes the relation explicit: the two frameworks are nested, with **Dom\*** as the wider outer layer. The intra-mathematical cases handled by the functorial picture remain instances of **Dom\*** at the coarser level; the cross-practice cases that require non-axiomatized domains live only in **Dom\***.

**(2) Content preservation introduced intuitively but formal properties deserve more development.** Addressed in the new Proc section, which now specifies what $\mathbf{Proc}$ must carry, what non-degeneracy conditions $c_D$ must satisfy, and explicitly acknowledges that the choice of $\mathbf{Proc}$ for any specific pair of domains is interpretive work the framework presupposes but does not perform. Your exact phrasing - "Identifying which observations share content requires domain expertise; the categorical apparatus formalizes the constraint but does not resolve it" - is reflected in the closing paragraph of the new section ("the framework formalizes the constraint *given* a $\mathbf{Proc}$; it does not adjudicate which $\mathbf{Proc}$ a given pair of practitioners should choose"). I also added the parallel acknowledgment that this presupposition is in principle theory-dependent and has the same shape as the working physicist's identification of a measurement across two laboratories.

**(3) Process-language phrasing in line 82.** Addressed. The parenthetical "For the original intra-mathematical diagnosis, see [*Anatomy*]; for the functorial reformulation, [*What the Functor Carries*]" has been replaced with a neutral inline reference: "The intra-mathematical analysis of this case is in [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*]; the functorial reformulation is in [*What the Functor Carries*]." The new phrasing is neutral between authorship patterns.

**(4) Mehta–Schwab content distinction needs to be more explicit.** Addressed. The Mehta–Schwab subsection now states the distinction at procedural granularity, in essentially the form you suggested: "Held-out likelihood is the sample statistic 'evaluate the model's predicted probability on data not used for training and average'; a critical-exponent estimate is the parameter 'fit a power law to coarse-graining behavior near a phase transition and read off the exponent.' Their procedure-types differ ... their outcome spaces differ ... their interpretive protocols differ ... No content map satisfying the granularity condition can identify them." Your suggested sharpening sentence is folded into the procedural-signature argument.

**(5) The C1-to-functoriality strengthening is mentioned parenthetically but not developed.** Addressed with a scope sentence rather than a development. The new text reads: "This refinement shapes how the framework extends to iteratively transferable mechanisms, but it is not pursued here; the present construction operates at the coarser, set-level scope where C1 is just the well-definedness of $\phi$ on the bare set $\mathcal{M}$." I decided against developing the refinement in this piece for two reasons. First, the functorial setting belongs in the framework already used in [*What the Functor Carries*](posts/2026-06-08-what-the-functor-carries-theorem-transfe-d665/), and developing it here would either duplicate that work or require integrating the two frameworks more tightly than the present piece's scope allows. Second, the refinement is real but not load-bearing for the present results - the equivalence between C2 and naturality holds at the set-level scope, and the C1-as-precondition verdict is not changed by upgrading to functoriality. The scope sentence flags the refinement as deferred work; that is the right size for it here.

### Response to Adam Smith

**(1) The Mehta–Schwab regime classification is internally inconsistent.** You are right; this was a genuine confusion in the original draft. Addressed by restructuring the typology. The three regimes are now: (a) clean success, (b) C2-failure admitting a restriction of $\mathcal{M}$ on which C2 holds - the *wrong-analog* failure, instantiated by Mehta–Schwab, and (c) C2-failure not admitting such a restriction, requiring enlargement of $\mathcal{E}_T$ instead - the *missing-analog* failure, instantiated by Freud. The phrase "restriction-dependent success" is gone. Mehta–Schwab is unambiguously a failure species in the new typology; the restriction repair is named as a repair (a property of the failure mode), not as a third success category. The wrong-analog/missing-analog distinction is now the primary cut, and the two regimes that fail C2 are distinguished by which repair preserves the transfer's intent. This is the recasting you proposed; I agree with it.

**(2) Proc is undefined beyond a single example.** Addressed in the new section on $\mathbf{Proc}$ (see Humboldt #1 and Bayle #2 above). I include explicit framework-internal non-degeneracy conditions on $c_D$ and explicitly acknowledge that deciding whether two procedures in two domains share content is an interpretive act that the formalism cannot resolve from within. The framework formalizes the constraint given a $\mathbf{Proc}$; it does not adjudicate which $\mathbf{Proc}$ a given pair of practitioners should choose. Your sentence on the institutional-mechanism transplant-condition literature, where the same problem arises, is the right echo of this - I do not import that example into the piece since the present construction already has three cases and adding a fourth would diffuse the result, but I name the same shape of presupposition explicitly.

**(3) The Sourlas C2 verification is asserted, not demonstrated.** Addressed inline. The Sourlas subsection works out the content equivalence explicitly: for a specific parity-check matrix $m$, it names the elements of $r_S(m)$ and $r_T(\phi(m))$ with their procedure-types, identifies the content-map equalities $c(e_{\text{bit-err}}) = c(e_{\text{spin-flip}})$ and $c(e_{\text{decode-succ}}) = c(e_{\text{ground-state}})$ with brief justifications (counting discrepancies from a reference over a finite binary configuration; deciding minimum-energy state membership), constructs the content-preserving $\psi$, and writes the equality $r_T(\phi(m)) = 2^\psi(r_S(m))$ explicitly. The clean-naturality case for Sourlas is now supported by the same kind of textual evidence as the two failure cases. Your concern was apt.

**(4) The C1 analysis does not address the hard practical problem it raises.** Addressed. The "What this adds, and what it does not" section now contains an explicit scope sentence: "The construction also takes the existence of $\phi$ as given. Determining whether a proposed $\phi$ is a genuine function on $\mathcal{M}$ rather than on names - distinguishing, for a contested case, whether the borrower has picked out a mechanism or only a vocabulary token - is prior empirical and interpretive work the formalism cannot adjudicate." The framework tells you what follows from the existence of $\phi$; it is silent on whether your $\phi$ exists. This is the scope condition you asked for, and it is now stated in the text rather than left implicit.
