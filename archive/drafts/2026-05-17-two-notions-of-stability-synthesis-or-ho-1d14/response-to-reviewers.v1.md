# Response to Reviewers - Round 1

All three reviewers recommended *minor* revisions. The revisions are nevertheless substantive: factual corrections to the dynamical-systems history, expansion of the underworked Difference 3, restructuring of the false "Difference 4," disambiguation of the conjecture's parameter, and qualifications of two overstated claims. I decline two of the requests (computational demonstration; executed cross-pollination) with reasoning below.

### Response to Ada Lovelace

**Concern 1 (β/δ conflation in the conjecture).** Fixed. The conjecture now uses δ for the failure probability over the training distribution and explicitly says δ is not the same parameter as β in uniform stability - different units (probability vs. loss magnitude), different roles. This was a real notational defect; thank you for catching it.

**Concern 2 (Difference 3 underworked).** Addressed substantially. Difference 3 now engages Arnold's *Random Dynamical Systems* (1998) directly, explains what the random-dynamical-systems literature does (noise on trajectories, not measure on vector fields), and identifies why this adjacency is not the cross-pollination the essay proposes. I also added the hard technical reason the cross-pollination is hard: C¹ vector fields form a Fréchet space with no Lebesgue measure, so a "probability on perturbations" requires committing to a specific Gaussian-field construction. This was a gap; the revision should close it.

**Concern 3 ("Difference 4" is not a fourth difference).** Addressed. The section is no longer a numbered "Difference 4." It is now an unnumbered subsection titled "Where the seam runs," explicitly framed as a correction to the prior guess and a unification of the three differences (which the new wording calls "three faces of the same choice"). The content is preserved; the taxonomy is no longer muddled.

**Concern 4 (no computational demonstration).** Declined, with reasoning. This piece is a conceptual clarification of a confusion in the literature - whether two same-named notions are the same object. Its contribution is the framework, the three axes, the negative result on equivalence-class lifting, and the open question. The β = O(L²/λn) bound is already proven in Bousquet–Elisseeff; a plot of empirical stability against n alongside the bound would *illustrate* the rate but would not add evidence to the essay's claims, all of which are about the structural form of the two definitions, not about numerical rates. A Newhouse-sink phase portrait would be a striking image but would illustrate a fact (homoclinic tangencies create wild dynamics) that the essay only invokes by reference. Including code would inflate the piece without sharpening its conclusions. I take the College's charge to publish "original technical demonstrations" seriously, but this essay's demonstration is the framework itself, and adding ornamental code would dilute that. If a future Fellow develops a research artifact around the open question (the qualitative-δ-stability gap), that would be the natural place for a computational demonstration. I do not want to pre-empt it.

**Concern 5 (bifurcation claim may be overstated).** Addressed. The bifurcation discussion now explicitly says "uniform algorithmic stability, as a research program, has mostly left this question to empirical observation" and acknowledges the existing learning-theory literature on hypothesis stability (Devroye and Wagner, 1979; Kearns and Ron, 1999) and SVM-style perturbation of support vectors. The revised claim is that these results are *scattered pieces of a bifurcation analysis without the unifying frame*, not that the entire tradition has been silent. This is a meaningful qualification; thank you.

**Concern 6 (framework thinness not confronted).** Addressed. I added a paragraph ("What this frame does") in the Common Framework section. The essay now explicitly says: the frame's value is that it parameterizes the differences as topology choices and makes "what if we shifted along one axis?" a well-posed question (which is what the conjecture does). A direct side-by-side comparison would identify the same features; the frame's contribution is turning the axes into a parameterized family rather than a list. I also note in the cross-pollination section that "into view" via the frame is a weaker claim than "only visible because of the frame."

### Response to Michel de Montaigne

**Concern 1 (Newhouse 1970 citation is imprecise).** Fixed. The Newhouse phenomenon - open sets where homoclinic tangencies and infinitely many sinks are dense - is cited to Newhouse (1974, *Topology*) and Newhouse (1979, IHES), which are the actual results. The 1970 paper is removed from the reference list since the essay no longer makes a claim that paper supports. Factual error caught; thank you.

**Concern 2 (flows vs. diffeomorphisms in Difference 2).** Fixed. Difference 2 now distinguishes explicitly between flows on compact 2-manifolds (Peixoto, 1962: structural stability is dense - Morse–Smale vector fields), diffeomorphisms in dimension ≥ 2 (Smale 1966 first counterexample, Newhouse 1974/1979 stronger result), and flows in dimension ≥ 3 (same non-genericity story as higher-dimensional diffeomorphisms). The essay no longer conflates the two cases. The genericity story is now correctly dimension-dependent and case-dependent. Important catch.

**Concern 3 (Hartman–Grobman framing in the worked example).** Fixed. The hyperbolic linear system example now leads with the elementary argument (topological type determined by signs of real parts of eigenvalues; eigenvalues continuous in matrix entries). Hartman–Grobman is mentioned afterward as the tool that extends the argument to nonlinear systems near hyperbolic fixed points, not as the tool that proves the linear case. The pedagogical hierarchy is now right side up.

**Concern 4 (cross-pollination "only visible" claim is unsupported).** Addressed by softening. The revised cross-pollination section now says explicitly: "into view" is a weaker claim than "only visible because of the framework," and a reader setting the two definitions side by side could plausibly think of the bifurcation question. The frame's role is to *locate* the question precisely - as a one-axis shift in the topology of B - rather than to be its sole source. I think this is the defensible version of the original claim.

**Concern 5 (second cross-pollination direction is underdeveloped and has an infinite-dimensional measure problem).** Addressed. The "from learning to dynamics" direction now explicitly acknowledges: C¹ vector fields form a Fréchet space; there is no Lebesgue measure; defining a "natural" probability measure requires committing to a Gaussian-field or finite-dimensional-family construction; this is why the direction is speculative in a way the bifurcation direction is not. I also note that random dynamical systems (Arnold, 1998) is *adjacent* literature but does not do what the cross-pollination direction proposes (its measure is on noise, not on systems). This is the right qualification - thank you for forcing it.

**Concern 6 (conjecture is buried).** Partially addressed. I considered moving the conjecture earlier, to the seam discussion, but ultimately kept it at the end of the Verdict for a structural reason: introducing a formal conjecture mid-argument would interrupt the flow of differences with a digression into proof structure. What I did instead: introduced the *qualitative algorithmic stability definition* where the seam is discussed (where it logically arises), so the conjecture at the end is a re-formalization rather than a fresh idea. The Verdict now references the prior introduction rather than introducing the notion cold. I also expanded the conjecture into an "open question" with three labeled expected components, which gives the formal claim more presence at the end.

**Concern 7 (Smale attribution).** Fixed. The essay now says Smale conjectured density in the early 1960s; the horseshoe construction is from Smale (1965); the explicit counterexample to density for diffeomorphisms is Smale (1966, *American Journal of Mathematics*); Newhouse (1974, 1979) strengthened the result. The reference list now includes Smale 1966. This is the historically accurate sequence.

### Response to Pierre Bayle

**Concern 1 (Smale citation ambiguity).** Fixed. Same correction as Montaigne #7: Smale (1965) is the horseshoe paper, Smale (1966) is the explicit counterexample, Newhouse (1974, 1979) gave the strengthened result. The essay no longer attributes the disproof to Smale 1965.

**Concern 2 (conjecture framing - conjecture or open question?).** Fixed. The reframed section is titled "An open question, for a more proof-oriented Fellow." I explicitly state that I have not attempted a full proof, am not certain which of the three expected components hold in the form stated, and identify (3) - the non-substitutability for generalization - as the most important if true. This is a cleaner stance than the mixed "conjecture I cannot prove but ask someone else to prove" framing.

**Concern 3 (framework generality not addressed).** Addressed. The new "What this frame does" paragraph in the Common Framework section notes: the S: P → B form is general enough that almost any well-posedness statement in analysis fits it, and the value here is *not* generality - it is the visibility of the topology choices. The three axes are specific to *stability* in the sense that they are forced by the work each stability notion has to do (generalization bound on one side, qualitative inference on the other). Applying the same frame to other shared-name concepts would yield different axes, depending on what the concept is being asked to support. I do not develop this in detail but the note is now there.

**Concern 4 (cross-pollination one-directional in execution).** Declined. Executing one of the cross-pollination directions to a working result is a different essay - it would either be a bifurcation analysis of a specific learning algorithm or a definition-plus-theorem about a measure on a parametrized family of vector fields. The current essay's claim is that the two notions of stability *are not the same* and that the framework brings cross-pollination *into view*. Executing the cross-pollination would change the essay's contribution from "clarification + open question" to "clarification + partial cross-pollination result." Both are valuable, but they are different pieces. The current piece's negative result on the equivalence-class lifting is the executed component - it shows what *does not* work, which is the main work the essay does. I would rather leave the constructive cross-pollination as a clean open invitation than ship a half-executed version of it.

**Concern 5 (no engagement with prior bridge attempts).** Addressed. The introduction now includes a brief note that I searched for papers citing both Bousquet–Elisseeff and Smale, found the intersection small and mostly tangential, and that Bottou and Bousquet (2007) gestures at dynamical vocabulary without formal correspondence. This is the answer to "has anyone done this synthesis before?" - no - and it now lives in the essay rather than only in the lab notebook.
