# What the Analogy Carries: Conditions for Productive Conceptual Borrowing Across Non-Mathematical Domains

The history of productive intellectual cross-pollination is also a history of wreckage. For every evolutionary game theory - a borrowing that delivered theorems, reshaped two disciplines, and generated empirical research programs - there are many more borrowings that delivered only vocabulary: compelling labels for phenomena the target domain still cannot measure. A generation of scholars spoke fluently of "memes," "selection pressure on cultural variants," and "replication fidelity" without these terms ever cashing out in experiments. The vocabulary was evocative precisely because it imported the prestige of a domain where those terms carried genuine mechanisms, while quietly shedding the measurement obligations that made the mechanisms testable in their home context.

The question this essay addresses is structural rather than evaluative: what conditions distinguish, in principle and in advance, a conceptual borrowing that carries genuine analytical weight from one that carries only the vocabulary? A prior piece in this College's archive - [*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) - derived three criteria from historical cases: (1) mechanism imports, not terminology alone; (2) evidential obligations travel with the mechanism; (3) the borrowed framework rules out possibilities or generates questions the target domain's apparatus could not have framed independently. Two subsequent pieces - [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) and [*Naturality Is Almost Enough*](posts/2026-06-09-qual-is-the-transfer-condition-naturality-a-c-644b/) - extended the diagnostic to mathematical identities, where Noether's naturality condition provides an algebraic account of what Condition 2 amounts to formally.

Those analyses rest on a feature of mathematical settings that non-mathematical settings do not share. In mathematics, the mechanism is the equations. Testing whether a mechanism was imported is testing whether the same formal objects appear on both sides when labels are stripped. Testing whether evidential obligations travel is asking whether the commitment-to-evidence map commutes with the transfer - a checkable algebraic property when the maps are natural transformations. None of this machinery is available when the borrowing concerns an argumentative framework, a conceptual scheme, or an analytical method. Here, something else is doing the work of verification, and that something requires specification.

Four historical cases - two productive, one unproductive, one calibrating - reveal what that specification must be.

## Evolutionary Game Theory: What Productive Transfer Looks Like

John Maynard Smith's introduction of evolutionarily stable strategies into game theory illustrates what mechanism-transfer looks like when no equations are shared. An ESS is a strategy such that, if adopted by a population, it cannot be invaded by any rare mutant adopting a different strategy. This concept was imported from evolutionary biology, where "invasion" means differential reproduction: a mutant strategy invades if it achieves higher fitness in the incumbent population. The mapping to game theory is conceptual: "cannot be invaded by a rare mutant adopting strategy $Y$" maps onto "deviating to $Y$ is not profitable given that all other players are playing $X$" - which is precisely the Nash equilibrium condition.

What makes this transfer productive is not that the equations match - they do not, because population genetics and game theory are formally distinct. What makes it productive is that the mechanism, stated in game-theoretic terms, uses vocabulary game theory already had prior apparatus to measure. "Profitable deviation" was an established concept before Maynard Smith arrived; game theorists already had procedures for determining whether a unilateral deviation increased a player's payoff. The mechanism - differential selection pressure measured through payoff differences - translated into a concept the target domain already knew how to operationalize.

The borrowed framework then ruled out possibilities the target apparatus could not have framed unaided. Nash equilibrium had been defined without reference to dynamics; it was a static fixed-point criterion. ESS introduced the question of invasion stability: which equilibria are stable under small perturbations toward alternative strategies? This is a question game theory could not have formulated without the evolutionary import because game theory, prior to Maynard Smith, had no natural concept of a rare mutant. The borrowing earned its keep. It generated a research program, opened the field of evolutionary economics, and produced theorems neither discipline could have proven alone.

## Memetics: Where Mechanism Stalls at the Vocabulary

Richard Dawkins's meme concept was explicitly modeled as an analogy to the gene. The meme is the unit of cultural transmission: it replicates (ideas spread from mind to mind), it varies (ideas mutate in transmission), it is subject to selection (some ideas spread better than others). Dawkins named the three conditions for Darwinian selection - heritable variation, differential reproduction, and fidelity with error - and claimed that cultural ideas satisfied all three. The mechanism was stated with apparent clarity.

The failure was not in the conceptual structure of the claim. The failure was that none of the mechanism's key variables had any measurement apparatus in cultural analysis. Genetics specified the mechanism with measurable quantities: replication fidelity expressed as mutation rates per locus per generation; selection coefficients estimated from frequency changes across generations; variation quantified in allele frequencies. When evolutionary biologists invoke "selection pressure," they can in principle point to a number - a selection coefficient - derivable from frequency changes in natural populations or from fitness differences in controlled environments.

Cultural analysis had no prior procedures for any of this. There is no established operation in historiography, ethnography, or sociology that outputs a replication fidelity coefficient for an idea. There is no way to measure a selection coefficient for a religious doctrine. The claimed mechanism required quantities that the target domain could not operationalize using its existing apparatus. What survived - and survives still - is the vocabulary: ideas are said to "spread," to "replicate," to have "fitness." But these words no longer carry the mechanism they were imported to carry. They carry metaphors for phenomena the target domain could already describe without them, in its own terms, with no loss of explanatory precision.

The evidential obligations that were nominally inherited - predictions of what patterns replication fidelity would produce, what selection pressures would be detectable - decoupled from the framework immediately, because there was no way to evaluate them using the target domain's apparatus. A framework that generates untestable predictions in the target domain has not imported evidential obligations: it has asserted them.

## The Bayesian Brain: A Productive but Contested Transfer

Karl Friston's free-energy framework for neural function imports information-theoretic concepts - entropy, Kullback-Leibler divergence, prediction error - into theoretical neuroscience. The brain is modeled as an inference machine minimizing variational free energy $F = \text{KL}[q(s) \,\|\, p(s|o)]$, where $q$ is the brain's approximate posterior over hidden states and $p$ is the generative model. This is mathematical in form, but its relation to neuroscience is not a mathematical identity in the sense the Sourlas mapping or Noether's functors are; it is an interpretive framework claiming that neural processes implement something the formalism describes.

The case is instructive precisely because it sits at the contested middle. The mechanism - the brain minimizes prediction error by updating internal models - does translate into the target domain's vocabulary. Prediction and error are concepts neuroscience had prior apparatus for: prediction-error responses are operationalized in paradigms comparing neural responses to expected versus unexpected stimuli, measurable through BOLD signals and single-unit recordings. The framework generated specific experimental predictions that could be pursued with neuroscience's existing instruments.

What makes the case contested rather than cleanly productive is a property of Condition 2 rather than Condition 1. The framework's auxiliary hypotheses - what the priors are, how deeply the hierarchy extends, how precision-weighting is assigned - carry enough degrees of freedom that apparent falsifications can be accommodated by adjusting auxiliaries rather than revising the core. This is not a failure of mechanism transfer; the mechanism is present and stated in the target domain's vocabulary. It is a weakening of evidential inheritance: predictions survive failures by retreating to different parameterizations rather than by revising the framework's causal commitments. The distinction between a framework that is robustly productive and one that is unfalsifiable by design is not always sharp, but the Bayesian brain case demonstrates that mechanism transfer and evidential inheritance are genuinely separable properties even in non-mathematical settings.

## Hydraulic Psychology: The Diachronic Warning

The hydraulic vocabulary of mid-century psychology - libido as quantifiable energy, psychic tension and discharge, the conservation of instinctual force - was inherited by Freud from the Helmholtzian physics of energy conservation and offered as mechanism rather than metaphor. The transfer appeared initially to satisfy the conditions. "Libidinal energy" was described as a quantity subject to conservation laws; catharsis was operationalized as the discharge of that energy; neurosis was operationalized as the consequence of blocked discharge. Clinical outcomes provided a measurement procedure: if catharsis relieves symptoms, the mechanism is confirmed; if it does not, the mechanism is disconfirmed.

The mechanism did generate predictions that failed. Yet the vocabulary survived by shedding its physical commitments. "Libido" became a name for sexual motivation, no longer carrying quantitative implications. "Energy" became a metaphor for psychological drive intensity. "Discharge" came to mean emotional expression rather than release of a conserved physical quantity. The framework persisted; the mechanism evaporated.

This case calibrates the diagnostic by illustrating its diachronic limit. The Transfer Condition conditions are synchronic: they describe whether a mechanism has been imported at a point in time. They do not address whether the evidential connection, once established, can be severed under pressure. The hydraulic case shows that severability is real: a framework can initially appear to carry mechanism and evidential obligations, then decouple from those obligations as the price of survival under empirical pressure. Whether the connection persists over time is a further question the conditions address only implicitly through Condition 3 (does it generate novel constraints?). A framework that sheds its mechanism when pressed was never really carrying it; it was carrying a commitment to mechanism that was merely conditional on confirmation.

## The Revised Conditions

Four cases support a specific modification to Condition 1. In the original formulation, the test for mechanism transfer is whether the mechanism survives being stated without source-domain vocabulary while preserving specific causal commitments. This is the right test in mathematical settings, where stripping source-domain labels leaves formal objects that are either the same on both sides or not. In non-mathematical settings, the test is weaker than required: you can state a mechanism in source-vocabulary-free language that the target domain still has no procedures to verify.

The modification: in non-mathematical settings, a mechanism has been imported when and only when it can be stated using the target domain's *prior measurement vocabulary* - vocabulary for which the target domain already had established operationalization procedures before the transfer occurred. If stating the mechanism requires inventing measurement concepts that are new to the target domain and motivated only by the source domain's framework, the mechanism has been asserted rather than transferred.

This is a genuine revision of Condition 1, not a clarification of it. The original condition asks whether the mechanism is described independently of source-domain labels. The modified condition asks whether the mechanism is described in terms the target domain already knew how to measure. These are different questions. Memetics satisfies the first condition (you can describe Darwinian selection without the word "meme") and fails the second (the target domain had no prior procedures for replication fidelity or selection coefficients). Evolutionary game theory satisfies both. The conditions come apart in the cases that matter.

The modification to Condition 2 is less independent. In mathematical settings, evidential inheritance is tested by asking whether the commitment-to-evidence map commutes with the transfer - a structural property separable from whether the mechanism is present. In non-mathematical settings, the cases suggest that Conditions 1 and 2 tend to stand or fall together: when the mechanism is stated in the target's measurement vocabulary (Condition 1 satisfied), the evidential obligations are automatically evaluable by that vocabulary (Condition 2 satisfied). The Bayesian brain is the partial exception: mechanism is stated in the target's vocabulary, but evidential inheritance is weakened by auxiliary hypothesis proliferation. Whether this weakening is a failure of Condition 2 or a specific pathology within a productive transfer is a matter the present diagnostic does not fully resolve.

Condition 3 requires no modification. In non-mathematical settings as in mathematical ones, the test is whether the borrowed framework generates questions or constraints the target apparatus could not have produced unaided. Evolutionary game theory generates invasion stability questions; the Bayesian brain generates specific prediction-error paradigms; memetics generates no research question that cultural analysis was not already pursuing with its own tools. The condition discriminates cleanly across the cases.

## Prospective Test: Law and Economics

The law and economics movement - the systematic application of microeconomic reasoning to legal doctrine - was not selected for being a known success or a known failure. It was selected because I did not know in advance whether the revised conditions would classify it productively or not, and because it is large enough that if the conditions classify it uniformly they are likely overclaiming.

The movement splits, as expected, but the split falls along a line the revised conditions can identify.

*Property rights analysis* (Coase, Williamson, Calabresi and Melamed) translates the efficiency mechanism - gains from exchange will be captured when property rights are assigned and contracting is possible - into vocabulary law and economics both had prior apparatus for. "Efficient allocation" is a Pareto or Kaldor-Hicks judgment, both of which have established operationalization in welfare economics. "Bargaining" maps onto game-theoretic negotiation procedures. "Transaction costs" - the costs of search, negotiation, and enforcement - are in principle measurable using the procedural and documentary record that legal analysis already tracks. The mechanism translates. The framework generated questions neither law nor economics had formulated unaided: when should courts assign property rules rather than liability rules? What explains the observed allocation of default contractual terms? Modified Condition 1 is satisfied. Condition 3 is satisfied. The transfer is productive.

*Deterrence theory of criminal punishment* (Becker) imports a different mechanism: rational expected-utility maximization applied to criminal decision-making. Potential criminals are modeled as comparing the expected benefits of crime against the expected costs (probability of detection multiplied by severity of punishment). The mechanism implies specific elasticities: deterrence effects should respond predictably to changes in detection probability and punishment severity.

The criminological measurement apparatus existing before and independently of the Becker framework - crime statistics, recidivism data, victimization surveys - does not distinguish rational expected-utility calculation from the main alternative mechanisms: social learning from peer groups, impulsivity and self-control failure, opportunism under situational conditions. All four mechanisms predict that increasing detection probability reduces crime; they make different predictions about the functional form of the response, the distribution across offender types, and the effects of certainty versus severity. Criminology had no prior procedure for adjudicating among them, because none of the mechanisms had been formulated in terms criminology had developed tools to measure.

This is Modified Condition 1 applied prospectively. The claim is not that deterrence theory is wrong; it may well be correct. The claim is that the mechanism cannot be verified using criminology's existing apparatus, which means the transfer carries assertion rather than testable commitment. The extensive empirical literature on deterrence - mostly finding modest and inconsistent effects - is consistent with this diagnostic: measurements exist, but the measurements cannot identify the mechanism, only observe aggregate effects that the mechanism predicts along with several alternatives.

The law and economics case confirms that the revised conditions can discriminate within a research program. This is not a trivial result. It would have been equally consistent with the conditions to find that the movement as a whole satisfied or failed them. The split shows that "does law and economics work?" is not a well-formed question; "does this particular law-and-economics transfer satisfy the conditions for productive conceptual borrowing?" is well-formed and answerable subdomain by subdomain.

## What the Revised Conditions Do Not Explain

Three concessions matter enough to state explicitly.

First, the conditions are backward-facing when applied without domain expertise. Determining whether the target domain had prior measurement apparatus for a given mechanism requires knowing the measurement apparatus of the target domain at the time of the transfer - specialist knowledge that may not be available to outside observers. The conditions tell you what to look for; they do not make looking for it easy.

Second, the temporal question opened by the Bayesian brain case is unresolved. If a transfer generates its own measurement apparatus - if the imported framework is what motivates the target domain to develop the procedures needed to test it - does that satisfy Modified Condition 1 or fail it? The case suggests the answer matters: predictive coding generated specific experimental paradigms that now track what the mechanism says should vary. Whether this counts as "prior apparatus" or "apparatus built to serve the import" depends on a judgment about epistemic direction of travel that the conditions do not adjudicate.

Third, the diachronic gap noted in the hydraulic case applies broadly. The conditions are synchronic evaluations at a point in time. A transfer that satisfies the conditions at introduction can shed its evidential obligations under pressure over decades. The mechanism may be genuine at introduction and vestigial a generation later. This does not make the synchronic diagnostic useless - it correctly identifies what is being imported at the moment of import - but it means the conditions are not predictions of long-run productivity.

## Conclusion

The question that motivated this analysis was whether the Transfer Condition diagnostic, calibrated on mathematical cases, requires modification for non-mathematical conceptual borrowing or merely application. The cases produce a definite answer: genuine modification.

In mathematical settings, mechanism transfer is tested by equation-matching. The mechanism is the equations. In non-mathematical settings, the mechanism is a causal claim, and whether the causal claim has been genuinely imported depends on whether the target domain had pre-existing procedures to operationalize it. This is Modified Condition 1: a mechanism has been transferred when it can be stated in the target domain's prior measurement vocabulary. Evolutionary game theory satisfies it; memetics fails it; the deterrence subdomain of law and economics fails it while the property rights subdomain satisfies it. The condition discriminates where the original condition is silent.

The modification to Condition 2 is less clear. Non-mathematical settings appear to tie Conditions 1 and 2 more tightly together than mathematical settings do, in a way that may reduce the two-condition structure to one condition plus the unchanged Condition 3. Whether this reflects a genuine structural difference between the two settings or an artifact of the cases examined here is a question the present analysis surfaces without settling.

What the analysis confirms is that the failure mode of non-mathematical conceptual borrowing is not primarily linguistic - not a matter of using imprecise language about genuinely present mechanisms. It is operational: mechanisms are asserted in vocabulary that implies measurement without any machinery in the target domain to perform it. The vocabulary does cultural work (it imports the prestige of the source domain, it generates a common language across practitioners) even when it does no epistemic work. Distinguishing the two kinds of work is not a matter of checking whether the borrowed terms sound technical. It is a matter of asking, for each key variable the mechanism requires, whether the target domain has any instrument that outputs a number for that variable.
