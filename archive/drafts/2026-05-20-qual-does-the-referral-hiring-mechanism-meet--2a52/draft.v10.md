# Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?

The finding that a large fraction of jobs are found through personal contacts is among the most replicated in organizational sociology and labor economics. From Granovetter's foundational Boston study through the formal models of Montgomery and the synthesis by Ioannides and Loury, the empirical record is dense: contacts intermediate job matches, the contacts are disproportionately weak ties rather than close friends, and jobs found through contacts produce better wages, longer employment durations, and distinctive demographic sorting patterns. A researcher who disputes these findings is disputing a substantial body of careful evidence.

The question this piece pursues is different. Replication of a finding is not replication of a mechanism. The question is whether the *mechanism account* attached to these findings satisfies the standard that mechanism-based explanation requires. Applying Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level framework as an audit tool, I find the following: the situational mechanism is well-specified, with genuine structural content that generates testable predictions; the employer's action formation mechanism is formally modeled; the referrer's action formation mechanism is assumed rather than derived; and the aggregation mechanism connecting bilateral referral events to labor-market-level segmentation is absent beyond verbal assertion. The gap that matters most is the one the literature has hidden inside an assumption about the referrer's decision.

## The Standard

Hedström and Ylikoski's 2010 framework for causal mechanisms in the social sciences builds on the Coleman bathtub diagram, which distinguishes macro-level phenomena from individual-level processes and requires an account of all three transitions: from macro context to individual situation, from individual situation to individual action, and from individual actions to the macro outcome. They call these the situational mechanism, the action formation mechanism, and the transformation (or aggregation) mechanism.

Against this standard, most published social-science explanations fail in one of two characteristic ways. The first failure is the macro-to-macro correlation: the analyst documents that macro variable X covaries with macro outcome Y and calls the covariance an explanation. No individual-level process is specified; there is no account of what actors are doing or why. The second failure is the partial mechanism: the analyst describes the situational conditions and gestures at individual-level causes - "trust," "network pressure," "information flow" - without specifying the behavioral logic that selects a particular action given those conditions, or the aggregation step that converts many individual decisions into the macro pattern. Hedström and Ylikoski are particularly attentive to a further problem: mechanism-level vocabulary that names a process without specifying the entities and activities that constitute it. "Network embeddedness" is not yet a mechanism; it is a name for the site where a mechanism would need to be specified.

The choice of Hedström and Ylikoski's mechanistic standard, rather than an interventionist causal framework, requires brief defense. Interventionist accounts (Pearl, Woodward) identify causal structure through structural equations and do not in principle require individualist action-formation models; a parameterized referral probability could satisfy an interventionist standard if the structural equations were correctly specified. The mechanistic standard is appropriate here because the canonical texts make it applicable by their own practice: they offer not just correlational claims but explanatory claims about *why* referrals produce better matches and *how* bilateral hiring decisions produce labor-market inequality. These are claims about mechanism in Hedström and Ylikoski's specific sense. More substantively, the interventionist and mechanistic frameworks converge on the same diagnostic question: an interventionist analyst who asked what happens when one intervenes on referral incentives would discover exactly the same ambiguity about which causal channel is active that this audit identifies. The two frameworks are complementary approaches to the same gap, not alternatives.

A positive-control case grounds the diagnostic and demonstrates that the standard discriminates rather than convicting every social mechanism by construction. Schelling's segregation model passes all three levels: the situational mechanism names the individual tolerance threshold and the local neighborhood composition that determines whether it is breached; the action formation mechanism is derived from the optimization logic of moving when locally outnumbered beyond tolerance; and the aggregation mechanism is fully specified - the cascade dynamics by which each relocation changes composition for former neighbors, deriving the macro segregation pattern from the individual rules without asserting it. Neither the individual tolerance thresholds nor their local operation requires any preference for segregation; the macro pattern is derived from their interaction through the structural logic of the cascade. This is what the framework requires. The referral hiring literature is measurably less complete, and identifying where it falls short is the work of this piece.

## The Canonical Texts

The corpus for this audit is five texts that together establish the empirical finding, supply the theoretical vocabulary, formalize the core mechanism, and synthesize the inequality claim: Granovetter's "The Strength of Weak Ties" (1973), his *Getting a Job* (1974), his "Economic Action and Social Structure: The Problem of Embeddedness" (1985), Montgomery's 1991 formal economic model, and Ioannides and Loury's 2004 synthesis in the *Journal of Economic Literature*.

The 1973 paper and the 1974 book appear together as Granovetter's foundational contribution rather than as alternative selections. The 1973 paper establishes the structural logic of weak-tie information access - the mechanism that explains *why* contacts produce better matches. The 1974 book provides the empirical foundation for the contact-based hiring finding and contains Granovetter's initial treatment of what happens at the level of the vacancy, the contact, and the job seeker. These are the theoretical and empirical halves of the same contribution; treating only one as a corpus text while drawing on both in the analysis would misrepresent the scope of review. Together they constitute what subsequent labor economics and organizational sociology took as foundational.

I treat this corpus as the object of the audit, not the outer boundary of the literature. The piece does not conduct a systematic review of the post-2004 referral hiring literature; two bodies of post-canonical work - Beaman and Magruder (2012) and Calvó-Armengol and Jackson (2004, 2007) - bear directly on the diagnosis and are engaged at the points in the argument where they matter. The audit findings about the canonical texts would stand regardless of what subsequent work has done, but readers should know the scope.

## Level One: The Situational Mechanism

This is the strongest part of the literature, and the first level is where the account genuinely delivers.

Granovetter's theoretical contribution, developed fully in "The Strength of Weak Ties" (1973) and extended in the 1985 embeddedness paper, specifies the structural content of network position with real precision. Actors in dense, closed networks have contacts who communicate with each other; they possess largely redundant information. Actors with bridging ties to other clusters - "weak ties" that connect socially distant groups - have access to non-redundant information from those clusters. The information about job vacancies flows predominantly through weak ties because that is where the structurally non-redundant information lives. This is a genuine situational mechanism: it names the relevant entities (actors, network ties, clusters), the relevant structural properties (tie strength, network closure, bridging), and the process by which structural position translates into differential information access. It generates predictions - about who will hear about vacancies, not just that contacts matter.

Ioannides and Loury extend the situational mechanism with neighborhood effects and spatial composition. Residential segregation produces network segregation: actors in segregated neighborhoods have contacts concentrated in the same neighborhood, amplifying the compositional effects of homophily. The employers to whom those contacts connect are concentrated in local industries and firms. The situational story by 2004 is genuinely rich in structural content.

The situational mechanism, in short, passes. Social structure shapes information access, and the structural features that govern how it does so are specified with enough precision to generate testable predictions.

## Level Two: The Action Formation Mechanism - A Bifurcated Verdict

This is where the audit reveals something the literature presents as unified but is actually split.

**The employer's action is modeled.** Montgomery (1991) provides the clearest treatment. In his partial equilibrium model, workers vary in quality (high or low type), employers cannot directly observe type, and referrals function as correlated quality signals. The correlation exists because network homophily produces networks in which high-quality workers are disproportionately connected to other high-quality workers. An employer who faces a referred candidate from a high-quality employee can rationally update upward on the candidate's quality. The employer's decision to prefer referred candidates is derived from an optimization problem under uncertainty - it is not assumed but follows from the structure of the information problem and the properties of the signal.

Montgomery's model generates predictions: wage dispersion will track network access; workers with contacts at good employers will be more likely to get good jobs; employer reliance on referrals will be greater when formal credentials are less informative. These are the hallmarks of a genuine action formation mechanism - it specifies not just that employers act differently toward referred candidates, but *why*, and the why generates testable implications.

**The referrer's action is assumed.** This is the gap. In Montgomery's model, the referral probability is parameterized: high-type workers refer with probability p_H, low-type workers refer with probability p_L, and p_H > p_L. The positive selectivity of the referral process - the assumption that referred workers are disproportionately high quality - is built into these parameters. They are stated as an assumption and are never derived from a model of the referrer's decision.

The distinction between a parameter that is *derived* from a model's structure and one that is *assumed into* it parallels a diagnostic developed in this College for a different domain: Poincaré and Bayle's three-part check distinguishes working mathematical identities (canonical identification of objects, term-by-term operational match, object-level invertibility) from vocabulary transfers that name where a derivation would need to go. Applied to `p_H > p_L`: the parameters are named and their role in the model is clear, but the structural derivation - the referrer's optimization problem whose solution would make the inequality hold as an output rather than an assumption - is absent. In the vocabulary of [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/), this is a vocabulary placeholder, not a working parameter: it identifies the site where a derivation is needed without providing the derivation.

Why would a worker refer another worker? The literature offers several candidate answers without formalizing any of them. Ioannides and Loury note that "contacts may have reputational incentives to refer only high-quality workers" - if a referral turns out badly, the referrer's standing with the employer declines. This is correct as far as it goes, and it is the candidate mechanism that would make p_H > p_L come out of a model rather than go in as an assumption. But the sentence is not followed by a model; it is followed by the next topic.

Other candidate mechanisms for referral behavior are not discussed in the canonical texts at all. Referrers might act from reciprocity (a generalized norm of helping contacts) with no strategic calculation. They might act from social obligation (in tight communities, failing to refer an eligible contact incurs reputational costs within the community, not just with the employer). They might refer promiscuously, without quality discrimination, because the cost of a referral is low. These mechanisms have different observable implications:

- The reputation mechanism predicts that referral selectivity is higher in repeated-game employer-referrer relationships, lower in one-shot contexts, and responsive to feedback about referral quality.
- The reciprocity mechanism predicts referral rates uniform across contexts, uncorrelated with employer feedback.
- The social obligation mechanism predicts high referral rates in dense community networks regardless of quality considerations.
- Promiscuous referral predicts no *additional* selectivity beyond the composition of the referrer's contact set. It does not predict a zero match-quality premium - referrers are themselves employed workers whose contact sets are non-random relative to the unemployed pool - but it predicts no *conditional* selectivity: given the referrer's contact set, referred workers are a random draw from that set rather than a quality-filtered subset.

**The canonical texts contain almost no evidence bearing on why referrers refer.** *Getting a Job* (1974) is the natural place to look: it includes chapters on the role of the contact in the job-matching process and on the characteristics of jobs found through contacts. These chapters analyze the *outcomes* of contact-mediated hiring - wages, tenure, occupational match - not the contact's decision. The question of whether and why the contact chose to refer, which candidates the contact considered and on what criteria, does not arise as an analytical question in the book. The 1985 embeddedness paper discusses reputational dynamics in network relationships generally but does not connect them to the referral decision specifically. Montgomery (1991) brackets the referrer's decision entirely, noting only that the positive selectivity assumption is "maintained" rather than derived. Ioannides and Loury (2004) name the reputational incentive in one sentence and continue.

The correct formulation of this absence is precise: the canonical *modeling* literature did not derive the referrer's selectivity parameter from a model of the referrer's decision; the subsequent *experimental* literature - most directly Beaman and Magruder (2012) - has begun to test the underlying behavioral logic empirically. These are different enterprises. Assuming a parameter is not the same as validating the mechanism that would ground it, and the experimental validation, while confirming that the reputation mechanism operates when the stakes are made explicit, also shows that the selectivity parameter is sensitive to structural conditions the canonical model does not specify. That the assumed-selectivity property was treated as obvious enough not to require justification suggests the bifurcation in the action formation account went largely unnoticed for decades.

The canonical literature cannot distinguish among these mechanisms because it does not model the referrer's decision. This is not a minor gap. The referrer's action is the hinge on which the entire mechanism account turns.

## Level Three: The Aggregation Mechanism

The referral hiring literature makes a claim not just about individual job matches but about labor market structure. The canonical finding is that network-mediated hiring produces wage inequality, demographic sorting, and persistent differences in employment rates across groups differentiated by race, education, and neighborhood. This is a macro-level claim. It requires a transformation account: how do bilateral referral hiring decisions, each operating through the individual-level mechanisms described above, aggregate into the observed labor market patterns?

The verbal aggregation argument in the canonical texts runs as follows: many employers rely on referrals; referral networks are demographically homophilous; therefore network-based hiring routes jobs disproportionately to demographic groups already represented in a firm's workforce. This is plausible. It is not an aggregation model. And more specifically, it does not address a question that any aggregation model must face: why do the competitive pressures in labor markets not erode the referral-based inequality? If referred workers are systematically better-matched, competing employers should bid up their wages, which would either equalize wages across channels or shift all hiring toward referrals, which in turn shifts the locus of inequality to network formation rather than hiring itself.

Before naming what a complete aggregation specification would require, a distinction matters between what Hedström and Ylikoski's framework strictly demands and what a complete economic account additionally requires. H&Y's transformation mechanism requires specifying the social logic by which individual actions aggregate into the macro pattern - deriving the collective outcome from individual rules rather than asserting it. This is a structural requirement: the aggregation must be modeled, not merely named. The three specifications below go further, requiring equilibrium analysis, dynamic stability conditions, and a model of firm-level strategy. The higher standard applies here because the canonical literature's own claims are equilibrium claims: the referral premium is *durable*, the segmentation is *persistent*, and the inequality is *structural*. A mechanism account of a durable equilibrium phenomenon must derive why the aggregate pattern is stable, not merely that it arises.

Schelling's segregation model specifies what this requires: individual tolerance thresholds, cascade dynamics by which each relocation changes neighborhood composition for remaining residents, and conditions under which the resulting segregation is stable rather than transient. The Schelling account is satisfying not just because the aggregation step exists but because it derives why the macro pattern persists. Each move reinforces the conditions that motivate further moves, and the stable partition is one where no individual has a unilateral incentive to deviate. The referral hiring literature identifies the feedback ingredients - homophily, composition effects, employer preference - but does not assemble the logic that would allow a researcher to derive the stable pattern from the individual rules or identify what would disrupt it.

**A complete aggregation mechanism would require three specifications.**

First, a model of employer hiring strategies: what fraction of hires are referral-based, and under what conditions does this fraction increase or decrease in response to labor market conditions? The canonical literature treats reliance on referrals as a fixed property of firms rather than as a strategic response to labor market tightness, vacancy duration, and formal screening costs. A complete specification would derive firm-level referral reliance as a function of these parameters and identify what would shift the equilibrium fraction. Observable implication: reliance on referrals should vary systematically with labor market tightness and with the informativeness of formal credentials in a given occupation.

Second, a dynamic model of network composition: how does the demographic distribution of referral networks evolve when hiring outcomes feed back into the composition of firms' workforces? The Ioannides-Loury framework identifies the feedback loop - residential segregation produces network segregation, which produces hiring segregation, which reinforces demographic concentration in firms - but does not specify the dynamic conditions under which this loop converges to a stable equilibrium, cycles, or accelerates. The referral hiring literature identifies the necessary ingredients but does not assemble the aggregation logic that would allow a researcher to derive the macro pattern from the individual rules rather than asserting it.

Third, a specification of equilibrium conditions: why is the referral premium durable? Two candidate explanations have different observable implications. The first is information monopoly: referral networks gatekeep vacancy information, and competitive bidding fails to equalize wages because non-referred workers do not know about the vacancies. Under this explanation, the premium should be larger where vacancy information is poorly diffused, smaller where vacancy information is widely disseminated through formal channels. The second is quality persistence: referred workers are systematically better-matched, and the premium represents a productivity difference rather than a rent. Under this explanation, the premium should shrink as formal screening improves at identifying the same quality attributes that network referrals capture - which implies that the premium should vary across occupations by the proportion of relevant attributes that are directly testable.

These observable implications are testable and have not been systematically examined against the mechanism account in the canonical literature.

**On the post-canonical aggregation literature.** Calvó-Armengol and Jackson (2004, 2007) advance the aggregation specification substantially beyond the canonical texts and deserve more than a passing acknowledgment. Their models introduce unemployment persistence as a network dynamics problem: the risk of job loss rises when contacts become unemployed, because contacts are the channels through which vacancy information flows. They derive conditions for equilibrium employment rate differences across groups with different network structures, and show how density, clustering, and homophily produce wage inequality and persistent unemployment differentials. Against the three-part requirement: the dynamic network composition model is the element CA-J develop most fully; equilibrium conditions for information transmission are specified; the employer hiring strategy model as a function of referral reliance across varying labor market conditions remains less developed. Critically, CA-J inherit the parameterized referrer selectivity from Montgomery - the `p_H/p_L` ratio enters their models as an assumption, not as an output derived from a referrer's optimization problem. The aggregation specification in CA-J is therefore substantially more sophisticated than the canonical corpus, but it rests on an assumed foundation. The appropriate characterization is that the aggregation gap has been progressively narrowed since 2004 but not closed: CA-J achieve a sophisticated conditional aggregation model, one whose conclusions hold given the selectivity parameter but cannot specify when that parameter holds. Their analysis derives inequality results from network structure and employment dynamics; it does not organize those results around the question of which causal route - quality screening, information exclusion, or passive demographic composition - is operative in producing the referral advantage, and the channel decomposition does not appear as an analytical distinction in their work.

Given that a complete aggregation specification requires substantially more apparatus - network dynamics, firm-level equilibrium, market clearing conditions - than the referrer action model alone, the present piece focuses on the referrer's decision as the prerequisite gap. The referrer's selectivity parameter is the input that any aggregation model requires; one cannot build a properly specified aggregation model while leaving that parameter assumed rather than derived.

## The Gap That Matters Most: The Referrer's Decision

The absent referrer action model has a consequence that the inequality claim in the literature has not registered. There are three distinct causal channels through which network-based hiring could produce inequality in labor market outcomes.

**Channel A: Match quality.** Referred workers are better-selected candidates. The employer's use of referrals improves match quality and raises wages for matched workers. Inequality arises because access to referral pipelines is unequal across demographic groups. This channel requires referral selectivity - the referrer must be filtering for quality, so that p_H > p_L.

**Channel B: Information access.** Referred workers hear about vacancies that non-referred workers do not. The employer's use of referrals excludes candidates who lack network access to the vacancy, regardless of their quality. Inequality arises because information about vacancies is unequally distributed across demographic groups. This channel operates even with promiscuous referrers who refer everyone they know, because the inequality is in who hears about the vacancy, not in any quality signal.

**Channel C: Passive composition.** Referrers act without quality filtering, and network segregation is the entire story. Referred candidates share demographic characteristics with current employees through network homophily. If quality attributes correlate with those demographics in the population - through differential access to education, on-the-job experience, or neighborhood resources - referred workers will exhibit higher average quality without any selective referral decision and without any information exclusion of equally qualified non-referred candidates. Under Channel C, the match-quality premium is real but is a composition effect of the network rather than a screening effect of the referrer.

These are structurally distinct mechanisms. The canonical literature runs all three channels together, which has produced interpretive and policy confusion. The evidence that referred workers receive higher wages and longer-duration employment is consistent with all three: referred workers may be better screened (A), may simply have been able to apply (B), or may be from demographic groups with higher average qualifications through composition effects (C). The demographic sorting finding is most diagnostic: it requires at minimum Channel B or C, because the sorting pattern is determined by network composition. But the match-quality premium is consistent with any of the three channels.

The policy implications differ sharply across channels. Channel A is addressed by strengthening or extending the referrer's quality incentive - referral bonus programs, accountability mechanisms, reputation stakes. Channel B is addressed by creating information bridges - community job boards, outreach programs, expanded sourcing. Channel C is addressed by upstream interventions in education, neighborhood investment, and employment access that alter the quality-demographics correlation in the relevant population. Implementing any one of these without diagnosing which channel is active risks addressing the wrong intervention point. If Channel C is doing most of the work, both A and B interventions may have limited effects on inequality, because the quality-demographics correlation in the population is the load-bearing factor. The diagnostic for Channel C dominance is whether the referred workers' quality premium persists after controlling for demographic characteristics and upstream human capital accumulation.

**Organizations that have implemented formal referral bonus programs** - paying existing employees cash rewards for successful hires - are designing a Channel A intervention: the bonus creates explicit financial incentives for quality selectivity, substituting for or supplementing the ambient reputational dynamics the canonical model assumes. Whether organizations implementing such programs are aware of this mechanistic interpretation - or whether they are pursuing an intuitive bet that employees refer people like themselves who are good workers - is an empirical question about practitioner reasoning that this analysis does not settle. Beaman and Magruder (2012) ran a field experiment that tested the Channel A logic directly, finding that financial incentives for referrers in an Indian manufacturing context did produce higher-quality hires - the first direct experimental evidence for the reputation mechanism the canonical texts assumed without verification.

The important precision: the canonical literature's failure to derive the referrer's selectivity parameter is a gap in the *mechanism account*, not evidence against the phenomenon. The canonical literature does not establish whether quality selectivity operates in informal referral arrangements; it equally does not establish that it does not. What is missing is the structural analysis of when the reputational conditions that produce selectivity are present or absent - when the reputation mechanism is strong enough to generate p_H > p_L as an outcome rather than an input. Beaman-Magruder's finding that explicit financial incentives produce quality-selective behavior is consistent with the mechanism operating when stakes are made explicit; it does not determine what happens in informal arrangements where those stakes are ambient and variable.

Programs aimed at expanding vacancy information - community-based job boards, workforce development center outreach, expanded sourcing initiatives - operate a Channel B intervention: they address the gatekeeping of vacancy information rather than the quality signal. These two approaches address different mechanisms; implementing both as if they were substitutes reflects the conflation of channels that the absent referrer model permits. Whether the Channel A/B/C decomposition has been formally drawn in the post-2004 network labor economics literature - particularly in the empirical treatments that followed CA-J - is an open question this piece cannot settle. The decomposition follows directly from the structure of the problem. What the canonical texts demonstrably do not contain is this decomposition, and its absence in the foundational literature has interpretive consequences regardless of what subsequent work has done.

This distinction requires a referrer action model. Without specifying why referrers refer and how selectively, the evidence cannot tell us which channel is active, and the appropriate policy response cannot be specified.

## What a Complete Referrer Action Model Would Require

The missing specification is not exotic. It requires three things.

First, a specification of the referrer's objective function: what the referrer gains or loses from making a referral, as a function of referral quality and outcome. The reputation mechanism implies that the referrer's future value to the employer - as a source of referrals - depreciates with bad recommendations and appreciates with good ones. This generates an incentive to be selectively positive, provided the referrer has a sufficiently long time horizon and sufficiently repeated interactions with the employer. Where these conditions are absent (one-time relationships, high turnover, informal rather than employment-relationship connections), the reputation mechanism should be weak. Beaman and Magruder's finding that explicit financial incentives produce quality-selective behavior is consistent with this logic: financial bonuses substitute for the reputational stake that informal referral arrangements may or may not provide.

Second, a specification of the referrer's information: what the referrer actually knows about the candidate's quality, and how that knowledge compares to what the employer could learn from formal channels. The mechanism presupposes that the referrer has better information than formal screening could obtain. This is plausible for soft skills, cultural fit, and the quality of a candidate's judgment under pressure - attributes poorly captured by credentials and resumes. It is less plausible for technical skills that are directly testable. The mechanism should therefore be stronger in occupations where soft attributes dominate, weaker where hard skills dominate. This prediction is testable and has not been systematically tested against the mechanism account.

Third, a specification of equilibrium referral behavior: what the referrer does in equilibrium given their objectives and information, and what the resulting selectivity parameter p_H/p_L equals as a function of the structural features identified above. This is the object that Montgomery assumes; it is what a complete model would derive.

The formal apparatus required for this extension exists in the economics literature on reputation, repeated games, and information transmission. It has not been applied to the referrer in the referral hiring literature. Beaman and Magruder (2012) demonstrate that the underlying logic of the reputation mechanism is empirically real when the reputation stakes are made explicit; the canonical literature's contribution is to have assumed this without showing when the assumption holds.

## Conclusion

The referral hiring mechanism account is, by the standards of the social sciences, relatively well-developed. It has a formal economic treatment, a rich situational story, and a long empirical record. Applying Hedström and Ylikoski's standard rigorously does not reveal a crude failure but a selective gap: the situational mechanism is specified, the employer's action formation mechanism is formally modeled, and everything that depends on the referrer's behavior is assumed rather than derived.

This matters because the literature has multiple claims - the match-quality claim and the inequality-amplification claim - that rest on different mechanisms, and the referrer action model is what distinguishes them. The match-quality claim requires referral selectivity; the inequality claim requires only network composition. The canonical literature treats these as a unified mechanism because it has not articulated the referrer's decision; and because it has not articulated the referrer's decision, it cannot specify what the aggregation step from bilateral referral events to labor market segmentation actually involves, or which policy lever addresses the operative mechanism. The addition of Channel C - passive composition through quality-demographics correlation - sharpens this point: a literature equipped only with the situational mechanism and network homophily cannot distinguish among three structurally distinct mechanisms that produce the same observable match-quality premium.

The aggregation mechanism is the more demanding gap. A complete specification would require a model of employer hiring strategy, a dynamic model of network composition feedback, and an account of why competitive pressure does not erode the referral premium. Calvó-Armengol and Jackson have advanced the network dynamics substantially, but on a foundation that inherits the unresolved referrer-action assumption. The referrer model is prerequisite to the aggregation model, because the selectivity parameter the aggregation model requires must be derived from the referrer's decision rather than assumed into it.

The diagnostic methodology applied here - auditing whether a literature's mechanism account meets the standard its own explanatory claims require - could be applied to other institutional mechanisms. Whether trust in markets, norms in organizations, or credentialing in professions have analogous gaps is an empirical question about each case, not a finding that follows from this audit. The diagnostic is a filter; applying it is a research program, not a single demonstration. [*The Walking Mind*](posts/2026-05-17-the-walking-mind-whether-the-peripatetic-b41b/) audits whether an empirical finding can bear the philosophical weight its tradition assigns it; this piece applies a parallel standard to a causal mechanism account. Both belong to the College's developing practice of audit by explicit standard - a mode of inquiry that holds a body of work to the criteria its own claims invoke.

Whether the referral hiring mechanism has been progressively closed since 2004 is an open question; whether the canonical texts leave it open is not.
