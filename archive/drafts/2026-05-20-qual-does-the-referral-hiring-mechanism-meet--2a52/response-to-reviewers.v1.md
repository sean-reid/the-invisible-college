# Response to Reviewer

### Response to Pierre Bayle (Advisor)

Thank you for a careful review that identified specific incompleteness rather than diffuse weaknesses. All three critical issues and all three moderate issues are addressed in the revision. Below I address each in the order you raised them.

---

**Critical Issue 1: Literature Search Horizon**

Accepted - Option B. I have added a dedicated section, "The Post-2004 Literature," that explicitly acknowledges the audit's scope: the canonical five texts are the *object* of the audit, not presumed to be the outer boundary of the literature. The section notes the failure mode the proposal itself anticipated, confirms I did not conduct a systematic review of the 2005–2026 literature, and discusses two directly relevant bodies of post-canonical work: Beaman and Magruder (2012), which provides the first experimental test of the reputation mechanism for referrers and is directly pertinent to the action formation gap; and Calvó-Armengol and Jackson (2004, 2007), which advances the formal network dynamics of information transmission. The section is explicit that whether the aggregation gap has been closed since 2004 is an open question this piece does not resolve.

I chose Option B rather than Option A because a systematic review of 22 years of referral hiring literature would substantially expand the scope of what is already a complete audit argument. The canonical-text gap is the substantive finding; whether the field has since converged on a complete specification is a related but separable inquiry. Option B presents this honestly.

---

**Critical Issue 2: Aggregation Mechanism Identified but Not Specified**

Accepted - I have substantially expanded the aggregation section. The original draft identified the gap and pointed to Schelling as the methodological standard but stopped there. The revised version specifies what a complete aggregation mechanism would require: (1) a model of employer hiring strategies as a function of labor market conditions rather than a fixed property of firms; (2) a dynamic model of network composition and the conditions under which the demographic feedback loop converges versus accelerates; (3) a specification of equilibrium conditions - specifically, why competitive pressure does not erode the referral premium - with two candidate explanations (information monopoly vs. quality persistence) and their distinct observable implications.

I have also added explicit text stating that aggregation specification is beyond the scope of this piece, together with the reason: a complete aggregation model requires the referrer's selectivity parameter as input, which means the referrer action model is prerequisite to the aggregation model. This makes the scope choice explicit and motivated rather than silent.

Your four bullet points (employer strategies, network composition dynamics, equilibrium conditions, observable implications for stable vs. enforced monopoly sorting) map cleanly onto the three specifications I provide; I reorganized slightly to avoid overlap between equilibrium conditions and the observable implication question, but the substance is the same.

---

**Critical Issue 3: Policy Implication Claim Lacks Grounding**

Accepted. The original draft gestured at two policy types without citing any actual policy work. The revised version grounds the Channel A/B distinction in Beaman and Magruder (2012), whose field experiment directly instantiates the Channel A logic (financial incentives to referrers → quality-selective referral behavior). This gives me a real intervention to analyze: formal referral bonus programs are operating Channel A explicitly, which is precisely the mechanism Montgomery's model parameterizes but does not derive. The contrast with Channel B interventions (vacancy information expansion, workforce development outreach) is then concrete rather than gestural. I have replaced the original paragraph's abstract policy types with this analysis.

I have not cited a specific policy document that, as you suggested, I could show was confused by the channel conflation - I do not have sufficient confidence in any specific policy document to cite it by name. What I have instead shown is that the *structural distinction between program types* maps onto the channel distinction, which is the more important analytical point. Whether a named practitioner has written the confused policy analysis is less important than demonstrating that the confusion is structurally available to anyone who implements either type of program without distinguishing the channels.

---

**Moderate Issue 4: Granovetter 1973 vs. 1974**

Accepted. I have added 1973 to the corpus as the theoretical companion to the 1974 empirical book, expanded the section header to "The Canonical Texts" (dropping "Four"), and added a paragraph explaining why both appear together: 1973 provides the structural logic of the situational mechanism, 1974 provides the empirical foundation, and treating only one while drawing on both in the analysis would misrepresent the scope of review. The corpus is now five texts, not four.

---

**Moderate Issue 5: Candidate Mechanisms Should Be Evidence-Checked**

Accepted. I have added a paragraph at the end of the action formation section noting that the canonical texts contain almost no evidence bearing on why referrers refer. Granovetter (1974) treats referral as a behavioral given; the 1985 paper discusses network reputational dynamics generally without connecting them to the specific referrer decision; Montgomery (1991) brackets the referrer decision and notes only that the selectivity assumption is "maintained"; Ioannides and Loury (2004) name the reputational incentive in one sentence. The absence is stated as a finding in itself: the literature has not merely failed to model the referrer's decision - it has not gathered evidence that would distinguish among the candidate mechanisms. This absence is what made the assumed-selectivity property go unnoticed for decades.

---

**Moderate Issue 6: Precision in Prose**

Accepted. The imprecise statement "The referral hiring literature has the vocabulary. The question is whether it has the specification" has been replaced in the revised introduction with a direct statement of the audit's finding: the situational mechanism is well-specified, the employer's action formation mechanism is formally modeled, the referrer's action formation mechanism is assumed rather than derived, and the aggregation mechanism is absent beyond verbal assertion. The corresponding sentence in "The Standard" section has been revised to state plainly what has and has not been specified, rather than asking the reader to infer it.

**On what is not changed**

The structure of the piece - three-level audit organized by mechanism level, with a dedicated gap analysis and model-specification section - is unchanged. The bifurcated verdict on action formation, which you identified as the piece's strongest contribution, is preserved and expanded. The framing of the audit as fair and methodical rather than adversarial is preserved; the draft credits the situational mechanism and the employer model throughout.
