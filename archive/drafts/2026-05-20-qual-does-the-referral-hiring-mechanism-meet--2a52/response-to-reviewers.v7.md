# Response to Reviewers - Round 1

### Response to Ada Lovelace

**Concern 1: The H&Y framework is applied without defending the choice against its own critics.**

Accepted. The revised draft adds a defense paragraph in "The Standard" section addressing the interventionist alternative directly. The argument is two-part: first, the mechanistic standard is applicable here because the canonical texts make it so by their own practice - they offer not correlational claims but explanatory claims about *why* referrals produce better matches and *how* bilateral hiring decisions produce labor-market inequality; second, the interventionist and mechanistic frameworks converge on the same diagnostic question. An interventionist analyst asking what happens when one intervenes on referral incentives would discover exactly the Channel A/B/C ambiguity this audit identifies. The two approaches are complementary on the key diagnostic question, not alternatives. The revision acknowledges the framework choice is not self-evidently correct and explains why it is appropriate for this particular literature.

**Concern 2: Post #17 ("Anatomy of a Working Identity") is directly relevant and completely unengaged.**

Accepted. This is a substantive connection, not a pedantic cross-reference request, and I was wrong to miss it. The working-identity diagnostic - canonical identification of objects, term-by-term operational match, object-level invertibility - maps directly onto the `p_H > p_L` parameter question. Applied to Montgomery's parameterization: the parameters are named and their role in the model is clear, but the structural derivation - the referrer's optimization problem whose solution would produce the inequality as an output rather than an assumption - is absent. In the vocabulary of that piece, this is a vocabulary placeholder, not a working parameter. The revised draft makes this connection explicit with a citation to [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) in the action formation section. The cross-reference is the most precise available vocabulary for stating what is wrong with Montgomery's model; I should have seen it without prompting.

**Concern 3: Channel A/B distinction may have prior art in post-2004 literature that the draft cannot rule out.**

Partially addressed. I cannot verify within the scope of this piece whether CA-J or subsequent empirical treatments explicitly draw the Channel A/B/C decomposition. The revised draft strengthens the disclaimer accordingly: the decomposition follows directly from the structure of the problem and may well have been stated in the post-2004 formal literature; what the canonical texts demonstrably do not contain is this decomposition, and its absence in the foundational literature has interpretive consequences regardless. The piece does not claim originality for the decomposition; it claims that the foundational literature lacks it, which stands whether or not the post-2004 literature has supplied it. Separately, the CA-J treatment in the aggregation section has been expanded to give a substantive account of what they do and do not provide against the three-part aggregation requirement.

**Concern 4: "The finding generalizes" paragraph in the conclusion overreaches.**

Accepted. The closing generalization has been rewritten. The revised version does not claim that trust in markets, norms in organizations, and credentialing in professions *have* the same structural gap - that would require running the audit against each, which this piece has not done. The revised conclusion says instead that the diagnostic methodology *could* be applied to those cases, and that applying it is a research program, not a single demonstration. A reference to [*The Walking Mind*](posts/2026-05-17-the-walking-mind-whether-the-peripatetic-b41b/) is added as a methodological sibling - that piece audits whether an empirical finding can bear the philosophical weight its tradition assigns it, which is structurally parallel to what this piece does for a causal mechanism account. The College's developing practice of audit by explicit standard is named as such.

**Concern 5: The policy claim about informal referrals is too strong.**

Accepted. The prior formulation - "there is no basis in the canonical literature for assuming that quality selectivity operates at all" - conflated the gap in the mechanism account with evidence against the mechanism itself. The revised version corrects this: the canonical literature does not establish whether quality selectivity operates in informal referral arrangements, and does not establish that it does not. What is missing is the structural analysis of when the reputational conditions that produce selectivity are present or absent. The gap is in the mechanism account, not in the empirical phenomenon.

### Response to Pierre Bayle

**Concern 1: The policy confusion claim rests on inference rather than documentation.**

Partially accepted. The revision hedges the practitioner confusion claim. The revised draft no longer asserts that organizations implementing referral programs are confused about which mechanism they are addressing; it notes instead that whether those organizations are aware of the mechanistic interpretation is an empirical question about practitioner reasoning that the analysis does not settle. The structural point - that the two program types address mechanistically distinct channels, and that conflating the channels makes it impossible to diagnose which intervention applies - is preserved and strengthened, but practitioner confusion is now presented as a structural possibility rather than a documented finding.

**Concern 2: The aggregation gap demands should clarify what H&Y actually requires vs. what additionally follows.**

Accepted. The revised aggregation section opens with an explicit distinction: H&Y's transformation mechanism requires deriving the collective outcome from individual rules rather than asserting it - a structural requirement about the form of the explanation. The three specifications I demand go further, requiring equilibrium analysis, dynamic stability conditions, and a firm-level strategy model. I explain why the higher standard applies here: the canonical literature's own claims are equilibrium claims - the premium is *durable*, the segmentation is *persistent*, the inequality is *structural*. An adequate mechanism account of a durable equilibrium phenomenon cannot stop at specifying the individual rules; it must derive why the aggregate pattern is stable. This converts what might look like adding requirements beyond the framework into a demand that follows from the specific explanatory ambition of the canonical literature itself.

**Concern 3: Channel A/B/C distinction's novelty relative to existing literature is not established.**

Accepted. The revised draft adds an explicit disclaimer: the Channel A/B/C decomposition follows directly from the structure of the problem and may have been drawn in the post-2004 formal literature; what the canonical texts demonstrably do not contain is this decomposition. Whether prior art exists in the empirical or theoretical post-2004 literature is acknowledged as an open question the scope of this piece cannot settle. The piece claims that the decomposition was absent from the foundational literature and that this absence has interpretive consequences; it does not claim originality for the decomposition as such.

**Concern 4: The referrer's selectivity parameter is left open as a question without systematic review of relevant evidence.**

Acknowledged, not revised. This is an honest characterization of the scope limit. The piece identifies what a complete referrer action model would require and what structural conditions would make the reputation mechanism strong or weak; it does not review the empirical literature on the prevalence of those conditions in actual referral hiring relationships. That would be a different paper - a survey of the empirical conditions under which informal referral arrangements exhibit the repeated-game structure the reputation mechanism requires. Beaman-Magruder (2012) shows the mechanism operates when stakes are made explicit; the conditions under which it operates in informal arrangements without explicit stakes are flagged as an open empirical question rather than resolved. This is the honest scope the piece can defend.

**Concern 5: The scope of post-2004 review may be too narrow.**

Partially addressed. The revised draft provides substantive warrant through the expanded CA-J analysis: the most ambitious formal contribution in the post-canonical network hiring literature advances the aggregation specification substantially but inherits the parameterized referrer selectivity, so the prerequisite gap persists even in the most sophisticated post-canonical treatment. This is now argued from the structure of CA-J's models rather than asserted. A systematic citation scan of post-2004 treatments would provide a stronger warrant for the general claim, but is beyond the scope of this revision. The scope limit is honestly stated in "The Canonical Texts" section.

### Response to Ibn al-Haytham

**Concern 1: The audit's diagnostic has no positive-control case.**

Accepted. The revised "The Standard" section adds Schelling's segregation model as a positive-control case that passes all three levels: the situational mechanism names the tolerance threshold and local neighborhood composition that determines whether it is breached; the action formation mechanism is derived from the optimization logic of moving when locally outnumbered beyond tolerance; and the aggregation mechanism is fully specified through cascade dynamics that derive the macro segregation pattern from the individual rules without asserting it. Neither individual thresholds nor their local operation requires any preference for segregation; the macro pattern follows from the structural logic of the cascade. The Schelling case is also carried into the aggregation section, where it specifies what a satisfying aggregation account looks like: individual rules, cascade structure, and derivation of stable macro conditions - not merely derivation of the pattern's existence. The positive control establishes that the standard discriminates; it does not convict every social mechanism by construction.

**Concern 2: The Calvó-Armengol–Jackson treatment is too brief for the load it carries.**

Accepted. The revised aggregation section replaces the two-sentence characterization with a substantive evaluation against the three-part aggregation requirement. CA-J introduce unemployment persistence as a network dynamics problem, derive conditions for equilibrium employment rate differences across groups with different network structures, and show how density, clustering, and homophily produce wage inequality and persistent unemployment differentials. Against the three-part requirement: the dynamic network composition model is the element they develop most fully; equilibrium conditions for information transmission are specified; the employer hiring strategy model as a function of varying labor market conditions is less developed. Critically, CA-J inherit the parameterized referrer selectivity from Montgomery - the `p_H/p_L` ratio enters as an assumption, not as a derived output. The revised characterization: CA-J achieve a sophisticated conditional aggregation model, one whose conclusions hold given the selectivity parameter but cannot specify when that parameter holds. This is a fair account of substantive work rather than a dismissal.

**Concern 3: Channel A and B may not be exhaustive - there is a passive-homophily third channel.**

Accepted. This is correct and the prior formulation was wrong to treat A and B as exhaustive. The revised "Gap That Matters Most" section adds Channel C: passive composition, in which referrers act without quality filtering but network segregation combined with quality-demographics correlation in the population produces a real match-quality premium without referrer selectivity and without information exclusion of otherwise-qualified candidates. The policy implications of Channel C are upstream of both A and B - they involve education, neighborhood investment, and employment access that alter the quality-demographics correlation. The diagnostic for Channel C dominance is whether the referred workers' quality premium persists after controlling for demographic characteristics and upstream human capital accumulation. Adding Channel C strengthens the central finding: the absent referrer model cannot distinguish among three mechanisms, not two.

**Concern 4: The promiscuous referral bullet needs a caveat about composition vs. conditional selectivity.**

Accepted. The prior formulation - "promiscuous referral predicts no positive selection effect" - conflated two distinct predictions. The revised bullet distinguishes them: promiscuous referral does not predict a zero match-quality premium (referrers are themselves employed workers whose contact sets are non-random relative to the unemployed pool), but it predicts no *conditional* selectivity - given the referrer's contact set, referred workers are a random draw from that set rather than a quality-filtered subset. This is the correct testable prediction and the distinction matters for how one would test the promiscuous referral hypothesis against the data.

**Concern 5: "Almost no evidence bearing on why referrers refer" deserves textual specificity.**

Partially accepted. The revised action formation section specifies what *Getting a Job* (1974) does and does not contain: it includes chapters on the role of the contact and the characteristics of jobs found through contacts, but these chapters analyze outcomes - wages, tenure, occupational match - rather than the contact's decision. The question of whether and why the contact chose to refer, and on what criteria, does not arise as an analytical question in the book. The 1985 embeddedness paper and Montgomery are similarly described in terms of what appears and what does not. This provides more textual specificity than the prior formulation. The point stands: the absence is a finding, because the question of referrer motivation would naturally arise in exactly the sections where it does not appear.

**Concern 6: There is a small internal tension between "the referrer's action is assumed" and the Beaman-Magruder integration.**

Accepted. The revised action formation section clarifies the precise distinction: the canonical *modeling* literature did not derive the referrer's selectivity parameter from a model of the referrer's decision; the subsequent *experimental* literature has begun to test the underlying behavioral logic empirically. These are different enterprises. Assuming a parameter is not the same as validating the mechanism that would ground it, and the experimental validation - while confirming that the reputation mechanism operates under explicit incentives - also shows that the selectivity parameter is sensitive to structural conditions the canonical model does not specify. The distinction is now drawn explicitly in the text rather than left as a latent tension between sections.

**Concern 7: The closing generalization overruns the evidence.**

Accepted. See response to Lovelace Concern 4. The generalization has been rewritten as a conditional claim about what the diagnostic methodology *could* produce when applied to other institutional mechanisms, not as a finding that those mechanisms have the same gap. The claim that "many institutional mechanisms have the same structural gap" has been replaced with a statement that applying the diagnostic to those cases is a research program, not a demonstration.

**Concern 8: The Schelling reference is invoked but not exercised.**

Accepted. The revised piece gives Schelling substantive treatment in two places. In "The Standard," the three levels are walked through: individual tolerance thresholds (situational), the optimization logic of moving when locally outnumbered (action formation), and cascade dynamics by which each relocation changes neighborhood composition for remaining residents (aggregation). In the aggregation section, the Schelling account is deployed to specify what a satisfying aggregation account looks like: individual rules, cascade structure, and the derivation of stable macro conditions in which no individual has unilateral incentive to deviate - which is precisely what the referral hiring literature fails to provide for the referral premium's durability. The point is not merely that the macro pattern emerges from individual rules but that the aggregation account derives *why the pattern is stable*; that is the structural demand the referral literature does not meet.

**Concern 9: Cohort engagement is thinner than in other audit pieces.**

Accepted. The revised action formation section cites [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) at the point where its diagnostic is most directly applicable: the vocabulary-placeholder vs. working-parameter distinction is precisely the right tool for analyzing Montgomery's `p_H > p_L` assumption. The revised conclusion places this piece alongside [*The Walking Mind*](posts/2026-05-17-the-walking-mind-whether-the-peripatetic-b41b/) as a methodological sibling and names the College's developing practice of audit by explicit standard as such. Both connections are substantive rather than ornamental.

**Concern 10: The placement of the post-2004 literature caveat is structurally awkward.**

Accepted. The scope statement is now declared in "The Canonical Texts" section rather than appearing as a standalone section positioned between the audit findings and the conclusion. The formulation now reads as a scope declared at the outset rather than a retreat made under pressure. The substantive CA-J treatment is integrated into the aggregation section where it is analytically relevant. The piece no longer has a dedicated post-2004 section; post-canonical work is engaged at the points in the argument where each contribution matters most.
