# Response to Reviewers

### Response to Florence Nightingale

**Concern 1 (one-pass formula diagnosis is illustrative, not conclusive).** Accepted in part. I have not added a computational check on the one-pass formula, as that would require a separate experiment not central to the paper's question (whether the standard two-pass implementation contributes to BCa coverage failure). However, I have tightened the language in the Mechanism section. The one-pass error is now explicitly characterized as "absolute error of order $10^3 \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}} \approx 10^{-13}$," followed by the statement that relative error is $\sim 10^{-13}/|\text{sum}|$, which diverges as the sum approaches zero. This makes clear that the figure is an asymptotic bound on the condition number, not a specific measurement-and that the condition number is infinite for perfectly symmetric data. The comparison is now more precise without requiring new data.

**Concern 2 (Table 2 structure invites misreading).** Accepted in part. The Gap (pp) column is now bolded throughout both tables, making the structural zero visually salient. A prose paragraph immediately following the tables now explains the structural zero mechanism before the reader can form the wrong impression. I have not consolidated Tables 1 and 2, because the two tables measure different objects (numerical error in the acceleration versus empirical coverage rates), and their separation preserves clarity about what is being measured at each step. The explicit prose now carries the burden the formatting alone did not.

**Concern 3 (pre-flight check conflates two levels of exactness).** Accepted fully. The Pre-Flight Results section is restructured from the ground up. The structural explanation now leads: why 256-bit and 512-bit agree, what that implies, and why it is the expected outcome of the pre-flight protocol. The empirical finding (bit-identical outputs) follows as a confirmation, not a surprise. A new final paragraph clarifies what the pre-flight's exact-zero agreement does and does not imply for the main experiment's 53-bit vs. 256-bit comparison-the two comparisons are structurally different in kind.

**Concern 4 (the (n-1) scaling in the Runbook deserves a clarifying sentence).** Accepted fully. A sentence is added in the Background section where the two-pass formula is first described, noting that the $(n-1)$ scaling cancels exactly in the ratio defining $\hat{a}$ and is a standard numerical convenience. The Runbook code also has an inline comment to the same effect. A reader comparing to a textbook formula will now see immediately that the result is algebraically identical to the standard form.

**Concern 5 (coverage rates lack confidence intervals on the gap).** Accepted with a structural argument rather than a sampling-uncertainty argument. A new prose paragraph after Table 2 explains that the zero gap is structural: because the relative error in $\hat{a}$ is at machine-epsilon scale, the absolute difference in $\hat{a}$ between the two implementations is too small-by orders of magnitude-to shift any bootstrap quantile index (the 1/500 bootstrap quantile spacing is $\sim 10^{12}$ times larger than the shift caused by the precision difference). BCa-double and BCa-mpmath produce bit-identical intervals for every sample in every cell. The zero carries no sampling uncertainty; reporting Clopper-Pearson intervals on the gap would overstate the nature of the uncertainty that exists. The marginal 95% CIs on the individual coverage estimates ($\pm 1.1$ pp) are reported and distinguished from the gap.

**Concern 6 (Conclusion overstates certainty).** Accepted fully. The Conclusion now reads: "is false for practical two-pass implementations, across the $t$ and Pareto distributions and sample sizes ($n \in \{50, 100, 200, 500\}$) that constitute the hardest cases for BCa." The qualifier specifies both the implementation class and the scope of tested distributions. The following sentence also removes "purely" from the characterization of BCa's failure mechanism, replacing it with the precise negative claim that is actually supported by the experiment.

### Response to Alexander von Humboldt

**Concern 1 (Tables 1 and 2 show 11/12 rows from a 40-cell design, but the paper claims all 40 cells).** Accepted fully. A note is now added before Table 1, before the t-distribution portion of Table 2, and in prose following the coverage table, stating explicitly that all 40 cells in the design show 0.0% fraction exceeding $10^{-6}$ and 0.00 pp coverage gap, that the rows shown are a representative selection spanning both distribution families and the full range of sample sizes, and that the complete cell-by-cell results are in `results.json` and `pareto_results_fixed.json`. The claim "all 40 cells" is now verifiable by the reader through the data files.

**Concern 2 (pre-flight explanation may read as self-refutation of the reference computation's validity).** Accepted fully. A new concluding paragraph in the Pre-Flight section addresses this directly. The 256-vs-512-bit agreement is structural-both precisions represent the same 57-bit inputs exactly and perform arithmetic far above that precision, so agreement is expected. This is distinct from the main experiment's 256-vs-53-bit comparison, where double precision performs all intermediate arithmetic with 53-bit rounding. The inputs may be the same, but every subsequent operation (subtraction, multiplication, three-halves power) differs in precision between the two implementations. The main experiment measures those accumulated differences; the pre-flight check establishes only that 256 bits is a sufficient reference. This paragraph closes the potential circularity without modifying the experiment.

**Concern 3 (Welford (1962) and Terriberry (2007) appear uncited in the reference list).** Accepted fully. Both are now cited in the Mechanism section, where they are introduced as examples of stable one-pass alternatives to the two-pass formula. The passage notes that Welford's running algorithm avoids the problematic intermediate sums of the algebraic expansion, and that Terriberry extends this to third central moments. The point is that the two-pass formula's stability is not unique-these online algorithms share it-and that the instability of the direct algebraic expansion is a property of that specific form, not of one-pass computation generally.

**Concern 4 (the floor $10^{-15}$ in the relative-error formula is unexplained).** Accepted fully. A sentence is added immediately after the formula in the Experimental Design section explaining the floor's purpose (prevent division by zero for identically symmetric samples), justifying the specific value (approximately ten machine epsilons, smaller than any physically meaningful acceleration observed in the main runs), and noting that the results are insensitive to this choice across any reasonable range ($10^{-16}$ to $10^{-12}$).

**Concern 5 ("identically zero" for the coverage gap should be confirmed as structural).** Accepted fully. A prose paragraph after Table 2 now explains the mechanism: the P95 relative error in $\hat{a}$ at typical acceleration magnitudes translates to a shift in the BCa quantile level orders of magnitude smaller than the 1/500 bootstrap quantile spacing. BCa-double and BCa-mpmath therefore produce bit-identical intervals for every sample in every cell. The zero gap is explicitly labeled structural, and the distinction from a noise-level zero is stated clearly.

**Concern 6 (Pareto cells have smaller median errors than $t$ cells, contrary to naive expectation, and this is unexplained).** Accepted fully. A new paragraph at the end of the Mechanism section, headed "Why Pareto errors are smaller than $t$ errors," explains the direction: Pareto's right skewness means the cubed deviation sum is dominated by large positive contributions, limiting cancellation and reducing the effective condition number relative to symmetric $t$ distributions. The $t$ cells show somewhat higher relative errors not because their absolute arithmetic errors are larger, but because the acceleration magnitudes in the denominator are smaller (the sum is closer to zero). The contrast between the two families confirms the two-pass formula's stability across both the symmetric case (worst-case for cancellation) and the skewed case (best-case).

### Response to Pierre Bayle

**Concern 1 (Table 1 presents 11 of 40 cells without explaining the omission).** Accepted fully. See response to Humboldt Concern 1 above; the same fix addresses both.

**Concern 2 (Table 2 shows gaps of exactly 0.00 without confidence intervals).** Accepted with a structural argument. The zero gap at 2,000-sample resolution is not an artifact of discrete resolution: with 500 bootstrap resamples, the smallest detectable non-zero coverage gap would indeed be $\sim 1/2000 = 0.05$ pp, but the actual gap between implementations is zero not because it falls below that resolution but because the two implementations produce bit-identical intervals for every sample. The structural zero argument (added as a prose paragraph after Table 2) explains why this is deterministic rather than statistical. Clopper-Pearson intervals on the gap would be misleading here because they would suggest sampling uncertainty where none exists on the gap itself. The marginal CIs on individual coverage estimates ($\pm 1.1$ pp) are reported separately.

**Concern 3 (the Pareto sampling code is not provided in the Runbook).** Accepted fully. A `pareto_sample` function is now included in the Runbook with the correct NumPy call (`rng.pareto(alpha, size=n) + 1`) and an inline comment explaining the parameter convention: `numpy.Generator.pareto(a)` draws Lomax variates with shape `a`; adding 1 yields classical Pareto with minimum 1, shape `alpha`, and mean $\alpha/(\alpha-1)$.

**Concern 4 (abstract's "no contribution exists at measurable scale" is vague).** Accepted fully. The opening paragraphs and the abstract now reference the pre-registered threshold of $10^{-6}$ explicitly. The revised language is: "relative numerical errors stay at least six orders of magnitude below the pre-registered meaningful threshold of $10^{-6}$." This operationalizes "measurable" in terms of the pre-committed standard rather than an undefined natural threshold.

**Concern 5 ("purely a property of the sampling distribution" overstates the finding).** Accepted. The Conclusion now reads: "BCa's failure for $t(3)$ data is driven by the sampling distribution of $\hat{a}$ when the population is symmetric with a non-existent third moment-numerical error in computing $\hat{a}$ is not a material factor." The word "purely" is removed; the negative claim (what the experiment actually establishes) is now stated directly rather than implied by an exclusivity framing.

**Concern 6 (process-narrative language in opening and Background).** Accepted fully. "The question, left open by prior work, is whether..." is revised to "The question is whether..." in the opening paragraphs. "The piece left open whether there was also a numerical component to this instability" is revised to "Whether there was also a numerical component to this instability remained an open question." These changes state the epistemic situation directly rather than framing the paper as a reply to a prior piece.

**Concern 7 (one-pass catastrophe explanation conflates absolute and relative error).** Accepted fully. The Mechanism section now explicitly distinguishes absolute error ($\sim 10^3 \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}} \approx 10^{-13}$) from relative error (approximately $10^{-13}/|\text{sum}|$, which diverges as the sum approaches zero for symmetric data). The point that the condition number is infinite for symmetric samples is stated before, not after, the quantitative bound, so the reader has the conceptual structure before encountering the number.
