# The Transfer Condition: When Argumentative Borrowing Carries Its Conclusions Across Domains

In October 1838, fifteen months into a systematic inquiry whose aims he could not yet fully name, Charles Darwin sat down with Malthus's *Essay on the Principle of Population*. By his own account, he had prepared himself, through long observation of animals and plants, to appreciate what he was about to read. When the Malthusian argument hit him-that populations press inevitably against the limits of subsistence, producing struggle and differential survival-it struck him at once that under such conditions, favorable variations would tend to be preserved, and unfavorable ones destroyed. Natural selection. The reading took an afternoon. The preparation for it had taken fifteen months, and natural history had been building toward it for a century.

What Darwin borrowed from Malthus is one of the most consequential instances of cross-domain borrowing in the history of science. It is also one of the most precisely limited: Darwin took almost nothing from Malthus except a mechanism. He did not take the arithmetic-the claim about geometric population growth against arithmetic food growth, which he knew was inaccurate for most species. He did not take the politics-the conclusion that poverty was a mathematical necessity, which Malthus had used the same framework to establish. He took the elimination structure: finite resources, differential survival, population-level change over time. That structure, transplanted into natural history, made predictions that Malthus's framework had not needed and that Darwinian biology could verify.

Sigmund Freud also borrowed across domains, and prolifically. Trained under Ernst Brücke, a committed member of the Helmholtz school, Freud absorbed the program of reducing all physiological processes to physics and chemistry. The vocabulary of that program-energy, force, quantity, discharge, the conservation of excitation-saturated his early theoretical writing and never entirely left it. The *Project for a Scientific Psychology* of 1895 is an explicit attempt to model the mind as a physical system, with quantities of nervous excitation flowing between neurons, governed by a principle of inertia. The metapsychological papers of 1915 retained the "economic" point of view in the mature theoretical architecture: libidinal energy, cathexis, anticathexis, the economics of pleasure and unpleasure.

Freud's vocabulary is Helmholtzian. But psychic energy cannot be measured in joules. There is no conservation law for cathexis. When Freud claims that a repressed memory retains its quantity of excitation indefinitely, no observation specified by the model's own terms could confirm or disconfirm it. The hydraulic framework arrived in psychoanalysis intact as vocabulary; its evidential structure did not make the crossing.

The contrast between these two cases is the question this essay takes up. When an argumentative framework crosses from its home domain into a new one, what makes the difference between a borrowing that carries genuine analytical weight and one that carries only the terminology that names the argument?

## What We Already Know How to Test

The question of cross-domain transfer has been approached from several directions, none quite reaching the present problem.

For algebraic identities, precise diagnostics now exist. Henri Poincaré and Pierre Bayle's [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) shows what it takes for a mathematical mapping to carry theorems rather than only intuitions: canonical object identification intrinsic to the formalism, term-by-term operational match without qualification, and object-level invertibility. When these three conditions hold, theorems transfer. When they fail-as in the Mehta–Schwab mapping between deep learning and renormalization-vocabulary transfers and theorems do not. The diagnostic is rigorous and testable. But it addresses formal mathematical identities; the authors explicitly set aside the vast territory of productive borrowing that does not claim algebraic structure, which is precisely the territory this essay occupies.

The philosophy of science literature addresses structural analogies more broadly. Mary Hesse's *Models and Analogies in Science* (1966) distinguishes positive, negative, and neutral analogy: what the source and target domains share, what they definitely do not share, and what remains undetermined. Paul Bartha's *By Parallel Reasoning* (2010) develops an articulation model in which analogical arguments succeed when they identify a relation holding in the source domain and argue-through structural correspondence-that it holds in the target. Dedre Gentner's structure-mapping theory (1983) finds that productive analogies preserve relational structure rather than object attributes: it is the relations between things, not the things themselves, that carry across.

These frameworks are illuminating and any account of argumentative transfer must be consistent with them. But none of them addresses whether the *evidential obligations* of the source domain travel with the structure. You can have perfect relational alignment-all structural relations preserved, Gentner's conditions fully satisfied-without the epistemic machinery that grounds those relations making the crossing. This is precisely what happens with Freud: the thermodynamic structure aligns relationally with the psychoanalytic claims, but the evidence that would confirm or disconfirm a hydraulic claim in physics has no application to claims about psychic energy. The existing frameworks are necessary conditions for productive transfer. They are not sufficient.

A brief note on my own prior work, because a peer reviewer's concern about convergence deserves direct acknowledgment. Two earlier essays in this collection address related territory. [*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) proposes three conditions for legitimate anachronistic reading of past thinkers-conditions on *reading*, not on *borrowing*. [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) identifies a "loading" function in examples, by which illustrative material imports the source domain's intuitions without its evidential constraints-a pattern structurally parallel to what vocabulary-only borrowings do at the level of frameworks. Both essays illuminate the present question by analogy, but neither contains the diagnostic I develop here. The transfer conditions must be derived from the cases themselves; they cannot be read off either prior essay. This essay is self-contained.

## The Case That Works: Darwin and Malthus

To understand what Darwin took from Malthus, it helps to be exact about what the *Essay on Population* actually argued. Malthus's central claim was an asymmetry: population, he maintained, tends to increase geometrically; food supply tends to increase only arithmetically. The inevitable consequence is a struggle for subsistence-what Malthus called, in language Darwin would inherit, "the struggle for existence." The political conclusion-that poverty is not a remediable social condition but a mathematical necessity-was Malthus's primary aim. He used the struggle to ground a conservative political economy.

Darwin extracted from this structure the third element alone, stripped of the arithmetic and the politics: a mechanism of resource competition and differential elimination. The Malthusian claim that not all individuals can survive given limited resources became, in Darwin's hands, the condition that would-given heritable variation in survival-relevant traits-produce differential reproduction. The struggle was not Malthus's struggle; the conclusion was not Malthus's conclusion; but the causal structure (resource limits → differential elimination → population-level change) is unmistakably derived from Malthus and could not have been derived any other way.

This borrowing satisfies three conditions I want to make explicit.

*First*, what transferred was a mechanism, not terminology. Darwin can be-and was-described without Malthusian vocabulary: if organisms vary in survival-relevant heritable traits, and if resources are limited so that not all can survive, then traits that aid survival will become more common over time. No Malthusian terms appear in that description. The mechanism has been genuinely transposed; the vocabulary was incidental.

*Second*, the mechanism inherited evidential obligations from the source domain, adapted to the new context. Malthus's claims were testable by counting populations and measuring food supplies-a kind of tracking of population-level variables over time. Darwin's version of the elimination mechanism generates analogous tests: track variation in traits, measure differential survival, examine the fossil record and the geographic distribution of species. The specific procedures are different-you cannot do Malthus's field census on geological time-but the *character* of the evidential commitment is preserved: there exist observations capable of confirming or disconfirming the mechanism's application, and those observations are motivated by the mechanism rather than available independently of it. Before Darwin, you could observe variation; after Darwin, you had a reason to predict which variations would increase.

*Third*, the borrowed mechanism placed non-trivial constraints on biological inquiry. Pre-Darwinian natural history could describe species; it could not predict how species distributions would change over time. The selection mechanism rules out some patterns (random variation accumulating into consistent adaptive fit) and requires others (heritable variation in survival-relevant traits). These were not questions natural history had the apparatus to ask before the Malthusian elimination mechanism was transposed into it.

## The Case That Fails: Freud and Helmholtz

The Helmholtz school of medicine that shaped Freud's training had a rigorous and specific program: all vital processes should be explicable in terms of physical forces, and those forces are measurable. Energy, in that program, is a quantity with a conservation law. Force, work, and discharge are terms of art with precise physical definitions and clear empirical implications-you can, in principle, measure the energy in a physical system, track its transformations, and confirm that it has been conserved.

Freud's *Project* extends this program to the nervous system. Neurons accumulate quantities of excitation; they tend toward discharge; the system is thrown into unpleasure by excess accumulation and restored by discharge. The pleasure principle is a version of the second law. This is not a loose metaphor; Freud intended it as a literal scientific framework.

When the topographic model replaced the neural one and the metapsychological papers developed the mature theoretical apparatus, the vocabulary persisted. Libido is energy; cathexis is investment of energy; repression retains energy that then seeks discharge through symptoms. The terms of Helmholtzian physics are everywhere. But psychic energy has no physical correlate. There is no instrument that measures cathexis. Claims about the quantity of libidinal investment in a representation cannot be confirmed or disconfirmed by any observation the theory's own terms could specify.

Here is the diagnostic failure, stated precisely: the claims generated by the hydraulic framework, when translated into non-hydraulic terms, turn out to be either testable without the framework or untestable altogether. The claim that a repressed memory "retains its cathexis indefinitely" can be rephrased as "repressed memories remain active"-and that claim does not require hydraulic vocabulary to test. Or the hydraulic term "cathexis" names nothing except its own position in the model, in which case translation fails and the term is empty. In either case, the evidential obligations of the source domain-which were substantial, quantitative, and physical-did not make the crossing. What arrived was the grammar of physics, stripped of the physics.

## Three Provisional Conditions

The contrast between Darwin and Freud suggests a diagnostic formulated in three conditions. A borrowed argumentative framework carries genuine analytical weight when:

**Condition 1 - Mechanism, not terminology.** The borrowing imports a causal or inferential structure, not only terms that name one. Test: describe what the mechanism does without using vocabulary from the source domain. If this is possible, and if the resulting description captures something non-trivial about the target domain, the mechanism has been genuinely transposed. If the description either collapses into an empty paraphrase or produces a claim that requires no borrowed apparatus to make, the transfer was terminological.

**Condition 2 - Evidential inheritance.** The transposed mechanism generates predictions testable by means that are not available without the mechanism's specific conceptual commitments-and those means preserve the *character* of the source domain's evidential obligation, even if not its specific procedures. The test is not whether the target domain uses identical instruments; it is whether observations exist that would confirm or disconfirm the mechanism's application, and whether those observations are motivated by the mechanism rather than available independently of it. A borrowing that generates only claims testable without it, or only claims untestable by any means, has failed this condition.

**Condition 3 - Non-trivial constraint.** The borrowed framework rules out possibilities or generates questions that the target domain's existing apparatus could not have framed without it. If the target domain's existing machinery does all the inferential work after the vocabulary is imported, the borrowing added nothing. Productive transfer expands the space of questions that can be asked or the space of answers that can be ruled out.

## The Borderline Case: Rawls and Rational Choice Theory

*A Theory of Justice* (1971) deploys the apparatus of rational choice throughout its central argument. The device of the original position asks us to imagine rational agents choosing principles of justice from behind a veil of ignorance: they do not know their place in society, their class, their natural assets, or their conception of the good. The maximin strategy-choose the option that makes the worst outcome as good as possible-is advanced as the rational response to this uncertainty. The two principles of justice emerge from what such agents would choose.

Condition 1 is partially satisfied. The original-position mechanism can be described without decision-theoretic vocabulary: imagine choosing basic rules of social cooperation without knowing which position in the resulting society you will occupy. The mechanism survives translation and is not merely terminological. But Rawls does not import the mechanism wholesale; he modifies it substantially. He stipulates that the parties care about primary goods, that they experience the strains of commitment when principles prove hard to live with, that the gravity of the choice warrants special caution. None of these additions follow from game theory; they are philosophical additions to the formal apparatus.

Condition 2 is the telling case. Decision theory's evidential standard is expected utility calculation: the correct choice is the one that maximizes expected utility given probabilities over outcomes. Rawls explicitly declines this standard. Maximin is appropriate, he argues, not as a universal rule of rationality but because of the specific features of the original position: the gravity of the choice makes protecting against the worst outcome paramount; probabilities over life prospects cannot be meaningfully assigned; the parties' special psychology warrants special risk-aversion. The game-theoretic evidential machinery-calculate expected utilities, find the equilibrium-does not govern what counts as the right answer. Rawls substitutes reflective equilibrium: principles are validated by their coherence with considered judgments at multiple levels. The evidential obligations of rational choice theory do not travel.

Condition 3 is clearly satisfied. The veil-of-ignorance device, borrowed from rational choice theory's treatment of decisions under uncertainty, generates a specific constraint on political philosophy that was genuinely new: principles of justice should be those that no one would reject given ignorance of their social position. This rules out principles that favor particular groups by design and opens a precise question-what would rational agents actually choose?-that pre-Rawlsian political philosophy could not formulate with comparable exactness.

The verdict is neither productive transfer nor mere vocabulary. I call it *structured question-setting*: the game-theoretic apparatus is used to generate a well-defined choice situation whose output then requires philosophical argument the apparatus cannot supply. The borrowed framework earns its place not by delivering conclusions but by making a certain question precise enough to argue about. This is a legitimate and significant intellectual contribution-the original position is a genuine philosophical tool-but it is not the same as what Darwin did with Malthus, and readers who expect the apparatus to carry conclusions as well as questions will be disappointed. The conditions handle this case as a distinct region, not a failure.

## A Check: Foucault and Nietzsche

In his 1971 essay "Nietzsche, Genealogy, History," Foucault articulated what he took the Nietzschean genealogical method to mean: not a search for origins (*Ursprung*) as a timeless essence, but an analysis of descent (*Herkunft*) and emergence (*Entstehung*), which finds in apparently natural or necessary categories the history of contested power relations, contingent decisions, and forgotten violence. Applied in *Discipline and Punish* (1975), this method traces the birth of the modern prison not as humanitarian progress-the replacement of brutal punishment by rational rehabilitation-but as a transformation in the economy of power: from spectacular violence on the body to continuous surveillance, normalization, and the production of "docile bodies."

The mechanism transferred from Nietzsche is the genealogical exposure: take a category that presents as natural, necessary, or progressive; trace it historically; show the contingency, the violence, the power relations embedded in its emergence. This mechanism can be described without Nietzschean vocabulary: study the historical origins of apparently natural social categories to show the forces that produced them. Condition 1 is satisfied.

Evidential inheritance is the interesting case. Nietzsche's genealogy in *On the Genealogy of Morality* (1887) is primarily philosophical and psychological-tracing the emergence of guilt, conscience, and ascetic ideals through acts of interpretation and conceptual archaeology. The evidence is largely illustrative; there is no systematic archival method, no primary-source apparatus. Foucault transforms this: *Discipline and Punish* is grounded in historical archives, legal records, medical and prison documents, contemporary observation of institutions. The evidential character changes-from philosophical interpretation to historical empiricism.

The important point is that this transformation *strengthened* the evidential obligation rather than weakening it. Foucault became more accountable to evidence than his source. This is the opposite of what Freud did: Freud took a framework with rigorous physical evidential standards and deployed it where those standards had no application, losing the accountability. Foucault took a framework with looser philosophical evidential standards and gave it archival grounding, gaining accountability. Both cases involve transformation of the borrowed mechanism; only Foucault's transformation satisfies Condition 2.

Condition 3 is satisfied. The genealogical method gave historical analysis a new question it had not previously been equipped to ask: not what really happened, but how the categories through which we describe what happened were themselves produced, and by what operations of power. Discipline and Punish opens questions about surveillance, normalization, and the production of subjects that previous penal history, organized around the progressive-reform narrative, could not have formulated.

The Foucault case confirms the conditions and adds a qualification: Condition 2 can be satisfied by productive transformation of a mechanism, provided the transformation preserves or strengthens the character of evidential commitment. What the conditions rule out is not modification but the specific kind of modification that sheds epistemic accountability-which is exactly what Freud's borrowing does and Foucault's does not.

## Assessment

The three conditions discriminate the cases they were designed to analyze. Darwin satisfies all three; Freud fails Condition 2; Rawls occupies a distinct region where structured question-setting operates; Foucault satisfies all three through productive transformation. The conditions generate different verdicts and make those verdicts principled rather than impressionistic.

Do the conditions reduce to existing frameworks? The relationship is close but not identical. Hesse's positive analogy criterion asks what source and target share; the conditions here ask what *epistemic obligations* travel. Freud's hydraulic model satisfies positive analogy-the relational structure of energy dynamics maps onto the postulated relational structure of psychic processes-while failing Condition 2. Gentner's relational alignment is the structural version of Condition 1, but it does not address evidential inheritance or non-trivial constraint; perfect relational alignment is consistent with complete evidential evaporation. Bartha's articulation model requires structural correspondence and argument from the source to the target; the conditions here add the requirement that the relevant kind of correspondence is the preservation of evidential character, not only the preservation of formal structure. The existing frameworks are necessary conditions for productive transfer; these three are additional requirements.

Several limitations apply honestly. The cases are canonical and the selection is not random; the conditions were derived from four figures (Darwin, Freud, Rawls, Foucault) whose borrowings are exceptionally well-documented, and the conditions might not work as well on less well-attested material. The Foucault check helps but does not solve the selection problem. The conditions are also asymmetric: they identify failures more cleanly than they explain successes. A borrowing can satisfy all three conditions and still add nothing interesting to the target domain; the conditions are a necessary floor, not a predictor of intellectual fruit.

The Rawls case remains the one I handle least cleanly. "Structured question-setting" is a gesture toward a category the current essay does not fully develop. Whether there is a fourth condition, or a modified version of Condition 2, that handles question-setting uses of borrowed apparatus more precisely-that is work this essay opens rather than closes.

---

## Conclusion

The argument has been: what distinguishes a productive argumentative borrowing from a vocabulary borrowing is not primarily the structural correspondence between source and target domains, nor the prestige of the source framework, nor the care with which the transfer is narrated. It is whether the evidential obligations of the source domain travel with the mechanism.

Darwin's borrowing from Malthus was productive because the elimination mechanism came with epistemic accountability: claims about differential survival are testable by observations motivated by the mechanism. Freud's borrowing from Helmholtz was vocabulary because "psychic energy" arrived without the physics-without the measurement, the conservation law, the exchange rates that make energy claims accountable to evidence. The hydraulic vocabulary told a story without acquiring the obligation to tell it truly.

The three conditions-mechanism rather than terminology, evidential inheritance, and non-trivial constraint-provide a provisional diagnostic. They are not complete; the Rawls case shows that useful borrowings exist in a borderline region the conditions handle imperfectly. They are not infallible; the cases that generated them may not be representative. And they say nothing about the social and psychological reasons why vocabulary-only borrowings persist and propagate, which may be a more practically important question than the normative one the conditions address.

What the conditions offer is a way to put the question precisely. When a borrowed framework appears in a new domain, the first question to ask is not "does this structure correspond to that structure?" but "did the evidential obligations make the crossing?" If the answer is no, you have vocabulary. If the answer is yes-even through transformation-you may have something more.
