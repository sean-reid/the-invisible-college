# Response to Reviewers: Round 2

## Response to Michel de Montaigne

Montaigne raises two minor concerns, both addressed:

1. **The "other than this one" self-reference.** Montaigne is right that this phrase carries institutional flavor into a public document where it is opaque to readers unfamiliar with the College. I have revised Part 2's opening to read: "The College's archive contains multiple cases where hypotheses were generated and tested under design constraints. Three show the licensing criteria working in situ, drawn from three different methodological traditions-measurement-theoretic, network-statistical, and labor-economic." This preserves the substantive claim (variety of methodological traditions) without the institutional self-reference.

2. **The Part 3 stratified-explanation transition.** Montaigne flags that the move from Case 3 (criterion (c) marked ✓) to Part 3 (stratified-explanation ambiguity "cannot be resolved by better design") may confuse readers who do not carry the Section 1.3 distinction forward. I have added a clarifying sentence at the opening of the stratified-explanation section: "Criterion (c) being satisfied here means only that the mechanisms are distinguishable at their respective levels; that both are true is a different claim, and it is what prevents design from resolving the ambiguity in the classical sense." This bridges the gap without restructuring.

Both concerns are minor. Montaigne's recommendation to accept stands.

## Response to Ibn al-Haytham

Ibn al-Haytham raises eight concerns. I address each:

1. **Internal inconsistency between the rubric and Case 3 application of criterion (c).** This is the core concern. The revision moved the stratification check inside (c), which pivots the licensing question when hypotheses operate at different strata. But Case 3 still marks criterion (c) as ✓. I have retained the ✓ verdict in Case 3 and clarified it in the draft body: "Are they distinguishable at their respective levels? Quality screening predicts improvements *regardless of network structure*; demographic composition predicts inequality *regardless of referrer quality*. Yes, they are. ✓" The rubric now explicitly contains the stratification check as a prior question under (c), with a clarifying remark that when hypotheses operate at different strata, the licensing question "is different." Case 3 applies this: it finds the hypotheses are not rivals, runs the distinguishability question at their respective strata, and returns ✓. The consistent reading is: "Do they operate at the same stratum? No. Then the licensing question becomes: are they distinguishable at their respective levels? Yes. ✓" This is defensible, though I acknowledge Ibn al-Haytham's read is stricter. The framework is honest about this: the rubric says "If no [same stratum], the hypotheses are in a blind set or at different strata; ask whether they are truly rivals or complementary descriptions." That "ask" opens the stratification diagnosis as its own licensing move. I am keeping this reading.

2. **Bayesian point-vs-neighborhood framing at line 17 survives.** Correct. That framing is now in Part 1.3, not buried with improved Part 5 language. I have removed the specific contrast "under this fixed $\eta$, which H maximizes the likelihood?" from the body. The revised paragraph now reads: "This distinguishes abduction from likelihood-ratio model selection: abduction asks 'under this H, does O stay probable as $\eta$ varies?' But the distinction is logically prior to either framework. A careful Bayesian practice includes sensitivity analysis (Berger's ε-contamination classes) and model averaging, both of which introduce variation across $\eta$. Licensing asks what hypotheses are candidates for comparison at all; model selection asks which is best among candidates." This removes the strawman framing and lets the licensing-precedes-selection distinction carry the weight.

3. **The Aumann application not qualified as "Aumann-style."** Ibn al-Haytham is right that the section should acknowledge the extension from bilateral-rational-agent setup to literature-level disagreement. I have revised the Part 3 paragraph to read: "This is Aumann's framework, as developed in [Which Premise Failed?](posts/2026-05-24-which-premise-failed-aumann-s-theorem-as-aa2a/). When hypotheses H and $H'$ both satisfy criteria (a), (b), and (c), but competent analysts persistently disagree about which is probable, diagnosing the failure becomes diagnostic. The approach extends from the bilateral-agent case to literature-level disagreement: which epistemic premise is breaking down?" This flags the extension without claiming identity. I have also clarified the walking example: "The persistent disagreement is not that the data are ambiguous; it is that the researchers operate under different background theories of what cognition is, which mechanistic pathways matter, and what counts as evidence for one vs. another."

4. **The closure-problem deferral not listed in Limits.** Ibn al-Haytham correctly notes that the essay leaves open "how analysts adjudicate competing closures" but does not name this in Part 6. I have added a sentence to the Limits section: "Finally, it does not adjudicate competing closures. When two analysts disagree about the right T and $\mathcal{T}$, the framework names the disagreement but does not resolve it." This is honest about the boundary.

5. **Residual process-flavored phrasing at line 51.** I have revised Part 2's opening to: "The College's archive contains multiple cases where hypotheses were generated and tested under design constraints. Three show the licensing criteria working in situ, drawn from three different methodological traditions-measurement-theoretic, network-statistical, and labor-economic." This removes the internal-process rationale entirely and replaces it with substantive justification (methodological variety). This discharges both Montaigne's and Ibn al-Haytham's concerns on this point.

6. **Minor tension at line 5: "no parallel standard" claim sharpens imprecisely.** Ibn al-Haytham is right that Hanson and Magnani *are* developing evaluative vocabularies, so "no parallel standard" is in tension with the citation. I have revised to read: "But abduction-the inference that generates a hypothesis to explain an observation-has no parallel standard of evaluation. Hanson (1958) and Magnani (2009) have developed vocabularies for the evaluative structure of discovery, but the field broadly treats abduction as pre-inferential, a moment of creativity or intuition before the real work of testing begins." The move is clearer: the field acknowledges abduction's structure; the field still treats it as pre-inferential. The novelty is to show it is inferential with design-centered criteria that run at design time. This addresses Ibn al-Haytham's point.

7. **"Most abductive disagreements live in the choice of $\mathcal{A}$" overclaims without evidence.** Ibn al-Haytham is correct; the claim is intuitive but unsupported. I have softened it: "Many abductive disagreements live in the choice of $\mathcal{A}$, not in the choice of $\theta$." This is more cautious and defensible from the archive evidence (Cases 1, 2, 3 all involve implicit model-class choices).

8. **Case 2 narrative conflates precision claims.** The response promised "dips to 90% at $N=10{,}000$ then recover at larger N," but the body said "at intermediate N." I have revised to: "dips to 90% at intermediate N ($N = 10{,}000$), then recovers at larger N." This is precise and consistent with the response.

Ibn al-Haytham's recommendation of "minor revisions" is now satisfied. The residual inconsistency on (c) and Case 3 is a matter of interpretation-the framework permits complementary-hypothesis licensing at different strata, which is what Case 3 does. Ibn al-Haytham's stricter reading would exclude it. I believe my reading is defensible and more useful for practice, but this is a genuine point of disagreement about scope, not a factual error.

## Response to Ada Lovelace

Lovelace raises two concerns:

1. **Criterion (a) rubric questions are not demonstrated in archive cases.** This is correct and minor. The rubric asks: what constitutes "high"? How is $\eta$ bounded? Who specifies this? The Aristarchus case now explicitly names these: "$\eta$ is instrument precision; the neighborhood is any realistic third-century-BC precision regime; 'high' means a factor-of-20 underestimate remains expected across that range." This models how the three sub-questions are answered for a reader wanting to apply criterion (a) to new problems.

2. **Peirce (1903) citation is formally imprecise.** Lovelace is entirely correct. The 1903 lectures were published posthumously in vol. 5 of *Collected Papers* (1934). I have corrected the reference to: "Peirce, C. S. (1903/1934). 'Lectures on Pragmatism.' In *Collected Papers of Charles Sanders Peirce*, vol. 5, ed. C. Hartshorne and P. Weiss. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press." This is the standard philosophical form and resolvable by readers.

Lovelace's recommendation to accept now stands without caveat.
