# Response to Reviewers

Round 1 returned three signed reviews. All three identify the essay's central disaggregation move - separating the tradition into four non-identical claims - as genuine and worth preserving. No reviewer proposes reconsidering the central argument. The revision accordingly focuses on citation gaps, epistemological imprecision, and specific textual weaknesses identified across multiple reviews.

---

### Response to Ada Lovelace

**Concern 1: Frith & Loprinzi citation is dangling.**

Addressed. The citation has been moved into the body of "What the Science Shows," where it now serves as a qualification of the original finding's scope: the relationship between walking and ideation proved parameter-sensitive when exercise intensity and duration were varied. The reference entry has been completed with volume, issue, and page numbers. The finding is now integrated rather than noted parenthetically in the reference list.

**Concern 2: The 81% figure is under-characterized.**

Addressed. The revised draft specifies that this figure comes from Experiment 1 - a within-subject comparison in which participants completed the alternate uses task on two separate days, once seated and once walking a campus route - and that 81 percent of participants in the walking condition outperformed their own seated baseline. The revision makes clear this is not a figure representing all participants across all four experiments and all conditions.

**Concern 3: "Constitutively hard to examine" needs defense.**

Addressed. The revised draft replaces this phrase with language that distinguishes between two positions: claims "structured in ways that resist operational translation into any paradigm requiring behavioral measurement of controlled populations" versus claims that are "in principle inaccessible to empirical methods." The revision explicitly holds these apart and uses the embodied cognition claim as a test case: the essay now specifies what evidence might reach it (naturalistic composition studies, manuscript analysis of prosodic structure, longitudinal studies of writers) while noting these exceed what the current experimental paradigm was built to do. The claim is "methodologically demanding" rather than unfalsifiable.

**Concern 4: Wordsworth mileage figure deserves a note.**

Addressed. The revised draft notes that De Quincey himself presented the figure as approximate and that subsequent biographers have treated it with proportionate skepticism. The figure is retained as evidence of a distinctive pattern of practice - Wordsworth was a sustained, habitual walker - while the impression of biographical precision is removed.

**Concern 5: Embodied cognition section arrives too late.**

Partially addressed. I have added a forward reference at the end of "What the Tradition Claims" flagging that the embodied cognition program will be taken up after the four close readings. I have declined the fuller structural reorganization Ada proposes - introducing Lakoff, Johnson, and Noë at the start of the tradition section as the theoretical spine and testing the four writers against it. The reason: the essay's disaggregation is the contribution, and it depends on reading each writer on their own terms before asking what a unifying framework would look like. If the embodied cognition framework arrives first, the four writers risk being organized into its slots rather than read in their own registers. The forward reference addresses the surprise-ending problem without sacrificing the discover-through-disaggregation logic.

**Concern 6: Convergent thinking asymmetry is underdeveloped.**

Addressed. A new paragraph in "Where the Match Holds and Where It Breaks" raises the question directly: whether the divergent boost might come at a cost to convergent performance. The revision notes that the study's design cannot answer this - walking and sitting conditions were tested separately in time, not simultaneously - but that the question matters precisely because the tradition's most ambitious claims concern quality of judgment, not quantity of output.

### Response to Henri Poincaré

**Concern 1: Hippocampal dual-role speculation relies on a wrong citation.**

Addressed. Poincaré is right that Andrews-Hanna et al. (2014) is about the default mode network and self-generated thought, not hippocampal dual-role in spatial navigation and episodic memory. The citation was a genuine error. The Andrews-Hanna citation has been removed entirely. The hippocampal claim is now anchored to O'Keefe and Nadel (1978), *The Hippocampus as a Cognitive Map*, the foundational text for the spatial-episodic overlap. The paragraph has been trimmed to remove the mechanism claim I cannot support, retaining only the structural observation that these systems overlap - and explicitly flagging the connection as a structural possibility, not a demonstrated mechanism.

Poincaré also notes, correctly, that the hippocampus is active across a vast range of conditions, including sitting still and mentally navigating imagined spaces - which undercuts the "walking specifically activates this" version of the argument. The revised paragraph does not make that specific activation claim. It offers the dual-role as a structural basis for speculation and no more.

**Concern 2: Wordsworth dog detail needs a source.**

Addressed. The dog detail has been removed. I cannot source it precisely to a specific entry in Dorothy's journals or a specific page in De Quincey's *Recollections*, and the detail reads - as Poincaré correctly suspects - as circulating folk-Wordsworth rather than documented biographical fact. The claims that remain - that Wordsworth composed aloud while walking, paced particular routes, and used physical rhythm to structure prosodic rhythm - are retained as grounded in De Quincey's account. The mileage figure is now flagged as De Quincey's rough estimate, not a precise measurement.

**Concern 3: Frith & Loprinzi is dead weight in its current form.**

Addressed. See response to Ada, Concern 1.

**Concern 4: The essay does not commit on the testability question.**

Addressed. See response to Ada, Concern 3. The revised draft now explicitly distinguishes "structured in ways that resist operational translation" from "in principle inaccessible," specifies what evidence might reach the embodied cognition claim, and uses the word "methodologically demanding" rather than any locution that implies unfalsifiability.

**Concern 5: The essay charitably reconstructs Nietzsche but does not interrogate him.**

Addressed. A new paragraph has been added to the Nietzsche section acknowledging the lyric-aphoristic mode of "Maxims and Arrows" and arguing that this mode is itself part of the essay's finding. The vindication narrative has mistaken a provocation - a rhetorical extreme meant to target sedentary academic philosophy - for a testable hypothesis. The paragraph argues that hyperbole in service of a real position is not empty of content: what Nietzsche is claiming about the disembodied character of desk-thought has genuine philosophical direction even if its aphoristic formulation is not itself a falsifiable proposition. The lyric register, rather than disqualifying the claim, sharpens the gap the essay is tracking.

**Concern 6: Rousseau's introspective access claim deserves more skepticism.**

Addressed. A paragraph has been added to the Rousseau section noting that the *Reveries* are a late work written under conditions of acute persecutory ideation, and distinguishing "Rousseau felt he had honest access to his own states" from "Rousseau had honest access to his own states." The text gives us the former; the latter is a much harder claim. The revised essay acknowledges that the experience of authentic self-encounter during walking is "genuine enough and consistent enough across the *Reveries* to constitute evidence of something" without asserting that the phenomenological report is a reliable description of what the walks produced.

**Concern 7: The conclusion repeats the taxonomy.**

Addressed. The conclusion has been revised to remove the second listing of the four writers and their claims. The revised conclusion refers to "the tradition's most demanding claims" collectively and uses the closing space to note what methods might reach them, rather than restating what the essay has already shown. The final movement is now forward-looking: what the current paradigm has not tried, and why that matters.

**Concern 8: The 81% figure is from one experiment.**

Addressed. See response to Ada, Concern 2.

**Concern 9: "She or he" in the Wordsworth paragraph.**

Addressed. Changed to "he."

### Response to Pierre Bayle

**Concern 1: The opening Flaubert quotation requires verification and in-text citation.**

Addressed, with explanation. The Flaubert quotation - "*On ne peut penser et écrire qu'assis (G. Flaubert)*" - appears at the head of aphorism 34 of *Twilight of the Idols*, where Nietzsche quotes and attributes it directly to "G. Flaubert." The citation chain is therefore: the quotation appears in Nietzsche's text, which the essay already cites. No independent Flaubert reference is required because the essay's source for the quotation is the Nietzsche aphorism, not Flaubert's letters or other primary texts. The revised draft makes this explicit: "he opens by quoting Flaubert directly - the French '*On ne peut penser et écrire qu'assis (G. Flaubert)*' appears at the head of the aphorism."

**Concern 2: Diogenes Laertius and Hermippus claims need sources.**

Addressed. The revised draft cites Diogenes Laertius, *Lives of the Eminent Philosophers*, Book V, section 2 - the life of Aristotle - and notes that the Hermippus attribution is transmitted through that same text. The Hicks translation (Harvard University Press / Loeb Classical Library) is added to the reference list.

**Concern 3: Specific Dorothy Wordsworth details require sources.**

Addressed in part. The dog detail has been removed (see response to Poincaré, Concern 2). The remaining claims about ambulatory composition and prosodic rhythm are retained as supported by De Quincey's *Recollections* and its references to Dorothy's journals. I acknowledge that page-level citations are not provided in the current draft and that a fully footnoted version of the essay would require more specific anchors; this draft identifies De Quincey as the source and treats the mileage figure as his rough estimate rather than a precise measurement.

**Concern 4: Arne Dietrich cited without specification.**

Addressed. The draft now cites Dietrich, A. (2004), "The cognitive neuroscience of creativity," *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 11(6), 1011–1026, which is the paper in which the critique of the alternate uses task as a measure of creativity appears. The reference has been added to the reference list.

**Concern 5: Andrews-Hanna citation may be mismatched.**

Addressed. Andrews-Hanna et al. (2014) has been removed. The hippocampal dual-role claim is now anchored to O'Keefe and Nadel (1978). See response to Poincaré, Concern 1.

**Concern 6: Frith and Loprinzi citation is incomplete.**

Addressed. Volume, issue, and page numbers have been added. The finding has been integrated substantively into the text. See response to Ada, Concern 1.

**Concern 7: Solnit's Wanderlust is in references but never cited.**

Addressed. Solnit has been removed from the reference list. The book informed the background reading for this essay but does not appear in the argument, and citing background reading as if it were in-text support is the kind of signal-dilution the essay's own standards forbid.

**Concern 8: "Constitutively hard to examine" needs more precision.**

Addressed. See response to Ada, Concern 3.

**Concern 9: The embodied cognition section leaves falsifiability open.**

Addressed. The revised draft specifies what evidence might reach the embodied cognition claim: naturalistic studies of composition in motion combining phenomenological report with behavioral and physiological measures; longitudinal studies of writers whose work shows structural signatures of ambulatory practice; manuscript analysis tracking prosodic or syntactic changes across ambulatory and non-ambulatory drafting periods. The section now closes with: "The claim is not unfalsifiable; it is methodologically demanding in ways the current experimental paradigm was not built to meet."

**A note on Bayle's "major" recommendation.**

Bayle recommends major revision, primarily on grounds of citation incompleteness and unanswered epistemological questions. The revisions above address each of these concerns substantively. The central argument has not changed direction, the essay's length and structure are similar, and no section has been reconceived from the ground up. Whether these repairs constitute "major" revision depends on whether citation gaps and epistemological hedging that can be resolved within the existing structure constitute major problems in themselves. I believe they do not - the argument was structurally sound; the execution was imprecise in specific and fixable ways - and I have fixed them.
