# The Attempt as Evidence: On What the Essay-Form Discloses That Argument Cannot

When Montaigne published his first two volumes in 1580, he gave them a title that most readers took as modesty: *Essais* - attempts, trials, testings-out. The modesty reading has persisted. We speak of the essay as tentative, exploratory, personal, and we contrast it with the argument, the treatise, the demonstration. That contrast carries a hidden demotion. If the essay is what you write before you know what you think, then essayistic form is preparatory rather than evidential: a staging area for real inquiry, not inquiry itself.

That demotion is what this piece contests. The essay-form carries epistemic weight the argument-form cannot replicate - not in all cases, and not by virtue of being tentative, but under specific conditions that can be stated and tested. Knowing those conditions is what allows us to distinguish an essay doing genuine epistemic work from one borrowing the form's authority without earning it.

---

## I. What This Piece Is Not

Two prior investigations in this archive have pursued related questions. [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) asked when examples *within* essays carry genuine evidential weight: when is an example constraining a conclusion, when is it illustrating a conclusion already reached, and when is it loading the reader's intuitions in a way that mimics evidence without providing it? The unit of analysis was the example - an element inside the form.

[*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) asked when an argumentative framework carries its conclusions across domains: when do the evidential obligations travel with the vocabulary, and when does the vocabulary detach and move alone? The unit of analysis was the framework in transit - a conceptual structure crossing a disciplinary boundary.

This piece asks the prior question. Before we can specify when an example does evidential work, we need to know what kind of inquiry can support that work. Before we can specify when a framework carries its obligations across domains, we need to know when the *form* of inquiry - not what it contains, not where it travels - is itself doing something that argument cannot replicate. The question is about the shape of the cognitive act, not its elements or its portability.

The relationship to the prior two pieces is foundational rather than additive. Both presupposed that some forms of inquiry are capable of carrying genuine evidential weight beyond what can be extracted and paraphrased. This piece attempts to specify when that presupposition is warranted - and to do so in a form that demonstrates rather than merely claims the answer.

## II. Montaigne on Cannibals: When the Subject Resists Closure

In "On Cannibals" (*Essais* I.31), Montaigne deploys a structure easier to observe than to describe. He begins with a report from the New World, filtered through a servant of his who had lived there for ten or twelve years - a man, Montaigne notes, too plain and stubborn to have invented anything. From Brazil: a nation whose people eat their enemies after battle, roast them, send pieces to absent friends, and issue the arm and thigh to enemies as challenge. From this report, Montaigne begins to evaluate.

The evaluation takes a characteristic form. First, a judgment is assembled - the cannibals are indeed barbarous - and then it is not refuted but undermined. The undermining is not a negation but a destabilization of the framework from which the judgment was made: "I think there is more barbarity in eating a man alive than in eating him dead." The comparison now runs the other direction. European judicial torture, religious warfare, the treatment of the poor - these are more barbarous by the very criteria applied to the cannibals.

If Montaigne were writing an argument, this is where the argument would end. He has made his case for cultural relativism, or for a more stringent application of the category "barbarous" to European practice. The conclusion is reachable by argument. What argument cannot do is what Montaigne does next.

He introduces Brazilian visitors who had been brought to Rouen to see the French court - Charles IX on the throne, a child in 1562. They were asked, through a bad interpreter and worse memory, what they found most admirable in France. They gave two answers, of which Montaigne says he can recall only one. What he remembers: they found it strange that so many tall armed men would obey a child, and that the poor men in the city did not take those gorged ones by the throat and set fire to their houses.

The effect of this passage is not to add further evidence for cultural relativism. It is to dismantle the comparison-framework itself. The Brazilian visitors are not applying the European framework to European practices and finding them wanting; they are applying a framework from which European hierarchy and poverty appear as strange as Brazilian cannibalism appears to Europeans. This means that no stable standpoint exists from which to adjudicate - not European, not Brazilian - because both standpoints are frameworks that the other can make strange.

An argument for cultural relativism would assert this conclusion. What Montaigne's essay does is *perform* the impossibility of stable judgment: the essay cannot settle into any position, because each position is vacated before it is occupied, and the reader does not merely learn that no stable standpoint exists but experiences the instability of their own standpoint as the essay moves through it. The form is the evidence. An argument extracted from the essay's conclusion would preserve the content while losing the demonstration that the framework of evaluation is itself what is at stake.

This is the first condition. The essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate when the subject constitutively resists closure - when the phenomenon being examined is the instability of the evaluative frameworks that would be required to examine it. An argument asserting this instability stands outside what it describes. An essay performing this instability provides evidence for it.

## III. Baldwin on Racial Violence: When Evidence Is Phenomenological

James Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son" (in *Notes of a Native Son*, Beacon Press, 1955) is structured around a coincidence: his father died the day his last child was born, and the day of the funeral coincided with the Harlem riot of 1943. The essay moves between these facts and a third: that three days before his father's death, Baldwin had nearly been charged with a crime in a New Jersey diner, when - in a state of murderous rage that had been building for six months of being refused service in restaurants and movie houses across New Jersey - he threw a mug of water at a waitress who had said, again, "We don't serve Negroes here." He ran before anyone could stop him.

The incident is the evidential core of the essay. Stated as a report, it sounds like anecdote: a young man lost his temper and narrowly avoided arrest. What makes it evidence is the claim that depends on it. Baldwin is not claiming that he once threw a mug at a waitress. He is claiming something about the psychological structure of the damage that systematic racial dehumanization inflicts on its objects - specifically, that the damage takes the form of a murderous hatred directed as much at the self as at the oppressor, and that this hatred is not chosen but is the psychologically necessary response to systematic erasure. His father's bitterness, the riots, his own near-crime: these are instances of the same structure.

That claim is general. The incident is evidence for it because the claim is phenomenological. What Baldwin needs to establish is not the *frequency* of racial violence, nor the sociological mechanisms that produce it, but its *internal structure as an experience*. What does it feel like to be at the edge of murder in a racialized encounter? What is the phenomenological signature of the moment when rage overwhelms self-protective calculus? No aggregate data answers this. No frequency distribution, no regression, no sociological mechanism provides access to the inside of that experience. Baldwin's first-person account does, because his first-person perspective is the only available access to the phenomenon in question.

This is what distinguishes the first-person case from anecdote. An anecdote illustrates a general claim that exists independently of the anecdote; a different illustration would serve equally well. Baldwin's diner incident is not interchangeable. The general claim - about the phenomenological structure of racial self-destruction - requires testimony from inside that structure. No third-person observer at the New Jersey diner could have established that claim. The first-person is not rhetoric here; it is the access condition.

Baldwin's essay also demonstrates what is lost when the phenomenological claim is extracted and stated abstractly. The essay's concluding movement paraphrases, roughly, as follows: one would have to hold two opposing ideas simultaneously - total acceptance of life and men as they are, alongside total refusal ever to accept injustice as commonplace. Stated abstractly, this is useful. But it is stated abstractly only after the reader has been inside the diner, has felt the six months of accumulated refusals, has watched Baldwin run through dark streets to his friend's house. The abstract conclusion is earned by the evidential case that precedes it. Without the case, the conclusion is something the reader can believe without having been shown. With the case, the reader has been shown the phenomenological structure from which the conclusion is the only rational exit.

This is the second condition. The essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate when the evidence is phenomenological - when the general claim is about an experiential structure accessible only from the inside, and when the first-person case provides access that no other form can provide.

## IV. Wittgenstein's Attempts: When Inquiry Is Evidence

*Philosophical Investigations* (1953, Blackwell) was left in typescript when Wittgenstein died, the product of nearly two decades of work. The published text is divided into numbered sections, most of them short, none of them continuous with the next in the way that chapters in an argument are continuous. Each section makes an attempt, encounters a difficulty, and trails off. The next section makes a different attempt from a slightly different angle.

This structure is universally observed. What is less often specified is why it is epistemically required rather than merely unconventional.

Wittgenstein's subject in the early sections is the phenomenon of meaning: what it is to understand a word, to follow a rule, to mean something by what one says. He opens by quoting Augustine's picture of how children learn language - every word has a meaning correlated with the word, an object for which the word stands - and then spends the first dozen sections showing what that picture cannot account for. The tools in a toolbox, he observes in §11, are as diverse in function as the words in a language: hammer, pliers, saw, screwdriver, rule, glue-pot, glue, nails, screws. The Augustinian picture fits some of them and fails the rest.

The difficulty is structural, not merely evidential. Any formulation of what language is - any attempt to say what understanding consists of or what it means to follow a rule - relies on a framework of concepts (meaning, understanding, following, rule) that is exactly what is under examination. A continuous argument about the nature of meaning would need to assume a working account of meaning in order to proceed. The attempt-and-failure structure of the *Investigations* is the form that remains when this difficulty is taken seriously: each attempt approaches from a different angle, each trails off when it encounters the framework-dependence problem, each is followed by another attempt.

The rule-following discussion makes the structure explicit. "This was our paradox," Wittgenstein writes in §201: "no course of action could be determined by a rule, because every course of action can be made out to accord with the rule." This is stated as a paradox rather than a conclusion because the formulation is itself suspect - it employs the concept "determined by a rule" in a statement that is supposed to show the concept cannot be unpacked. The numbered fragment is the form that expresses this predicament: it says something, acknowledges what it cannot say, and stops. The next section begins again.

An argument about rule-following could assert the paradox. What the *Investigations* does is demonstrate it by performing the failure to escape it. Each new attempt to specify what understanding consists of runs into the framework-dependence problem again. The trail of attempts is not a record of failing to find the right argument; it is evidence that the subject has a structure that resists the systematic grasp that continuous argument assumes it can achieve. The form discloses something the content could only claim: that this is the kind of subject on which continuous argument keeps breaking.

This is the third condition. The essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate when the movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the subject - when the failure to settle into any position, the return to the same difficulty from different angles, discloses that the subject resists the systematic grasp that argument assumes. Wittgenstein could have extracted the rule-following paradox and stated it as a theorem. He could not have preserved, in that statement, the evidence that the paradox is not local but is embedded in the structure of the subject itself.

## V. When the Form Is Imposture

Against these three cases, a type should be identified - a recognizable mode of writing that borrows the essay's formal properties without satisfying any of the conditions just derived.

The type proceeds as follows. A writer takes a general claim - social-scientific, historical, cultural - and supports it with a personal anecdote. The anecdote is genuine; the first-person voice is genuine; the prose moves between the general and the particular in the way that essay prose does. Tentative phrasing is present. Digression is present. The essay resists too-clean conclusions. The reader has the experience of following a mind working through a problem.

What has not happened: the subject does not constitutively resist closure - it is a claim that aggregate data could settle, and the essay's tentativeness is not performing the instability of the evaluative framework but mimicking the social register of open-mindedness. The evidence is not phenomenological - the first-person anecdote is providing a vivid illustration of a claim that exists independently of the anecdote, and a different illustration would serve equally well. The movement of inquiry is not itself evidence about anything - the conclusion was determined before the anecdote was chosen, and the essay's discursive shape traces a path to a predetermined destination rather than disclosing the structure of a recalcitrant subject.

The essayistic form is ornament here. The same claim, stated as an argument with the social-scientific evidence marshaled directly, would be neither stronger nor weaker epistemically. The essay has added texture - warmth, specificity, voice - but has not added evidence that could not be paraphrased away. Adorno's account of the essay, which treats its resistance to system as always epistemically valuable, cannot distinguish this type from the three genuine cases. My account can: if none of the three conditions is met, the essay is not doing something that argument cannot do. Whatever the writing is doing, it is not doing it by virtue of being an essay.

This type is not rare. It is, in fact, the dominant mode of what passes for the personal essay in popular literary culture. Writers deploy the formal markers - first person, tentative phrasing, digression, anecdote, resistance to too-clean conclusions - while conducting what is, epistemically, an illustrated argument. The form generates an appearance of inquiry that the epistemology does not support.

## VI. The Typology and Its Limits

Three conditions, then:

**(1) The subject constitutively resists closure.** When the phenomenon being examined is the instability of the evaluative frameworks required to examine it - when stable adjudication is exactly what is at stake - then the essay-form can perform what argument can only assert. Montaigne's cannibals.

**(2) The evidence is phenomenological and requires first-person access.** When the general claim is about an experiential structure accessible only from the inside, the first-person case is not anecdote but evidence of the only available kind. Baldwin's diner.

**(3) The movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the subject.** When the failure to settle, the return to the same difficulty from different angles, discloses that the subject resists the systematic grasp that continuous argument assumes - then the trail of attempts is evidence that no concluded argument could provide. Wittgenstein's fragments.

Three predictions follow that go beyond the cases.

First, any essay deploying essayistic form without satisfying at least one condition is doing work argument could replicate equally well. This is a testable claim about what would be lost if the form were converted to argument. Where nothing is lost, the form is ornamental.

Second, the conditions can be satisfied simultaneously. An essay may have a phenomenologically accessible subject that also constitutively resists closure; the movement of inquiry may disclose something about phenomenological structure that is also evidence of framework instability. The conditions are not exclusive of each other, and the strongest essays will likely satisfy more than one. This predicts that the most epistemically distinctive essays are not simply personal, or simply tentative, or simply about resistant subjects, but essays in which the conditions reinforce each other.

Third, the conditions are not jointly exhaustive. I cannot exclude that there are cases of genuine epistemic weight that do not fall under any of the three. The typology is provisional.

One limit I can identify: the conditions do not yet sharply distinguish the essay from autobiography and from phenomenological description, two adjacent genres that can also satisfy conditions (1) and (2). Autobiography is the form in which a self is disclosed through the record of experience; phenomenological description is the form in which experience is characterized in structure. Both can be about subjects that resist closure; both can provide first-person evidential access. What the essay specifically requires - what makes condition (3) the essay's proprietary contribution - is that the movement of inquiry itself must be visible and unfinished. The essay includes the attempt as part of its form. Autobiography does not require that the inquiry be visible; it requires that the self be revealed. Phenomenological description does not require that the inquiry be unfinished; it requires that experience be characterized. The essay requires both.

Even this distinction is not yet sharp. Plato's dialogues include visible, unfinished inquiry. Some of Husserl's descriptive work includes the movement of attempts. Whether these count as essays - whether condition (3) is sufficient to mark something as essayistic - is an open question. I mark it as one rather than closing it.

## VII. The Self-Reflexive Dimension

A piece that argues for the epistemic weight of the essay-form while writing as if in the argument-form would be self-refuting. The requirement the proposal set for itself - that this essay *demonstrate* rather than merely claim the conditions - demands that the piece be tested against its own typology.

The question "under what conditions does the essay-form carry epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate?" satisfies, I believe, all three conditions.

It satisfies condition (1): the subject resists closure. Any systematic account of when systematic inquiry fails is tested by its own systematicity. An argument specifying the conditions under which argument fails can always be asked whether it itself fails those conditions. This essay has occupied and partially vacated several positions: the claim that the typology is not circular, the claim that it distinguishes the essay from autobiography and phenomenological description, the claim that condition (3) is specific to the essay rather than to a broader class. None of these is fully settled.

It satisfies condition (2): there is a phenomenological dimension. The claim that certain essays carry evidential weight by virtue of first-person access requires some account of what that access consists of - and that account cannot be fully specified without attending to what it is like to read and write in different forms. This essay has gestured at that experience without fully establishing it. The gesture is honest rather than adequate.

It satisfies condition (3): the movement of inquiry is itself evidence. Whether it is evidence of the right thing - whether the essay keeps escaping systematic grip because the subject has that structure, or because I have not yet found the right grip - I cannot entirely say from inside the inquiry. Montaigne's move, in "On Cannibals," is to let the reader experience the instability of their evaluative standpoint. This essay has not done that as vividly. The failure is recorded rather than concealed.

---

## Conclusion

The essay-form is not a comfort or a style. It is an epistemic vehicle with specifiable operating conditions. Knowing those conditions allows a reader to distinguish an essay doing work only the essay-form can do from an essay dressed in essayistic clothing while doing work argument would do more economically.

Montaigne named his form well. The *essai* - the attempt - is not preliminary to the result. Under the conditions specified here, the attempt *is* the result. When the subject resists closure, the sustained inhabiting and vacating of unstable positions is the only form of inquiry that does not lie about the subject's structure. When the evidence is phenomenological, the first-person case is not anecdote but testimony to which there is no third-person equivalent. When the movement of inquiry discloses the resistance of the subject to systematic grasp, the trail of attempts is evidence that no concluded argument could provide.

These conditions are provisional, open to cases that disconfirm them, and bounded by a limit I have named but not resolved: the distinction of the essay from autobiography and phenomenological description when all three conditions are simultaneously met. The essay-form's specific epistemic contribution is condition (3) - the visible, unfinished movement of inquiry as evidence - and that contribution is not yet sharply distinguished from inquiry that is visible and unfinished in other forms.

An essay about essays is the appropriate form for a conclusion that cannot be delivered with the confidence of a theorem. Whether this essay has satisfied its own conditions - whether it has demonstrated rather than merely claimed the account - is for the reader to determine. That uncertainty is not evasion. It is what the attempt looks like from the inside.
