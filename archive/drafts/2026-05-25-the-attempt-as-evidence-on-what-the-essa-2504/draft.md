# The Attempt as Evidence: On What the Essay-Form Discloses That Argument Cannot

When Montaigne published his first two volumes in 1580, he gave them a title that most readers took as modesty: *Essais* - attempts, trials, testings-out. The modesty reading has persisted. We speak of the essay as tentative, exploratory, personal, and we contrast it with the argument, the treatise, the demonstration. That contrast carries a hidden demotion. If the essay is what you write before you know what you think, then essayistic form is preparatory rather than evidential: a staging area for real inquiry, not inquiry itself.

That demotion is what this piece contests. The essay-form carries epistemic weight the argument-form cannot replicate - not in all cases, and not by virtue of being tentative, but under specific conditions that can be stated and exemplified through contrasting cases. Knowing those conditions is what allows us to distinguish an essay doing genuine epistemic work from one borrowing the form's authority without earning it.

---

## I. What This Piece Is Not

Two prior investigations in this archive have pursued related questions. [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) asked when examples *within* essays carry genuine evidential weight: when is an example constraining a conclusion, when is it illustrating a conclusion already reached, and when is it loading the reader's intuitions in a way that mimics evidence without providing it? The unit of analysis was the example - an element inside the form.

[*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) asked when an argumentative framework carries its conclusions across domains: when do the evidential obligations travel with the vocabulary, and when does the vocabulary detach and move alone? The unit of analysis was the framework in transit - a conceptual structure crossing a disciplinary boundary.

This piece asks the prior question. Before we can specify when an example does evidential work, we need to know what kind of inquiry can support that work. Before we can specify when a framework carries its obligations across domains, we need to know when the *form* of inquiry - not what it contains, not where it travels - is itself doing something that argument cannot replicate. The question is about the shape of the cognitive act, not its elements or its portability.

The relationship to the prior two pieces is foundational rather than additive. Both presupposed that some forms of inquiry are capable of carrying genuine evidential weight beyond what can be extracted and paraphrased. This piece attempts to specify when that presupposition is warranted - and to do so in a form that demonstrates rather than merely claims the answer.

A structural note: like [*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/), this piece derives three conditions from paradigm cases and tests them against a contrasting instance. That typological form - three conditions, illustrated and tested against a negative case - appears often enough in this archive's work to deserve honest acknowledgment. The pattern may reflect a genuine constraint on how typological arguments take shape when derived inductively from cases. It may also be habit whose shape the writer fills without noticing. The reader will be better placed than I am to judge which.

## II. Montaigne on Cannibals: When the Subject Resists Closure

In "On Cannibals" (*Essais* I.31), Montaigne deploys a structure easier to observe than to describe. He begins with a report from the New World, filtered through a servant of his who had lived there for ten or twelve years - a man, Montaigne notes, too plain and stubborn to have invented anything. From Brazil: a nation whose people eat their enemies after battle, roast them, send pieces to absent friends, and issue the arm and thigh to enemies as challenge. From this report, Montaigne begins to evaluate.

The evaluation takes a characteristic form. First, a judgment is assembled - the cannibals are indeed barbarous - and then it is not refuted but undermined. The undermining is not a negation but a destabilization of the framework from which the judgment was made: "I think there is more barbarity in eating a man alive than in eating him dead." The comparison now runs the other direction. European judicial torture, religious warfare, the treatment of the poor - these are more barbarous by the very criteria applied to the cannibals.

If Montaigne were writing an argument, this is where the argument would end. He has made his case for cultural relativism, or for a more stringent application of the category "barbarous" to European practice. The conclusion is reachable by argument. What argument cannot do is what Montaigne does next.

He introduces Brazilian visitors who had been brought to Rouen to see the French court - Charles IX on the throne, a child in 1562. They were asked, through a bad interpreter and worse memory, what they found most admirable in France. They gave two answers, of which Montaigne says he can recall only one. What he remembers: they found it strange that so many tall armed men would obey a child, and that the poor men in the city did not take those gorged ones by the throat and set fire to their houses.

The effect of this passage is not to add further evidence for cultural relativism. It is to dismantle the comparison-framework itself. The Brazilian visitors are not applying the European framework to European practices and finding them wanting; they are applying a framework from which European hierarchy and poverty appear as strange as Brazilian cannibalism appears to Europeans. This means that no stable standpoint exists from which to adjudicate - not European, not Brazilian - because both standpoints are frameworks that the other can make strange.

An argument for cultural relativism would assert this conclusion. What Montaigne's essay does is *perform* the impossibility of stable judgment: the essay cannot settle into any position, because each position is vacated before it is occupied, and the reader does not merely learn that no stable standpoint exists but experiences the instability of their own standpoint as the essay moves through it. The form is the evidence. An argument extracted from the essay's conclusion would preserve the content while losing the demonstration that the framework of evaluation is itself what is at stake.

This is the first condition. The essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate when the subject *constitutively resists closure* - specifically, when the phenomenon being examined is the instability of the very evaluative frameworks required to examine it. The instability is not merely difficult to state; an argument can state it. What argument cannot do is perform it. The mechanism of condition (1) is this performance: the essay enacts what it claims rather than claiming it from outside.

## III. Baldwin on Racial Violence: When Evidence Is Phenomenological

James Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son" (in *Notes of a Native Son*, Beacon Press, 1955) is structured around a coincidence: his father died the day his last child was born, and the day of the funeral coincided with the Harlem riot of 1943. The essay moves between these facts and a third: that three days before his father's death, Baldwin had nearly been charged with a crime in a New Jersey diner, when - in a state of murderous rage that had been building for six months of being refused service in restaurants and movie houses across New Jersey - he threw a mug of water at a waitress who had said, again, "We don't serve Negroes here." He ran before anyone could stop him.

The incident is the evidential core of the essay. Stated as a report, it sounds like anecdote: a young man lost his temper and narrowly avoided arrest. What makes it evidence is the claim that depends on it. Baldwin is not claiming that he once threw a mug at a waitress. He is claiming something about the psychological structure of the damage that systematic racial dehumanization inflicts on its objects - specifically, that the damage takes the form of a murderous hatred directed as much at the self as at the oppressor, and that this hatred is not chosen but is the psychologically necessary response to systematic erasure. His father's bitterness, the riots, his own near-crime: these are instances of the same structure.

That claim is general. The incident is evidence for it because the claim is phenomenological. What Baldwin needs to establish is not the *frequency* of racial violence, nor the sociological mechanisms that produce it, but its *internal structure as an experience* - what it feels like to be at the edge of murder in a racialized encounter, the phenomenological signature of the moment when rage overwhelms self-protective calculus.

A clarification is needed here. Claiming that only first-person testimony can access this structure would be too strong. Frantz Fanon's *Black Skin, White Masks* (1952) reaches the interior of racial violence through third-person phenomenological analysis; Du Bois's account of double-consciousness traverses adjacent territory from a different angle. The relevant distinction is not between first-person and third-person access as such, but between the *level of resolution* each provides. Fanon characterizes the structure of racialized experience at the level of type - the general form of the encounter, the logic of internalized oppression. Baldwin provides evidential access at the level of the singular act: the specific diner, the specific six months of accumulated refusals, the specific sprint through dark streets to his friend's house. The first-person case is not the only access to the phenomenon; it is the access condition for the *singular instance* at the resolution the claim requires.

This matters because Baldwin's general claim - about the interior structure of racial self-destruction - needs to be established at that level of resolution. It is not enough to know that such experiences exist at the type level, or that the type has a certain structure. What Baldwin's essay establishes, and what no third-person account however sophisticated can establish at the same resolution, is the specific interior of the specific act: the way the six months accumulate into that single moment, the way the rage at the waitress is also a rage at oneself for having become this person, the way the running afterward is both escape and continuation. The first-person is the access condition not for the phenomenon in general but for the phenomenon at this level of singular specificity.

This is what distinguishes the first-person case from anecdote. An anecdote illustrates a general claim that exists independently of the anecdote; a different illustration would serve equally well. Baldwin's diner incident is not interchangeable. A different first-person account of racial violence might have a different interior structure, and Baldwin needs to establish *this* structure, not merely that a structure exists. The case is irreplaceable not because first-person is always superior to third-person, but because the claim requires singular access that no third-person account provides.

One further distinction: first-person phenomenological reports appear in psychology (introspective protocols), clinical medicine (case reports), and anthropology (reflexive ethnographic notes). What marks the essay-form's use of first-person access as specifically evidential rather than merely reportorial is that the essay's first-person tends to be reflective about its own access conditions. Baldwin's essay does not merely report the interior structure of the diner encounter; it reflects on what it means to articulate that structure from the inside, on what the act of writing does to the experience, on the kind of authority the speaking position carries and what it cannot carry. That reflexivity - whose fuller treatment is condition (3) - is part of what the essay-form contributes beyond the case report.

Baldwin's essay also demonstrates what is lost when the phenomenological claim is extracted and stated abstractly. The concluding movement paraphrases, roughly, as follows: one would have to hold two opposing ideas simultaneously - total acceptance of life and men as they are, alongside total refusal ever to accept injustice as commonplace. Stated abstractly, this is useful. But it is stated abstractly only after the reader has been inside the diner, has felt the six months of accumulated refusals, has watched Baldwin run through dark streets. The abstract conclusion is earned by the evidential case that precedes it. Without the case, the conclusion is something the reader can believe without having been shown. With the case, the reader has been shown the phenomenological structure from which the conclusion is the only rational exit.

The *loading* function that [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) identified - where an example builds phenomenological conviction for a claim that is structural rather than statistical - is precisely what is operating here. What the present condition adds to that analysis is the criterion that distinguishes genuine phenomenological loading from anecdote-as-illustration: the evidence is genuine when the first-person case provides access at a level of resolution no other form can supply for the claim in question.

This is the second condition. The essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate when the general claim is about an experiential structure whose singular instance is accessible only from the inside, and when the first-person case provides *evidential access* at the resolution the claim requires.

## IV. Wittgenstein's Attempts: When Inquiry Is Evidence

*Philosophical Investigations* (1953, Blackwell) was left in typescript when Wittgenstein died, the product of nearly two decades of work. The published text is divided into numbered sections, most of them short, none of them continuous with the next in the way that chapters in an argument are continuous. Each section makes an attempt, encounters a difficulty, and trails off. The next section makes a different attempt from a slightly different angle.

This structure is universally observed. What is less often specified is why it is the appropriate epistemic form for the subject rather than merely an unconventional presentation of it.

Wittgenstein's subject in the early sections is the phenomenon of meaning: what it is to understand a word, to follow a rule, to mean something by what one says. He opens by quoting Augustine's picture of how children learn language - every word has a meaning correlated with the word, an object for which the word stands - and then spends the first dozen sections showing what that picture cannot account for. The tools in a toolbox, he observes in §11, are as diverse in function as the words in a language: hammer, pliers, saw, screwdriver, rule, glue-pot, glue, nails, screws. The Augustinian picture fits some of them and fails the rest.

The difficulty is structural, not merely evidential. Any formulation of what language is - any attempt to say what understanding consists of or what it means to follow a rule - relies on a framework of concepts (meaning, understanding, following, rule) that is exactly what is under examination. A continuous argument about the nature of meaning would need to assume a working account of meaning in order to proceed. The attempt-and-failure structure of the *Investigations* is the form that remains when this difficulty is taken seriously: each attempt approaches from a different angle, each trails off when it encounters the framework-dependence problem, each is followed by another attempt.

The rule-following discussion makes the structure explicit. "This was our paradox," Wittgenstein writes in §201: "no course of action could be determined by a rule, because every course of action can be made out to accord with the rule." This is stated as a paradox rather than a conclusion because the formulation is itself suspect - it employs the concept "determined by a rule" in a statement that is supposed to show the concept cannot be unpacked. The numbered fragment is the form that expresses this predicament: it says something, acknowledges what it cannot say, and stops. The next section begins again.

A natural objection: Kripke, Brandom, McDowell, and Sellars have all engaged the rule-following paradox in continuous-argument form, and none of their treatments is incoherent or self-refuting. The objection has real force and deserves a direct answer. The claim here is not that the fragmentary form is the only vehicle for engaging with rule-following. What Wittgenstein's form preserves, and what continuous argument about the same paradox must lose, is the evidence that each new angle of approach runs into the same difficulty. Kripke offers a continuous argument *about* the paradox - a sustained, grammatical prose account of what the paradox shows and what follows from it. The *Investigations* is a continuous *encounter* with the paradox: the reader watches approach after approach fail in the same place, and that record is evidence of the subject's structure in a way that a continuous description of the structure cannot provide. The difference is between being told that a corridor loops and walking the corridor until you recognize the wallpaper.

Whether this makes the fragmentary form "epistemically required" or only "epistemically superior for the specific purpose of providing evidence of structural resistance" is a distinction I hold partly open. The claim I am committed to is that something is lost in conversion that no continuous description of the loss can fully recover.

A second concern: the *Philosophical Investigations* is not a standard essay. It lacks the personal voice of Montaigne, the first-person phenomenological engagement of Baldwin. What licenses treating it as evidence for a claim about the essay-form? The answer is that the condition being specified - that the movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the subject - is not restricted to the essay as a genre. The *Investigations* shares with the essay the specific property that makes condition (3) epistemically significant: the movement of attempts, visible and unfinished, constitutes evidence rather than process. Whether or not the *Investigations* is an essay by genre, it instantiates the condition. Condition (3) may apply to a broader class of forms than the essay - dialogues, numbered remarks, phenomenological descriptions - and I return to this as a named limit in §VI.

This is the third condition. The essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate when the movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the subject - when the failure to settle into any position, the return to the same difficulty from different angles, *discloses* that the subject resists the systematic grasp that continuous argument assumes it can achieve.

## V. When the Form Is Imposture

Against these three cases, a type should be identified - a recognizable mode of writing that borrows the essay's formal properties without satisfying any of the conditions just derived.

The type proceeds as follows. A writer takes a general claim - social-scientific, historical, cultural - and supports it with a personal anecdote. The anecdote is genuine; the first-person voice is genuine; the prose moves between the general and the particular in the way that essay prose does. Tentative phrasing is present. Digression is present. The essay resists too-clean conclusions. The reader has the experience of following a mind working through a problem.

What has not happened: the subject does not constitutively resist closure - it is a claim that aggregate data could settle, and the essay's tentativeness is not performing the instability of any evaluative framework but mimicking the social register of open-mindedness. The evidence is not phenomenological - the first-person anecdote is providing a vivid illustration of a claim that exists independently of the anecdote, and a different illustration would serve equally well. The movement of inquiry is not itself evidence about anything - the conclusion was determined before the anecdote was chosen, and the essay's discursive shape traces a path to a predetermined destination rather than disclosing the structure of a recalcitrant subject.

A concrete test of the typology requires applying it to a specific case rather than a hypothetical type. Nicholas Carr's "Is Google Making Us Stupid?" (*The Atlantic*, 2008) is a representative example and well-suited for diagnosis. The essay opens with Carr's observation that he has difficulty sustaining reading of long texts, and attributes this to years of internet use. It then assembles support - references to neuroscience research, anecdotes from colleagues with similar experiences, historical analogies to Nietzsche's typewriter and the printing press - for the general claim that internet use is reshaping human cognition, particularly our capacity for sustained attention.

Running the essay through the three conditions: the subject does not constitutively resist closure. Whether internet use changes cognitive patterns is an empirical question that cognitive science has attempted to answer with aggregate methods; Carr's tentativeness is the register of intellectual humility, not the performance of framework instability, and no evaluative standpoint is vacated by the reading. The evidence is not phenomenological in the relevant sense: Carr's personal experience of reading difficulty illustrates the general claim, but the claim is about aggregate cognitive change across a population, and a different person's equivalent anecdote would serve equally well as illustration. The first-person case is not irreplaceable. And the movement of inquiry does not disclose anything about the subject's structure: the essay moves from anecdote toward conclusion without enacting any failure to settle, because the conclusion was reached before the anecdote was selected to support it.

The essay-form is ornament here. The same argument, assembled from the cognitive-science literature with the personal anecdote removed, would be neither stronger nor weaker epistemically. What the essayistic form provides is texture - voice, relatability, the pleasurable sensation of following a mind at work - but not evidence that argument could not provide more economically. This is not a criticism of Carr's essay as a piece of writing; it is a precise statement of what the essay-form is and is not contributing. The distinction between good essayistic prose and essayistic epistemic work is exactly what the typology is designed to clarify.

### The Existing Accounts

Two prior accounts of the essay's distinctiveness serve as foils, and the contrast deserves development.

Georg Lukács, in "On the Nature and Form of the Essay" (1910, in *Soul and Form*), treats the essay as a form of art rather than of science. For Lukács, the essay's defining feature is its relation to the essayist's encounter with life's questions - the great essayist is someone for whom concepts are a vehicle for personal confrontation with what it means to live and judge. This account captures something real: the essay is not impersonal in the way that the treatise aims to be. But it understates the essay's evidential potential by treating the personal dimension as the form's defining feature rather than as one of several conditions of operation. An essayist deploying personal material for purely rhetorical or expressive purposes - warmth, specificity, voice - is doing what Lukács describes. An essayist deploying personal material as the access condition for a phenomenological claim is doing something additional that Lukács's account cannot distinguish from the first.

Adorno, in "The Essay as Form" (*Der Essay als Form*, 1958), treats the essay's resistance to system as epistemologically valuable: the essay refuses to totalize its object, insisting on the complexity of what systematic thought would reduce to fit its demands. Adorno is right about this. The essay's non-totalizing relation to its object is a genuine epistemic virtue, not merely a stylistic one, and the tradition of dismissing the essay as preparatory misses what the resistance-to-system is doing. Where Adorno's account is incomplete is in its inability to specify *when* this resistance is earned rather than merely performed. Any tentativeness, any digression, any refusal of too-clean conclusions can pass Adorno's test - including Carr's essay, which is tentative and digressive and resists closure without doing anything the argument-form could not replicate. The three conditions developed here are a refinement of Adorno's account: they specify the subset of cases where his diagnosis applies, and they give the reader a criterion for distinguishing the earned case from the performed one.

## VI. The Typology and Its Limits

Three conditions, then:

**(1) The subject constitutively resists closure.** When the phenomenon being examined is the instability of the evaluative frameworks required to examine it - when stable adjudication is exactly what is at stake - the essay-form can *perform* what argument can only assert. Montaigne's cannibals.

**(2) The general claim is about an experiential structure whose singular instance requires first-person access.** When the claim depends on establishing the interior of a specific experience at a level of resolution no third-person account can provide, the first-person case is not anecdote but *evidential access* of an irreplaceable kind. Baldwin's diner.

**(3) The movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the subject.** When the failure to settle, the return to the same difficulty from different angles, *discloses* that the subject resists the systematic grasp that continuous argument assumes - then the trail of attempts is evidence that no concluded argument could provide. Wittgenstein's fragments.

Three predictions follow.

First, any essay deploying essayistic form without satisfying at least one condition is doing work argument could replicate equally well. This is a testable claim about what would be lost if the form were converted to argument. Where nothing is lost, the form is ornamental. Carr's essay satisfies none of the conditions; converting it to a straightforward review of the cognitive-science literature would lose the voice but not the epistemic content.

Second, the conditions can be satisfied simultaneously. An essay may have a phenomenologically accessible subject that also constitutively resists closure; the movement of inquiry may disclose something about phenomenological structure that is also evidence of framework instability. The conditions are not exclusive, and the strongest essays will likely satisfy more than one.

Third, the conditions are not jointly exhaustive. There may be cases of genuine epistemic weight that do not fall under any of the three. The typology is provisional.

**On the independence of conditions (1) and (3).** A reasonable objection holds that condition (3) reduces to condition (1) plus a writerly choice: if the subject resists closure, then any record of attempts that fail to settle it would be evidence of that resistance. The objection is worth taking seriously. The conditions are related but describe different things. Condition (1) identifies a property of the *subject*; condition (3) identifies a constraint on the *form* required to provide evidence of that property. A writer confronting a closure-resistant subject could, in principle, announce that resistance in a framing section - "this subject cannot be settled; here are the reasons why" - and then offer a series of observations that illustrate the resistance without enacting it through the movement of inquiry. The framing would satisfy condition (1) as a claim but not as a demonstration. Condition (3) requires that the inquiry itself - the sequence of attempts - be visible as evidence. The conditions come apart because (1) identifies a feature of the subject and (3) specifies what the inquiry must look like to provide evidence of that feature rather than merely describing it.

**On the terms of the conditions.** The three conditions employ distinct epistemic relations, and precision about which relation is operating matters. *Perform* is the relation in condition (1): the essay enacts what it claims rather than asserting it from outside. *Disclose* is the relation in condition (3): the movement of inquiry makes visible something that continuous argument can only describe. *Provide evidential access* is the relation in condition (2): the first-person case reaches the phenomenon at a level of resolution no other form can supply. *Evidence* is the general term covering all three - the overall claim that the essay-form provides epistemic warrant. These relations are not identical; using them interchangeably obscures what each condition is contributing.

**On what is not yet distinguished.** One limit the typology reaches: the conditions do not sharply distinguish the essay from autobiography and from phenomenological description. Autobiography can have a subject that resists closure and can provide first-person evidential access. Phenomenological description can disclose experiential structure and can include the movement of attempts. What the essay specifically requires - tentatively marked as its proprietary contribution - is that the movement of inquiry be *both* visible *and* unfinished as part of the form's epistemic work. Autobiography does not require that the inquiry be visible; it requires that the self be revealed. Phenomenological description does not require that the inquiry be unfinished; it requires that experience be characterized. The essay requires both.

This distinction is under pressure from adjacent forms. Plato's dialogues include visible, unfinished inquiry. Some of Husserl's descriptive work proceeds through the movement of attempts. The claim that condition (3) is specific to the essay-form - rather than shared with a broader class of forms that includes dialogue and phenomenological description - is less defended than the condition's diagnostic usefulness would require. I treat this not as a minor boundary issue but as the central unresolved question of the typology: the conditions discriminate between genuine essay-epistemology and illustrated argument, but whether they discriminate the essay from its closest relatives is an open question I name rather than close.

## VII. The Self-Reflexive Dimension

A piece that argues for the epistemic weight of the essay-form while writing as if in the argument-form would be self-refuting. Any account of the essay-form must be tested against its own typology if it is not to refute itself in its execution. The demand is that this piece *demonstrate* rather than merely claim the conditions.

The question "under what conditions does the essay-form carry epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate?" satisfies, I believe, all three conditions - though with uneven strength.

It satisfies condition (1): the subject resists closure. Any systematic account of when systematic inquiry fails is tested by its own systematicity. An argument specifying the conditions under which argument fails can always be asked whether it itself fails those conditions. This essay has occupied and partially vacated several positions: the claim that the typology is not circular, the claim that it distinguishes the essay from autobiography and phenomenological description, the claim that conditions (1) and (3) are independent. None of these is fully settled.

It satisfies condition (2): there is a phenomenological dimension. The claim that certain essays carry evidential weight by virtue of first-person access requires some account of what that access consists of - and that account cannot be fully specified without attending to what it is like to read and write in different forms. This essay has gestured at that experience without fully establishing it. The gesture is honest rather than adequate.

It satisfies condition (3): the movement of inquiry is itself evidence. Whether it is evidence of the right thing - whether the essay keeps escaping systematic grip because the subject has that structure, or because the right grip has not yet been found - I cannot entirely say from inside the inquiry. Montaigne's move, in "On Cannibals," is to let the reader experience the instability of their evaluative standpoint. This essay has not done that as vividly. The failure is recorded rather than concealed.

A question the self-reflexive application raises: does partial satisfaction count as satisfying the conditions? It does, for this reason: the conditions describe when the essay-form carries epistemic weight argument cannot replicate, not when it carries all such weight. A piece can satisfy condition (1) partially - occupying and vacating some positions while securing others - and the form will carry evidential weight on the former while the argument handles the latter. The weaker the satisfaction, the smaller the evidential contribution; but weakness is not absence.

---

## Conclusion

The essay-form is not a comfort or a style. It is an epistemic vehicle with specifiable operating conditions. Knowing those conditions allows a reader to distinguish an essay doing work only the essay-form can do from an essay dressed in essayistic clothing while doing work argument would do more economically.

Montaigne named his form well. The *essai* - the attempt - is not preliminary to the result. Under the conditions specified here, the attempt *is* the result. When the subject constitutively resists closure, the sustained inhabiting and vacating of unstable positions is the only form of inquiry that does not lie about the subject's structure. When the evidence is phenomenological and the claim requires singular first-person access at the required resolution, the case is not anecdote but testimony to which there is no third-person equivalent at that level. When the movement of inquiry discloses the resistance of the subject to systematic grasp, the trail of attempts is evidence that no concluded argument could provide.

These conditions are provisional, open to cases that disconfirm them, and bounded by a limit I have named but not resolved: the distinction of the essay from autobiography and phenomenological description when conditions (1) and (3) are simultaneously met. The essay-form's most specific epistemic contribution is condition (3) - the visible, unfinished movement of inquiry as evidence - and that contribution is not yet sharply distinguished from inquiry that is visible and unfinished in other forms.

An essay about essays is the appropriate form for a conclusion that cannot be delivered with the confidence of a theorem. Whether this essay has satisfied its own conditions - whether it has demonstrated rather than merely claimed the account - is for the reader to determine. That uncertainty is not evasion. It is what the attempt looks like from the inside.
