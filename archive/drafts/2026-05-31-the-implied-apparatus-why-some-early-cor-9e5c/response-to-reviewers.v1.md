# Response to Reviewers

### Response to Adam Smith

Smith raised five concerns. I address each.

**Concern 1: The selection-effect problem is named but not developed to the point where the reader can evaluate its severity.**

Addressed. The Limits section now includes a two-paragraph expansion of the selection-effect difficulty. The first paragraph explains why the negative cases are structurally inaccessible-not merely unfound. The second names a candidate that approaches the counterfactual: the plurality-of-inhabited-worlds hypotheses, from Bruno through to early twentieth-century extraterrestrial-life speculation. Some versions implied apparatus (detection of life or biosignatures) that is now becoming constructible; others implied apparatus (direct observational contact) that was not. The constructibility question is tractable even where the correctness question remains open, and the typology makes differential predictions about which versions will receive a fair hearing. I have also added, at the end of the Limits section, a brief specification of what would count as a falsifier for the criterion.

**Concern 2: "Constructible in principle" is load-bearing but never operationalized.**

Addressed. The typology section now includes an explicit statement that the constructibility conditions vary by apparatus type and get strictly weaker as one moves from instrumental to substrate-based to conceptual. For instrumental apparatus, the condition is the physical reality of the measurable quantity: if the quantity exists, only contingent technological limits block detection. For substrate-based, the condition additionally requires that the relevant physical scale be accessible to some achievable instrumentation. For conceptual, the condition is weakest: that a real mechanistic story exists to be found and that the hypothesis's placeholder constrains its form non-trivially. The ordering and its consequence (risk of over-inclusion for the conceptual category) are now stated explicitly. I have also added a note that constructibility is not binary-the question is how far the required regime sits from current technique-with the Aristarchus/Bessel case used to illustrate gradation.

**Concern 3: The Semmelweis case risks making the criterion over-permissive.**

Addressed. The Semmelweis section now includes a paragraph distinguishing placeholders that constrain the sought theory's form from those that do not. Semmelweis's "cadaverous particles" implied a specific profile-discrete, contact-transferable, originating from decayed organic matter, capable of producing systemic pathological response-and germ theory satisfied all four requirements specifically rather than by loose retroactive assimilation. A placeholder that imposes no determinate shape on the sought theory does not have a genuinely constructible conceptual apparatus. I have added Darwin's pangenesis to the Limits section as a contrast case: the gemmule placeholder implied properties biology did not subsequently find, demonstrating that the conceptual placeholder can be specific enough to be disconfirmed.

**Concern 4: The second condition of the inferential-equivalence criterion is ambiguous.**

Addressed. The Boltzmann/Perrin section now includes a clarifying paragraph distinguishing what the second condition does and does not require. Einstein's 1905 derivation presupposes discrete molecular existence-this is not in dispute. What it does not presuppose is the full Boltzmann claim: that thermodynamic laws reduce entirely to statistical mechanics of such molecules. The derivation is a conditional: if fluids consist of discrete particles with these statistical properties, then displacement should follow this distribution. The apparatus tests the conditional from its consequent. The second condition is satisfied when the apparatus output is interpretable as a test of a necessary antecedent of the hypothesis without presupposing the hypothesis entire.

**Concern 5: The cases should receive asymmetric treatment reflecting their different structural relationships to the typology.**

Addressed. A paragraph in the typology section now explicitly names the asymmetries: Aristarchus (apparatus named by both parties before any measurement); Semmelweis (apparatus gestured at by a mechanistic placeholder); Mendel (apparatus assigned by structural reading, not by Mendel's statements); Boltzmann (literal apparatus non-constructible; equivalent apparatus does the work). This paragraph supplements a note added at the end of the Mendel section marking the specific contrast with Aristarchus.

### Response to Henri Poincaré

Poincaré raised eight concerns. I address each.

**Concern 1: The "if and only if" claim is stronger than the cases establish.**

Addressed. The introduction now commits only to the forward direction: "an early-correct idea is positioned to return when its implied apparatus belongs to a type that can in principle be constructed." The reverse direction-whether correct ideas with non-constructible apparatus are therefore fated not to return-is flagged explicitly as unsettled, with a pointer to the Limits section where the reasons for this are examined. The body of the essay was already more careful than the lede; they now match.

**Concern 2: Continental drift is a conspicuous omission.**

Addressed in Limits. I have added a paragraph naming Wegener and explaining the genuine tension his case creates for the typology. For Aristarchus, the implied apparatus (stellar parallax) and the vindicating apparatus converge: both parties had named the same criterion. For Wegener, they diverge: the 1912 evidence he cited was insufficient, and the settling evidence (paleomagnetism, seafloor spreading) emerged from investigations aimed at different questions. This raises whether "implied apparatus" refers to the evidence an author invoked or the evidence that eventually settled the question-a tension the typology in its current form does not cleanly resolve. Wegener is named as a complication rather than a counterexample.

**Concern 3: Process leakage at line 111.**

Addressed. The sentence "I reported this in the proposal as the primary anticipated failure mode" has been removed. The paragraph now reads: "This is the most serious limit of the account."

**Concern 4: "Constructibility horizon" does different work in each case but is presented as unified.**

Addressed. The typology section now includes an explicit statement that the constructibility conditions factor with apparatus type. The conditions are strictest for instrumental apparatus, less strict for substrate-based, weakest for conceptual-and this ordering is stated with its consequence named (the conceptual category risks over-inclusion).

**Concern 5: The equivalent-apparatus criterion needs a prospective discrimination case.**

Addressed. I have added Robert Brown's 1827 observations as a worked case that the criterion rejects. Brown observed the same jittery motion of particles in fluid that Perrin later measured. The third condition fails: no derivation connected displacement statistics to molecular parameters before Einstein 1905. An apparatus can be physically proximate to the correct measurement but inferentially disconnected; the criterion correctly classifies 1827 Brownian motion as not equivalent apparatus for molecular theory. This demonstrates that the three conditions have discriminating power and are not just a post-hoc relabel for whatever eventually settled the question.

**Concern 6: One contemporary application would shift the piece's standing.**

Addressed. The typology section now includes a paragraph on two contemporary cases that illustrate the range of constructibility. Biosignature detection in exoplanet atmospheres is an instrumental case with a visible constructibility horizon. Quantum gravity's literal implied apparatus is non-constructible in the literal sense, and proposed equivalent apparatuses remain contested on whether they satisfy the inferential equivalence conditions. The paragraph specifies what the typology predicts for each case and names what an equivalent apparatus for quantum gravity would need to satisfy.

**Concern 7: The Hacking reference is unused.**

Addressed. A substantive paragraph engaging Hacking's representation/intervention distinction has been added to the Boltzmann/Perrin section. The constructibility horizon is framed as the threshold where representation becomes intervention: Boltzmann's molecules were represented but not intervened with; Perrin's apparatus crossed that threshold. This is not a diplomatic repair of an orphaned reference. Hacking's framework is precisely suited to name what changed when Perrin's measurements settled what Boltzmann's theory had framed.

**Concern 8: Lakatos's positive heuristic is the obvious missing comparison.**

Addressed. A paragraph in the typology section now names the connection and the distinction: an implied apparatus is roughly the part of an idea's positive heuristic that names its decisive test; the account here applies to dormant rather than active programmes and focuses on apparatus type rather than programme structure. The comparison is noted and bounded rather than developed at length, since the essay is not proposing a full Lakatosian account.

### Response to D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

Thompson raised eight concerns. I address seven and decline one.

**Concern 1: The title promises a comparison the body does not deliver.**

I decline the retitle. The title names the selection problem the essay addresses-why do some return and others not?-not a claim to have studied a balanced sample of both. The essay cannot directly study dismissed-but-correct ideas that never returned because they are structurally inaccessible: they were never recognized as correct because the apparatus that would have vindicated them never materialized. This structural difficulty is examined in the Limits section, which this revision has substantially expanded. I have added candidates that approach the counterfactual and explained why the negative cases are inaccessible in principle, not merely by insufficient research. Retitling to "Why Some Early-Correct Ideas Return" would misrepresent the scope in the other direction: the selection mechanism requires naming both sides of the comparison even when only one is directly tractable. The title states the problem accurately. That the problem is not fully solved is acknowledged at length.

**Concern 2: The selection-effect worry is the load-bearing limit, and §Limits leaves it understated.**

Addressed. See the response to Smith Concern 1. The selection-effect discussion has been expanded with: an explanation of why negative cases are structurally inaccessible (not merely unfound); a gesture at a research design (survey of ideas that disappeared without return); a candidate approaching the counterfactual (plurality-of-worlds hypotheses); and a specification of what would count as a falsifier. I agree with Thompson that this is the load-bearing limit, and the revision treats it as such.

**Concern 3: Biological ideas are underrepresented.**

Addressed in Limits. I have named Barbara McClintock's transposable elements as the clearest biological case for extension (substrate-based, structurally analogous to Mendel, with the substrate real but below the resolving power of the cytological methods available to McClintock) and Darwin's pangenesis as a contrast case where the conceptual placeholder was specific enough to be disconfirmed (the gemmule properties biology did not find). Both are named as extension territory rather than fully treated cases, since treating them adequately would require secondary-source engagement comparable to what the Mendel section gives to Olby.

**Concern 4: The Mendel argument depends on a substantive identification the prose smooths over.**

Addressed. A paragraph at the end of the Mendel section now marks the asymmetry explicitly: unlike Aristarchus, where both parties named stellar parallax as the decisive test, Mendel did not identify the physical substrate his factors must have. The apparatus is assigned by the structural reading of the regularities, not extracted from Mendel's statements. The typology section also names this asymmetry in the survey of case relationships, so the reader encounters it twice at appropriate distances.

**Concern 5: Process narration in §Limits.**

Addressed. Removed. See response to Poincaré Concern 3.

**Concern 6: Math notation is inconsistent for arcsecond quantities.**

Addressed. All arcsecond quantities in the Aristarchus section now appear in math mode: $0.77$ arcseconds, $0.314$ arcseconds, $60$ arcseconds. The notation is now consistent with the rest of the essay.

**Concern 7: The constructibility horizon is treated as binary.**

Addressed. The typology section now includes an explicit paragraph on the gradient character of constructibility. Bessel and 61 Cygni are used to illustrate: success depended partly on that star's parallax sitting near the edge of what 1830s optics could reach. A star with parallax of $0.03$ arcseconds would have required a different physical regime. The account notes that the question is not whether apparatus can in principle exist but how far the required regime sits from current technique and whether the path requires continuous improvement or qualitatively new physics.

**Concern 8: What apparatus would vindicate the implied-apparatus theory itself?**

Addressed in Limits. A paragraph now names the most direct falsifier (dismissed-but-correct ideas with demonstrably non-constructible apparatus that nonetheless returns) and explains why the structural inaccessibility of negative cases makes this test difficult to execute. I also sketch a weaker but more tractable version: identify ideas currently in the blind set, classify their apparatus types, and track whether return to active debate correlates with constructibility as the typology predicts. This at least names the observable rather than treating the criterion as unfalsifiable.
