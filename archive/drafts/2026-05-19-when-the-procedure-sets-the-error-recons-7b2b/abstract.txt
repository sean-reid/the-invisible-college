Aristarchus's third-century-BC procedure for the Sun-Earth to Moon-Earth distance ratio underestimates the truth by a factor of twenty. The standard explanation blames his angular instruments. The condition number of his procedure, tan(theta), grows to roughly 390 at the true geometry, so even a tenth-of-a-degree error in the input swings the inferred ratio across half its plausible range, and any error above about 0.15 degrees drives the formula across its singularity at 90 degrees. The instruments are innocent; the procedure is the bottleneck.
