# The Legitimate Anachronist: When Reading a Past Thinker Through Later Concepts Teaches Rather Than Distorts

There is a short distance between illumination and imposture in the reading of past thinkers, and Quentin Skinner spent fifty years trying to map it. His 1969 essay "Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas" is one of the most cited papers in the philosophy of history, and it is cited as often by those who think he went too far as by those who think he was exactly right. Both parties tend to agree that he identified something real. The disagreement is about what that something licenses in practice — whether it forbids the kind of reading that has produced some of the most illuminating intellectual history of the last century, or whether there is a form of anachronism that evades his critique because it is doing something different from what he attacked.

That is the question I want to work through here. My claim is that there is a legitimate anachronism, and that it can be distinguished from the distorting kind by three conditions that are not arbitrary but follow from a careful reading of what Skinner's objection actually requires. I am not certain these conditions are jointly sufficient. I am reasonably confident they are jointly necessary. And in the borderline case — Judith Butler's reading of Sophocles' *Antigone* through the lens of gender theory — they strain in an instructive way.

## What Skinner's Objection Actually Says

The usual gloss on Skinner is that he prohibits anachronism — that he demands we read past thinkers purely in their own terms. This is an imprecise rendering, and the imprecision matters. What Skinner actually argues is about the nature of textual meaning, not about the date of one's conceptual vocabulary.

His framework draws on speech-act theory: to explain a text's meaning is to explain what its author was *doing* — what speech act they were performing, within a context of conventions that made certain utterances intelligible as certain kinds of act. To explain a logical treatise is to identify it as an act of demonstration within a tradition of formal argument. To explain a political pamphlet is to identify it as an act of intervention in a specific public debate. The meaning is not a free-floating content that the words contain; it is what the author was performing with those words, given the resources available to them.

This is the hinge. What a thinker can be doing is constrained by what they have the conceptual equipment to do. Locke could not have been "anticipating natural rights" in a modern sense because performing that speech act — making that specific claim, within that specific theoretical framework — required conceptual resources that did not exist for him and could not have been deployed even if he had wanted to deploy them. The anachronist who discovers this "anticipation" in Locke has found a verbal parallel and mistaken it for a conceptual identity. They have described an action Locke could not have been performing.

This is precise and powerful. It demolishes a whole genre of intellectual history: the genre that finds the origins of contemporary doctrines in thinkers who had, at best, a superficial verbal resemblance to those doctrines and, at worst, worked within a framework that would have made the contemporary doctrine incoherent. It is not a complaint about careless reading. It is a complaint about a kind of care applied to the wrong object.

But notice what the objection targets: it targets *attribution*. It says you cannot attribute to a past thinker the performance of an act they lacked the resources to perform. What it does not obviously target is a different kind of claim — the claim that a practice or argument has a structural feature that a later concept correctly identifies, independent of whether the practitioner had that concept, intended to instantiate it, or could have articulated it.

This distinction is the opening that Pierre Hadot exploits, productively, in *Philosophy as a Way of Life*.

## Hadot and the Structural Reading

Pierre Hadot's central argument about ancient philosophy is that we have been misreading it as doctrine when we should be reading it as practice. The ancient philosophical schools, he argues, were not primarily in the business of producing correct theories about the universe, though they did produce theories. They were primarily in the business of producing practitioners of a certain kind — people who had transformed their relationship to their own mental life through disciplined exercise. The *Meditations* of Marcus Aurelius, read as a philosophical treatise, is puzzling: it is repetitive, it does not advance arguments, it circles back constantly to the same Stoic commitments without elaborating them. Read as a spiritual exercise — a practice of daily self-correction, performed on paper, aimed at training the practitioner's attention and response — it is entirely coherent.

The concept "spiritual exercise" is Hadot's, not Marcus's. Marcus does not use it. The ancients did not have it as a recognized category. Hadot knows this; he says it explicitly. His claim is not that Marcus was consciously doing spiritual exercises in the modern sense. His claim is that the practice Marcus was engaged in has structural features — it is iterative, self-addressed, aimed at transformation of the practitioner's response-dispositions, resistant to summary as propositional doctrine — that the concept "spiritual exercise" accurately identifies.

Here Skinner's objection does not engage. Hadot is not attributing to Marcus a speech act he lacked the resources to perform. He is not saying Marcus was claiming something he could not have claimed. He is saying the form of the *Meditations* — independently identifiable, derivable from the text before the concept is introduced — has properties that a later concept names accurately. The reading is structural rather than attributive. It asks: what is this practice *doing*, at a level of description that the concept "spiritual exercise" captures? And it answers: that level of description is not the practitioner's own self-description but a structural account that a later observer can give honestly.

What makes this legitimate? Three conditions, I will argue. Let me state them and then test them.

## Three Conditions

**Condition one: the later concept must pick out a genuine structural feature of the earlier practice or argument — not a verbal resemblance but a structural identity.**

Hadot's concept "spiritual exercise" identifies a structural feature: the practice of repeated, self-addressed attention-training performed in writing. This feature is independently identifiable before the concept is introduced. You can see it from the *Meditations*' form alone — from its resistance to summary, its iterative character, its address to "you" rather than to a reader. The concept accurately names something already there. This rules out the case where a later reader finds a word that resembles a later term — finds "soul" and reads psychoanalytic interiority, finds "nature" and reads ecological systems theory — without identifying any structural property that the two share.

**Condition two: the reading must be capable of surprise by the original text — it must have something at stake when it encounters specific passages, rather than being a framework that can absorb any encounter without resistance.**

I originally stated this as "the reading must generate falsifiable claims." I now think "falsifiable" reaches for the right thing imprecisely. What the condition requires is that the reading engage with specific textual evidence, make specific claims about it, and be capable of frustration. A reading that can incorporate any possible text as a "variant" or "inadequate expression" of its central concept is not engaged with the text — it is using the text as occasion. Hadot's reading is capable of surprise: it generates specific claims about the *Meditations* (that it is not doctrinal in form, that it is self-addressed, that it resists being excerpted as argument) that could be wrong. If someone showed that Marcus did intend the *Meditations* as a treatise, or that the form is in fact propositional throughout, Hadot's reading would need to change.

**Condition three: the reading must not require attributing the later framework's metaphysical or ideological presuppositions to the earlier thinker.**

Hadot is not claiming Marcus believed in the modern therapeutic model of psychological self-improvement, or that he shared a contemporary conception of the self as a site of transformation. He is claiming the *practice* had these structural features regardless of Marcus's own theoretical commitments. The later concept names the structure; it does not require the earlier thinker to have endorsed the later theory that makes sense of that concept within its original home. This is different from reading Marcus as a proto-cognitivist or a proto-Buddhist. Those readings would require Marcus to have shared commitments he did not and could not have had. Hadot's reading requires only that the structural description is accurate.

## Hegel and the Colonizing Reading

Hegel's reading of the pre-Socratics in the *Lectures on the History of Philosophy* is, by contrast, a relatively clear case of distortion. The standard criticism is that Hegel projects his own system backward and turns the ancients into proto-Hegelians working out the earliest, most abstract moments of Spirit's self-recognition. This is right, but I want to be precise about which condition fails.

Consider the reading of Thales. For Hegel, Thales' claim that "all is water" is the first positing of the Absolute as simple, undifferentiated unity — a "beginning of philosophy" in the sense of Spirit's first attempt to think itself through material self-identity. The structural feature this identifies is: the move from mythological explanation to a single material principle. This is independently identifiable and is not wrong as a description of what Thales does. So far, condition one is not obviously failed.

The failure is at condition two. Hegel's developmental schema — Spirit's dialectical self-recognition through successive stages — is constitutively unfalsifiable as a reading of the pre-Socratics. Whatever Anaximander says, it can be framed as Spirit's next moment, a slightly less inadequate self-comprehension. Whatever Heraclitus says can be framed as the moment where Spirit recognizes contradiction as part of its own nature. There is no possible text that would force the reading to register surprise. The framework is not engaged with the evidence — it is using the evidence as confirmation of a schema that is immune to evidence. This is the failure Skinner's mechanism implies: when a reading cannot be frustrated by the text, it is not really a reading of the text. It is a reading of the reader's own framework, illustrated with passages.

The failure at condition three is a consequence of this, but it is also independent: Hegel does attribute to the pre-Socratics a kind of Hegelian agency. On his reading, Thales is not merely doing something that we can describe in Hegelian terms — he is a moment in Spirit's actual self-development. This requires attributing to Thales not just a structural feature but a metaphysical role within a framework Thales could not have known he was participating in. The framework's presuppositions — that Spirit develops historically through inadequate self-expressions — are attributed to the pre-Socratics as agents, not merely applied to them as observers.

## Butler's Antigone: The Productive Borderline

Judith Butler's *Antigone's Claim* is described in the proposal for this piece as a "borderline case," and that description survives the close reading. But it is a more interesting borderline than I initially expected, for reasons that turn on the complexity of what Butler is actually doing.

Butler is reading Sophocles' *Antigone* through Hegel's reading of it in the *Phenomenology of Spirit*, and simultaneously reading both through contemporary theories of kinship, gender, and political intelligibility. She is doing multiple anachronisms in sequence. And at each layer, the three conditions apply differently.

At the level of Sophocles' text, Butler's reading passes conditions one and two more clearly than might be expected. The structural feature she identifies — Antigone's position as simultaneously inside and outside the political order, making a claim that the city cannot recognize as either legitimate or simply criminal — is independently identifiable before Butler's theoretical frame is introduced. It is there in Sophocles: Creon cannot simply execute Antigone as a rebel, because her appeal to divine law has a force the city's law cannot absorb. The claim exceeds the available categories on both sides. This is structural, not merely an imposition, and Butler's reading can be surprised: she finds genuine resistance in Antigone's own language, particularly in her appeals to eternal law and to the specific kinship obligations of a sister to a brother. These resistances do not fit neatly into Butler's framework, and she acknowledges them as complications rather than dismissing them.

Condition three is where the borderline appears. Butler's reading does require that the structural features she identifies participate in something like "the constitution of the politically intelligible subject" — a concept that carries presuppositions about discourse, subject-formation, and the regulatory power of norms that neither Sophocles nor Hegel had. The question is not whether this projection occurs — it does — but whether it is marked as the reader's analytical frame rather than the text's own commitment.

Here Butler's practice is, I think, genuinely careful rather than genuinely resolved. She marks the conceptual imports explicitly; she does not pretend Antigone is doing queer theory. But she does allow the framework to determine which structural features are worth isolating and which are treated as secondary. Her reading is not equally attentive to all of Antigone's resistances — the religious dimensions, the specific Mediterranean kinship structures — because her frame trains attention toward the political-normative ones. This is a partial failure of condition one: the structural feature she picks out is genuinely there, but it is selected from among other structural features, and the selection is governed by the later framework in ways that are not fully acknowledged.

The honest verdict is: Butler's reading is more legitimate than Hegel's on the same play, because it is capable of surprise and it does not require Antigone to have been operating within Butler's metaphysics. But it is less clean than Hadot's, because the selection of which structural features to analyze is partially governed by the later framework in ways that are only partially visible.

## What the Conditions Actually Do

These three conditions do not provide an algorithm for evaluating anachronistic readings. What they provide is a diagnostic — a set of questions that, if answered honestly, tell you where a reading stands and why.

The first condition asks: can you state what structural feature the later concept picks out, using terms that don't require the later framework? If the answer is no — if the structural description is entirely internal to the later vocabulary — then condition one has not been met and the reading is more likely projection than illumination.

The second condition asks: can you state what evidence would frustrate this reading? Not necessarily refute it — interpretation rarely works by single decisive experiments — but what specific passages or features of the text would force a revision? If the answer is that any passage can be absorbed, condition two has not been met.

The third condition asks: does your reading require the earlier thinker to have endorsed the presuppositions of the later framework, or only that the later framework accurately describes what the thinker was doing at a structural level? The former colonizes; the latter describes.

What these conditions do in practice is locate where the reading's weight falls. A reading that fails condition two is not engaged with the text — it is using the text. A reading that fails condition three is not illuminating the thinker — it is conscripting them. A reading that fails condition one is not identifying a structural feature — it is finding a verbal parallel and inflating it into identity.

## Where the Conditions Fall Short

I want to be honest about what these conditions do not settle. They are, I believe, necessary. I am uncertain they are sufficient. A reading could pass all three and still be, in some sense, distorting — if, for instance, the structural description, though accurate as far as it goes, systematically excludes other structural features that a different conceptual frame would illuminate equally well.

This is close to Butler's case. Her reading is not wrong; it is partial, and the partiality is shaped by her framework in ways that condition one, as I have stated it, does not fully capture. To state condition one fully, one would need to add: the structural features picked out must not be selected by the later framework in a way that excludes other structural features equally present in the text, unless the selection is explicitly acknowledged. But this addition is difficult to operationalize, and the risk of pushing toward it is that the conditions become too demanding — that they rule out not only the colonizing readings but also the productive partial readings that are, in practice, what the best interpretive work produces.

The deeper limit is that these conditions are evaluative but not generative. They can help a reader assess an anachronistic reading they encounter; they cannot tell a reader how to construct one. The positive capacity — the interpretive judgment required to identify which structural features are worth isolating, which later concepts illuminate rather than distort — is real and necessary, and it cannot be fully formalized. What the conditions do is mark the domain within which that judgment operates honestly, and identify the failure modes that indicate it has left that domain.

Skinner is more right than his critics typically allow: the field of arbitrary anachronism is larger than the field of productive anachronism, and the discipline the Cambridge School enforced has genuine value. But the conditions I have traced here suggest that some of Hadot's most illuminating readings, and some of Butler's most productive ones, occupy territory that Skinner's critique does not reach. The legitimate anachronist is the reader who can answer the three questions honestly. That is not most readers. It is not nothing.
