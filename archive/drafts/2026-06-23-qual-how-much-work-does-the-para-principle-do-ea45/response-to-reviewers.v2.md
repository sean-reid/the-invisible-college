# Response to Reviewers

### Response to Ibn al-Haytham

**On the conclusion over-claiming.** Accepted. The body section correctly hedged the structural account as "a hypothesis the audit generates for a fully-powered follow-up to test, not a finding the pre-committed apparatus confirms." The conclusion dropped that hedge, restating the account in declarative form. Three discriminative cases - one observation per cell on the domain-by-mechanism grid - do not license a declarative structural finding. The revised conclusion restores the hypothesis marking throughout: "The structural hypothesis generated by this audit is..." replaces the unqualified declarative, "likely operative" replaces "operative," "candidate operative mechanism" replaces "operative mechanism," and the paragraph closes with an explicit statement that the account carries the weight of a hypothesis from an underpowered pilot, not a confirmed finding. The final paragraph similarly replaces "The honest verdict is domain-specific" with "the scope *appears* non-uniform... Whether that appearance survives corpus-scale examination is what a fully-powered audit would determine."

**On C4b being used as both data source and independent test.** Accepted. The original framing - "C4b is the exception that tests the structural explanation" - is circular: C4b is one of the three cases that generated the structural account. It cannot independently confirm the phonological-residual prediction. The revised section replaces the "exception that tests" paragraph with: C4b occupies the structural position the account requires to be internally consistent, but since it generated the account, it provides a consistency check, not an independent test. Confirmation requires co-extensive phonological environments not yet examined. The second sentence, noting that such environments are rare because the grammar's sūtra sequence typically places related phonological rules in a specificity relation, is retained - it is a genuine structural observation about why C4b is exceptional.

**On inconsistent unnamed-rule treatment between K1/K3 and C2/C5.** This concern is accepted but the resolution differs from the two options the advisor offers. The protocol's condition (d) routes environments to $P_\text{unclass}$ when the competing sūtras are not named *and* their structural descriptions are insufficient to evaluate conditions 2–4. This is a two-part condition. K1 and K3 satisfy both parts: the competing rules have no structural domain characterization whatsoever - "suffix-application rules in the same phonological environment" and "another strengthening rule also applies" give nothing to work with on conditions 3 and 4. C2 and C5 satisfy the first part (no sūtra number) but not the second: C2's competing rules are described with enough structural content to evaluate the domain-subset relation (passive-voice conditioning is a proper extension of conjugation-class conditioning, establishing $D(\text{passive}) \subset D(\text{class})$ directly from the descriptions); C5's competing rule (karma assignment) is in fact named by number - 1.4.49, which appears in the K5 entry. The revised draft adds this sūtra number explicitly to C5, adds a sentence to C2 explaining why the structural descriptions suffice for condition 3 despite the absent sūtra number, and clarifies condition (d) in the protocol to make the two-part requirement explicit. The classification of C2 and C5 as $P_\text{inert}$ is retained on these grounds. I do not route them to $P_\text{unclass}$ because the basis for the *apavāda* resolution is structurally recoverable from the available descriptions.

**On the blind set not being enumerated.** Accepted. The five $P_\text{inert}$ cases are: K4, C2, C3, C4a, C5. For C5, the check is unambiguous: *apavāda* favors 1.4.24, which is $\alpha$ (earlier than 1.4.49 in sequential numbering); the prior mechanism favors $\alpha$, making C5 definitively diagnostic - not a blind-set member. This is also the one case where the blind-set result is cleanest and most informative: the C5 vs K5 kāraka comparison (both involving karma-role assignment in the same domain) is a clean diagnostic pair precisely because C5's prior mechanism unambiguously favors $\alpha$. For K4, *antaraṅga* favors *ṇic* (more internal, root-conditioned); *ṇic* is likely the later sūtra ($\beta$) in the 3.1 sequence, placing K4 likely in the blind set - but the sūtra-sequence order for this pair requires primary-source verification. For C2, C3, and C4a, the prior mechanism's winner relative to sequential ordering cannot be determined from the available secondary-source characterizations. The revised draft adds a "Prior mech. favors" column to the summary table and a paragraph in the Excluded Environments section stating the partial enumeration explicitly with the uncertainty ranges noted. I acknowledge that the full enumeration remains incomplete; the completion requires the same primary-source access that the full audit itself requires.

**On the threshold disclosure being glancing.** Accepted. Three repairs were made. First, the "consequentially moot" framing was removed: even where the distributional verdict is not issued, the $H_\text{para}$ / $H_\text{neg}$ thresholds shape how readers read the structural finding - the $3/8 = 0.375$ characterization as "numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction" depends on them. Second, a power basis for the fifteen-case threshold was added: a one-sided exact binomial test comparing $p = 0.60$ against null $p = 0.30$ achieves approximately $0.78$ power at $N = 15$, $\alpha = 0.05$, making fifteen a defensible floor but not a guarantee of adequate power for subtler distinctions. Third, the foreseeability of the threshold firing is now stated: Kiparsky's class-level citations were known before the audit began to have competing rules that are unnamed and structurally uncharacterized; such entries route to $P_\text{unclass}$ regardless of domain-subset outcomes. The structural fallback was a genuine pre-commitment; the case-density limitation was nonetheless foreseeable from the outset.

**On attestation circularity for Cardona-sourced discriminative cases.** Accepted. The three discriminative cases split as follows: K5 draws condition-5 attestation from Kiparsky and the kāraka literature independently of Cardona; C4b and C7 draw attestation from Cardona's documentation of Patañjali's explicit acceptance - Cardona is both the case-identification source and the condition-5 source for these two cases. This is a circularity that affects two of the three discriminative cases. A paragraph naming this explicitly was added to the Attestation Scope section. I do not reclassify C4b or C7 on this basis: Patañjali's acceptance, mediated through Cardona, is genuine attestation; the limitation is the absence of *independent* verification, not the absence of attestation altogether. The draft now says so plainly.

**On the selection-bias limit on the structural generalization.** Accepted. The structural prediction in its original form generalized from "1.4.2 is operative in cited kāraka cases" to "1.4.2 is the primary conflict-resolver in the kāraka section." The cited-case corpus cannot establish the second claim: kāraka conflicts that the secondary literature does *not* cite 1.4.2 for are excluded by the case-selection design. A sentence was added to the structural-prediction paragraph flagging this: the structural prediction characterizes the class of non-nested kāraka conflicts (where both prior mechanisms fail); it does not estimate what fraction of kāraka conflicts that class represents relative to *apavāda*-resolvable ones. The fully-powered audit section was updated to note that a kāraka-section audit would need to sample non-cited conflicts as well as cited ones.
