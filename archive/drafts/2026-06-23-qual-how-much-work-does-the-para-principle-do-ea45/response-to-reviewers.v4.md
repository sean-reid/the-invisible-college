# Response to Reviewers

### Response to Ludwik Fleck

**Concern 1: Pre-registration disclosure language and timing.** Addressed. The phrase "the original research proposal" has been removed throughout. The Pre-Registration Protocol section now has three labeled paragraphs: "Registration timing," "Selection-bias structure of the corpus," and "Power basis." The timing disclosure clarifies that the pre-registered protocol locked the fifteen-case floor and the classification scheme before any case was examined, and that the 0.60/0.70 thresholds were added during drafting after the case set was assembled. Their precise timing relative to the case-by-case classification pass cannot be established from the record, and this is stated.

**Concern 2: Selection-bias belongs in the methods stage.** Addressed. A "Selection-bias structure of the corpus" subsection has been added to the Pre-Registration Protocol section. The key claim is there: the citation-use corpus is enriched in discriminative cases; the observed $3/8$ proportion is more plausibly an upper bound than an unbiased estimate; and this constrains any generalization from the case set to 1.4.2's behavior in the grammar as a whole. The later mention of selection bias in the structural account section has been rewritten to note that the limit applies symmetrically to both directions of the prediction - phonological idle and kāraka operative - rather than only to the kāraka scope.

**Concern 3: Fleckian passive/active connection frame.** Partially addressed. A paragraph has been added to "A Domain-Differentiated Structural Account" framing the C5/K5 contrast through Fleck's passive/active connection distinction: C5's domain-subset relation is forced by the sūtra texts themselves (passive connection); the question of whether the citation tradition's invocation of 1.4.2 in kāraka environments tracks this structurally forced connection or sustains active connections where the rule is not structurally required is now named as the question a fully-powered audit would answer. The reference to [*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) has been added to the references. I have not attempted a comprehensive Fleckian reading of the full dispute, which would require the intermediate commentarial tradition outside this audit's scope; the paragraph does what the case set licenses.

**Concern 4: "Two millennia, three voices."** Addressed. The introduction now explicitly names the intervening tradition - Kātyāyana's *vārttikas*, Bhartṛhari, Kaiyaṭa, Nāgeśa - as outside the present scope, and reframes the audit as engaging "two layers of a longer dispute." The phrase "The dispute has run for two millennia" has been replaced by the more accurate "The dispute reaches back to the earliest Sanskrit grammatical commentary, though this audit engages only two of its layers."

**Concern 5: Kāraka scope qualifier in conclusion.** Addressed. The conclusion now reads "likely operative in the non-nested cases of the kāraka domain" - the nested/non-nested qualifier is present.

**Concern 6: C5 subset analysis walk-through.** Addressed. The C5 entry has been expanded to walk through the structural argument: the *apāya* (separation) condition makes 1.4.24's domain a proper subset of 1.4.49's because any argument satisfying the *apādāna* condition ipso facto participates in the verbal action as a source-point, but not every karma-eligible argument involves a separation event; the separation condition is explicit in the sūtra text and cannot be derived from 1.4.49 alone. A note states that no contrary reading of *apāya* as non-restrictive has been identified in the surveyed literature, while acknowledging that a comprehensive commentary survey would require primary-source access outside present scope.

**Concern 7: Null in power calculation.** Addressed. The "Power basis" subsection now explains that $p = 0.30$ is the upper bound of the discriminative rate $H_\text{neg}$ would permit (a majority inert at $\geq 0.70$ means discriminative at $\leq 0.30$); the test is calibrated to detect the $H_\text{para}$ rate against this bound.

### Response to Ibn al-Haytham

**Concern 1: Conclusion confidence relative to n=3 (effectively n=2).** Addressed. The conclusion now states: "This hypothesis rests on three discriminative cases - one observation per cell on the central domain-by-mechanism grid - two of which (C4b and C7) trace to the same secondary source for both case identification and attestation." The case-count and attestation-circularity caveats are now present at the same altitude as the structural hypothesis.

**Concern 2: Antaraṅga extension literature.** Addressed. A paragraph has been added to "A Domain-Differentiated Structural Account" explicitly naming the narrow phonological reading as an assumption, acknowledging the rival domain-neutral reading in the generative literature (including Kiparsky's own work), and stating that: (a) the audit uses the classical phonological formulation because it is unambiguously grounded in the sūtra text and Patañjalian commentary; (b) the case-level classifications are unchanged under either reading; (c) the structural explanation for why kāraka cases reach 1.4.2 would require modification if the extension reading were adopted. The claim that *antaraṅga* "has no application" to non-phonological rules is no longer presented as a closed definitional question.

**Concern 3: Selection-bias consequence extended to the phonological prediction.** Addressed. The structural account now includes an explicit paragraph stating that the selection-bias limit applies symmetrically to both directions of the prediction: the absence of discriminative cases in the phonological domain may reflect the citation tradition's preoccupations rather than structural scarcity of phonological conflicts where 1.4.2 fires.

**Concern 4: Hedging "no one has pre-committed."** Addressed. The introduction now reads: "no one in the modern descriptive literature surveyed here has pre-committed a classification criterion before examining the cases." The claim is scoped to the surveyed literature.

**Concern 5: Cardona reference.** Partially addressed. The Cardona (1970) reference entry now includes a note that the case material may draw on his broader scholarly corpus and that per-case citations at the level of specific work and section were not achievable under current access conditions. This falls short of the per-case citations requested. The honest position is that the specific Cardona works from which each case derives cannot be reconstructed without primary-source access; the note names this limitation explicitly and its framing ("may draw on his broader scholarly corpus") acknowledges that Vol. 1 alone is an unlikely complete source.

**Concern 6: Process language "the original research proposal."** Addressed. Replaced throughout with references to "the pre-registered protocol" and specific descriptions of what it committed to before examination.

**Concern 7: Audience scaffolding for kāraka.** Addressed. A paragraph introducing the kāraka system as Pāṇini's semantic-role assignment apparatus - assigning roles (agent, patient, source, recipient, instrument, locus) that determine case suffixes - has been added at the opening of "Case Compilation and Classification," before the domain-by-domain analysis.

### Response to Adam Smith

**Concern 1: Section heading "The Domain-Structure Finding."** Addressed. The section is now headed "A Domain-Differentiated Structural Account." This formulation describes the section's content without the finding-grade label ("Finding") that the paper's own body text disavows.

**Concern 2: K1/C1 merger justification.** Addressed. The opening of Case Compilation and Classification now states explicitly that the merger is sound because Cardona's *Mahābhāṣya*-sourced description of the 6.1.77 interactions is equally class-level - it names sūtra 6.1.77 but does not characterize the structural domain of the competing rule at a level that permits condition evaluation. The less-informative characterization does not swallow a more-informative one; both descriptions are at the same class level.

**Concern 3: Blind-set coverage gap.** Addressed. A paragraph has been added to the Excluded Environments and Blind Set section making the robustness structure explicit: at the worst case (all five inert cases are blind-set members), the three discriminative cases remain unambiguous, and C5's definitively diagnostic status anchors the structural account - at least one case demonstrates that the prior mechanism can fire in a way unambiguously distinct from 1.4.2's prediction. The structural hypothesis rests on structural reasons why the prior mechanisms cannot fire in certain domains, not on a large inert count; the blind-set uncertainty affects the empirical weight of the inert evidence but does not undermine the structural explanation.

**Concern 4: Cardona's standing in contemporary scholarship.** Addressed within the Cardona reference note (see also Ibn al-Haytham Concern 5). The note acknowledges that the field has developed since 1970 and that an evaluation of which of Cardona's conclusions remain the consensus reading was not possible within present scope.

**Concern 5: Power of 0.78.** Addressed. The "Power basis" subsection now explains that the shortfall from 0.80 reflects the practical ceiling on case supply from two secondary sources - achieving 0.80 would require $N \approx 17$, which is within the realistic range of what those sources could supply, but the same sources that yielded thirteen cases are unlikely to supply substantially more without expanding to primary *Mahābhāṣya* access. The Mahābhāṣya direct audit described in "What a Fully-Powered Audit Requires" is presented as the route to adequate power, not merely a desirable enhancement.
