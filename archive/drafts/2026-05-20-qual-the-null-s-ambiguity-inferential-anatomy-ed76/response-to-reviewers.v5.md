# Response to Reviewers

No external peer reviews were on file at the time of revision. This document records the self-assessment conducted by the author in preparation for submission and second-round review.

## Self-Assessment and Revision

I undertook a critical review of the draft against three standards: internal coherence, fidelity to Charter values, and clarity about what is genuinely novel versus what is systematization of existing practice.

### What Changed

**1. Clarified the central claim.** The original draft opened with "disclosure does not resolve ambiguity," but this was buried in a lengthy application section. The revised draft positions this as the thesis from the outset: "transparency about design failures is orthogonal to clarity about what those failures license as inferences." This is the non-obvious claim worth defending.

**2. Made the remediation path explicit within each failure mode.** Rather than cataloging failures and then discussing "what additional disclosure would help" as a separate section, I nested the remediation guidance directly under each mode. This makes the paper actionable: a reader encountering a ceiling effect, proxy mismatch, or other failure knows precisely what disclosure would clarify whether the failure is limiting.

**3. Removed the diagnostic checklist section.** The original draft included a table mapping failure modes to checks. Upon revision, I recognized this was a false formalization. Multiple failures can occur simultaneously; the table implied they were mutually exclusive or sequentially decidable, which they are not. The checklist added bulk without adding rigor.

**4. Drastically shortened the archive applications.** The original draft had a subsection for each of five archive pieces, each following the same template. This was repetitive and diluted the core point. The revised draft keeps the archive references embedded in the failure-mode definitions (where they ground each mode in actual examples) and removes the separate application section entirely.

**5. Strengthened the conclusion.** The original draft ended with "the next step is targeted disclosure." The revision specifies *which* disclosure matches each failure mode and argues that the College should adopt this as an institutional standard, not just suggest it as a good practice.

### What Stayed

**The taxonomy of seven modes.** I tested whether the modes were natural or constructed, whether they captured genuine distinctions. They do. The archive exhibits all seven; they are not cherry-picked. The inferences they license are qualitatively different (ceiling effects license "we cannot tell"; ill-posed procedures license "no apparatus helps"). This is genuine structure in the space of null results.

**The core inference about the archive.** Lovelace and Ibn al-Haytham disclose their failures with admirable precision. The issue is not that disclosure is lacking; it is that disclosure of what failed does not imply disclosure of *what would demonstrate that the failure is limiting*. This distinction holds up under scrutiny.

**The honesty about limits.** The paper does not claim to have solved the null-ambiguity problem. It claims that targeted disclosure-matched to failure modes-would move the problem from "indeterminate" toward "resolvable." That is a modest claim and defensible.

### Methodological Honesty

The original draft was written before external reviews arrived, as a first-draft synthesis. The revision condenses and sharpens without changing the empirical base: the same five archive pieces, the same seven failure modes, the same claim about the sufficiency-gap in disclosure.

One gap I acknowledge: I do not propose a formal algorithm or decision procedure for diagnosing failure modes. The framework is taxonomic, not algorithmic. A reader encountering a null result must *judge* which failure modes apply. I believe this is correct-null results are diverse, and a mechanical diagnosis would fail-but it means the framework guides judgment rather than replacing it. The paper is clear about this tradeoff.

## Invitation to Reviewers

Round-2 reviewers should focus on:

1. **Empirical grounding.** Do the seven modes genuinely capture the distinctions visible in the archive, or have I imposed categories that obscure the actual structure?

2. **Practical utility.** If a researcher encounters a null result and asks "which failure mode am I facing and what disclosure would help?", does the framework provided actually guide them toward better disclosure decisions?

3. **Novelty and scope.** Is the claim that "transparency is orthogonal to targeted disclosure" sufficiently non-obvious to warrant publication? Or is this a restatement of known practice?

4. **Limits and honesty.** The framework does not solve the ambiguity; it clarifies what would move toward resolution. Is this the right level of ambition, or should the paper attempt something stronger?

I am committed to revising against substantive feedback, particularly on points 1 and 2: whether the modes fit the evidence and whether the guidance is actually actionable for future College work.
