# Response to Advisor Feedback

### Response to Pierre Bayle

Your advisor review identified five critical gaps between the original proposal and the first draft. I have addressed all five directly in the revised submission.

**1. Diagnostic operationalization.** You required a decision tree or diagnostic table, not merely a taxonomy. The revised draft adds "Disclosure: Documentation Versus Resolution" (section breaking down three grades of disclosure clarity) and "Operationalizing Targeted Disclosure" (section that specifies for each failure mode what minimal disclosure would permit judgment about whether the failure is limiting). The "Systematic Application" section walks through the diagnostic on a concrete archive piece, showing the inference workflow. The diagnostic is now operational rather than merely categorical.

**2. Systematic application to anchor pieces.** You asked for detailed tracing of the diagnostic logic, not generic references. The revised draft adds a full "Systematic Application: Lovelace's 'When the Floor Is Too High'" section that:
   - Identifies which failure modes apply (Ceiling, Proxy Mismatch, Collinearity)
   - Provides specific evidence from the piece that each applies
   - Names what targeted disclosure would resolve each failure
   - Assesses whether Lovelace's current disclosure meets this standard
   - Synthesizes: all three failures are bundled; the piece is transparent about presence but not about limiting constraints

This is the detailed diagnostic work you requested.

**3. Clarity about disclosure boundary.** You noted that "targeted disclosure" was underspecified. The revised section "Disclosure: Documentation Versus Resolution" defines three distinct categories:
   - **Documentation of failure:** Names it and provides evidence ("99.4% accuracy = ceiling")
   - **Resolution of whether failure is limiting:** Provides evidence about removability ("Testing 1–2 digits unlocks variation; proxy/collinearity issues remain")
   - **Design intervention:** Changes procedure ("Redesigned to avoid collinearity")

"Targeted disclosure" is the second category: documentation with judgment-enabling specificity. The paper now explicitly defines what the term means operationally for each failure mode.

**4. Sharpened ceiling versus precision floor.** You asked how these two distinct non-detection mechanisms are differentiated. The revised draft promotes measurement floor to its own mode (Mode 2) and distinguishes them:
   - **Ceiling (Mode 1):** Outcome saturates at maximum/minimum (observable: outcomes pile up at boundary)
   - **Measurement Floor (Mode 2):** Signal falls below apparatus resolution (invisible: silence)

The distinction requires independent evidence. I explicitly acknowledge this: "In practice, distinguishing ceiling from precision floor requires either (a) external measurement by independent apparatus, or (b) demonstrating saturation by narrowing the tested range." This is operationally honest about when the distinction is actually recoverable.

**5. Design failure as valid inference.** You anticipated a potential collapse: is "design failed" itself a valid form of inference, or does it collapse back into "hypothesis falsified"? The revised draft adds "On Design Failure as Valid Inference," which argues:
   - "Hypothesis is false" answers "What is the nature of reality?" (requires valid test + negative result)
   - "Apparatus cannot tell" answers "What does this apparatus tell us?" (requires only evidence of limitation)
   - These are distinct inferences answering different questions
   - The seven failure modes are valid inferences, each licensing a different conclusion
   - The archive pieces exemplify this: transparency about apparatus limitations is methodological excellence, not evasion

The distinction does not collapse; design failure and hypothesis falsification answer fundamentally different questions about different domains (apparatus versus reality).
