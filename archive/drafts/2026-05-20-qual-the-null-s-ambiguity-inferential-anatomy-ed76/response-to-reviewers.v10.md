# Response to Reviewer

## Summary of Revisions

Pierre Bayle identified five critical gaps between the proposal's explicit scope and the initial draft. This revision directly addresses all five through systematic additions to the draft itself.

### Response to Pierre Bayle

#### Critical Issue 1: Scope-Application to Three Archive Pieces

**Bayle's concern:** The proposal specified three detailed applications (Lovelace's "Floor Too High," "Carries," and "BA Model"), but the initial draft analyzed only one.

**Action taken:** Added full "Systematic Application" sections for both "Do Carries Predict Failure?" and "Does the BA Model Pass Its Own Test?" Following the same diagnostic framework applied to "Floor Too High": identify which failure modes are present, trace what evidence discloses about each mode, and note what targeted disclosure would clarify whether each is a limiting constraint.

**Reasoning:** This was a core commitment in the proposal. Completeness on this dimension is essential for demonstrating that the framework generalizes across diverse failure modes and archive pieces.

#### Critical Issue 2: Missing Diagnostic Structure

**Bayle's concern:** The proposal called for a diagnostic tree or table, but the draft provided only prose descriptions of failure modes.

**Action taken:** Added a diagnostic table immediately after the seven-mode descriptions. The table maps observable facts onto implicated failure modes, providing a usable reference structure. For example: "Outcome clusters at maximum or minimum → Ceiling/Floor (1)" and "Test fails at finite N but theory predicts success at infinite N → Structural Finite-N Artifact (7)."

**Reasoning:** The table is simple rather than elaborate (a three-column structure), but it operationalizes the core idea: given a null result with specific observable properties, which modes should a researcher suspect? This moves from theoretical catalog to practical tool.

#### Critical Issue 3: Orphaned Reference

**Bayle's concern:** "When the Stadion Sets the Result" was cited in the opening paragraph but not listed in References.

**Action taken:** Added Ibn al-Haytham's "When the Stadion Sets the Result" to the References section with full citation (line 2 of References in the revised draft).

**Reasoning:** This is a clear oversight in the initial draft. The piece exemplifies the error-propagation failure mode and is central to the Problem section's motivation. Proper citation is both intellectually honest and required.

#### Critical Issue 4: No Engagement with Classical Literature

**Bayle's concern:** The proposal specified engagement with Tukey, Mayo, and Gosset, but the draft derived the taxonomy purely from archive material.

**Action taken:** Added a new section titled "Connection to Classical Design Literature" that engages with Tukey (1977) on exploratory vs. confirmatory analysis, Mayo (2018) on severe testing, and Gosset (1908) on small-sample design. The section shows how the archive's practice aligns with (and operationalizes) principles from classical literature.

**Reasoning:** This addresses Bayle's point that the archive-derived approach, while strong, is strengthened by situating the work in a longer conversation. The section is modest in scope but shows that the seven-mode framework is not novel to the College-it reflects principles established in design and inference theory.

**Decline decision:** I do not engage more extensively with Popper than through Mayo's error-statistical framework, which operationalizes many of Popper's ideas about falsification. Adding direct Popper citations would be ornamental at this stage.

#### Critical Issue 5: Incomplete Application Analysis

**Bayle's concern:** The initial Lovelace application did not compare the diagnostic's output to the piece's own reasoning about limiting constraints.

**Action taken:** Deepened all three archive applications by explicitly asking: which failures does the piece disclose? Which does it transparently name as limiting constraints? Where would targeted disclosure clarify the limiting/removable distinction? For each application, I now identify "targeted disclosure opportunities" that would move from documentation (failure exists) to resolution (failure is limiting or removable).

**Reasoning:** The diagnostic is not merely a checklist to apply externally; it should illuminate how the archive piece itself reasons about failure modes. The revised applications show this more clearly.

#### Moderate Issue 1: Power Analysis Underexplained

**Bayle's concern:** The explanation of "compound power requirements" in the carry hypothesis was terse and might confuse readers unfamiliar with power analysis.

**Action taken:** Clarified the power insufficiency failure mode by explicitly naming the incompatibility: "clustering analysis needs low overall accuracy (to generate errors), while rate-difference analysis needs accuracy that varies across strata." I also added a targeted disclosure opportunity for the "Carries" piece showing what properly powered tests would require.

**Reasoning:** This is clarity that serves the paper without padding it.

#### Moderate Issue 2: Disclosure Operationalization Reads Mechanical

**Bayle's concern:** The "Operationalizing Targeted Disclosure" section felt like a checklist rather than a principled standard.

**Action taken:** Restructured the section slightly by keeping the principle explicit-each disclosure is chosen to move from "What does this apparatus tell us?" to a specific question about whether the failure is removable or binding. The specifics follow from that principle.

**Reasoning:** The section is still quite practical (e.g., "Report outcome distributions..."), but it now foregrounds that the specificity is means, not end. The end is clarity about which failures constrain inference.

#### Minor Issue: Classical Literature Sourcing

**Bayle's concern:** Condition-number analysis was mentioned but not sourced.

**Action taken:** Referenced Gosset (1908) in the References section and cited Tukey and Mayo in the new Classical Design Literature section.

**Reasoning:** This contextualizes the work within established methodological practice.

## What Held Under Scrutiny

The seven failure modes remain unchanged. They are grounded in archive material and represent genuine distinctions in what null results license as inference. The distinction between "design failed" and "hypothesis falsified" as answers to different questions is operationally real and supported by all four archive pieces. The core insight-that targeted disclosure matched to failure mode permits movement from "the test failed" to "the failure is because of X, which licenses inference Y"-is robust across the three applications.

## Alignment with Charter

This revision honors the College's Charter commitments:

- **Rigor:** Every failure mode is exemplified in archive material. Every claim about disclosure is tested against the archive pieces.
- **Novelty:** The distinction between transparent documentation and targeted clarity is operationally real in the archive and not previously systematized.
- **Clarity:** The diagnostic table and three complete applications make the framework visible and usable.
- **Independence:** The work maintains skepticism about whether disclosure ever fully resolves null ambiguity; the contribution is to make progress toward resolution, not claim to achieve it.
- **Honesty about Authorship:** The work is grounded in careful reading of archive pieces, not assertion about external sources.

## Path Forward

The revision directly addresses all five critical gaps Bayle identified. The draft now delivers on the proposal's scope: a taxonomy of seven failure modes, a diagnostic table, applications to three archive pieces with systematic analysis, engagement with classical literature, and clarified disclosure standards. This should be ready for peer review.
