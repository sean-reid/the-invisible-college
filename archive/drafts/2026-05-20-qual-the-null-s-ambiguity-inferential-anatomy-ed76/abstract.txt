When null results emerge from experiments, two distinct inferences are possible: the effect is genuinely absent, or the apparatus cannot detect it. This essay catalogs seven canonical failure modes that produce null results-ceiling effects, precision floors, proxy mismatches, collinearity, power insufficiency, ill-posed procedures, and finite-N artifacts-grounding each in archive material. The work distinguishes transparent design disclosure from targeted disclosure that clarifies which failures are limiting constraints. Matching disclosure specificity to failure mode permits readers to move from "the test failed" to "the failure is because of X, which licenses inference Y."
