### Response to Henri Poincaré

**Concern 1: The separability of Channel C from Channel A is asserted more than argued.**

Accepted. The objection-that Channel C is simply what Channel A produces when actors are heterogeneous-is formally correct, and the draft was not engaging with it. I have added a paragraph in the Channels section that grants the objection's formal premise and then specifies what Channel C adds: the question it asks, which the frequency account cannot reach. A monitoring program can satisfy the Deterrence metric while worsening the Selection metric-these are distinct evaluative dimensions that require different data to distinguish. This is not a claim of separate causal mechanism but of a distinct evaluative frame and a distinct policy failure mode.

**Concern 2: The "harm correlates with undetectability" condition needs an explicit counterexample.**

Accepted. The condition was carrying more weight than a structural argument without adversarial cases can justify. I have added petty tax non-compliance and food safety small operators as domains where the correlation plausibly runs the other way-where unsophisticated, visible violators cause more aggregate harm than sophisticated ones-and specified what structural conditions produce this reversal (low coupling between capability and harm magnitude). The general argument is now presented as a structural hypothesis with named scope conditions rather than a universal claim.

**Concern 3: Goodhart's Law and Campbell's Law are unnamed.**

Accepted. I have added a paragraph in the Channels section locating Channel C within and against Goodhart and Campbell. The argument: Goodhart and Campbell describe a general instability of measure-as-target without specifying the compositional consequence for the residual violator population. A Goodhart story is consistent with uniform harm distribution; Selection additionally requires heterogeneous actors with harm negatively correlated with detectability. The contribution is compositional and distributional. Goodhart and Campbell are now cited in the references.

**Concern 4: The Kaplan-Irvin shift underplays a Channel B (Substitution) reading.**

Accepted in part. I have added a paragraph acknowledging the alternative reading-that the 57%→8% shift could be Substitution rather than Deterrence, with investigators changing what they report without changing underlying conduct. I then explain why I still read it primarily as Deterrence: the mechanism structure of mandatory pre-registration predicts a change concentrated in future trial conduct rather than in retrospective reporting by prior investigators. The data cannot fully partition the two components; I am honest about that.

**Concern 5: The credit rating case demonstrates Goodhart, not Channel C specifically, without an explicit compositional step.**

Accepted. I have added the explicit step: the Selection claim requires not just that issuers optimize against the disclosed model, but that the issuers who can do so most precisely-those with sophisticated financial engineering capacity, large balance sheets, and institutional access-are systematically those capable of larger systemic harm. The residual pool of misrated securities concentrates at the dangerous tail not because all issuers game equally, but because sophisticated gaming is itself correlated with scale. This paragraph converts the Goodhart observation into the specific Selection claim.

**Concern 6: The Ostrom reference is gestural and applies the wrong design principle.**

Accepted. The reviewer is correct that Ostrom's monitoring principle (Principle 4) is about community-accountable monitors, not about participants defining detection criteria-and that the load-bearing claim requires the collective choice arrangement (Principle 3), which is about affected parties participating in modifying operational rules. I have corrected the citation to name both Principle 3 (collective choice) and Principle 4 (community accountability) explicitly, and developed the argument a paragraph further: when monitored parties participate in defining criteria and monitors are accountable to them, engineering around the criteria requires engineering around violations you yourself defined. The Ostrom connection is now less gestural.

**Concern 7: The Channel A/B/C labeling is hard to carry through a long essay.**

Accepted in principle, partially addressed. I have added descriptive names-Deterrence, Substitution, Selection-to the channel labels throughout, used the names consistently in body text, and reserved the letter codes for their first introduction. The result is that a reader who encounters "Selection" in the conclusions section does not need to translate from the letter code.

**Concern 8: The conclusion's policy implication understates the analysis.**

Accepted. The numerical hypothetical ("50% reduction, 2x more harmful") was weaker than the analysis supports. The revised conclusion makes the stronger claim directly: detected-violation count is not a sufficient statistic for expected harm when Selection is operating. The standard evaluative apparatus is measuring the wrong unit-frequency reduction and harm-composition change are distinct outcomes that can move in opposite directions.

**Concern 9: The piece would benefit from a non-social cognate.**

Accepted. I have added a sentence in the Channels section noting that selection under detection pressure is structurally identical to mechanisms in evolutionary medicine and adversarial machine learning-antibiotic stewardship concentrating the resistant population, deployed defenses probed at their detection boundaries-and noting that formal models for this structure exist in those fields. This costs little and establishes that the mechanism is not ad hoc.

**Concern 10: The cross-reference to the referral hiring piece reads as authorial throat-clearing.**

Partially accepted. The reviewer is right that the phrasing emphasized method continuity over analytical connection. I have revised the sentence to foreground what Channel C specifically picks up from the referral piece's framework: the referral audit found the aggregation step most often asserted rather than demonstrated, and the selection mechanism analyzed here is precisely an aggregation-level claim-deterrence of some actors produces a compositionally different residual pool. This makes the connection analytical rather than biographical.

### Response to Ibn al-Haytham

**Concern 1: The Kaplan-Irvin (2015) attribution is factually misdated.**

Accepted; this is the most important correction in the revision. The draft said the comparison was anchored to the FDAAA 2007 mandate. The reviewer is correct that Kaplan and Irvin's before-after comparison is anchored to 2000, when NHLBI began requiring prospective registration in ClinicalTrials.gov for the trials it funded. I have corrected Case 1 throughout: the NHLBI's 2000 mandate is now identified as the intervention, the FDAAA 2007 is described as the generalization beyond NHLBI, and the Kaplan-Irvin figures are presented as a comparison between pre-2000 and post-2000 trials. The Channel A finding survives intact; what changes is which policy produced it.

**Concern 2: Goodhart's Law and Campbell's Law are unnamed.**

Accepted; see Response to Poincaré Concern 3 above. Both are now engaged in the Channels section with a paragraph specifying what Selection adds that neither Goodhart nor Campbell provides.

**Concern 3: Channel B is set up more weakly than the actual literature warrants.**

Accepted. The draft stated Channel B predicts a constant harm distribution without qualification. I have added a sentence acknowledging that Channel B via crowding can in principle produce composition effects-if monitoring selectively undermines intrinsic motivation among those who were previously the least-harmful compliers, the post-monitoring population differs by motivation type. I then specify the distinguishing empirical signature: Substitution via crowding predicts residual population differs by prior motivation type; Selection predicts it differs by detectability under the monitoring criterion. Channel B is no longer a strawman, and the cases are now clearly predicting different patterns about who is in the residual pool.

**Concern 4: Selection on the dependent variable in case choice-no negative cases.**

Partially accepted. A full third case of Selection failing is beyond this piece's scope, but I have added a structural counterexample in the General Structural Condition section: the FAA's Aviation Safety Action Program (ASAP), which offers regulatory immunity for self-reported violations. By making disclosure non-punitive, it extends the detection frontier to violations that would otherwise remain hidden. The selection pressure that produces Channel C is largely removed when the monitored population has a structural incentive to extend the detection frontier themselves. This gives the three joint conditions a context in which one of them (detection asymmetry) is structurally low, and names what it looks like when the mechanism does not operate.

**Concern 5: The third "joint condition" is definitional and not parallel to the first two.**

Accepted. The draft presented three parallel empirical conditions, but the third (insufficient sophistication deterrence) is by definition satisfied if Selection is producing a residual population. I have restructured it: two empirical conditions (detection asymmetry; harm correlation with undetectability) plus a definitional consequence (if Selection is operating, sophisticated violators were not deterred). The text now names this explicitly-"rather than a third independent empirical condition, this is the consequence that Selection is operating at all."

**Concern 6: No formal expression of the selection claim.**

Partially accepted. I have added a compact formal statement in the General Structural Condition section: violators characterized by detectability D and harm h; the residual pool has expected harm E[h | D ≤ T]; Selection dominates when Cov(h, D) < 0. This is a paragraph, not a half-page derivation. The derivation of which joint-distribution shapes produce dominance is a natural follow-up but would require specifying the harm distribution's functional form, which I cannot justify without additional empirical grounding. The formal expression as written disciplines the harm-correlation condition into something precise and connects the verbal conditions to the mathematical object they are providing structural support for.

**Concern 7: The act-itself vs. side-effect structural distinction is not pulled through to observable implications.**

Accepted. I have added a paragraph in the Observable Implications section on this asymmetry specifically as it applies to the detection-lag test: in side-effect Selection, the detection frontier can be expanded to capture adjacent violations; in act-itself Selection, expanding disclosure compounds the problem because it sharpens the optimization target. The two structural forms predict opposite signs for the detection-lag test-whether expanding monitoring helps or hurts. This pulls the Case 2 structural observation through to a testable prediction.

**Concern 8: "That was not in the original design" is ambiguous-could read as process leakage.**

Accepted. The phrase has been replaced with "An additional implication follows beyond the main mechanism." The implication itself is unchanged.

### Response to D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

**Concern 1: The intellectual heritage of Channel C is understated.**

Accepted; see Response to Poincaré Concern 3 (Goodhart/Campbell) and Concern 9 (biological analogue). Both gaps are now addressed. Goodhart and Campbell are named and engaged with in the Channels section; the biological analogue (antibiotic resistance, predator-prey arms races, adversarial ML) is acknowledged in a sentence that also points toward where formal models exist. The novelty claim is now narrowed to the compositional and distributional formalization, not to the absence of predecessors.

**Concern 2: The harm-correlation assumption needs more defense, including acknowledgment that it can fail.**

Accepted; see Response to Poincaré Concern 2. The revision adds petty tax evasion and food safety small operators as domains where the correlation plausibly reverses, specifies the structural conditions under which this happens, and presents the harm-correlation condition as an empirical hypothesis with named scope limits rather than a structural universal.

**Concern 3: Case 2 is weaker than the piece treats it; the partial empirical support is for the wrong stage.**

Accepted. The Case 2 framing now leads with the structural form (act-itself Selection) rather than with empirical documentation. The pre-crisis Coval et al. and Benmelech-Dlugosz evidence is presented as motivating context demonstrating that optimization against inferred models produces adverse selection-the stage-one mechanism is real. The stage-two mechanism (formal disclosure as sharper optimization target) is explicitly marked as a structural prediction that the post-2010 literature has not yet traced. The conclusion similarly describes Case 2 as demonstrating "the structural form in which Selection can be embedded in the compliance act itself, with empirical motivation from the pre-crisis period and confirmation of the full causal chain pending." The "partial empirical support" language is gone.

**Concern 4: A simple formalization is missing.**

Partially accepted; see Response to Ibn al-Haytham Concern 6. I have added a compact formal statement. I am not developing the full mixture-distribution decomposition the reviewer describes, because doing it rigorously requires specifying functional forms I cannot justify empirically. The piece is an essayistic mechanism analysis with a formal anchor, not a formal economics paper.

**Concern 5: The transparency-vs-opacity policy implication is undercooked and potentially dangerous.**

Accepted. The original phrasing ("transparent, auditable, formally disclosed monitoring criteria may be more vulnerable") was offered without acknowledging the strong case for transparency in regulatory design. The revised paragraph now: (a) explicitly states that the established case for transparency-accountability, predictability, due process, capture-resistance-is not undone by this observation; (b) narrows the claim to fully published quantitative methodologies inviting optimization in a way that qualitative case-by-case assessments do not; and (c) develops the Ostrom resolution as a third path that is transparent to participants without creating a public optimization target. The implication is now bounded and cannot be read as a general case for opacity.

**Concern 6: "Structurally prior to" overstates the relationship to Frey and Jegen.**

Accepted. The phrase has been replaced with "addresses a question the crowding literature does not reach." This is the warranted claim: Selection asks about violation-pool composition at any level of frequency reduction, which is not a question Frey and Jegen are asking. The stronger claim about structural priority was not necessary and was not quite right, since the crowding mechanism can interact with Selection in the way the revised Channel B discussion now acknowledges.

**Concern 7: The biological/evolutionary analogue is conspicuously absent.**

Accepted; see Response to Concern 1 above and Response to Poincaré Concern 9. The analogue is now named explicitly in a sentence in the Channels section.
