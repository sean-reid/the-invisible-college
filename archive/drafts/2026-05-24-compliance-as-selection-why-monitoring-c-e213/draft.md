# Compliance as Selection: Why Monitoring Concentrates the Violations It Cannot See

When a regulatory body installs a formal monitoring regime, the announced purpose is deterrence: raise the expected cost of violation and reduce its frequency. Something else often happens alongside. The violations the regime was designed to catch are caught, or deterred. The violations it was not designed to catch-the sophisticated, the novel, the ones that require planning and institutional access-remain. The residual violation pool is not a random draw from the original distribution. It is a selection for the violations most capable of surviving scrutiny, which tend also to be the violations capable of the most harm on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect.

This piece names that mechanism, specifies it at the three levels a social explanation requires, and examines three cases where the evidence is strong enough to test the claim. The analytical structure used here-extracting the mechanism, specifying it at the situational, action-formation, and aggregation levels, naming what would falsify each step-runs parallel to the audit in [*Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/). Where that piece found the aggregation step most often asserted rather than demonstrated, the selection mechanism analyzed here is precisely an aggregation-level claim: deterrence of some actors produces a compositionally different residual pool.

## The Three Channels

The compliance literature has developed two mechanism accounts of what monitoring does to violation rates.

**Channel A: Deterrence.** Monitoring raises the expected cost of violation for actors who weigh costs. Violations previously carried low detection probability; monitoring raises it. Frequency declines among actors who respond to expected cost. This is the classical account-the one that justifies compliance programs as cost-benefit interventions.

**Channel B: Substitution.** Monitoring redirects actor effort from avoiding the underlying violation to satisfying the monitoring criterion. Actors who cannot escape monitoring change the surface form of their conduct without changing its substance. This is the mechanism Power (1997) documents across public and private institutions: auditable process substitutes for unauditable substance. Frey and Jegen's (2001) motivation-crowding analysis extends this: monitoring can signal distrust, which undermines the intrinsic motivation that was producing compliance before monitoring was introduced. In principle, the crowding mechanism can produce composition effects-if monitoring selectively undermines intrinsic motivation among those who were previously the least-harmful compliers, the post-monitoring population differs by motivation type. The empirical signature of this form differs from the selection mechanism described below: Substitution via crowding predicts the residual population differs by prior motivation type; Selection predicts it differs by detectability under the monitoring criterion.

**Channel C: Selection.** Monitoring defines a detection frontier. Violations inside the frontier carry higher expected cost because they are detectable by the monitoring criterion. Violations outside the frontier carry expected costs close to the pre-monitoring baseline, because the probability of detection by the installed mechanism is low. When monitoring is effective at the center of the violation distribution-the typical, past-pattern violations it was designed to catch-it clears the detectable end. What remains is the undetectable end. The violation pool that survives is selected for the properties that made violations invisible to the detection criterion in the first place.

The distinguishing signature: Substitution predicts that violations maintain a constant harm distribution, because substance is preserved even as form changes. Selection predicts that the harm distribution shifts toward the severe end. The mean harm per surviving violation is drawn from a population with systematically different properties-specifically, those properties that allowed it to escape detection. Whether those properties correlate with harm magnitude on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect is an empirical question in any given case, but there are structural reasons to expect the correlation runs in the wrong direction, developed in the General Structural Condition section below.

Selection under detection pressure is the social-institutional instance of a mechanism familiar from other fields: antibiotic stewardship concentrates the resistant population; deployed security defenses are probed at their detection boundaries; predator-prey arms races select for evasion capability. The formal models for this structure exist in evolutionary medicine and adversarial machine learning. The cases examined here are the social version.

The mechanism has ancestors in the broader measurement literature. Goodhart's Law-"when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"-names the general dynamic by which criteria used for decision-making invite optimization. Campbell's (1979) reformulation extends this to social indicators: the more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures. Selection is downstream of these but is not identical to either. Goodhart and Campbell describe a general instability of measure-as-target; they do not specify the compositional consequence for the residual violator population. A Goodhart story is consistent with uniform harm distribution: if all actors optimize equally against the measure, harm per violation stays approximately constant. Selection additionally requires that actors are heterogeneous in detectability and that harm and undetectability are positively correlated, so that the actors who survive monitoring scrutiny are systematically more harmful on the original norm's criterion than those who were deterred. The contribution is compositional and distributional, not merely the observation that measures invite gaming.

A natural objection is that Selection is not a separate mechanism but simply what Deterrence produces when actors are heterogeneous. If actors differ in detection-evasion capability, the residual pool from deterrence acting on a heterogeneous population is, by construction, weighted toward capable evaders. The objection is formally correct: the underlying mechanism in both cases is actors responding to expected costs. What Selection adds is the question it asks, which the frequency account cannot reach: not "how many violators remain?" but "what are the properties of those who remain, specifically their harm distribution?" A monitoring program can satisfy the Deterrence metric-frequency falls-while worsening the Selection metric-expected harm per surviving violation rises. Distinguishing these outcomes requires different data and different evaluation criteria. The policy apparatus that tracks detected violations and their rate of change is measuring the wrong unit when Selection is operating.

This argument addresses a question the crowding literature does not reach. Frey and Jegen ask whether monitoring undermines compliance frequency by crowding out intrinsic motivation. That is a Deterrence/Substitution question. Selection is a question about the composition of the violation pool that survives any level of frequency reduction-a question the frequency account cannot reach.

## Case 1: Clinical Trial Registration

The pharmaceutical trial registration system offers the cleanest empirical entry point because the before-after comparison is unusually direct and the relevant evidence has been collected by independent researchers with no stake in the regulatory question.

In 2000, the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI) began requiring prospective registration in ClinicalTrials.gov for all trials it funded-the first systematic mandate in the United States. The Food and Drug Administration Amendments Act (FDAAA) of 2007 extended mandatory registration more broadly, covering all applicable clinical trials. Before these requirements, clinical trials were subject only to partial and voluntary registration. Selective non-publication of unfavorable results was an identified problem: trials showing positive outcomes for an intervention were substantially more likely to be published than trials showing null or negative outcomes, creating a biased evidentiary base for clinical practice. The registration mechanism addressed this directly. By requiring trial existence to be recorded before results were known, it made the non-publication decision visible: a registered trial with no published results is evidence of suppression.

Kaplan and Irvin (2015), examining major cardiovascular trials in the NHLBI cohort registered in ClinicalTrials.gov, found that trials registered before 2000-predating NHLBI's prospective registration requirement-reported positive results 57% of the time. Trials registered after 2000, under mandatory registration, reported positive results 8% of the time. This fifty-percentage-point shift documents Deterrence operating at scale: mandatory pre-registration, by making trial existence visible prior to results, substantially reduced selective non-publication of unfavorable findings.

One alternative reading of the Kaplan-Irvin shift deserves acknowledgment. The 57%→8% drop could be partly Substitution rather than Deterrence: the same investigators, now required to register, reporting the null results they previously withheld-a change in what gets reported without a change in underlying conduct. If so, the harm distribution before and after registration would be approximately unchanged. Against this reading: if Substitution were the primary mechanism, the change should be concentrated at investigators who had previously published selectively; Deterrence predicts a change concentrated among future trials, where the registration requirement removes the option of strategic post-hoc publication decisions. The Kaplan-Irvin data cannot fully partition the shift into these components; my reading of it as primarily Deterrence reflects the mechanism structure of mandatory pre-registration rather than a direct test of the decomposition.

The Selection question is what the shift leaves behind. Selective non-publication sits squarely inside the registration detection frontier: if a trial is registered but no results are published, the silence is evidence of suppression. The detection probability for this violation is high and grows with time and auditing attention.

The violations outside the registration frontier are different in kind. Outcome switching-specifying one primary endpoint in the registered protocol and analyzing a different endpoint when results are known-requires a comparison of the registered protocol against the published analysis. The registration system records what was planned; it does not automatically flag deviations. Mathieu et al. (2009), examining 323 trials with results published in high-impact journals, found that 31% had discrepancies between pre-specified and analyzed outcomes, with deviations systematically favoring statistically significant results. Analytical flexibility in covariate selection, missing-data handling, subgroup definition post-hoc, and the distinction between per-protocol and intention-to-treat analyses is unconstrained by registration, because registration specifies what to measure rather than how to analyze.

The Selection claim for this case: mandatory registration removed the detectable violation, clearing the easy-to-deter end of the distribution. Actors conducting outcome switching and analytical manipulation were already conducting these violations before registration. Registration did not cause them to begin; it did not stop them. After registration, these actors operate in an environment where their less sophisticated competitors have been deterred. The residual violation pool is selected for actors who were already employing the violations that registration cannot reach.

The distributional test that would confirm Selection for this case requires longitudinal data on violation composition before and after the registration requirement, with coding of violations by their detectability under the registration criterion. If the proportion of outcome-switching and analytical-manipulation violations grew relative to selective non-publication violations after 2000, Selection is confirmed. If violations declined uniformly across type, Deterrence alone explains the pattern. This data exists in principle in the ClinicalTrials.gov database and published protocols; protocol-versus-publication matching at scale is technically feasible and has been partially attempted. The systematic comparison has not been completed in a form that answers the Selection question directly.

The Selection mechanism is consistent with the available evidence. It is not yet proven by it.

## Case 2: Credit Rating Methodology Disclosure

The credit rating case illustrates Selection in a structurally different form: the compliance requirement and the selection mechanism are nearly identical. Disclosing how ratings are produced is transparency toward the market, but it is also a specification document for those who are trying to engineer securities to achieve target ratings. In this form of the mechanism-call it act-itself Selection-the compliance act itself is the optimization specification, not a side-effect of it.

Post-crisis financial regulation, including Dodd-Frank Section 932 (2010), required rating agencies to disclose their rating methodologies in substantially greater detail than previously required. The stated rationale was standard: transparency improves market function, enables investors to discount or adjust for systematic biases, and creates accountability. These are all defensible Deterrence-channel rationales.

The Selection analysis starts with the structure of the problem. Structured product design is an optimization problem. The issuer selects the composition of an underlying asset pool, the tranche structure, and the credit enhancements to maximize return on equity subject to achieving a target rating. Before methodology disclosure, the designer had to infer the rating agency's model from published ratings, analyst communications, and negotiation. After formal disclosure, the designer has the model. The optimization problem becomes substantially easier and more precise.

This case sits close to the boundary with Goodhart: disclosure of the rating model makes the model a target, and optimization follows. What specifically makes it a Selection story is the compositional claim about who does the optimizing. Structured product engineering requires sophisticated financial engineering, large balance sheets, legal infrastructure, and institutional access-resources concentrated in a subset of issuers. The issuers who optimize against the disclosed model are systematically different from those who do not: more sophisticated, capable of larger notional exposures, and capable of larger systemic harm when tail risks materialize. The residual pool of misrated securities therefore concentrates at the dangerous tail-not because all issuers are gaming the model equally, but because the issuers who can game it most precisely are exactly those whose positions pose the greatest systemic risk.

Coval, Jurek, and Stafford (2009) described the pre-crisis dynamic: rating agencies produced ratings using models calibrated on historical default correlations and loss assumptions. Structured product designers selected assets and structures that performed well within those models while bearing substantially greater tail risk than the models detected. Benmelech and Dlugosz (2009) documented systematic downgrade patterns in structured credit products-AAA-rated CDO tranches showed far higher default rates than comparably rated corporate bonds, suggesting systematic optimization against the models.

Two limitations apply. First, the most thoroughly documented engineering behavior-described by Coval et al. and Benmelech and Dlugosz-is pre-crisis (2004-2007), before Dodd-Frank's mandatory disclosure. The pre-crisis engineering was conducted against inferred models rather than formally disclosed ones. The act-itself Selection mechanism I am describing operates in two stages: the pre-crisis period demonstrates that optimization against inferred models produced adverse selection; the post-Dodd-Frank period creates an even sharper optimization target. Stage one is empirically documented; stage two is a structural prediction about what formal disclosure enables that the post-2010 literature has not yet fully traced. Second, Griffin and Maturana (2016) document rating shopping and post-rating adjustment behavior consistent with optimization against disclosed criteria, but the causal link to Dodd-Frank's specific disclosure requirements has not been isolated from other changes in the regulatory environment.

What the credit rating case contributes to the Selection argument that the clinical trial case does not: it shows that Selection can be embedded in the compliance act itself. Mandatory trial registration was designed to catch one violation and happened to miss others. Mandatory methodology disclosure was designed to provide transparency and happened to provide engineering specifications. The mechanism sits inside the compliance requirement, not adjacent to it.

## Case 3: Academic Integrity Software

I present this case in a weaker epistemic register than the first two. The mechanism hypothesis is structurally clear; the distributional evidence is thin. The case is a structured conjecture with specified observable implications rather than an established finding.

Turnitin-style plagiarism detection is deployed across a large fraction of higher education institutions globally. It detects textual similarity between submitted work and an indexed database of prior submissions, published papers, and web content. The detection frontier is defined by the similarity threshold: submissions that exceed the threshold in textual overlap with indexed sources are flagged; those below are not.

Deterrence is documented: rates of verbatim copying detected in academic misconduct proceedings have declined in jurisdictions with widespread plagiarism detection software (Curtis and Clare, 2017). The easy violation-copying text that appears in the indexed database-now carries high detection probability.

The Selection prediction is that the residual violation pool shifts toward violations outside the detection frontier. Three forms fit this description:

*Contract cheating*: commissioning original work from a third party, which produces text with no prior match in the database. The submitted work is genuine text, genuinely novel, and genuinely not the student's own.

*Sophisticated paraphrase*: rewriting substantially enough to fall below the similarity threshold while not producing genuine original analysis. This requires deliberate effort to evade detection-more work than verbatim copying, and producing a surface form that obscures the underlying violation.

*AI-assisted writing*: generating text from a language model, which produces novel text not indexed in the similarity database. This form was not part of the detection landscape at the time plagiarism software was designed, placing it definitionally outside the original detection frontier.

The academic integrity concern in each of these forms arguably exceeds the concern in verbatim copying on the criterion the norm was designed to protect: did the submitted work represent the student's own thinking and learning? Contract cheating involves complete outsourcing of intellectual effort. Verbatim copying at least involves selecting the relevant passage-a cognitive act, however minimal. If Selection is operating, the monitoring regime has produced an adverse composition shift in the violation pool even as detected violations decline.

What cannot be established from available evidence is the distributional claim: whether the proportion of violations represented by these sophisticated forms has grown relative to simple copying, or whether violations declined across the board with composition approximately constant. Newton (2018) and Rowland et al. (2018) document growth in contract cheating in recent surveys, and Clarke and Lancaster (2006) documented its existence before widespread software deployment, but these series are not constructed comparably enough to establish compositional change rather than absolute growth. The data required for the distributional test-longitudinal violation-type data across institutions before and after deployment-are not published in a form that answers the Selection question.

The case illustrates the detection-lag observable implication most clearly: as plagiarism detection technology improves to catch sophisticated paraphrase and AI-generated text, the violations it newly captures should, under Selection, be less harmful on the underlying integrity criterion than the violations already captured. Newly captured violations are those just above the old detection frontier; the most sophisticated violations remain outside the new frontier. Whether this prediction holds will be testable as next-generation detection technology is deployed.

## The General Structural Condition

Selection dominates Deterrence in expected harm when the following conditions hold.

**Detection asymmetry**: The monitoring criterion must have a frontier that is sharper for some violation types than others. This is a structural feature of any detection mechanism designed around past-pattern violations: the patterns in the training data are the center of the detection distribution; the frontier is by construction less sensitive to violations that do not resemble past patterns. A detection mechanism that catches all violations uniformly would eliminate Selection. No real detection mechanism achieves this.

**Harm correlation with undetectability**: The violations outside the detection frontier must be systematically worse on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect. This is not analytically guaranteed-and there are domains where the correlation runs the other way. In petty tax non-compliance, much aggregate harm comes from unsophisticated undercounting by many small actors rather than from sophisticated shelter construction by a capable few. In food safety, catastrophic failures sometimes involve unsophisticated errors by under-resourced operations rather than systematic evasion by sophisticated actors. The condition fails when capability and evasion skill are not strongly coupled with harm magnitude.

In the three cases examined here, the condition holds for structural reasons: outcome switching systematically corrupts the evidence base in ways more damaging than selective non-publication (publication bias can eventually be corrected by tracking registered trials; detecting outcome switching requires systematic protocol-versus-publication auditing that is not routine); model-optimized structured products concentrate tail risk in precisely the scenarios where repayment probability matters most; contract cheating and AI-assisted writing substitute entirely for the learning the original norm was designed to produce. The general structural argument-that violations requiring sophisticated multi-step concealment tend to be conducted by actors capable of larger harm on the same dimension-applies most clearly when institutional access is a prerequisite for the violation.

**A definitional consequence**: If Selection is producing a residual population, the monitoring must not deter the sophisticated violators. This holds by definition when detection probability for sophisticated violations is low enough that expected cost of detection is less than the benefit of violation. Rather than a third independent empirical condition, this is the consequence that Selection is operating at all.

The selection claim admits a compact formal statement. Suppose violators are characterized by detectability D ∈ [0,1] and potential harm h, with joint distribution P(D, h) across the population. Before monitoring, expected harm is proportional to E[h]. After a monitoring regime that raises expected costs substantially for actors with high D, the residual pool has expected harm E[h | D ≤ T] for some threshold T. Selection dominates Deterrence in expected total harm when the increase in harm per surviving violation exceeds what the frequency reduction recovers-formally, when Cov(h, D) < 0, meaning harm and detectability are negatively correlated across the violator population. The two conditions above are the structural reasons to expect this covariance to be negative in the domains examined here, and to be positive in domains-petty tax evasion, food safety small operators-where capability and harm magnitude are less tightly coupled.

The conditions also identify where Selection fails to operate. In monitoring regimes with genuinely broad-spectrum detection criteria-capturing violations regardless of sophistication-detection asymmetry is low and the selection argument weakens. The FAA's Aviation Safety Action Program (ASAP), which offers regulatory immunity for voluntarily self-reported safety violations, is a structural counterexample: by making disclosure non-punitive, it extends the detection frontier to violations that would otherwise remain hidden precisely because they are hard to detect externally. When the monitored population has a structural incentive to extend the detection frontier themselves, the selection pressure is largely removed.

An additional implication follows beyond the main mechanism: transparent, auditable, formally disclosed monitoring criteria may be more vulnerable to Selection than opaque ones. Formal disclosure of a detection methodology converts the monitoring criterion into an optimization target; a model, once disclosed to adversarial designers, becomes the specification for the next generation of evasion. This is not an argument for opacity. The well-established case for transparency in regulatory design-accountability to the monitored population, predictability, protection against capricious enforcement, due process, and capture-resistance-is not undone by this observation. A narrower claim survives: fully published quantitative methodologies invite systematic optimization in a way that qualitative, case-by-case assessments do not, because an algorithm can be reverse-engineered while a skilled reviewer exercising judgment cannot. The credit rating case shows this most clearly: formal disclosure of quantitative models creates a sharper optimization target than previously inferred models had been.

Ostrom's design principles for governing common-pool resources offer a structural alternative that neither requires opacity nor creates an optimization target. Her collective choice arrangement (Design Principle 3)-most individuals affected by the operational rules can participate in modifying those rules-and her community accountability of monitors (Design Principle 4)-monitors are themselves members of the community or are accountable to it-together address the adversarial dynamic. When those subject to monitoring participate in defining the detection criteria, and when monitors are accountable to that population, criteria reflect participants' own normative commitments. Engineering around criteria you helped design requires engineering around violations you yourself defined-a constraint that closes the gap between formal criterion and underlying violation. Whether this design logic transfers from common-pool resource governance to financial regulation or clinical research is a question the Selection analysis makes worth pursuing.

## Observable Implications

For the three cases examined, the evidence that would distinguish Selection from its alternatives takes three general forms.

**The harm distribution test**: Substitution predicts that harm per violation is constant before and after monitoring, because substance is preserved even as form changes. Selection predicts that harm per violation increases as detected violations decline. Observable: collect violation-harm data pre- and post-monitoring, coded by violation type. A shift in the harm distribution toward the severe end, concurrent with a decline in detected violations, is the Selection signature. A constant harm distribution with declining frequency is consistent with Deterrence or Substitution.

**The sophistication test**: Selection predicts violations become systematically more sophisticated over time as the detection frontier clears less sophisticated actors. Observable: code violations by markers of deliberateness, planning horizon, resource requirements, or institutional access. A sophistication trend that outpaces general behavioral trends is Selection evidence.

**The detection-lag test**: As monitoring technology improves and the detection frontier expands, Selection predicts that newly captured violations are less harmful than violations already captured-because newly captured violations are those just above the old frontier, while the most sophisticated violations remain outside the new frontier. A monitoring improvement that captures less-harmful violations than the prior regime is a Selection signature.

An important asymmetry applies to the detection-lag test across the two structural forms of Selection. In side-effect Selection-where the compliance act misses violations adjacent to those it targets, as in the clinical trial case-the detection frontier can be expanded by extending monitoring coverage, and the test predictions apply directly. In act-itself Selection-where the compliance act itself is the optimization specification, as in the credit rating case-expanding disclosure can compound the problem, because more precise disclosure means a sharper optimization target. The detection-lag test in act-itself cases would therefore predict that expanded disclosure captures fewer violations among sophisticated engineered products, because more precise optimization has stayed ahead of the expanded criterion. These two forms of Selection make opposite predictions about whether expanding the monitoring program helps or hurts.

These tests require data that monitoring programs rarely collect. Standard monitoring output tracks violations detected, type from among monitored categories, and sanction administered. It does not typically track harm magnitude by violation type, sophistication indicators, or comparison of within-frontier and beyond-frontier violation rates. Designing monitoring programs to produce these data is a precondition for evaluating Selection, and is itself a policy implication of the analysis.

## Conclusion

The compliance literature has a mature debate about whether monitoring produces deterrence or compliance theater, and whether it crowds out intrinsic motivation. It has not named or systematically analyzed Selection: the mechanism whereby effective monitoring of detectable violations concentrates the residual pool toward violations that are more sophisticated, harder to detect, and potentially more harmful on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect.

The three cases examined here support the mechanism hypothesis with varying degrees of empirical grounding. The pharmaceutical case is the strongest: the Kaplan-Irvin before-after evidence on publication bias is well-documented, the Deterrence effect is established, and the mechanism by which outcome switching and analytical manipulation survive the registration frontier is specified and documentable. The credit rating case demonstrates the structural form in which Selection can be embedded in the compliance act itself-formal disclosure as optimization specification rather than side-effect-with empirical motivation from the pre-crisis engineering behavior and confirmation of the full causal chain from mandatory disclosure to engineered selection pending. The academic integrity case is a structured conjecture with specified observable implications, awaiting the longitudinal distributional data that would confirm or disconfirm it.

The policy implication, if the mechanism holds at these scales, is that standard compliance program evaluation is measuring the wrong unit. Detected-violation count is not a sufficient statistic for expected harm when Selection is operating, because frequency reduction and harm-composition change are distinct outcomes that can move in opposite directions. A monitoring program that achieves a fifty-percent reduction in detected violations while concentrating the residual pool in violations twice as harmful may be net-neutral in expected harm, or worse-and the standard evaluative apparatus cannot distinguish this outcome from a genuinely successful regime. The evaluative question the standard apparatus asks-are detected violations declining?-is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a regime that is working on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect.

This is not an argument against monitoring programs. Deterrence effects are real, consequential, and the primary mechanism in most settings. But it is an argument for designing monitoring programs with Selection awareness: asking, before implementation, which violations fall outside the detection frontier; whether those violations are systematically worse on the underlying harm criterion; and whether any supplementary mechanism closes the gap at the sophisticated end of the distribution. Those questions are not currently standard practice in compliance program design. They should be.
