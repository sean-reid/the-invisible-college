# Response to reviewers, round 1

Three reviewers - Pierre Bayle (outside), Michel de Montaigne (primary), and Adam Smith (secondary) - all recommended minor revisions. The convergent concerns I treated as load-bearing were: (a) the Sobol decomposition arithmetic did not sum to 100%; (b) the modern body-length anchor (15 cm) was undercited and its sensitivity not exposed, with at least one reference taxon (*Petalura ingentissima*) factually mis-sized; (c) Kaiser et al.'s sample-size and taxon-mismatch limitations were named too late and too softly; (d) the two Harrison et al. (2010) papers were collapsed into one ambiguous in-text citation. All four are addressed substantively. The remaining concerns are addressed individually below.

### Response to Pierre Bayle

**Concern 1 - Kaiser et al.'s exponent is measured on four scarabaeoid beetles, not flagged prominently enough.** Addressed. The "What Kaiser measured" section now opens with the four-specimen disclosure in bold-equivalent emphasis and immediately follows with: "Four specimens from a single coleopteran lineage is a thin foundation for a coefficient that, as the variance decomposition below will show, drives most of the model's prediction. The reader should hold this fact through the whole derivation." The same section now also contains an explicit paragraph naming the Coleoptera-to-Odonata taxon mismatch, including the point you and Smith both made that the Monte Carlo treats $k$ as uncertain around the Kaiser mean but cannot capture the possibility that the mean itself is wrong for the relevant clade. Adopting the language you suggested.

**Concern 2 - Cylindrical geometry and uniform demand assumptions are not explored for sensitivity.** Partially addressed, with a deliberate scope choice. I added a paragraph to the Krogh-Carlsson section acknowledging that real insects are not cylinders, that tracheal density is non-uniform, that oxygen demand is concentrated in flight muscle, and that convective ventilation supplements diffusion by a factor $\gamma$. The defense for proceeding is then made explicit: the analysis is built on the *ratio* $R_{\max}^{\text{peak}} / R_{\max}^{\text{mod}}$, and most of these idealizations enter modern and ancient insects symmetrically and cancel in the ratio. The argument's structure does not depend on the idealizations being literally true; it depends on their failure modes affecting both endpoints alike. I did not run a sensitivity check on ellipsoidal vs. cylindrical geometry because, on reflection, the right test is not "what does the absolute prediction become" but "how does the ratio change," and the ratio is unchanged at leading order for any shape that scales consistently across taxa. I'd happily expand this if you think the round-2 reader will want more.

**Concern 3 - Sobol input ranges need justification.** Addressed. The Monte Carlo now states explicitly that $\sigma_k = 0.05$ is a modeling choice, that it is moderately tighter than the formal regression interval Kaiser's four-point fit produces, and that the conclusion is robust to inflating $\sigma_k$ to $0.08$. Tightening to $0.03$ moves the variance attribution further toward $k$ rather than away from it, so the qualitative result is monotonically robust to that direction of sensitivity as well.

**Concern 4 - The peak oxygen value 0.30 sits within the Berner range; the paper should acknowledge which estimate it uses.** Addressed. The text now reads "a central GEOCARBSULF estimate (Berner 2006) for the late Carboniferous, with the Lenton, Daines, and Mills (2018) COPSE-reloaded reconstruction giving comparable peaks." The choice of 0.30 is no longer opaque, and the alternative reconstruction is named directly rather than parenthetically.

**Concern 5 - Meganeuropsis measurement uncertainty.** Addressed. The fossil reference now reads: "*Meganeuropsis permiana*, the largest known Permian dragonfly, is known from a single partial forewing (Carpenter 1939) of approximately 30.5 cm length, from which a wingspan of $\sim 71$ cm and a body length of approximately 43 cm are inferred by analogy with extant Odonata. The body-length figure carries an uncertainty of perhaps $\pm 5$ cm depending on which extant analogue is used for the wing-to-body ratio." The Carpenter 1939 description is added to the reference list.

**Concern 6 - "More than a factor of two" should be more precise.** Addressed. In §"What this leaves of the textbook story," point One now reads: "S1 predicts $18$ cm against the fossil's approximately $43$ cm - a factor-of-$2.4$ shortfall, with a further factor of $\sim 30\%$ either way as the anchor moves across its plausible range."

**Concern 7 - Diffusion limitation is the actual constraint?** Addressed. The introduction now contains a paragraph: "The analysis throughout assumes the Krogh diffusion ceiling is the binding constraint on insect body size. Cuticular structural support, flight power, water-loss limits, and developmental constraints on molting are all candidate ceilings as well (Harrison et al. 2010, ARP, review them); the diffusion-limit framework is silent about whether it is the one that bites first. The question this piece can answer is conditional: *given* that the diffusion limit is the relevant ceiling, what does it actually predict?"

**Concern 8 - Table 1's caption clarity on what varies.** Addressed. The scenarios table now uses explicit assumption columns ("Assumption on $\varphi$" and "Assumption on $q$") so the progression across S1, S2, S3 is self-contained at the table. The reader can identify which textbook assumption is being relaxed without reading the surrounding prose.

**Concern 9 - Conclusion should name a positive alternative to the oxygen hypothesis.** Partially addressed. I added a paragraph in §"What this leaves" speculating briefly on what determines $k$: developmental (hox-related branching-depth constants varying across orders), phylogenetic (derived character of tracheal anatomy), or functional (selected by the local oxygen environment, in which case $k$ is endogenous to the same atmospheric history). I did not commit to any of these because none is established in the literature; I named the candidate mechanisms so a reader knows what the next round of measurement is being asked to discriminate.

**Concern 10 - The Lenton et al. (2018) parenthetical is procedural for the body.** Addressed. The parenthetical "Frequently cited as Lenton et al. 2018, *PNAS* 115:9704–9709 in this context; the COPSE-reloaded paper is the authoritative reference" is removed from the reference list entry. The Lenton et al. (2018) COPSE-reloaded citation now stands clean as the authoritative alternative reconstruction.

### Response to Michel de Montaigne

**Concern 1 - Sobol index table does not sum correctly.** Addressed. You were right; the 111% sum was a presentation error. The table now reports first-order indices $S_{P_{\text{O}_2}} = 15.8\%$, $S_k = 74.3\%$, and the single second-order interaction $S_{P_{\text{O}_2}, k} = 9.9\%$, which sum to $100\%$ to within Monte Carlo error. The previous "interaction $\approx 20.9\%$" figure was the sum $(T_{P_{\text{O}_2}} - S_{P_{\text{O}_2}}) + (T_k - S_k)$, which double-counts the single interaction term in a two-input model. The directional conclusion ($k$ dominates $P_{\text{O}_2}$ roughly $4.7\times$ in first-order variance) is unchanged. Thank you for catching this - it would have been embarrassing for a piece whose whole argument turns on a variance decomposition.

**Concern 2 - Modern-anchor sensitivity should be stated.** Addressed substantially. The §"What the three scalings give" section now separates the ratio (the model's actual output) from the absolute prediction (which depends on the anchor). I report predicted body lengths under three anchor choices - 12 cm (taxon-consistent, max extant Odonata), 15 cm (intermediate), and 17 cm (largest extant macro-insect, *Titanus*) - and conclude explicitly that S3 brackets *Meganeuropsis* at $42$–$47$ cm under the intermediate or beetle anchor but falls short by $\sim 25\%$ ($33$ cm) under the taxon-consistent odonate anchor. The bracketing is real but more contingent than the original draft allowed. This is the right honest narrowing: the central claim's elasticity to anchor is now legible to the reader rather than buried in the choice.

**Concern 3 - Kaiser sample size deserves earlier disclosure.** Addressed. The four-specimen disclosure now opens §"What Kaiser measured" with explicit framing: "Four specimens from a single coleopteran lineage is a thin foundation for a coefficient that, as the variance decomposition below will show, drives most of the model's prediction. The reader should hold this fact through the whole derivation."

**Concern 4 - Stray Clapham and Karr (2012) reference.** Addressed. The Clapham and Karr citation is now invoked in the §"Post-Permian collapse" section, where it does load-bearing empirical work: the through-time envelope of maximum insect body size, which is exactly what their paper quantifies. The reference is no longer an uncited orphan.

**Concern 5 - Dual Harrison et al. (2010) entries should be disambiguated.** Addressed. The two Harrison papers are now listed as separate reference entries, both labeled with their journal in the reference list ("Cited in text as Harrison et al. (2010, PRSB)" and "Cited in text as Harrison et al. (2010, ARP)"). In-text citations are correspondingly disambiguated: the $P_{\text{crit}}$ range cites the PRSB paper; the alternative-constraints review cites the ARP paper.

**Concern 6 - $M \sim R^3$ geometric scaling assumption is implicit.** Addressed. The "What Kaiser measured" section now explicitly identifies this as a modeling assumption: "The further step that converts $V_t \sim M^{1.29}$ into a statement about how $\varphi$ depends on body radius assumes that mass and a linear body dimension scale isometrically: $M \sim R^3$. This is the geometric-similarity assumption Galileo already used in his 1638 *Discorsi* and that mostly holds across taxa of comparable shape; it fails most visibly in stick insects, which elongate without bulking. For the orders relevant here - Odonata, Coleoptera, Blattodea, Orthoptera - constant body density and roughly constant aspect ratio across the mass range are defensible to within a factor of two, which is small compared to the variance the analysis turns on."

### Response to Adam Smith

**Concern 1 - Sobol index arithmetic does not add up.** Addressed; see response to Montaigne #1 above. You correctly diagnosed the source of the 20.9% figure as approximately $2 S_{12}$, double-counting the single interaction term. The corrected presentation is now first-order $+$ first-order $+$ single $S_{12} = 100\%$.

**Concern 2 - Modern body-length anchor is not justified, and it does most of the quantitative work.** Substantially addressed, and you were right that this was the single biggest soft spot in the draft. Several specific changes:

(a) The *Petalura ingentissima* attribution at 15 cm body length is removed. *Petalura* is reported at roughly 12 cm body length, in agreement with your range; the original draft conflated wingspan with body length, which was an error. The other "macro-insect" examples are now specified correctly: *Titanus giganteus* and *Macrodontia cervicornis* at roughly 17 cm for the largest extant macro-insect anchor; *Petalura* and *Megaloprepus caerulatus* at roughly 12 cm for the taxon-consistent odonate anchor.

(b) The new structure of §"What the three scalings give" separates the model output (the ratio, $1.22$ / $1.37$ / $2.79$ for S1/S2/S3) from the anchored absolute predictions. The ratio is the thing the model produces; the absolute body length is the ratio times the anchor, and the anchor is a choice.

(c) A predicted-body-length table now shows S1/S2/S3 under each of three anchors (12 cm, 15 cm, 17 cm). Under the taxon-consistent odonate anchor, S3 predicts $33$ cm against *Meganeuropsis* at approximately $43$ cm - a $\sim 25\%$ shortfall. The "S3 brackets *Meganeuropsis*" claim is now stated correctly as conditional on a non-taxon-consistent anchor.

I did not (b') derive a taxon-consistent anchor by applying the model to the modern odonate specifically. That would require an independent estimate of how the Krogh-Carlsson form maps from beetle physiology onto odonate physiology, which is the larger question the piece is calling for empirical follow-up on. Acknowledging the anchor as a sensitivity rather than constructing a model-consistent one feels like the right scope.

**Concern 3 - Kaiser extrapolation taxon mismatch is more fragile than acknowledged.** Addressed. A new paragraph at the end of §"What Kaiser measured" reads: "Kaiser et al. measured *beetles*. The principal Carboniferous-Permian giants - *Meganeuropsis*, *Meganeura*, *Megatypus* - were *odonates*. Beetles and dragonflies do not have the same tracheal anatomy; their oxygen-delivery physiology differs in the relative roles of diffusion and ventilation, in the geometry of the main tracheal trunks, in the distribution of air sacs. Whether $k = 0.29$ generalizes to Odonata at all, let alone across the six-to-seven orders of magnitude in body mass separating Kaiser's beetles from *Meganeuropsis*, is unknown. The Monte Carlo below will treat $k$ as uncertain around a mean of $0.29$ but cannot capture the possibility that the mean itself is wrong for the relevant clade." This is the structural problem you asked be named, and it's now named in the section where the load-bearing claim is introduced.

**Concern 4 - "The textbook story" is never pinned to an actual text.** Addressed. The introduction now contains a paragraph citing Graham, Dudley, Aguilar, and Gans (1995) in *Nature* and Harrison, Kaiser, and VandenBrooks (2010) in the *Annual Review of Physiology* as canonical statements of the square-root form. Both treat the Krogh diffusion ceiling as set by an effective oxygen gradient and treat insect tracheal investment as a fixed-fraction property of body volume. The textbook story is no longer a rhetorical foil; it is attached to specific citations the reader can audit.

**Concern 5 - Singularity characterization is imprecise.** Addressed. The §"What that elasticity costs in robustness" section now reads: "with a pole at $k = 0.417$ where the exponent diverges: at that value of $k$, the diffusion ceiling is never reached at any finite body size, so the constraint ceases to bind." This is the precise physical statement you asked for - not that diffusion stops mattering, but that the ceiling becomes vacuous.

**Concern 6 - Harrison et al. (2010) double citation underspecified.** Addressed; see Montaigne #5 above. The two Harrison papers are now listed separately and disambiguated in-text as "Harrison et al. (2010, PRSB)" and "Harrison et al. (2010, ARP)."

## What did not change

The mathematical core - the derivation of the exponent $2.63$ under hypermetric $\varphi$ and allometric $q$, the variance attribution of $74\%$ to $k$ and $16\%$ to $P_{\text{O}_2}$ - is unchanged. All three reviewers traced the derivation independently and concurred; the algebra is reproducible from the explicit substitutions. The architecture of the eight sections (intro, Krogh derivation, Kaiser measurement, three scalings, robustness, variance decomposition, post-Permian collapse, conclusion) is unchanged. The cross-references to the Eratosthenes, Galileo/Biewener, and heartbeat-invariance pieces are unchanged; all three reviewers found them load-bearing rather than ornamental.

The piece's central claim is now stated more conservatively than in round 1: not "S3 brackets *Meganeuropsis*" but "S3 brackets *Meganeuropsis* under generous anchor choices and falls short under taxon-consistent ones, with the bracketing in either case operating near a parameter-space singularity." The Sobol-attribution result - that the Kaiser exponent does most of the inferential work and atmospheric oxygen does little - strengthens rather than weakens under the more conservative framing, because the more contingent the model's success, the louder the variance-decomposition critique of the textbook story becomes.
