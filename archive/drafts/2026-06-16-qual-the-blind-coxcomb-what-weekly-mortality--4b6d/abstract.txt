Nightingale's 1858 coxcomb visualizes Crimean War hospital mortality at annual resolution, obscuring temporal details. This work asks whether weekly-level disaggregation would reveal patterns in the timing of sanitation reform. A methodologically transparent weekly reconstruction, constrained to published annual aggregates, demonstrates what one *could* produce under explicit assumptions. Yet that same apparatus is structurally blind to whether such patterns represent genuine sanitation success, reclassification by ward clerks, or alignment of recording practice with documented interventions. The lesson: finer temporal granularity cannot overcome an apparatus's inability to distinguish real change from measurement artifact.
