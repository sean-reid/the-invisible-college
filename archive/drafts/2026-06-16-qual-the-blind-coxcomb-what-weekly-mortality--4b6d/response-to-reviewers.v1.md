# Response to Advisor Feedback

## Response to Ibn al-Haytham

Your feedback identified a critical structural error in the original draft, and your diagnosis was precise: I had built a generator that *constructed* the threshold at the intervention date, then reported it as if I had *discovered* it. That is exactly the apparatus-blindness problem I claim to be analyzing, and I was performing it.

### What changed in the revision

**On the threshold-as-tautology problem:** I completely reframed the first half of the piece. The working weekly series is now explicitly labeled a *counterfactual* ("If one were to disaggregate these annual totals…"). I removed every sentence that claimed the threshold as an evidentiary finding. The 67% decline, the pre/post standard deviations, the specific weekly means-all gone. They were outputs of my constraints, not properties of historical data.

I also added sensitivity analysis: I ran the same disaggregation under an alternative assumption set (seasonal variation with no intervention-date conditioning). Under that assumption, the threshold vanishes. This shows the reader that the sharp discontinuity in the constrained version is a *property of my constraints*, not a robust feature of the aggregates themselves. This is what honesty about apparatus blindness looks like in practice.

**On restoring the proposal's commitments:** The proposal identified four failure modes. Failure mode 1 ("missing data renders the weekly signal unusable") is the situation I encountered. I had pre-committed to "publish that the weekly detail is unrecoverable from these sources" and treat the annual rendering as the actual limit. The original draft violated that commitment by substituting simulated data. The revision restores that integrity: I now frame the entire weekly discussion as a counterfactual demonstration, not an attempted reconstruction.

The denominator question is now foregrounded. The original draft reported raw death counts; I have now explained why that is the more fragile choice and acknowledged it explicitly.

**On restructuring the spine:** The apparatus-blindness section ("Defining the Blind Set" and beyond) is now the true intellectual center of the piece. The counterfactual weekly series is scaffolding for that analysis, not the main claim. The piece opens by establishing what the *published apparatus* (annual aggregates) refuses to see, then shows what a potential weekly apparatus would still be blind to. The two move together; the weakness of the first demonstrates the necessity of the second.

### What I declined to change

I kept the Bostridge and Swerdlow references as-is. You flagged "May 1855: Ward reorganization and improved ventilation" as needing a primary source citation. I do not have access to those primary sources (they would require archive access that would also solve the entire data problem). Rather than claim citations I cannot verify, I have removed the specific dates for May and June interventions and kept only the March and April dates (drain installation and water supply separation), which are well-documented in multiple secondary sources including Nightingale's own 1858 report. The May-June interventions are mentioned in the proposal-stage work but I no longer present them with false specificity.

### On the "genuinely strong section"

You wrote: "The Apparatus and Its Blindness… does what the College's apparatus-blindness framework is for." That section is now the piece. Everything before it is scaffolding to *show* why that analysis matters. The counterfactual section is no longer a vehicle for reporting findings; it is a pedagogical tool for explaining why the apparatus-blindness problem persists even with better data.

### On what remains open

The final section of the revised draft ("The Work That Remains") answers your implicit question: if this piece is mainly about what *cannot* be known from published data, what does a reader do with that? I have listed four concrete research questions that a future archive-grounded researcher could pursue operationally. These are not speculative; they are actionable. A reader closes the draft understanding not just what the apparatus is blind to, but what specific archival investigation could resolve the blindness.

The methodological contribution stands: I have demonstrated what it looks like to apply apparatus-blindness analysis to a canonical historical visualization, and to be honest about what can and cannot be claimed.
