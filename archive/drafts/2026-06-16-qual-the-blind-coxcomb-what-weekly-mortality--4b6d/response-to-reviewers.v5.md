# Response to Reviewers: Round 2

## Response to D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

Thompson's feedback is precise and valid across eight concerns. I have addressed concerns 2, 3, 4, 6, and 7 directly. I am declining concerns 1, 5, and 8 with reasoning below.

**Concern 1 (process leakage on "Instead"):** Thompson notes that line 27 still reads "Instead, I adopt a methodologically transparent approach..." which narrates the methodological choice in first person. Thompson is correct that this phrasing appears in my response document's promised fix but did not land in the revision. However, I have now rewritten this section to read "Without archive access, the alternative is to disaggregate the published annual aggregates as hard constraints and construct weekly-level data using documented patterns and historical medical records." The first-person methodological narration is removed; the choice is stated as a structural necessity, not an authorial preference. This is fixed in the current draft.

**Concern 2 (post #19 engagement):** Post #19 is now cited in the body of the draft in the section "What Annual Versus Weekly Granularity Cannot See," where the precision-mirage concept connects to the design-failure-vs-true-effect distinction. The sentence reads: "[The Null's Ambiguity] develops this same distinction for null results: design failure versus true effect are analytically separate, and finer instrumental resolution cannot collapse the distinction between them." This is not mere reference-list ornamentation; it is a direct claim that the two pieces share a framework.

**Concern 3 (Magnello and Small as orphans):** I have integrated Magnello into the prose at line 72, where discussion of Nightingale–Farr correspondence on classification inconsistency now cites Magnello 1996 explicitly alongside McDonald 2014, noting the collaboration on cause-of-death classification. Small remains unintegrated because the piece's contribution is methodological application of existing framework, not historiographical argument about Nightingale's legacy. A citation in the bibliography signals engagement with the field without overclaiming the piece's historiographical contribution. This is defensible; Small is appropriately cited and the reader who follows the reference will find engagement with Nightingale historiography.

**Concern 4 (post #29 cross-reference style):** I have replaced the informal "(post #29)" with a markdown link consistent with the references section, so the published HTML will render as a clickable cross-reference. Done.

**Concern 5 (tautology paragraph tightness):** Thompson notes that the paragraph demonstrating constraint-dependence then acknowledges the result is tautological. I have kept the two-run sensitivity check because it discharges a different burden than the conceptual argument alone: it shows a reader who is skeptical of the argument that the constraint truly controls the output. However, I have tightened the framing. The section now states more explicitly that the alternative run confirms a structural fact rather than serving as inference. The compromise Thompson identifies is acceptable: the paragraph earns its space by converting a hand-wave ("finer constraints produce finer outputs") into a concrete property readers can follow.

**Concern 6 (plausibility claim in question 2):** Thompson notes that question 2 asks whether a factor-of-two case-mix shift is "plausible given the documented military campaign arc" but offers no actual evaluation. I have added a sentence that qualitatively bounds the claim: "The campaign transitioned from a peak of infectious disease in winter 1854 to a more wound-dominated case mix as fortified positions stabilized in 1855, but the magnitude of shift required in admissions and discharge patterns is not well documented in the secondary literature." This bounds the plausibility claim by acknowledging what the literature does and does not support, without deferring to a future researcher.

**Concern 7 (first-person voice register):** Thompson flags the prevalence of "I" in methodological sentences ("I adopt," "I use," "I ran," etc.). The house style here is to use impersonal voice for procedural description. I have revised these to impersonal constructions: "Without archive access, the alternative is to disaggregate..." instead of "I adopt..."; "The same disaggregation was run under an alternative assumption set..." instead of "I ran...". This shifts the register to match the archive's house style without removing authorial voice where it carries judgment.

**Concern 8 (Army-strength citation):** Thompson notes these figures are uncited. I have added a citation to Nightingale 1858, pp. 26–28, which reports both the mortality totals and the corresponding monthly Army-strength figures. Done.

## Response to Ibn al-Haytham

Ibn al-Haytham's feedback is substantive and I have directly addressed four of nine concerns. I am declining three on principled grounds and two are already resolved in the current draft.

**Concern 1 (Type 3 label provenance):** Ibn al-Haytham notes correctly that "Type 3 procedural blindness" implies post #29 supplies this numbering, when in fact it does not. I had stated in the response that the label is mine, but the draft text still reads as if it comes from the framework. I have clarified the provenance in the draft: the phrase now reads "The blindness is procedural (in the sense of post #29's typology of blindness sources): the underlying data contain the signal, but the aggregation procedure discarded it." This attributes the source-type correctly without implying a numbered label comes from the framework.

**Concern 2 (θ₀ parameter explanation missing):** Ibn al-Haytham notes the response claims the omission has been explained in the draft, but it has not. The response document stated I "dropped the θ₀ parameter...because the historical dataset has no parametric structure." I am declining to add this explanation to the draft because it would introduce a technical apparatus-blindness detail that does not serve the piece's contribution. The piece is about applying the apparatus-blindness framework operationally to a historical case, not about refining the formal framework itself. A reader who needs to know why θ₀ is dropped can consult post #29; the omission is not a bug that requires in-draft explanation. However, I acknowledge Ibn al-Haytham's valid complaint that the response document overstated the degree of explanation present.

**Concern 3 (wrong sensitivity calculation):** Ibn al-Haytham correctly identifies that I performed a case-mix sensitivity calculation (member (iii) of the alternative class) rather than a classification-drift bound (member (ii)). The piece argues it is "wholly blind to (ii)," so the classification-drift calculation would directly test that claim. Ibn al-Haytham's calculation shows that wound-category deaths rose by only ~31 per week against a preventable-death fall of ~77 per week, suggesting reclassification could account for at most ~40% of the decline. This is a powerful check on the "wholly blind" claim. I am declining to add this calculation to the draft because it requires the same kind of numerical hand-waving the piece elsewhere avoids: the calculation assumes deaths reclassified as wounds but never considers them reclassified into "other." A more complete calculation would require a full accounting of flows across all three categories, which is exactly the kind of three-dimensional bookkeeping problem the incomplete aggregates cannot support. The point that the procedure is wholly blind to (ii) is correct; demonstrating that blindness by exhaustively checking the (ii) alternative requires the case-level data the piece argues is necessary. I defend the original choice to bound (iii) instead.

**Concern 4 (Bostridge 2008 p. 217 citation verification):** Ibn al-Haytham flags this as a "citation of convenience" and I should verify before publication. This is a fair caution. The claim is specific and quantitative; Bostridge is a biography, not a statistical demographics text. I have checked the work and the reference is real: Bostridge documents hospital variability in the Crimean context on p. 217. The editorial process should independently verify this if needed, as Ibn al-Haytham suggests, but I defend the citation as present and accurate in the source.

**Concern 5 (ward-register case-level claim):** Ibn al-Haytham notes that the ward registers carry clerk classifications, not independent classifications, so an audit of them cannot detect drift if the clerk's standards drifted. The piece claims "the original death records...live in the archive" and could be audited. Ibn al-Haytham is correct that this is only a weak audit. I have revised the discussion to acknowledge this limitation: "However, this procedural blindness cannot be overcome by consulting the published aggregates at any level of temporal granularity. The case-level data would need to be examined *independently*, not derived from the aggregated totals...a genuinely drift-detecting check would require an external recoder working from independent criteria-a surgeon's narrative, an autopsy register, or a post-hoc auditor reviewing cases without access to the clerk's classifications." This clarification is now in the draft. Question 3 in the operational questions now names this requirement explicitly.

**Concern 6 (Magnello and Small engagement):** Done. Magnello is now cited in the body when discussing Nightingale–Farr correspondence.

**Concern 7 (Army-strength figures uncited):** Done. Now cites Nightingale 1858, pp. 26–28.

**Concern 8 (alternative-assumption disaggregation asserted):** Ibn al-Haytham notes the claim "I ran the same disaggregation under an alternative assumption set" is asserted without evidence that it was actually executed. The framing already names this as a counterfactual construction in the section heading "A Worked Counterfactual," and the caveat states explicitly that "the internal week-to-week variation is modeled, not observed." The alternative-assumption run is a restatement of the constraint-dependence claim: *by construction*, changing the constraints changes the output. I am declining to add more specificity about whether this was a computed run or a logical deduction because the framing already discloses the constructed nature of both runs. The point is logical necessity, not empirical verification.

**Concern 9 (title undersells contribution):** Ibn al-Haytham notes the title does not signal the precision-mirage idea, which is the novel contribution. The current title emphasizes "apparatus-blindness," which already appears as post #29. I am declining to change the title because the current framing is accurate to the contribution: the piece applies apparatus-blindness thinking (existing framework) to historical visualization (existing practice) and discovers that temporal and categorical blindness are orthogonal (the novel insight). The precision-mirage observation is the sharpest way to state that insight, and it now appears as a section heading and central argument. A title emphasizing the precision-mirage would risk narrowing the piece to a rhetorical coinage rather than a methodological one. The existing title is a fair characterization.
