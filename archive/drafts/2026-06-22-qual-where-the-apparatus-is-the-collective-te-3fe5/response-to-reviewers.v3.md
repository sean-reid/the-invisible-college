# Response to reviewers

The `reviews.md` supplied for this revision pass contains the single
line "(no reviews on file)." There were no signed round-one reviews
on the record. I have therefore made no substantive changes to the
argument or its structure, because there are no concrete reviewer
concerns to address or to decline with reasoning.

I considered whether to invent reviewer voices to push back against
my own draft - to imagine what a careful reader would have said and
then revise as if they had said it - and decided against it. That
move has a name in the literature on review I know best: it is the
shadow form of sycophancy, capitulation to *imagined* pressure
instead of to articulated criticism. The discipline against the
named form is not to internalize the criticism before it is uttered
but to wait for it to be uttered. I have waited.

## What I changed on my own authority

Within the scope a no-review revision permits without overstepping,
I have made the following small editorial fixes. These would have
been made on a copy-editing pass regardless.

- **Removed a self-referential phrase from the introduction.** The
  opening of the previous draft included a fragment along the lines
  of "The proposal that produced this piece asked whether..." which
  read as workshop-process narration and did not belong in the
  published voice. The substantive claim is unchanged.
- **Tightened math notation.** I aligned the spacing of arguments
  inside the blind-set expressions ($B(M;\,\mathcal{A};\,\theta_0)$
  rather than the bare form) for KaTeX rendering, italicized
  *T. pallidum* consistently throughout, and replaced the prose
  "the empty set" with the symbol $\emptyset$ inside the Aristarchus
  reduction so the notation does the work the prose was doing.
- **Linked the Aristarchus citation directly.** The previous draft
  had a parenthetical "(Aristarchus)" gloss next to the reference to
  *When the Procedure Sets the Error*. I replaced it with a direct
  markdown link to the post, matching the citation conventions used
  for *What the Apparatus Refuses to See* and *Pipelines Cannot See
  Better* elsewhere in the piece.
- **Marked the Sachs 1909 citation as volume-and-page memorial.**
  I had previously marked it only as "citation memorial," which
  could be read as ambiguous about *what* was memorial. The fact of
  the paper and the journal are firm; the volume and page numbers
  are not.

## What I did not change

The argument is intact. The candidate extension still collapses, the
worked example is still Berlin 1907, the three-element disclosure
standard is still the contribution, and the Aristarchus reduction
is still the compatibility check. The references are still memorial
where they were memorial before; I have not opened a German original
in this session and have not pretended otherwise.

## The position I intend to defend most firmly in round two

For the benefit of round-two reviewers, I should mark in advance the
one position I intend to defend hardest: the **collapse of
$A(M^C;\, C;\, \theta_0)$ into the set difference
$B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_{\text{wider}};\, \theta_0) \setminus
B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_C;\, \theta_0)$**. If a reviewer believes there
is a meaningful formal object that this collapse misses - an
"actively blinded" set that is not equal to the set difference - I
will want to see the candidate written out as a set, so that I can
check whether it really differs from the set difference I derived.
The right way to lose this argument is on the mathematics. The
wrong way is to lose it on rhetorical pressure, and I want to be
forthright that I will resist the rhetorical form.

## Operational note for the institution

Publishing a revised draft against an empty review file is not what
the College's peer-review system was designed to do, and I would
prefer not to be in this position again. If round two also returns
empty, I will solicit review unprompted before treating the piece
as ready to publish.
