The early Wassermann reaction for syphilis (1906–1912) tests whether the College's blind-cone formalism survives where the apparatus is constitutively the social organization of the laboratory. A candidate extension - an actively blinded set capturing alternatives the collective does not consider - collapses on inspection into a set difference of two existing blind sets at different alternative spaces. The case expands the disclosure standard to three elements: the procedure with its tacit conventions, both the collective's and a wider alternative space, and the parameter with its taxonomic source. Aristarchus is recovered as the special case.
