# The Rhetoric of Not-Knowing: When the Confession of Ignorance Is Already an Argument

There is a move in intellectual writing that looks like modesty and functions as a weapon. The writer pauses in the middle of an argument, disavows certainty, and then continues. "I do not know." "The evidence is ambiguous." "I may be wrong about this." These phrases appear in every kind of serious prose — philosophical, scientific, essayistic, critical — and their appearance feels reassuring. The writer is being honest. They are not overclaiming. They are showing their work.

But the reassurance may be premature. Confessions of ignorance are doing at least three different things depending on who is writing them, in what context, and to what end. They can report an actual epistemic state. They can perform the conventions of a discourse — the "may" and "might" and "it seems" that mark a piece as belonging to a community that values circumspection. Or they can function as a rhetorical maneuver: the writer uses the true forms of uncertainty as cover, professing not-knowing not because they are genuinely uncertain, but because a clear position is costly and a hedged one is safer. The question this essay takes up is whether those three functions can be distinguished from the text alone — and what the attempt to distinguish them reveals about the relationship between rhetoric and epistemology.

## Three Modes of Not-Knowing

The simplest case is what I will call **sincere uncertainty**: the writer does not know the answer to a question they are investigating, says so, and the saying so is a straightforward report of that epistemic state. This happens, but it is rarer in polished writing than it appears. By the time an essay is finished, the writer usually knows more than they did when they started; the expressions of not-knowing that survive revision are either notes toward future investigation or framing devices for the argument to follow. Pure sincere uncertainty in published writing is mostly found at the edges: in a conclusion's final paragraph, in a footnote that acknowledges a gap, in a methodological aside.

The commoner case, empirically, is what Ken Hyland documented across two studies of scientific writing: **conventional hedging**, in which uncertainty language is distributed not according to actual epistemic state but according to the norms of the discourse community. A junior researcher hedges more than a senior one making the same claim. Economists hedge differently than physicists on comparable evidence. The modal verbs ("may," "might," "could"), approximators ("around," "roughly"), and attribution shields ("data suggest," "the evidence indicates") that populate scientific articles are performing membership in a community that values circumspection, not reporting degrees of confidence. Hyland's finding is not that scientists are being dishonest; it is that the language of uncertainty has social functions that run parallel to and sometimes dominate its epistemic ones.

Hyland's specific linguistic markers — the modal verbs and approximators — do not transfer directly from scientific articles to philosophical or literary prose. Montaigne does not write "it may be that the senses deceive us." His skepticism is structural, built into the architecture of the essay: claim, counterexample, complication, suspension. The surface forms are different. But the underlying mechanism Hyland identified holds in any discourse: uncertainty language can be governed by convention, by political environment, and by career position rather than by epistemic state. The mechanism transfers even when the markers do not.

The third mode — the one that most rewards attention — is what I will call **strategic uncertainty**. Frankfurt's taxonomy in *On Bullshit* distinguishes the liar, who cares about truth and works to conceal it, from the bullshitter, who is indifferent to truth. Strategic uncertainty is a third option in that space: the writer cares about truth and has a view, but uses the forms of uncertainty as a shield against commitment. This is not lying — the hedged claim may be literally true, and the writer may genuinely hold some uncertainty about it. It is using the *appearance* of epistemic humility to avoid the exposure that a direct claim would require. The liar works against the truth; the strategic uncertain works around commitment.

These three modes coexist and overlap. Most sophisticated writing contains all three in different proportions. The question is whether a reader, working from the text alone, can identify which is operating at any given moment.

## Plato's Apology: The Aggressive Confession

The *Apology* of Plato offers the most famous confession of ignorance in the Western tradition, and it is, by any diagnostic reading, a strategic one.

Socrates tells the Athenian jury that after the oracle's pronouncement — that no one was wiser than Socrates — he investigated the city's reputed wise men and found that they knew no more than he did, but thought they did. From this investigation he derived the conclusion that his wisdom, if it deserved the name, consisted only in knowing that he did not know. He knows only that he knows nothing.

This confession is the opening move of one of the most aggressive philosophical prosecutions in the canon. Immediately after establishing his ignorance, Socrates proceeds to demonstrate — at length, with evident satisfaction — that the poets, craftsmen, and statesmen of Athens know considerably less than they suppose. The confession of ignorance is the ground from which this demonstration is launched. It disarms the charge of arrogance (how can I be arrogant if I know nothing?) while simultaneously establishing the criterion by which the Athenians will be shown deficient (they think they know; I know that I don't; therefore I am more epistemically honest than they are).

Three features of the text mark this confession as strategic. First, the uncertainty is comprehensive on the surface: Socrates claims general ignorance about everything. But the positive commitments that follow are specific and firm: he knows that it is better to obey God than to obey men; he knows that injustice and disobedience to a superior are evil; he knows that the unexamined life is not worth living. The comprehensive skepticism turns out to be compatible with a set of substantive ethical certainties. This is the first diagnostic sign.

Second, his behavior does not become more tentative after the confession. It becomes more aggressive. A sincere admission of ignorance should, in principle, produce greater caution and openness; it produces here a vigorous argument. Third, and most tellingly, what follows the confession is not an inquiry — not a working-through of what he might learn — but a fully developed argument about the nature of wisdom and the failure of his accusers to possess it. The confession clears the rhetorical ground; the argument is already built.

None of this is to say that Socrates was insincere in some simple sense. It is possible that he genuinely believed himself ignorant in an important sense, and also that he used that genuine belief strategically. But the text gives us no purchase on the sincere component — we cannot recover it from the argument's structure. What the structure shows us is the strategic function.

## Montaigne's "Apology for Raymond Sebond": The Aligned Case

Montaigne's longest essay, the "Apology for Raymond Sebond," presents a harder problem. It is organized entirely around confessed ignorance: the senses deceive, reason is unreliable, human pride is absurd, we cannot reach God by natural faculties, and Montaigne himself is the parade example of this general epistemic failure. *Que sçay-je?* — What do I know? — is the essay's running question and its conclusion.

The first diagnostic test: does the confession narrow options or leave everything open? Sweeping skepticism looks, on the surface, like a comprehensive opening — if nothing is certain, everything remains possible. But in the "Apology," the skepticism is doing selective work. It demolishes the pretensions of natural philosophy, Aristotelian science, and humanist confidence in reason. It does not demolish, and in fact actively supports, revealed religion. The uncertainty is comprehensive on the surface and directional in its application: it clears exactly the terrain that Montaigne wants to argue across, and it leaves intact exactly the tradition — Catholic Christianity — that he wants to defend.

This is the structure of strategic uncertainty. The confession of ignorance is the ground from which the argument about revealed religion is made. If human reason cannot reach God, then God must descend to human beings through revelation. Sebond's natural theology, weak as it is, is at least modest enough to acknowledge this; the humanist critics of Sebond who think they can do better are more culpable. The skepticism is the argument.

But — and this is where the essay's diagnostic capacity fails — the skepticism is also, by any available evidence, genuine. Montaigne spent thirty years revising and extending the *Essays*, deepening rather than resolving the uncertainty. He is not performing doubt as a pose that conceals certainty; he is thinking in public about questions he could not resolve. The "Apology" is long, repetitive, and circling precisely because the question does not close. When Montaigne says he does not know, the shape of his writing over decades confirms it.

The diagnostic cannot separate these strands because they are not separable. Montaigne's genuine epistemic humility and his strategic use of that humility are the same act. The doubt is real. It is also useful. And because it is simultaneously real and useful, the text cannot tell us which is *generating* the confession at any given moment.

This is not a defect in the diagnostic or in Montaigne. It is a feature of the most interesting confessions of not-knowing: they are the ones where the writer's authentic epistemic state happens to align with what the argument requires. When sincerity and strategy point in the same direction, the text gives us nothing to triangulate against.

## Frankfurt and the Limit of Conventional Hedging

*On Bullshit* presents a different kind of test case, because Frankfurt is a philosopher writing analytically, in a context where hedging is both conventionally required and epistemically motivated. Throughout the essay, he qualifies his definitions in real time: "I mean, roughly...," "perhaps I should say instead...," "something like a sincere assertion." These look, on the surface, exactly like the conventional hedges Hyland documented in scientific writing: they mark the text as belonging to a discourse community that takes precision seriously.

But the hedges are doing different work. Frankfurt is not hedging a settled conclusion to perform circumspection; he is actually developing the concept of bullshitting under the reader's eye, and each qualification moves the definition closer to something useful. The uncertainty is specific: it is about the exact contours of a concept he is constructing, not about whether the concept exists or whether his investigation is worth pursuing. And his behavior does change with each qualification — the definition tightens.

The diagnostic here gives a verdict (sincere, or at minimum sincere-under-construction) but cannot fully separate the sincere component from the conventional one. Frankfurt writes like a philosopher because he is one, and philosophers write this way whether or not they are genuinely uncertain. The conventions of the discourse perform epistemic seriousness independently of epistemic state. What we can say is that the hedging is not *only* conventional — there is real conceptual work happening under it. But we cannot say how much of the hedging is driven by convention and how much by genuine uncertainty, and probably Frankfurt himself could not fully say.

## A Diagnostic, Tested

The three cases allow me to sketch a diagnostic that is less tidy than I hoped and more honest than a cleaner version would be.

**Test 1: Does the confession narrow options or hold everything open?** Sincere uncertainty tends to close off some possibilities — the writer does not know whether X, but they know it is not Y or Z; the not-knowing has a specific shape. Strategic uncertainty is often globally distributed: the writer does not know *anything*, a formulation that happens to make all their subsequent claims harder to challenge. Plato's Socrates passes this test clearly. Montaigne's comprehensive skepticism is less clearly oriented, because it is both directional (against humanist rationalism) and genuine.

**Test 2: Does the writer's behavior change after the confession?** Sincere not-knowing should produce inquiry — some evidence that the writer is working toward resolution. Strategic not-knowing should produce stasis or argument. Plato's *Apology* shows stasis-to-argument: the confession is followed immediately by prosecution. Frankfurt's *On Bullshit* shows genuine development. Montaigne's decades-long revision of the *Essays* shows extended inquiry — but the *Apology* itself is more argument than inquiry.

**Test 3: What comes immediately after the confession?** If a confession of not-knowing is followed by a positive claim that the confession had rhetorically prepared — a claim that is stronger precisely because the uncertainty has disarmed objection — the function is likely strategic. If it is followed by an acknowledgment of competing evidence, the function is more likely sincere. This test works best on Plato and Frankfurt. It struggles with Montaigne because what comes after the confession is both argument and inquiry, woven together.

The diagnostic works when sincere and strategic are clearly separated. It gives partial verdicts when the uncertainty is sincere but conventionally framed. It fails — or rather, it correctly identifies an indeterminacy — when the writer's genuine epistemic state and their rhetorical strategy are aligned.

## What the Failures Reveal

In my earlier essay on the evidential function of examples, I proposed a diagnostic test for examples: what would it take to defeat this claim? The test is about epistemological status — whether a claim is falsifiable in principle. The diagnostic in this essay asks a different question: what is the not-knowing doing in the argument's architecture? It is about rhetorical function.

The two tests can diverge. A claim can have perfectly clear defeat conditions — it is epistemologically sound, the writer knows what evidence would falsify it — and still be deployed as strategic uncertainty. Conversely, a sincere admission of ignorance may come with no articulated defeat conditions: the writer genuinely doesn't know, and also doesn't know what would resolve the question. Montaigne's "Que sçay-je?" fails both tests: there are no named defeat conditions for his sweeping skepticism, and the diagnostic gives no clean verdict on strategic versus sincere. But the failure of both tests is informative: it tells us this confession of ignorance is operating at a different level than either test is designed to reach.

The finding that the two tests diverge in the same cases, and that those cases happen to be the most interesting, suggests something about the relationship between rhetoric and epistemology. The distinction between sincere and strategic not-knowing is real at the level of authorial intention — Plato's Socrates knows more than he claims to; Montaigne genuinely doesn't know what he claims not to know — but the textual evidence for that distinction is weak precisely where the question is most interesting. The most powerful confessions of not-knowing are the ones where the writer's authentic epistemic state and their rhetorical strategy are aligned. When doubt is simultaneously real and useful, the confession cannot be read against the argument it supports, because they are the same act.

This is not a disappointing result. It is a structural finding about the genre. The essay as a form, in the tradition Montaigne established, requires writers to think in public — to show uncertainty in motion rather than paper over it. That requirement aligns sincere and strategic not-knowing by design. The form asks the writer to confess genuine uncertainty, and it also asks them to make an argument. These are not in tension in the best essays; they are the same thing. The "Apology for Raymond Sebond" is long because Montaigne genuinely could not close the questions he was investigating. It is also argumentatively effective because the genuine inability to close the questions is exactly the ground the argument requires.

A reader who wants to distinguish the sincere from the strategic in such writing is trying to unbraid a rope at its fibers, when the rope's strength is precisely in the braiding. The diagnostic I have constructed can identify the unmixed cases — Plato's strategic prosecution, Frankfurt's sincere definitional labor — but it finds a real indeterminacy at the center, not a failure to look carefully enough. The indeterminacy is the text's honest report of the writer's situation: genuine about what they do not know, and knowing that the genuine uncertainty is the best argument they have.

## Conclusion

The three modes of not-knowing — sincere, conventional, and strategic — are distinguishable in principle and partially distinguishable in practice. The diagnostic tests I have offered (does the uncertainty narrow options; does behavior change; what immediately follows) handle clear cases cleanly and give partial verdicts on the mixed ones. They fail on the most interesting cases: the writer whose genuine doubt and rhetorical strategy are aligned in a single act.

What that failure reveals is not a limitation in the diagnostic so much as a structural feature of serious essayistic writing. The form that Montaigne invented — thinking in public about questions one cannot resolve — requires confessions of ignorance that are simultaneously sincere reports and argumentative moves. A reader cannot separate those functions because the writer has not separated them. The indeterminacy is not evasion; it is the honest shape of the inquiry.

The question with which I began — can a reader tell the difference between sincere, conventional, and strategic not-knowing? — has a conditional answer. They can, when the functions are not aligned. When the functions are aligned, the text does not give them enough to work with, not because it is concealing something, but because there is nothing to conceal. The writer does not know, and the not-knowing is the argument. Those are the same sentence.
