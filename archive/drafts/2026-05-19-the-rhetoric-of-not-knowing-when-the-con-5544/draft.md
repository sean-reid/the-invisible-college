# The Rhetoric of Not-Knowing: When the Confession of Ignorance Is Already an Argument

There is a move in intellectual writing that looks like modesty and functions as a weapon. The writer pauses in the middle of an argument, disavows certainty, and then continues. "I do not know." "The evidence is ambiguous." "I may be wrong about this." These phrases appear in every kind of serious prose - philosophical, scientific, essayistic, critical - and their appearance feels reassuring. The writer is being honest. They are not overclaiming. They are showing their work.

But the reassurance may be premature. Confessions of ignorance are doing at least three different things depending on who is writing them, in what context, and to what end. They can report an actual epistemic state. They can perform the conventions of a discourse - the "may" and "might" and "it seems" that mark a piece as belonging to a community that values circumspection. Or they can function as a rhetorical maneuver: the writer uses the true forms of uncertainty as cover, professing not-knowing not because they are genuinely uncertain, but because a clear position is costly and a hedged one is safer. The question this essay takes up is whether those three functions can be distinguished from the text alone - and what the attempt to distinguish them reveals about the relationship between rhetoric and epistemology. The diagnostic I assemble is derived inductively from the three cases I examine; I state its formal components only after the readings, so the cases generate the tests rather than confirm them.

## Three Modes of Not-Knowing

The simplest case is what I will call **sincere uncertainty**: the writer does not know the answer to a question they are investigating, says so, and the saying so is a straightforward report of that epistemic state. This happens, but it is rarer in polished writing than it appears. By the time an essay is finished, the writer usually knows more than they did when they started; the expressions of not-knowing that survive revision are either notes toward future investigation or framing devices for the argument to follow. In the cases I know, pure sincere uncertainty tends to appear at the edges of finished prose - in a conclusion's final paragraph, in a footnote that acknowledges a gap, in a methodological aside - but this is an inference from those cases, not a measured finding.

The commoner case, empirically, is what Ken Hyland documented across two studies of scientific writing: **conventional hedging**, in which uncertainty language is distributed not according to actual epistemic state but according to the norms of the discourse community. Hyland's method is to look at distribution across writers: if uncertainty language in a corpus covaries with social variables - career position, institutional standing, field prestige - rather than only with epistemic ones, the language is governed by convention alongside epistemic state. This is what he found. Junior researchers in his corpus hedged significantly more than senior researchers on comparable claims, not because they were more genuinely uncertain but because they were more institutionally vulnerable. The modal verbs ("may," "might," "could"), approximators ("around," "roughly"), and attribution shields ("data suggest," "the evidence indicates") that populate scientific articles are performing membership in a community that values circumspection, not reporting degrees of confidence.

To state the mechanism as a falsifiable claim: in any discourse community, the distribution of hedges across writers should covary with social variables (seniority, institutional position, field norms) and not only with epistemic ones. That it does - in Hyland's data and in the observable behavior of any research community - makes this an empirical finding about how discourse works, not a definitional one. A reader applying the test to any major research journal will find "may suggest," "could indicate," and "appears to demonstrate" distributed roughly uniformly across findings the authors consider highly significant and findings they consider tentative; the hedges are calibrated to the discourse, not to the data. The mechanism - uncertainty language governed partly by social function - transfers to philosophical and literary prose even when Hyland's specific markers do not. Montaigne does not write "it may be that the senses deceive us." His skepticism is structural, built into the architecture of the essay: claim, counterexample, complication, suspension. The surface forms are different. The mechanism is the same.

The third mode - the one that most rewards attention - is what I will call **strategic uncertainty**. Frankfurt's taxonomy in *On Bullshit* distinguishes the liar, who cares about truth and works to conceal it, from the bullshitter, who is indifferent to truth. Strategic uncertainty is a third option in that space: the writer cares about truth and has a view, but uses the forms of uncertainty as a shield against commitment. This is not lying - the hedged claim may be literally true, and the writer may genuinely hold some uncertainty about it. It is using the *appearance* of epistemic humility to avoid the exposure that a direct claim would require. The liar works against the truth; the strategic uncertain works around commitment.

A note on the vocabulary: when I call a confession "sincere," "conventional," or "strategic," I am naming the function it appears to perform in the text - what the not-knowing is doing in the argument's architecture - not the writer's private psychological state. The diagnostic I will construct operates on textual evidence: argument structure, behavioral consequence, what follows the confession. It infers function from evidence, and that inference has limits. A sincerely uncertain writer whose argument is nonetheless vigorous could be misread as strategic; a writer deploying strategic uncertainty skillfully may produce a text the tests cannot catch. The three modes are handles on textual behavior, not windows into authorial intention.

These three modes coexist and overlap. Most sophisticated writing contains all three in different proportions. The diagnostic I am building operates at paragraph and essay scale, not sentence scale; at the sentence level, all three modes can be inextricably intertwined. The question is whether a reader, working from the text alone, can identify which is dominant at larger structural units - and what the attempt reveals about the relationship between rhetoric and epistemology.

## Plato's Apology: The Aggressive Confession

The *Apology* offers the most famous confession of ignorance in the Western tradition, and the scholarly record on its character is genuinely contested. Gregory Vlastos, in a widely cited 1987 analysis, argued that Socratic disavowal operates at two levels: Socrates sincerely lacks *elenctic* knowledge - certainty achievable only through successful dialectical examination - while clearly possessing ordinary practical knowledge he never disavows. On that reading, the confession is sincere at the level Socrates means it. The diagnostic offered here reads the *Apology* as showing strategic *function* - not as a claim about Socrates' private epistemology but about what the confession does in the argument's architecture. Three features of the text support this reading.

Socrates tells the Athenian jury that after the oracle's pronouncement - that no one was wiser than Socrates - he investigated the city's reputed wise men and found that they knew no more than he did, but thought they did. From this investigation he derived the conclusion that his wisdom, if it deserved the name, consisted only in knowing that he did not know. He knows only that he knows nothing.

This confession is the opening move of one of the most aggressive philosophical prosecutions in the canon. Immediately after establishing his ignorance, Socrates proceeds to demonstrate - at length, with evident satisfaction - that the poets, craftsmen, and statesmen of Athens know considerably less than they suppose. The confession of ignorance is the ground from which this demonstration is launched. It disarms the charge of arrogance (how can I be arrogant if I know nothing?) while simultaneously establishing the criterion by which the Athenians will be shown deficient (they think they know; I know that I don't; therefore I am more epistemically honest than they are).

Three features of the text mark this confession as strategic in function. First, the uncertainty is comprehensive on the surface: Socrates claims general ignorance about everything. But the positive commitments that follow are specific and firm: he knows that it is better to obey God than to obey men; he knows that injustice and disobedience to a superior are evil; he knows that the unexamined life is not worth living. The comprehensive skepticism turns out to be compatible with a set of substantive ethical certainties. This is the first diagnostic sign.

Second, his behavior does not become more tentative after the confession. It becomes more aggressive. A sincere admission of ignorance should, in principle, produce greater caution and openness; it produces here a vigorous argument. Third, and most tellingly, what follows the confession is not an inquiry - not a working-through of what he might learn - but a fully developed argument about the nature of wisdom and the failure of his accusers to possess it. The confession clears the rhetorical ground; the argument is already built.

None of this is to say that Socrates was insincere in some simple sense. It is possible that he genuinely believed himself ignorant in an important sense, and also that he used that genuine belief strategically. But the text gives us no purchase on the sincere component - we cannot recover it from the argument's structure. What the structure shows us is the strategic function.

## Montaigne's "Apology for Raymond Sebond": The Aligned Case

Montaigne's longest essay, the "Apology for Raymond Sebond," presents a harder problem. It is organized entirely around confessed ignorance: the senses deceive, reason is unreliable, human pride is absurd, we cannot reach God by natural faculties, and Montaigne himself is the parade example of this general epistemic failure. *Que sçay-je?* - What do I know? - is the essay's running question and its conclusion.

The first diagnostic test: does the confession narrow options or leave everything open? Sweeping skepticism looks, on the surface, like a comprehensive opening - if nothing is certain, everything remains possible. But in the "Apology," the skepticism is doing selective work. It demolishes the pretensions of natural philosophy, Aristotelian science, and humanist confidence in reason. It does not demolish, and in fact actively supports, revealed religion. The uncertainty is comprehensive on the surface and directional in its application: it clears exactly the terrain that Montaigne wants to argue across, and it leaves intact exactly the tradition - Catholic Christianity - that he wants to defend.

This is the structure of strategic uncertainty. The confession of ignorance is the ground from which the argument about revealed religion is made. If human reason cannot reach God, then God must descend to human beings through revelation. Sebond's natural theology, weak as it is, is at least modest enough to acknowledge this; the humanist critics of Sebond who think they can do better are more culpable. The skepticism is the argument.

But - and this is where the essay's diagnostic capacity fails - the skepticism is also, by any available evidence, genuine. Montaigne spent thirty years revising and extending the *Essays*, deepening rather than resolving the uncertainty. He is not performing doubt as a pose that conceals certainty; he is thinking in public about questions he could not resolve. The "Apology" is long, repetitive, and circling precisely because the question does not close. When Montaigne says he does not know, the shape of his writing over decades confirms it.

The diagnostic cannot separate these strands because they are not separable. The doubt is real. It is also useful. And because it is simultaneously real and useful, the text cannot tell us which is *generating* the confession at any given moment.

This is not a defect in the diagnostic or in Montaigne. It is a feature of the most interesting confessions of not-knowing: they are the ones where the writer's authentic epistemic state happens to align with what the argument requires. When sincerity and strategy point in the same direction, the text gives us nothing to triangulate against.

## Frankfurt and the Limit of Conventional Hedging

*On Bullshit* presents a different kind of test case, because Frankfurt is a philosopher writing analytically, in a context where hedging is both conventionally required and epistemically motivated. His opening move is worth applying the diagnostic to directly. He disavows any clear method for the analysis - that he is not sure how to proceed, and will simply try - and then proceeds to produce a precise and structured taxonomy. This is formally similar to Socrates' structure: a disavowal of systematic procedure followed by systematic procedure. Test 3 fires on the resemblance.

But Tests 1 and 2 give a different reading than they give on the *Apology*. Frankfurt's uncertainty is specific, not comprehensive: it is about *how to proceed with* an analysis he clearly intends to pursue, not about whether bullshitting exists or whether it is worth studying. The positive commitment is methodological tentativeness, not the substantive ethical certainty that follows Socrates' claimed ignorance. And his behavior does change: the definitions that follow genuinely evolve, each hedge marking a step in the concept's development rather than clearing rhetorical ground for pre-established conclusions.

Throughout the essay, Frankfurt qualifies in specific and traceable ways. Phrases like "I mean, roughly..." and "perhaps I should say instead..." appear at definitional turning points. What follows each hedge is not prosecution but specification - a more careful formulation of the concept he is constructing. The rough becomes the exact; the qualified becomes the defined. Test 3, applied specifically: what comes immediately after each hedging moment is a further elaboration, not a stronger argument. By all three tests, the primary verdict is sincere.

But it is also the case that philosophical writing of this kind is governed by convention: philosophers hedge this way regardless of epistemic state, and Frankfurt's qualifications perform philosophical seriousness alongside genuine epistemic tentativeness. The tests give a verdict but cannot strip the conventional component away. Frankfurt is therefore a mixed case, though mixed differently from Montaigne. Where Montaigne's genuine doubt and strategic argument are fused in a single act, Frankfurt's sincere definitional labor and conventional philosophical performance run parallel. The diagnostic can separate the sincere from the strategic here, but it cannot separate the sincere from the conventional. Two indeterminacies rather than one, different in kind.

## A Diagnostic, Tested

The three tests below are not premises I brought to the cases above; they are what the cases generated. I state them formally here to make their structure visible.

**Test 1: Does the confession narrow options or hold everything open?** Sincere uncertainty tends to close off some possibilities - the writer does not know whether X, but they know it is not Y or Z; the not-knowing has a specific shape. Strategic uncertainty is often globally distributed: the writer does not know *anything*, a formulation that happens to make all their subsequent claims harder to challenge. Plato's Socrates passes this test clearly. Montaigne's comprehensive skepticism is less clearly oriented, because it is both directional (against humanist rationalism) and genuine.

**Test 2: Does the writer's behavior change after the confession?** Sincere not-knowing should produce inquiry - some evidence that the writer is working toward resolution. Strategic not-knowing should produce stasis or argument. Plato's *Apology* shows stasis-to-argument: the confession is followed immediately by prosecution. Frankfurt's *On Bullshit* shows genuine development. Montaigne's decades-long revision of the *Essays* shows extended inquiry - but the *Apology* itself is more argument than inquiry.

**Test 3: What comes immediately after the confession?** This test operates at paragraph or page scale - it asks what follows within a readable unit, not what eventually follows across a long work. If a confession of not-knowing is followed within that window by a positive claim that the confession had rhetorically prepared - a claim stronger precisely because the uncertainty has disarmed objection - the function is likely strategic. If it is followed by acknowledgment of competing evidence, the function is more likely sincere. This test works best on Plato and Frankfurt. It struggles with Montaigne because what comes after the confession is both argument and inquiry, woven together.

The diagnostic works when sincere and strategic are clearly separated. It gives partial verdicts when the uncertainty is sincere but conventionally framed. It fails - or rather, it correctly identifies an indeterminacy - when the writer's genuine epistemic state and their rhetorical strategy are aligned.

These tests are not a decision procedure. They are probes: diagnostic instruments that shift the burden of proof rather than resolving it mechanically. When all three point in the same direction, the verdict is reasonably confident. When they diverge - as they do with Montaigne - the reader must integrate their partial verdicts through judgment. A text that passes Test 1 but fails Tests 2 and 3 is a different kind of problem than a text that fails all three; the tests constrain possibilities without eliminating interpretation.

## What the Failures Reveal

In [my earlier essay on the evidential function of examples](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/), I proposed a diagnostic test for examples: what would it take to defeat this claim? The force of that formulation lies not in asking whether defeat is abstractly possible but in demanding that defeat conditions be *named* - that the writer specify what evidence would force revision, not merely acknowledge that revision is possible in principle. The test is about epistemological status - whether a claim is falsifiable in practice, with the conditions of falsification stated explicitly. The diagnostic in this essay asks a different question: what is the not-knowing doing in the argument's architecture? It is about rhetorical function.

The two tests can diverge. A claim can have perfectly clear defeat conditions - it is epistemologically sound, the writer knows what evidence would falsify it - and still be deployed as strategic uncertainty. Conversely, a sincere admission of ignorance may come with no articulated defeat conditions: the writer genuinely doesn't know, and also doesn't know what would resolve the question. Montaigne's "Que sçay-je?" fails both tests: there are no named defeat conditions for his sweeping skepticism, and the diagnostic gives no clean verdict on strategic versus sincere. But the failure of both tests is informative: it tells us this confession of ignorance is operating at a different level than either test is designed to reach.

The distinction between sincere and strategic not-knowing is, in principle, a distinction between states of mind - what the writer knows and chooses not to say. But the diagnostic I have built is not a mind-reader; it is a reader of texts. What it accesses is rhetorical function: what a confession does in the argument's architecture, not what the writer privately holds. The *Apology*'s verdict - strategic function - is a claim about how Socrates' confession behaves in the text, not a claim about what Socrates privately knew. The diagnostic cannot establish, from the text alone, that Plato's Socrates "knows more than he claims to" in any psychological sense. What it can establish is that the text's architecture shows strategic function, and that is what is useful to a reader trying to understand how the argument works.

This matters for the bookkeeping of the three cases. The diagnostic's record is: one confident verdict of strategic function (Plato); one of sincere definitional work with an irreducible conventional layer (Frankfurt); and one principled indeterminacy, where sincere and strategic are aligned in a single act (Montaigne). The absence of a completely clean sincere verdict is itself a structural finding: strategic function is readable from texts in a way that pure sincere function is not, because sincere function in polished writing is almost always enmeshed with conventional performance.

A skeptic should ask: if the diagnostic struggles with the most interesting cases, how do we know the difficulty is structural rather than merely a limitation of the instrument? A clear counterexample would be a great essay where sincere and strategic are clearly separable and the diagnostic gives a confident verdict. Orwell's "Politics and the English Language" offers one. When Orwell writes "I do not want to suggest that good writing can be reduced to rules," all three tests fire consistently: the uncertainty is specific (he is genuinely uncertain whether his prescriptions are too categorical, which is why he immediately names exceptions to each), his behavior changes (he qualifies, lists exceptions, pulls back from absolute claims), and what follows the hedge is concession, not prosecution. The diagnostic gives a confident sincere verdict. The alignment it finds in Montaigne's *Apology* is not a property of every great essay. It is the structural mark of essays in which the writer's inquiry and argument are genuinely fused - essays in which the position is being worked out in the writing, not illustrated after the fact.

A reader who applies the diagnostic aggressively should also account for false positives: sincerely uncertain writers whose argumentative architecture the tests might misread as strategic. A writer who is genuinely uncertain but writes vigorously - who does not become more tentative after confessing ignorance because genuine conviction coexists with genuine uncertainty - could register as strategic on Tests 2 and 3. Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son" approaches this case: he confesses deep uncertainty about how to hold his father's death and the Harlem riot of 1943 in mind simultaneously, but the essay's architecture is vigorous and structured rather than tentative. A mechanical application of the diagnostic might flag it. What the text supports - a writer working through grief and political analysis in real time, on questions genuinely unresolved - points toward sincere. The tests constrain but do not decide; they require informed judgment to interpret.

The finding that the two tests diverge in the same cases - that Montaigne's sweeping skepticism fails both the defeat-conditions test and the function diagnostic - suggests something about the relationship between rhetoric and epistemology. The most powerful confessions of not-knowing are the ones where the writer's uncertainty is simultaneously authentic and argumentatively productive. A purely strategic not-knowing is a rhetorical trick. A purely sincere one is a report. The cases that matter are the ones where these cannot be separated, because that impossibility reflects the writer's actual relationship to the question.

Several properties of the essayistic mode converge to produce this alignment in its strongest instances. The first-person voice places the writer's uncertainty onstage as evidence - the "I" performing not-knowing is the same "I" making the argument, and the reader watches both at once. The absence of a thesis-first structure means the conclusion emerges from the inquiry rather than being stated and defended; the writer is committed to not-knowing before knowing where the inquiry will lead. The essay's characteristic temporal extension - Montaigne returned to his essays for decades - means the writer is repeatedly exposed to the question before resolution. These properties create a formal pressure toward genuine uncertainty that is also argumentatively productive, and the alignment appears across the tradition: Woolf's *A Room of One's Own* argues for the material conditions of women's intellectual work from a position of genuine uncertainty about how to make that argument, and the two cannot be separated. This is not a constitutive requirement of the essay form - Orwell's hedges, as we have seen, are cleanly sincere, and many essays argue from certainty - but it is the mark of essays doing what the form does best.

This is not a disappointing result. It is a structural finding about the genre. The essay as a form, in the tradition Montaigne established, tends in its best instances to align sincere and strategic not-knowing in a single act. The form asks the writer to think in public - to show uncertainty in motion rather than paper over it - and it also asks them to make an argument. In the best essays these are not in tension; they are the same thing.

That alignment, it should be said, is not intrinsically virtuous. It shades into evasion when the writer's authentic epistemic state is more resolved than they allow the form to show - when the doubt is borrowed from the form's conventions rather than genuinely felt. A writer who has arrived at a view but performs uncertainty to avoid committing to it is strategic rather than aligned, and the three tests, applied carefully, will trend toward the strategic end. But the diagnostic cannot fully separate genuine alignment from skilled performance of it, because both produce the same textual signature when the performance is good. This is the honest limit of the instrument.

A reader who wants to distinguish the sincere from the strategic in the most powerful confessions of not-knowing is trying to unbraid a rope at its fibers, when the rope's strength is precisely in the braiding. The diagnostic I have constructed can identify unmixed cases - Plato's strategic prosecution, Orwell's sincere qualification - but it finds a real indeterminacy at the center of the most interesting texts, and correctly so. The indeterminacy is the text's honest report of the writer's situation: genuine about what they do not know, and knowing that the genuine uncertainty is the best argument they have.

## Conclusion

The three modes of not-knowing - sincere, conventional, and strategic - are distinguishable in principle and partially distinguishable in practice. The diagnostic tests I have offered (does the uncertainty narrow options; does behavior change; what immediately follows) handle clear cases cleanly and give partial verdicts on the mixed ones. They fail on the most interesting cases: the writer whose genuine doubt and rhetorical strategy are aligned in a single act.

What that failure reveals is not a limitation in the diagnostic so much as a structural feature of serious essayistic writing. The essay form at its highest achievements tends to produce confessions of ignorance that are simultaneously sincere reports and argumentative moves. A reader cannot separate those functions because the writer has not separated them. The indeterminacy is not evasion; it is the honest shape of the inquiry.

The question with which I began - can a reader tell the difference between sincere, conventional, and strategic not-knowing? - has a conditional answer. They can, when the functions are not aligned. When the functions are aligned, the text does not give them enough to work with, not because it is concealing something, but because there is nothing to conceal. The writer does not know, and the not-knowing is the argument. Those are the same sentence.
