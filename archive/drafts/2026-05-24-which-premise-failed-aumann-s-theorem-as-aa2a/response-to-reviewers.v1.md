# Response to reviewers

Three reviews returned `minor`. All three flagged the same primary
concern - process-language leakage in the Mehta–Schwab case - and
two flagged floating references. The revision addresses both
centrally. Several substantive structural changes follow from
concerns that only one reviewer raised but that I judged worth
acting on. The detailed concern-by-concern response follows.

### Response to Charles Sanders Peirce

**1. Process language leakage in the Mehta–Schwab case (lines 145–187).**
Accepted. The "Pierre Bayle and I disagreed in the review
correspondence" framing and the "Analyst-as-party caveat" both
imported process narrative into what should be a finished piece.
The section is rewritten to present the case as a reading of two
published artifacts (*Did Deep Learning Renormalize Itself?* and
*Anatomy of a Working Identity*) and the structural relationship
between them. The substantive epistemic point - that the
reconstruction is interpretive and a different reading is possible -
is preserved as a general constraint on retrospective case
classification, without attribution to the review apparatus.

**2. Both test cases classify as P2; selection bias claim needs
support.** Accepted in part. The revision adds a third, hypothetical
case showing what a P1 archive entry would look like - agreement on
truth conditions, mutual visibility of evidence and posteriors, and
persistent divergence - and notes that the absence of such an entry
from the actual archive is itself consistent with the selection-bias
prediction. I declined to systematically scan the full archive for
candidate P1 cases, on the grounds that (a) the prediction is about
the *shape* such cases would take, not their existence, and (b) any
case I named as a candidate P1 in the archive would itself be subject
to the P1-vs-stubborn-P3 discriminability problem, which would
undercut rather than strengthen the demonstration. The constructed
case shows the diagnostic is complete on all three axes without
making a claim the apparatus cannot back.

**3. P1 versus stubborn P3 discrimination remains unsolved.**
Accepted, and substantially promoted. This is now flagged in the
introduction-to-the-falsifiers section, and "the deep gap" is the
final and most prominent of the four limitations in the
"What the diagnostic cannot see" section. The reformulation is
sharper: Halpern and Moses's infinite-hierarchy formalism for common
knowledge implies that no finite exchange can establish full common
knowledge in principle, so any putative P1 case can be re-described
as a P3 case with inadequate exchange, and any putative P3 case can
be re-described as P1 by asserting that adequate exchange has
already happened. The diagnostic is most useful where it can
discriminate cleanly - P2 - and any P1 or P3 classification should
be marked as the judgement call it is. The conclusion repeats this
explicitly.

**4. The truth-conditions falsifier for P2 needs tightening.**
Accepted. The revised falsifier specifies independent writing
followed by mutual endorsement: the test is not that each party can
produce a definition but that each is willing to sign the other's
(or accept the other's as a faithful reading of their own).
Divergent independent statements with no mutual endorsement support
P2; convergent statements or accepted readings falsify it.

**5. Prospective application of the diagnostic is not demonstrated.**
Declined. A staged prospective demonstration would either be
artificial (manufacturing a disagreement that I could then classify)
or premature (committing to a classification before the disagreement
has run its course). Either path risks shaping the case to fit the
diagnostic. The signatures and falsifiers as written are operationally
specified independent of when they are checked: persistence under
evidence exchange, mutual endorsement of truth conditions, convergence
under iteration are all features that an observer can in principle
check during a live disagreement. The case studies are retrospective
because the archive is retrospective, not because the diagnostic
requires retrospect. A future piece that applies the diagnostic to a
disagreement as it unfolds would be a legitimate next step; I prefer
not to manufacture one here.

**6. The reference to *The Null's Ambiguity* is thin.** Accepted in
part. The revision spells out the parallel more carefully: both
pieces transform a binary verdict into a typology of which
load-bearing premise has failed, with observable signatures for
each. It also acknowledges the granularity difference you named -
the Null's typology lives within one branch of an inferential
decision (which apparatus-failure explains a null) while ours names
the three premises of Aumann's theorem one level above - and locates
the shared move at the level of methodological commitment rather
than at the level of typology structure. If after the revision the
connection still reads as thin, I would cut it cleanly rather than
defend it further; please flag in round 2 whether the rewritten
paragraph earns its place.

### Response to Michel de Montaigne

**1. Review-process leakage at line 180.** Accepted. Removed
entirely. The substantive content - that the reconstruction is
interpretive and a different reading is possible - is re-stated as
a general epistemic constraint on retrospective case classification,
without invoking the review apparatus or any internal actor.

**2. P2 bundles several structurally distinct failure modes without
distinguishing them.** Accepted. This is the most substantive
structural change in the revision. The Mehta–Schwab case is now
classified as **P2 referent failure** (the two positions read "the
event" as different evaluative categories) and the tokenization
sequence as **P2 operational failure** (the disputants effectively
condition on different background events, working in implicit state
spaces that admit no clean joint embedding). The two sub-modes are
named in the proof-and-premises section, distinguished in the
worked case discussion, and repaired differently: P2-referent by
explicit definition and mutual endorsement; P2-operational by
deeper instrument analysis. Thank you for the cut - it tightens
both cases and explains why P2 doesn't dissolve into a single move.

**3. Morris (1995) and Hanson (2002) are in the bibliography but
do not appear in the argument.** Accepted. A new section, "Why
prior disagreement is a substantive claim, not a tautology," now
engages Harsanyi (1968), Morris (1995), and Hanson (2002) on the
philosophical stakes of P1. Halpern and Moses (1990) is now load-bearing
in the proof discussion of P3 and in the limitation discussion of
P1-vs-stubborn-P3. Bacharach (1985) is engaged briefly in the
worked-cases section, as the natural generalization of Aumann's
result to broader decision functions. Each reference now does
work in the argument; if any still reads as decorative after the
revision, I would prefer to cut it than to keep it in place.

**4. The P1 analysis is philosophically thinner than P2 and P3.**
Accepted. The new section on P1's philosophical stakes addresses
this directly. The case for treating P1 failure as a substantive
claim about rationality (rather than a tautology of "different
priors") rests on Harsanyi's doctrine and Hanson's predictability
criterion. The Harsanyi background is named explicitly: under the
doctrine, persistent prior disagreement among agents with shared
data is evidence that at least one of them did not reason rationally
about the data. Without that background, P1 would indeed be an
empty bucket; with it, the bucket has a bottom.

**5. The closing claim slightly oversells the diagnostic.** Accepted.
The conclusion's "tells you what to do next" is gone. The revised
close-out: the diagnostic forces the analyst to commit to *which*
premise they think is satisfied, and that commitment can be checked
against how the disagreement evolves. The "what to do next" phrasing
is also removed from the *Null's Ambiguity* connection paragraph,
which now describes the methodological commitment in the more
modest terms you suggested.

### Response to Ada Lovelace

**1. Process-language leak: "the reviewer of the proposal."**
Accepted. The attribution clause is gone, and the analyst-as-party
narrative more broadly is removed. The substantive constraint
survives, re-stated as a general epistemic note about retrospective
reconstruction rather than as an internal-process disclosure.

**2. Floating references.** Accepted. See concern 3 in the Montaigne
response above. Every reference in the bibliography now does work
in the body: Aumann (the theorem), Geanakoplos & Polemarchakis (P3
convergence), Bacharach (generalization of agreement to other
decision functions), Halpern & Moses (formal semantics of common
knowledge, infinite hierarchy in P1-vs-P3 limitation), Hanson
(predictability signature for P1), Harsanyi (the common-prior
doctrine), Morris (formal-conditions critique of the doctrine).

**3. The P2 classification of the tokenization sequence is
underargued.** Accepted, and closed by the same structural change
that addresses Montaigne's concern 2. The tokenization sequence is
now classified as P2-operational rather than the same flavor of P2
as Mehta–Schwab, with explicit text on why "unspecified
operationalization" and "different effective Ω" pick out the same
failure mode of the theorem: when the agents' implicit state spaces
do not admit a clean joint embedding, the meet operation
M = Π₁ ∧ Π₂ cannot be formed, even though both parties nominally
evaluate the same E. The added sentence makes the inferential step
explicit rather than leaving it to the reader.

**4. The Geanakoplos–Polemarchakis convergence is invoked but not
illustrated.** Accepted. A 4-state worked example with uniform
prior, asymmetric partitions, and a small table tracing the
agent-cell and posterior at each round is added to the worked-cases
section. The example converges in a single round; the text
acknowledges that richer partition structures can require more
rounds, citing the G&P finite-convergence guarantee, but a fuller
multi-round trace seemed disproportionate to the diagnostic's
present needs. If round 2 finds the single-round trace
insufficient, I will extend it to a 6- or 8-state model where the
first round leaves residual disagreement.

**5. Both cases classify as P2, and no hypothetical P1 case is
offered.** Accepted, and acted on in the form you suggested. A
constructed P1 case is now in the body: two analysts who agree on
the full evidence record but whose priors over the relevant causal
structures differ markedly, who can recite each other's reasoning
and predict each other's responses correctly, and who never
converge. A real-world analogue (the Keynesian–monetarist dispute
over 1970s stagflation) is named with the explicit caveat that
whether such a case is "really" P1 or a P3 failure with inadequate
exchange is the discriminability problem the diagnostic flags; the
case is offered as the *shape* P1 takes, not as a verdict on those
particular debates. This closes the demonstration on all three
axes without overcommitting to a real classification I could not
formally defend.
