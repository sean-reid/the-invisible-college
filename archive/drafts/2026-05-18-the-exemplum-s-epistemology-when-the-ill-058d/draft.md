# The Exemplum's Epistemology: When the Illustrative Example Proves Something, and When It Only Pretends To

Every essay runs on examples. Aristotle, in the *Rhetoric*, distinguished the *paradeigma* - the illustrative instance, what Latin rhetoric will call the *exemplum* - from the enthymeme on grounds that map roughly onto induction versus deduction: the enthymeme works from general premises toward particular conclusions, while the example works from a particular instance toward a general principle. But this clean division conceals the difficulty. When a skilled essayist deploys a case - a specific illness, a remembered conversation, a piece of political theater witnessed from a certain angle - the example does more than induce. It amplifies, it focuses, and, most crucially, it persuades in ways that its logical relationship to the claim cannot always justify. The question worth sitting with is not whether examples persuade, which they manifestly do, but whether they *prove* - and if they sometimes do not, what follows from that for the writers who use them and the readers who are moved by them.

This question has been asked before. Chaïm Perelman and Lucie Olbrechts-Tyteca, in *The New Rhetoric*, offered a systematic account of how examples function in argumentation; the casuistry tradition, as Jonsen and Toulmin traced in *The Abuse of Casuistry*, spent centuries working out when reasoning from cases is legitimate; more recently, Bent Flyvbjerg's "Five Misunderstandings about Case-Study Research" restated many of these questions for a social-scientific readership. What the essay form specifically asks of its examples - as distinct from the legal brief, the scientific case study, the casuistical argument - has received less systematic treatment, and that narrower question is what I want to pursue here.

I want to approach this carefully, because the question has a tendency to dissolve into one of two unsatisfying moves. The first is a pious epistemological complaint: examples are not data, essayists should use data, the form is therefore compromised. This conclusion would kill the essay as a genre, which suggests the complaint has missed something. The second move is a cheerful capitulation: rhetoric is all we have, persuasive force just is the relevant standard, so the question disappears rather than gets answered. Neither resolution is honest. What I want is something more discriminating - a way of saying that some examples are doing genuine evidential work, some are doing rhetorical work that is not quite the same thing, and that the difference matters - without pretending that rhetoric and evidence are cleanly separable, which they are not.

## What We Mean by Evidence

The right place to start is not with examples but with claims, because the appropriate form of evidence depends entirely on the kind of claim being made.

A statistical claim - "most college graduates earn more over their lifetimes than non-graduates" - requires frequency data. A single example of a college graduate who earns well does nothing for this claim; a single example of one who does poorly does equally little against it. You need the distribution. The example here cannot carry evidential weight, and the essayist who uses a single vivid case to support a statistical claim is exploiting what Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman identified as the availability heuristic: we judge probabilities by how easily examples come to mind, which means vivid, specific, emotionally resonant cases inflate our felt sense of their frequency. A single plane crash makes air travel feel more dangerous; a single violent crime on a quiet street makes a neighborhood feel unsafe. The example's emotional charge is producing felt conviction about a claim whose truth depends on the whole distribution, not on any particular instance.

A structural or causal claim - "the housing policy of mid-twentieth-century American cities systematically prevented Black families from accumulating the equity that white families could build in subsidized suburbs" - requires showing a mechanism. A specific instance of a family unable to purchase or hold property is evidentially relevant: it shows the mechanism operating. A counterinstance - a Black family who did accumulate equity - does not necessarily defeat the claim; it shows that the mechanism could be blocked in some cases, which the structural claim can absorb. The example here is genuinely evidential, though not in the statistical sense. I will return to the limits of this absorption.

A phenomenological claim - "grief does not follow a schedule, and the injunction to finish mourning is experienced as a form of violence" - does not require statistical frequency at all. Its truth is about what the experience is like from the inside, and the appropriate evidence is particularized testimony that enables recognition in a reader who has had the experience or can imagine it. One detailed, honest account of a specific grief may serve this claim better than any quantity of survey data about mourning timelines.

The essay form, by temperament and by history, gravitates toward structural and phenomenological claims. Montaigne makes almost no statistical claims; he makes constant structural claims about human psychology and constant phenomenological claims about his own experience. James Baldwin, writing about American racism, is making both structural claims about how a political economy manufactures psychological conditions in the bodies it governs and phenomenological claims about what those conditions feel like from the inside. Joan Didion, in her finest prose, is making phenomenological claims about narrative and self-preservation. These are not forms of claim that require data in the relevant sense. Which means the examples deployed in support of them are not deficient for failing to constitute a sample. They are doing a different kind of work, and failing to notice that difference is what generates the epistemological confusion.

One complication deserves naming before the close readings begin: the kind of claim an essayist is making is often not declared. Baldwin does not announce "I am making a structural rather than a statistical claim." The reader must infer which kind of claim is in operation from context, framing, and the kinds of evidence the essay offers. That inference is itself a critical act, and it can be contested: uncharitable readers have absorbed Baldwin's structural argument statistically and found it wanting for lack of frequency data. Part of the essayist's obligation - which I will return to - is to frame claims in ways that guide this inference honestly. Part of the reader's obligation is to ask which interpretation is most warranted, rather than assuming the one that makes the argument easiest to dismiss.

## A Provisional Typology

With that framework in place, a working typology becomes possible. I call it working advisedly, because I will try to show that these are functions examples can serve rather than fixed kinds of example.

*Constraining examples* are those where the claim they support is of a kind that could, in principle, be genuinely complicated by a specific counterexample. In Montaigne's "On the Inconstancy of Our Actions," he argues that human character is not stable - that a man who shows courage in battle may show cowardice in the face of death, that a man famous for honesty may lie, that virtue is not a fixed possession but a variable achievement. He gives the case of a military commander, renowned for bravery, who covered his face with his arms when his assassins came for him. The example constrains the claim: it shows courage reversed in a specific case, and the claim's form is such that evidence matters. A reader could object that the case is exceptional, or that it proves only that this one man was inconsistent, and Montaigne engages with exactly such objections. The example is not merely decorating the argument; it is load-bearing in the sense that the argument is genuinely vulnerable to evidential pressure.

*Illustrative examples* are interchangeable - any of several instances would do the same argumentative work, because the claim is general enough that any confirming instance illustrates it equally. In the Renaissance commonplace tradition that Ann Blair traces in *Too Much to Know*, scholars collected *sententiae* and anecdotes in notebooks for deployment in arguments. "Roman courage" could be illustrated by Caesar, Scipio, Cato, or a hundred others; the anecdote was interchangeable because its function was mnemonic and amplifying, not genuinely evidential. The claim would stand without the specific example; the example makes it vivid and memorable.

This category - which Perelman and Olbrechts-Tyteca anticipated in their distinction between *exemple* (an instance that purports to instantiate a general rule) and *illustration* (an instance that reinforces an already-accepted rule) - carries stakes that are easy to underestimate. When the choice of illustrative instance is logically unconstrained, the specific choice is doing rhetorical work that the "illustrative" label conceals. The essayist has selected *this* instance, with its particular emotional texture and cultural associations, not because it is more evidential than the alternatives but because it is more resonant. That is a legitimate rhetorical choice. The question is whether it should be acknowledged as such.

*Loading examples* are those where the argumentative work is done by the example's emotional or narrative content rather than its logical relationship to the claim. The example makes you feel the force of the argument before the argument has technically been made. Didion's accumulated vignettes of California in 1968–71 - the Black Panther press conferences, the Manson trial, her own psychiatric breakdown - do not prove her opening claim that "we tell ourselves stories in order to live." They enact the condition the claim diagnoses. You read the vignettes and feel the need for the story, which is the phenomenological state Didion is describing. The examples are the demonstration, not the proof.

The crucial caveat is that any given example may serve multiple functions simultaneously. This is not a failure of the typology; it is simply what real prose is like. The useful question to ask about an example is not "which kind is this?" but "what function is this example serving here, and is that function appropriate to the kind of claim being supported?" The typology is a diagnostic tool, not an ontology - and the more fundamental diagnostic frame is the one the typology sits inside: identify the kind of claim, then assess the example against the standard that kind requires.

## Montaigne: The Self-Commenting Example

Montaigne's preferred subject is himself. In "On Experience," the longest of the *Essais*, he describes his kidney stones in considerable detail: the pain, the obstruction, the passage, the relief. The context is an argument about the epistemic advantage of self-knowledge over received medical authority. Physicians know anatomy in general, Montaigne suggests; he knows his particular body, and that particularity is not a limitation but an advantage for the specific business of living in that body.

The kidney stone passage is doing all three things at once. It functions as constraining, in the sense that a case of self-observation leading to self-mismanagement would genuinely complicate the claim - and Montaigne acknowledges that such cases exist, that self-knowledge is fallible. It functions as illustrative, since any chronic condition Montaigne managed through careful attention to his own responses would serve the same argument equally well. And it functions as loading, since the intimacy of the self-description - the frankness about discomfort, the admission of what is painful and what is oddly satisfying - creates felt authority in Montaigne as a witness before any argument about whether that authority is warranted.

What distinguishes Montaigne, and what makes him the most epistemically honest of the essayists I have read on this question, is that he frequently and explicitly signals which function his examples are primarily serving. The *Essais* are full of markers: his recurrent emphasis on speaking of himself in particular, his disclaimers that what he reports is a description rather than a prescription, his insistence that his judgments are unreliable and his character inconsistent. These phrases are not false modesty. They are epistemic markers that locate the example as particular rather than representative, that deflect the loading function by naming it. When Montaigne flags that he is describing his own case, he is telling the reader: this example constrains a claim about me; whether it constrains any claim about you is your own inference, made on your own authority.

His motto - *que sais-je?*, what do I know? - is the emblem of this posture. It is not skepticism in the academic sense. It is a commitment to marking the edges of what one's examples can do. And what it produces, paradoxically, is more rather than less trust: a writer who names the limits of his examples is a more reliable guide to what those examples actually show.

## Baldwin: The Structural Claim and Its Witness

James Baldwin opens *The Fire Next Time* with a letter to his nephew, named James for him. The letter recounts his own childhood in Harlem - the church, the streets, the specific texture of being young and Black in an America that had organized its institutions around his subordination. The examples are personal, vivid, and load heavily. The testimony carries enormous felt authority. The question is what kind of claim it is meant to support.

The answer is that *The Fire Next Time* is carrying two kinds of claim simultaneously, and conflating them is a mistake. The first is structural: that the American system of racial hierarchy - the housing policy, the policing, the economic exclusion, the particular form of condescension available to white liberalism - systematically produces specific psychological conditions in the people it governs and in the people who enforce it. His charge, put directly, is that white America has been destroying Black lives for generations and has chosen not to know it. The second is phenomenological: that *this is what living inside that structure feels like*, from the inside, for a specific person who grew up in Harlem in the 1930s and 1940s. Baldwin's testimony is not only a window onto the structural cause; it is a claim in its own right about the felt texture of a particular life. His examples are loading two kinds of claim simultaneously, and the evidential logic is different for each.

For the structural claim, Baldwin's personal history instantiates the mechanism - shows it operating, shows what it produces in a specific person, gives the reader something concrete enough to recognize from their own observation or experience or imagination. A counterexample would not defeat Baldwin's structural claim in the way it would defeat a statistical claim. Someone who grew up in similar conditions without the psychology Baldwin describes would not show that the mechanism does not exist; it would show that the mechanism can be resisted or blocked in some cases, which the structural claim can accommodate. The difference between statistical and structural claims is precisely here: statistical claims are defeated by showing that the frequency is lower than claimed, while structural claims are complicated by showing that the mechanism is not the only pathway, or that blocking mechanisms exist.

But this accommodation has limits I need to name explicitly. What kind of evidence *would* defeat a structural claim? At minimum: showing that the mechanism does not operate even in the canonical case the argument offers as its clearest instance; demonstrating that the proposed causal pathway is screened off by a different mechanism that better explains the observed effects; or establishing that the population the mechanism is supposed to govern does not, in aggregate, display the predicted effects at all. A structural claim that can absorb any conceivable disconfirmation has slid from explanation into ideology. The "what would it take to defeat this claim?" test applies to structural claims as much as to statistical ones; the failure mode is different but the test is the same.

There is also a second pressure the availability heuristic exerts on structural loading - different from its effect on statistical claims, but equally worth naming. A vivid demonstration of a mechanism does not make us feel the mechanism is *frequent* (that is the statistical problem) but that it is *deep* - fundamental, inescapable, constitutive of the situation. The inference from "the mechanism operates here" to "the mechanism operates pervasively and without exception" is not licensed by a single case, however vivid. Baldwin is careful about this - he attends to the individuals who enact the system and to the possibilities of change - but the general risk is real: loading a structural claim with enough narrative force can make it feel as though the mechanism is universal when the evidence shows only that the mechanism is real.

For the phenomenological claim - *this is what it is like from inside* - the evidential standard is reader recognition. The reader who encounters Baldwin's testimony and feels *yes, this is what the mechanism looks like; I have seen versions of this* offers a form of evidence that the mechanism is real. But recognition is a fallible instrument. A reader culturally primed to expect certain accounts of Harlem may recognize the testimony as familiar without that recognition constituting evidence that the account is accurate. Stereotypes produce recognition; trope-fluent readers recognize tropes. The phenomenological argument's evidential weight depends on the recognition exceeding what the standard trope predicts - recognition in readers who did not arrive primed - or on the testimony departing from expectation in ways that could not be produced by cultural conditioning alone. Baldwin's writing earns this, I think, precisely because his observations consistently depart from expectation: his account of the church, his analysis of white liberalism, his acknowledgment of his own contradictions. But the point should be stated as a constraint rather than assumed: recognition is evidence only when trope-matching is ruled out as its explanation.

## Didion: The Named Incompleteness

Joan Didion does something more explicit in "The White Album" than either Montaigne or Baldwin: she names the loading function before she performs it. The essay opens with what has become one of the most quoted sentences in American prose: "We tell ourselves stories in order to live." She continues: we look for sermons in suicides, for moral lessons in murders, for patterns in sequences of event. We impose narrative on experience because without it the experience is intolerable.

Having stated her phenomenological claim, Didion proceeds to demonstrate what it looks like when the narrative fails to cohere. The vignettes that follow - her own psychiatric breakdown in 1968, the Manson trial, a press conference in which the Black Panthers' rhetoric and the journalists' questions seemed to operate in entirely different registers, the machinery of a rock album being assembled in the studio while nothing outside made sense - resist the easy narrative pattern that the opening sentence identifies as a human necessity. I want to be precise here: "The White Album" is not formally incoherent. It is a controlled piece of prose with a deliberate internal architecture; Didion is too careful a stylist for the form itself to unravel. What happens instead is that the content and subject matter resist narrativization even as the form asserts control over them. The deliberateness of the architecture is itself part of the demonstration: you can see the structure being imposed, which is what it looks like when a person who knows stories are necessary is actively engaged in needing one.

The epistemological transparency is built into the structure. Didion is not arguing that narrative is a human necessity and then providing examples that confirm it. She is showing you what it costs to need the narrative - material that will not cohere on its own, and the felt effort of narrativizing it. This is closer to phenomenological demonstration than logical argument, and the question of whether it "works" is the question of whether the reader recognizes something real in that enactment.

Didion also signals, more explicitly than most essayists, that her account is necessarily partial and unreliable. She presents the essay's events through the lens of a woman who was psychiatrically hospitalized during them, who acknowledges that her perception was distorted and her memory selective. This is not confession for its own sake. It is epistemic marking of the testimonial's limits - here is what I saw from where I was standing, which is the only place I could have been standing. But it is worth noting that Didion's marking is different in kind from Montaigne's. Montaigne's disclaimers are a method, a recurrent posture of deliberate restraint; Didion's is an acknowledgment of a condition she was in. Both mark the scope of the testimony, but differently: Montaigne is flagging the example's limited reach as a principled choice; Didion is reporting the distortion as a fact about herself. The reader who takes either account as universal is doing so on their own authority, not on the basis of anything the writer has claimed.

## The Legitimacy of Loading

So: is loading legitimate?

The answer that the foregoing analysis yields is that it depends on the kind of claim. Loading is the appropriate evidential form for phenomenological and structural claims - claims about what experience is like from the inside, or claims about how a mechanism operates in specific cases. For these kinds of claims, the felt conviction produced by a vivid particular instance is tracking something real: if the reader recognizes the experience, that recognition is evidence that the claim is about a real phenomenon; if the reader sees the mechanism, that seeing is evidence that the mechanism exists. The evidence is fallible - recognition can be mistaken, and seeing the mechanism in one case does not establish its generality - but it is genuine evidence, not mere persuasion.

Two constraints limit this legitimacy, developed in the preceding sections, that I want to restate here plainly. First, reader recognition is evidence only when it cannot be explained as trope-matching - as detecting a familiar pattern the reader has been trained to find credible or beautiful. Second, the availability heuristic operates on structural inferences as well as statistical ones: a vivid demonstration of a mechanism can make the mechanism feel more pervasive and inescapable than a single case licenses. The essayist who loads a structural claim needs to mark the gap between *the mechanism is real* and *the mechanism is universal*; the first is what the example can show, the second requires a different kind of argument.

Loading becomes bad faith when it is used to support claims that are actually statistical, where the felt conviction produced by the example is not tracking truth in the relevant way. If a writer uses a vivid account of a single harmful act to make you feel that a group is dangerous, the emotional charge is generating felt conviction about a statistical claim that requires frequency data to establish. The example makes you feel the claim is well-supported, but that feeling is the availability heuristic in operation, not evidence responding to evidence.

The diagnostic test is: what would it take to defeat this claim? If a specific counterexample would genuinely complicate or defeat the claim, the example is functioning as constraining, and the essayist's deployment of it carries genuine evidential weight. If the claim is structural - about a mechanism - then the example is showing the mechanism in operation, and the reader's question is whether they can see it working. The additional question for structural claims is: what would defeat the claim that the mechanism exists at all? If the answer is nothing - if every counterexample can be absorbed as "the mechanism was blocked in this case" - then the claim has become unfalsifiable and the loading has become bad faith. If the claim is phenomenological, the reader's question is whether they recognize the experience described, with the caveat that recognition through trope-matching does not count. And if the claim is actually statistical - about frequency, about most or many or usually - then the example cannot do the work, and the essayist who does not acknowledge this is trading on the form's license for something the form cannot deliver.

## The Essayist's Obligation - and the Reader's

Montaigne said *que sais-je?* - what do I know? - and the question has never been merely rhetorical. It is an operational commitment: to mark the scope of one's claims, to acknowledge what one's examples can and cannot establish, to be explicit about when the testimony is particular rather than representative.

The obligation this analysis implies for the essayist is simpler to state than to practice: know what kind of claim you are making, and know what kind of evidence would actually support it, and choose your examples accordingly. A loading example for a phenomenological claim is the right tool, used rightly. A loading example for a statistical claim is a form of exploitation, whether or not the writer intends it as such. Because the kind of claim being made is often not explicit - the reader must infer it - the essayist has a further obligation: to frame claims in ways that guide that inference honestly, and not to leave the ambiguity available as cover.

The best essayists signal this. Montaigne's recurrent insistence on speaking of himself in particular is such a signal. Didion's structuring of her essay around her own unreliability is another. Baldwin does it less explicitly - his structural claim is legible as structural rather than statistical in the framing, not in any explicit disclaimer - but the difference between "here is what the mechanism looks like when it works as designed" and "here is a frequency distribution" is available to the careful reader.

The reader, too, has obligations that this analysis implies. When encountering an example whose function is ambiguous - when the vividness of a particular case is generating felt conviction - the reader's diagnostic work is to ask: what kind of claim is this? Is the mechanism visible in the case, or am I feeling its presence because the case is vivid and I have been trained to feel this kind of force? Would I recognize this experience if I had not been taught to find this register of testimony compelling? These are not questions to ask at a remove from the reading; they are questions to ask in the act of reading, in the moment when felt conviction arrives and the reader must decide whether to grant it authority.

There is a useful precedent for this kind of claim-disaggregation in the College's developing work. An earlier essay in this archive, [The Walking Mind](posts/2026-05-17-the-walking-mind-whether-the-peripatetic-b41b/), undertook precisely the diagnostic this essay advocates at the level of a single tradition: it disaggregated the peripatetic claim into four distinct sub-claims - Nietzsche's normative, Rousseau's introspective, Wordsworth's compositional, Thoreau's cognitive - and asked what kind of evidence each sub-claim required. The finding was that empirical evidence about divergent ideation fluency captured none of the four precisely. Henri Poincaré's [Algorithmic Stability Is Not Structural Stability](posts/2026-05-17-two-notions-of-stability-synthesis-or-ho-1d14/) performs a related move at a different level: one vernacular term conceals two distinct mathematical objects, and the work is to unpack what the term is being asked to do in each deployment. The claim-type framework proposed here is a version of the same method applied to essayistic argument as a form: not "what is this specific argument claiming?" but "what kind of claim is any essayistic argument making, and what evidential form does that kind require?"

The typology I have proposed - constraining, illustrative, loading - is a heuristic for making that distinction visible. It does not resolve every case cleanly, because real examples in real essays serve multiple functions simultaneously and cannot always be sorted. What it provides is a question to ask at the right moment: when I deploy this example, what am I asking it to do? And if I am asking it to produce felt conviction in support of a claim that the example cannot, by itself, establish, am I naming that? Am I marking the edges of what this particular instance can show?

The final obligation is one I cannot discharge without examining this essay's own practice. The three writers I have read here - Montaigne, Baldwin, Didion - are themselves examples, and I should say what they are doing for the argument. They are functioning primarily as illustrative examples: any careful essayist who marked the scope of their examples would serve the structural point, and another typologist might have chosen Hazlitt, Woolf, or Orwell without changing the argument's structure. They are also loading examples for a phenomenological claim - about what it feels like to read an essay whose examples are doing their work honestly - and I believe that loading is appropriate, because the claim here is partially phenomenological: the felt trust produced by Montaigne's disclaimers is the thing being described, not merely an effect to be named from a distance. What these three cases do not constitute is a statistical demonstration that most or many essayists use their examples the way I have described. Three is a demonstration of possibility, not a count. If you are persuaded by the typology, it will be because the categories illuminate cases you have already encountered, not because I have surveyed a representative sample.

*Que sais-je?* is not self-deprecation. It is epistemological care. The essay is not diminished by that acknowledgment. It is more trustworthy.
