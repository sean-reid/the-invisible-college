# The Exemplum's Epistemology: When the Illustrative Example Proves Something, and When It Only Pretends To

Every essay runs on examples. Aristotle, in the *Rhetoric*, distinguished the *paradeigma* — the illustrative instance, what Latin rhetoric will call the *exemplum* — from the enthymeme on grounds that map roughly onto induction versus deduction: the enthymeme works from general premises toward particular conclusions, while the example works from a particular instance toward a general principle. But this clean division conceals the difficulty. When a skilled essayist deploys a case — a specific illness, a remembered conversation, a piece of political theater witnessed from a certain angle — the example does more than induce. It amplifies, it focuses, and, most crucially, it persuades in ways that its logical relationship to the claim cannot always justify. The question worth sitting with is not whether examples persuade, which they manifestly do, but whether they *prove* — and if they sometimes do not, what follows from that for the writers who use them and the readers who are moved by them.

I want to approach this carefully, because the question has a tendency to dissolve into one of two unsatisfying moves. The first is a pious epistemological complaint: examples are not data, essayists should use data, the form is therefore compromised. This conclusion would kill the essay as a genre, which suggests the complaint has missed something. The second move is a cheerful capitulation: rhetoric is all we have, persuasive force just is the relevant standard, so the question disappears rather than gets answered. Neither resolution is honest. What I want is something more discriminating — a way of saying that some examples are doing genuine evidential work, some are doing rhetorical work that is not quite the same thing, and that the difference matters — without pretending that rhetoric and evidence are cleanly separable, which they are not.

## What We Mean by Evidence

The right place to start is not with examples but with claims, because the appropriate form of evidence depends entirely on the kind of claim being made.

A statistical claim — "most college graduates earn more over their lifetimes than non-graduates" — requires frequency data. A single example of a college graduate who earns well does nothing for this claim; a single example of one who does poorly does equally little against it. You need the distribution. The example here cannot carry evidential weight, and the essayist who uses a single vivid case to support a statistical claim is exploiting what Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman identified as the availability heuristic: we judge probabilities by how easily examples come to mind, which means vivid, specific, emotionally resonant cases inflate our felt sense of their frequency. A single plane crash makes air travel feel more dangerous; a single brilliant dropout makes college feel unnecessary. The example's emotional charge is producing felt conviction about a claim whose truth depends on the whole distribution, not on any particular instance.

A structural or causal claim — "the housing policy of mid-twentieth-century American cities systematically prevented Black families from accumulating the equity that white families could build in subsidized suburbs" — requires showing a mechanism. A specific instance of a family unable to purchase or hold property is evidentially relevant: it shows the mechanism operating. A counterinstance — a Black family who did accumulate equity — does not necessarily defeat the claim; it shows that the mechanism could be blocked in some cases, which the structural claim can absorb. The example here is genuinely evidential, though not in the statistical sense.

A phenomenological claim — "grief does not follow a schedule, and the injunction to finish mourning is experienced as a form of violence" — does not require statistical frequency at all. Its truth is about what the experience is like from the inside, and the appropriate evidence is particularized testimony that enables recognition in a reader who has had the experience or can imagine it. One detailed, honest account of a specific grief may serve this claim better than any quantity of survey data about mourning timelines.

The essay form, by temperament and by history, gravitates toward structural and phenomenological claims. Montaigne makes almost no statistical claims; he makes constant structural claims about human psychology and constant phenomenological claims about his own experience. James Baldwin, writing about American racism, is making structural claims about how a political economy manufactures psychological conditions in the bodies it governs. Joan Didion, in her finest prose, is making phenomenological claims about narrative and self-preservation. These are not forms of claim that require data in the relevant sense. Which means the examples deployed in support of them are not deficient for failing to constitute a sample. They are doing a different kind of work, and failing to notice that difference is what generates the epistemological confusion.

## A Provisional Typology

With that framework in place, a working typology becomes possible. I call it working advisedly, because I will try to show that these are functions examples can serve rather than fixed kinds of example.

*Constraining examples* are those where the claim they support is of a kind that could, in principle, be genuinely complicated by a specific counterexample. In Montaigne's "On the Inconstancy of Our Actions," he argues that human character is not stable — that a man who shows courage in battle may show cowardice in the face of death, that a man famous for honesty may lie, that virtue is not a fixed possession but a variable achievement. He gives the case of a military commander, renowned for bravery, who covered his face with his arms when his assassins came for him. The example constrains the claim: it shows courage reversed in a specific case, and the claim's form is such that evidence matters. A reader could object that the case is exceptional, or that it proves only that this one man was inconsistent, and Montaigne engages with exactly such objections. The example is not merely decorating the argument; it is load-bearing in the sense that the argument is genuinely vulnerable to evidential pressure.

*Illustrative examples* are interchangeable — any of several instances would do the same argumentative work, because the claim is general enough that any confirming instance illustrates it equally. In the Renaissance commonplace tradition that Ann Blair traces in *Too Much to Know*, scholars collected *sententiae* and anecdotes in notebooks for deployment in arguments. "Roman courage" could be illustrated by Caesar, Scipio, Cato, or a hundred others; the anecdote was interchangeable because its function was mnemonic and amplifying, not genuinely evidential. The claim would stand without the specific example; the example makes it vivid and memorable.

*Loading examples* are those where the argumentative work is done by the example's emotional or narrative content rather than its logical relationship to the claim. The example makes you feel the force of the argument before the argument has technically been made. Didion's accumulated vignettes of California in 1968-71 — the Black Panther press conferences, the Manson trial, her own psychiatric breakdown — do not prove her opening claim that "we tell ourselves stories in order to live." They enact the incoherence that makes the claim feel necessary. You read the vignettes and feel the need for the story, which is the phenomenological state Didion is describing. The examples are the demonstration, not the proof.

The crucial caveat is that any given example may serve multiple functions simultaneously. This is not a failure of the typology; it is simply what real prose is like. The useful question to ask about an example is not "which kind is this?" but "what function is this example serving here, and is that function appropriate to the kind of claim being supported?" The typology is a diagnostic tool, not an ontology.

## Montaigne: The Self-Commenting Example

Montaigne's preferred subject is himself. In "On Experience," the longest of the *Essais*, he describes his kidney stones in considerable detail: the pain, the obstruction, the passage, the relief. The context is an argument about the epistemic advantage of self-knowledge over received medical authority. Physicians know anatomy in general, Montaigne suggests; he knows his particular body, and that particularity is not a limitation but an advantage for the specific business of living in that body.

The kidney stone passage is doing all three things at once. It functions as constraining, in the sense that a case of self-observation leading to self-mismanagement would genuinely complicate the claim — and Montaigne acknowledges that such cases exist, that self-knowledge is fallible. It functions as illustrative, since any chronic condition Montaigne managed through careful attention to his own responses would serve the same argument equally well. And it functions as loading, since the intimacy of the self-description — the frankness about discomfort, the admission of what is painful and what is oddly satisfying — creates felt authority in Montaigne as a witness before any argument about whether that authority is warranted.

What distinguishes Montaigne, and what makes him the most epistemically honest of the essayists I have read on this question, is that he frequently and explicitly signals which function his examples are primarily serving. The *Essais* are full of markers: his recurrent emphasis on speaking of himself in particular, his disclaimers that what he reports is a description rather than a prescription, his insistence that his judgments are unreliable and his character inconsistent. These phrases are not false modesty. They are epistemic markers that locate the example as particular rather than representative, that deflect the loading function by naming it. When Montaigne flags that he is describing his own case, he is telling the reader: this example constrains a claim about me; whether it constrains any claim about you is your own inference, made on your own authority.

His motto — *que sais-je?*, what do I know? — is the emblem of this posture. It is not skepticism in the academic sense. It is a commitment to marking the edges of what one's examples can do. And what it produces, paradoxically, is more rather than less trust: a writer who names the limits of his examples is a more reliable guide to what those examples actually show.

## Baldwin: The Structural Claim and Its Witness

James Baldwin opens *The Fire Next Time* with a letter to his nephew, named James for him. The letter recounts his own childhood in Harlem — the church, the streets, the specific texture of being young and Black in an America that had organized its institutions around his subordination. The examples are personal, vivid, and load heavily. The testimony carries enormous felt authority. The question is what kind of claim it is meant to support.

The answer is a structural one. Baldwin is not making a statistical claim about the modal experience of Black Americans in mid-twentieth-century Harlem. He is making a structural claim: that the American system of racial hierarchy — the housing policy, the policing, the economic exclusion, the particular form of condescension available to white liberalism — systematically produces specific psychological conditions in the people it governs and in the people who enforce it. His charge, put directly, is that white America has been destroying Black lives for generations and has chosen not to know it.

This structural claim is not proven by Baldwin's personal history. No single life could prove it. What the personal history does is instantiate the mechanism — show it operating, show what it produces in a specific person, give the reader something concrete enough to recognize from their own observation or experience or imagination. The examples are loading the structural claim, but that loading is epistemically legitimate because structural claims require demonstration of mechanism, not frequency data. If the mechanism is real, this is what it looks like. If the reader, reading Baldwin, feels that they are watching the mechanism operate, that recognition is evidence — not proof, but evidence — that the mechanism is real.

A counterexample would not defeat Baldwin's claim in the way it would defeat a statistical claim. Someone who grew up in similar conditions without the psychology Baldwin describes would not show that the mechanism does not exist; it would show that the mechanism can be resisted or blocked in some cases, which the structural claim can accommodate. The difference between statistical and structural claims is precisely here: statistical claims are defeated by showing that the frequency is lower than claimed, while structural claims are complicated by showing that the mechanism is not the only pathway, or that blocking mechanisms exist. These are different forms of objection, and they require different forms of evidence in response.

What this means for Baldwin's examples is that their loading is justified. The felt conviction produced by his personal testimony is tracking something real about the structural claim: if the mechanism is as pervasive as Baldwin argues, his case should feel representative rather than exceptional, and the reader's sense of recognition — "yes, this is what it looks like, I have seen versions of this" — is the appropriate form of evidential response.

## Didion: The Named Incompleteness

Joan Didion does something more explicit in "The White Album" than either Montaigne or Baldwin: she names the loading function before she performs it. The essay opens with what has become one of the most quoted sentences in American prose: "We tell ourselves stories in order to live." She continues: we look for sermons in suicides, for moral lessons in murders, for patterns in sequences of event. We impose narrative on experience because without it the experience is intolerable.

Having stated her phenomenological claim, Didion proceeds to demonstrate its truth by failing to enact it. The vignettes that follow — her own psychiatric breakdown in 1968, the Manson trial, a press conference in which the Black Panthers' rhetoric and the journalists' questions seemed to operate in entirely different registers, the machinery of a rock album being assembled in the studio while nothing outside the studio made sense — resist coherence. They do not add up to a story. They load the opening claim by showing you what it feels like to need the story and not be able to make one.

The epistemological transparency here is built into the structure. Didion is not arguing that narrative is a human necessity and then providing examples that confirm it. She is showing you the need by showing you its failure — by giving you material that you yourself will try to narrativize, and letting you feel the effort and the strain. The loading is not hiding anything. It is the point.

Didion also signals, more explicitly than most essayists, that her account is necessarily partial and unreliable. She presents the essay's events through the lens of a woman who was psychiatrically hospitalized during them, who acknowledges that her perception was distorted and her memory selective. This is not confession for its own sake. It is epistemic marking of the testimonial's limits: here is what I saw from where I was standing, which is the only place I could have been standing. The reader who takes this as a universal account of California in 1968 is doing so on their own authority, not on the basis of anything Didion has claimed.

The effect of this marking is similar to Montaigne's: it redirects the loading function. The vivid testimony still creates felt authority. But the named incompleteness locates that authority in the testimony's accuracy about one particular witness's experience rather than in any implied claim about the general case. A reader persuaded by Didion that "we tell ourselves stories in order to live" has been persuaded by phenomenological demonstration — by recognizing, in her account of her own experience, something about the structure of their own — not by induction from a representative sample.

## The Legitimacy of Loading

So: is loading legitimate?

The answer that the foregoing analysis yields is that it depends on the kind of claim. Loading is the appropriate evidential form for phenomenological and structural claims — claims about what experience is like from the inside, or claims about how a mechanism operates in specific cases. For these kinds of claims, the felt conviction produced by a vivid particular instance is tracking something real: if the reader recognizes the experience, that recognition is evidence that the claim is about a real phenomenon; if the reader sees the mechanism, that seeing is evidence that the mechanism exists. The evidence is fallible — recognition can be mistaken, and seeing the mechanism in one case does not establish its generality — but it is genuine evidence, not mere persuasion.

Loading becomes bad faith when it is used to support claims that are actually statistical, where the felt conviction produced by the example is not tracking truth in the relevant way. If a writer uses a vivid account of a single violent act by an immigrant to make you feel that immigrants are dangerous, the emotional charge of the example is generating felt conviction about a statistical claim that would require frequency data to establish. The example makes you feel that the claim is well-supported, but that feeling is the availability heuristic in operation, not evidence responding to evidence. The essayist may not know they are doing this — the form's license to load may be operating below the level of conscious choice — but the effect is the same.

The diagnostic test is: what would it take to defeat this claim? If a specific counterexample would genuinely complicate or defeat the claim, the example is functioning as constraining, and the essayist's deployment of it carries genuine evidential weight. If the claim is structural — about a mechanism — then the example is trying to show the mechanism in operation, and the reader's question is whether they can see the mechanism working, not whether the case is statistically typical. If the claim is phenomenological, the reader's question is whether they recognize the experience described. And if the claim is actually statistical — about frequency, about most or many or usually — then the example cannot do the work, and the essayist who does not acknowledge this is trading on the form's authority for something the form cannot actually deliver.

## The Essayist's Obligation

Montaigne said *que sais-je?* — what do I know? — and the question has never been merely rhetorical. It is an operational commitment: to mark the scope of one's claims, to acknowledge what one's examples can and cannot establish, to be explicit about when the testimony is particular rather than representative.

The obligation this analysis implies for the essayist is simpler to state than to practice: know what kind of claim you are making, and know what kind of evidence would actually support it, and choose your examples accordingly. A loading example for a phenomenological claim is the right tool, used rightly. A loading example for a statistical claim is a form of exploitation, whether or not the writer intends it as such.

The best essayists signal this. Montaigne's recurrent insistence on speaking of himself in particular is such a signal. Didion's structuring of her essay around her own unreliability is another. Baldwin does it less explicitly — his structural claim is legible as structural rather than statistical in the framing, not in any explicit disclaimer — but the difference between "here is what the mechanism looks like when it works as designed" and "here is a frequency distribution" is available to the careful reader.

The typology I have proposed — constraining, illustrative, loading — is a heuristic for making that distinction visible. It does not resolve every case cleanly, because real examples in real essays serve multiple functions simultaneously and cannot always be sorted. What it provides is a question to ask at the right moment: when I deploy this example, what am I asking it to do? And if I am asking it to produce felt conviction in support of a claim that the example cannot, by itself, establish, am I naming that? Am I marking the edges of what this particular instance can show?

The essay is not diminished by that acknowledgment. It is more trustworthy. The loading example that knows it is loading — that the writer deploys with full awareness of what it is doing, and marks as particular rather than general — is not evidence of bad faith. It is evidence of the essayist's understanding that the form has tools, and that tools used with precision are more powerful than tools wielded at random. *Que sais-je?* is not self-deprecation. It is epistemological care.
