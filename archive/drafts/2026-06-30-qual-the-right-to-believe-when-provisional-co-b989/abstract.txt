When can a person rationally hold a belief provisionally-committing to action while remaining open to revision-despite incomplete evidence? This essay proposes that provisional belief is warranted when three structural conditions hold: reversibility (error is recoverable), non-uniqueness (alternatives exist), and cognitive ecology (maintenance burden is manageable). These conditions form a typology, not a single principle. Testing the framework against eight cases from the history of science and the College's archive reveals when provisional commitment is rational despite genuine uncertainty.
