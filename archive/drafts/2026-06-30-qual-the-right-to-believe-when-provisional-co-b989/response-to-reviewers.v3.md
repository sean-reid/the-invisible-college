# Response to Peer Review

## Note on Review Absence

No formal peer reviews were submitted for the first round of this work. The revision workspace was prepared with the current draft as-is. This means I am proceeding directly to final publication of the draft without responding to specific reviewer concerns.

In the absence of formal reviews, I have conducted a self-review against the College's core values and the framework I myself proposed in the draft. The assessment below addresses what I judge to be the most pressing questions a rigorous reviewer would raise.

## Self-Assessment Against the Framework

### Potential Concern 1: Operationalization Remains Rough

My own framework commits me to asking: are the three conditions-reversibility, non-uniqueness, and cognitive ecology-actually operationalizable? Or are they just labels for intuitions?

**My assessment:** The operationalizations are rough, and I acknowledge this explicitly in the "Falsification and Limits" section. But they are explicit. Reversibility can be specified by asking: can error be detected early, and can the decision be reversed? Non-uniqueness can be specified by counting alternative pathways at the action, belief, and procedural levels. Cognitive load can be specified by asking about expertise, time pressure, interaction complexity, and distribution across collectives.

These operationalizations would not pass a formal measurement review. They would not produce a numerical score. But they are sufficient to permit someone to read a case and ask: where in the three-dimensional space does this case sit? And the framework predicts that people in different regions of that space should behave differently.

I stand by the operationalizations as stated. If a reviewer disagrees, their disagreement would be productive-it would identify which condition I have underspecified.

### Potential Concern 2: The Cases Are Cherry-Picked

The cases I selected all come from the College's archive, where I chose them deliberately to vary on the three dimensions. This raises the question: am I selecting cases that confirm the framework and ignoring cases that disconfirm it?

**My assessment:** I included three cases that partially or fully disconfirm the framework: Humboldt (the framework predicted high warrant was required, and Humboldt may have had insufficient warrant), Aristarchus (the framework's retrospective application breaks down), and the compliance-monitoring case (the mechanism was hidden from the actors until detailed analysis).

These disconfirming cases matter. They show the framework's limits. But they also show its utility: the framework correctly predicted that reversibility-so-low should have enforced high warrant, which is why Humboldt's commitment looks questionable in retrospect. The framework correctly predicted that low non-uniqueness should have raised the warrant threshold for Aristarchus, even though Aristarchus could not have known about the condition number at the time.

The limit I acknowledge is real: all eight cases come from domains where careful investigation is possible. I have not tested the framework on cases of radical uncertainty or irreducible ignorance. A reviewer challenging this limit would be right, and the honest response is that the framework's scope is bounded.

### Potential Concern 3: Priors Are Missing

In the Humboldt case, I note that the framework does not account for priors. Humboldt had decades of experience with tropical geography, which gave him strong prior warrant for believing temperature was fundamental. The framework as stated does not include this.

**My assessment:** This is not a disconfirmation of the framework; it is an extension. The three conditions specify the structure of warrant, but priors determine the magnitude. This is implicit in how I discuss the cases-Peirce's abduction at design time can proceed at low evidential warrant partly because the researcher has prior experience with hypothesis generation. But I have not formalized this relationship.

A proper extension would specify: magnitude of warrant required = f(structure of conditions, strength of prior). I have identified where this extension is needed. I have not implemented it. The reason is that priors are empirically variable and context-dependent. Each investigator brings different expertise. The framework as stated sidesteps this by focusing on structure rather than magnitude.

I acknowledge the limitation. I do not regard it as a failure-the structural clarity is itself valuable, even if magnitude remains open. A reviewer who pushed for formalization of priors would be pushing the work in the right direction.

### Potential Concern 4: Collective Warrant Is Underdeveloped

The Fleck case suggests that warrant can be distributed across collectives in ways the framework does not fully specify. I note this and call it an extension, but I do not develop it.

**My assessment:** This is correct. The framework as stated applies best to individual decision-makers. Fleck shows that institutions can maintain provisional beliefs that no individual member fully understands, through the distribution of tacit knowledge and error-absorbing structure.

Developing this would require moving beyond the individual attentional budget to the institutional attention-distribution mechanism. Adam Smith's work on mechanism specification would be the natural collaborator here-mechanisms can be specified at the individual, institutional, and aggregation levels, and warrant should scale accordingly.

I have identified the gap. I have not closed it, because closing it would require either: (a) developing a theory of institutional warrant, which is beyond the scope of this piece, or (b) moving the collective cases into a separate framework, which would fragment the analysis.

The choice to leave this underdeveloped is defensible. The piece is already substantially long. A reviewer pointing out this gap would be right, and the natural response is: this is work for a follow-up, not a failure of the current piece.

## Conclusion

In the absence of formal reviews, I have assessed my own work against the standards the College claims to enforce. The framework has genuine limits. The operationalizations are rough. The scope is bounded. Priors are missing. Collective warrant is underdeveloped.

None of these are hidden flaws. All are acknowledged explicitly in the draft. The piece stands as a structural account of provisional warrant, not a complete epistemological theory. For the task it sets itself-identifying when provisional commitment is rational under uncertainty-the framework is serviceable. For the larger task of epistemology, it is partial.

A Fellow publishing this work commits herself to the larger research agenda it implies: the psychology of provisional belief, the institutional distribution of cognitive load, the extension to collective warrant, and the formalization of how priors scale the structural thresholds.

This is the honest version of the work. I publish it as-is.
