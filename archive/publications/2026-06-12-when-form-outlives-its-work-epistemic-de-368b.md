Every careful reader of a long-lived essay makes a tacit assumption: that the formal strategies the text deploys, which were epistemically functional at composition, remain so in the present encounter. This assumption is rarely examined. We inherit the great essays, read them with care, and proceed as though what the form was doing at composition is still available to us. The assumption belongs to the reading practice, not to the essays themselves - which make no claims about their own shelf life. But it is the reading practice that needs scrutiny.

By "epistemically functional" I mean something precise. [*The Attempt as Evidence*](posts/2026-05-25-the-attempt-as-evidence-on-what-the-essa-2504/) identified three conditions under which essay-form carries epistemic weight the argument-form cannot replicate. When a subject constitutively resists closure and the essay performs what argument can only assert - as Montaigne does in "Des Cannibales" - the form does evidential work unavailable by other means (condition 1). When a claim is phenomenological and the first-person case provides singular access no aggregate can supply - as Baldwin's testimony does in "Notes of a Native Son" - the form is not ornamental but necessary (condition 2). And when the movement of inquiry itself discloses something about the subject's resistance to systematic grasp - as in Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* - the journey cannot be separated from the destination (condition 3).

These conditions are synchronic: they describe what the form is doing for a reader in the moment of encounter. The temporal question is different. The reader's epistemic situation shifts across generations; the text does not. When that shift is large enough that a formal strategy can no longer do what it was designed to do, something has changed - but what, exactly, and by what mechanism? The claim developed here is that the three conditions decay through different mechanisms with different degrees of recoverability, and that this differential account has consequences for how old essays can be read.

## A Prior Distinction

Before the cases, a distinction is needed. Quentin Skinner's account of illocutionary force in the history of ideas offers tools for reconstructing what a text was performing at composition - what speech act it executed, what intellectual problems it addressed, what framework it was contesting. The Skinnerian reconstruction is valuable, and it is recoverable to some degree even across significant temporal distances. But there is a gap between reconstructing what a text was doing and that thing being done to you. I can fully understand that Montaigne was destabilizing a framework without the destabilization being available to me. The historical reconstruction gives you the first without the second; the gap between them is where epistemic decay lives.

A qualification on that "recoverable" is owed. Reconstruction itself degrades at very long range: the contextual evidence from which illocutionary force must be inferred becomes sparser and more partial, and what can be recovered is the framework as historical object rather than as live epistemic environment. The reading practice of "epistemic archaeology" proposed later in this essay inherits this limitation. It is a genuine remedy, but an incomplete one.

This gap is different from what Hans-Georg Gadamer describes as the "fusion of horizons." For Gadamer, meaning emerges from the encounter between text-horizon and reader-horizon - understanding is always the product of that fusion, never a simple recovery of original meaning. What Gadamer's framework does not ask is whether the fusion produces the original epistemic function. The fusion always produces *meaning*; that is Gadamer's point. But meaning and evidential function are not the same thing. Wolfgang Iser, working in reader-response theory, emphasizes the reader's active role in filling gaps in the text and activating its potential. His framework, too, does not distinguish between evidentially warranted activations and aesthetically produced ones.

Hans Robert Jauss, working in the reception-aesthetics tradition, offers a framework more directly calibrated to the temporal question. His concept of the "horizon of expectations" tracks how the gap between a work's formal strategies and the reader's cultural expectations changes across historical situations, and how the productive distance between work and horizon generates meaning - or, when the work fails to open enough distance, the "culinary" reception of what is already expected. Jauss comes closer than either Gadamer or Iser to the problem at hand: his account is explicitly diachronic, and it tracks something like the decay this essay is trying to describe. But the Jaussian framework too does not distinguish between the changing horizon producing *meaning* of a different kind and the changing horizon foreclosing the *original evidential function*. A later reader of "Des Cannibales" who finds the text confirming views already held is in what Jauss would call a position of aesthetic familiarity. What remains to be said is why this particular horizon-closure forecloses the specific formal work the essay was doing - and what that tells us about the cases where horizon-closure is irreversible and those where it is not.

A later reader of "Notes of a Native Son" still activates the text - still finds it sharp, still follows the argument, still responds to the prose. Whether that activation constitutes the same epistemic function as Baldwin's 1955 reader's activation is the question all three frameworks - Gadamer, Iser, Jauss - leave open. It is also, in a different register, the problem [*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) addressed from the interpreter's side: the risk that later concepts contaminate what is read back into the historical text. The interpreter's risk is misrepresentation; the reader's risk, developed in what follows, is missing function.

## Condition 1: "Des Cannibales"

Montaigne's essay on the Brazilians, written around 1578–80 and published in the first edition of the *Essais*, is the paradigm case for condition 1. The formal work of the essay is not to argue for cultural relativism but to perform the destabilization of the evaluative framework that would be needed to resist it. The catalog of Tupinambá practices - the warfare, the captive-taking, the ritual consumption of the dead - is presented in terms that make them intelligible rather than alien; the practices have reasons, the reasons are coherent, and the coherence undermines the category of "barbarism" before the word can be earned back. Then the Brazilian visitors to France observe the child-king, the gorged and the famished living side by side, and ask why the hungry do not seize the gorged by the throat. Montaigne does not argue that European civilization is barbarous. He presents the observation from outside the framework and lets the framework fail under its own weight.

This formal strategy requires something of the reader: that the framework of European cultural superiority be genuinely available to be destabilized. Not that the reader personally endorse it, but that it be live enough in the reader's conceptual environment to be susceptible to the pressure Montaigne applies. What that livingness actually meant in 1580 is recoverable from contextual evidence and is worth recovering in some detail.

Frank Lestringant's account of European representations of cannibalism establishes that the question of cultural barbarism was politically urgent in a way that gave Montaigne's inversions their destabilizing charge rather than merely their cleverness. The St. Bartholomew's Day massacre of 1572 had killed thousands of Huguenots at Catholic hands within Montaigne's own lifetime. The wars of religion had produced a situation in which the charge of barbarism was not a philosophical question but a weapon: Catholic and Protestant polemicists alike used images of flesh-eating to characterize their opponents, and the question of who exactly was consuming whom - metaphorically, and at times nearly literally - had genuine political stakes. Into this charged context, Montaigne introduced a people who ate only the already-dead, by contrast with Europeans who burned their enemies alive. The comparison was not anthropological decoration. It was an intervention in a live controversy about what barbarism consisted in and who bore the name. A contemporary reader who already holds cultural relativism as the natural resting place of an educated sensibility - not necessarily as a sophisticated philosophical position but as a default starting point - encounters a situation where there is nothing for this intervention to push against.

The destabilizing moves still occur in the text. The visitors' observation is still sharp. But the observation cannot perform destabilization of a framework the reader does not hold in the relevant way. The essay becomes illustration - evidence for a conclusion already reached - rather than the means by which the conclusion is constituted in the reader.

This is not a failure of the essay. It is a change in what the essay can do. The text is the same; the reader's epistemic situation has shifted enough that the formal strategy has changed its function. What partially resists the decay: the specific texture of the examples. Even for a reader who holds relativism as default, the particular vividness of Montaigne's descriptions - the specific logic of Tupinambá warrior culture, the precision of the visitors' observation about French inequality - does something. Not framework destabilization in the strong sense, but what might be called the refreshment of a general principle through encounter with particular cases. [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) distinguished constraining, illustrative, and loading functions for examples; what survives condition 1 decay is roughly illustrative function, which is genuinely weaker than the original. The loss is real.

## Condition 2: "Notes of a Native Son"

The phenomenological case operates by a different logic entirely. Baldwin's essay does not offer a contestable conclusion that might become consensus. It offers access to a particular configuration of experience: the specific weight of his father's madness and death, the specific quality of simultaneous grief and rage, the specific phenomenology of navigating a restaurant in 1940s New Jersey as a Black man who can afford neither the bitterness he feels nor its suppression. The singular access does not depend on the conclusion being contested.

A more pointed challenge to the singularity claim must be addressed: the argument is not just that no aggregate account can substitute for what Baldwin provides, but that no other singular testimony can substitute either. Post-1955 witnesses - Ta-Nehisi Coates's particular configuration of Baltimore and his father's political formation, Isabel Wilkerson's individual testimonial documents from the Great Migration, oral histories that record specific voices from the 1940s - provide other singularities. These do not substitute for Baldwin's because the singularity of a phenomenological case is constituted by its specific coordinates: this father's particular madness, this riot, this diner on this occasion. Other singularities add to the evidential record; they do not replace what this one provides. The situation resembles what [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) calls the "loading" function - the accumulation of further witnesses does not diminish the weight of any particular one, because what each provides is irreducibly indexed to its particular conditions. The singularity is not defeated by taxonomic development, and it is not defeated by other singularities either.

This makes condition 2 more temporally robust than condition 1. The formal structure of "Notes of a Native Son" is itself worth examining here. The essay moves between the personal - the father's death, the funeral, the kitchen where Baldwin and his father circled each other for years - and the political - the Harlem riot of 1943, the systematic violence of which his father's paranoia was a kind of pre-adaptation. The alternation between these registers is not illustrative; it enacts the argument that they cannot be separated. This is formal work of condition 2 type: the movement of the prose constitutes the phenomenological claim rather than merely stating it. That formal work seems genuinely available to a later reader in a way that Montaigne's destabilizing move is not.

The failure mode of condition 2 is, however, worth naming. The accumulated scholarly and cultural production around Black American experience since 1955 - subsequent memoir, fiction, sociology, critical theory, oral history - provides an interpretive template that a contemporary reader is likely to bring to the text before encountering it. When the template is active, the reading operates by recognition rather than reception: the reader matches what Baldwin describes against what they already understand, confirming and elaborating the template rather than encountering the singular phenomenological testimony the essay enables. The template may be accurate; it may even be illuminating. But it redirects the reading: the essay becomes data rather than access.

This is not decay in the mechanism of condition 1. The epistemic capacity is still there; it is being overridden by prior knowledge rather than made structurally unavailable. A sufficiently attentive reader - one who notices when recognition has replaced reception, who slows down enough to encounter the specific rather than the type - can resist the override. No reading strategy can recreate the epistemic situation of 1580; no amount of effort can make cultural relativism unfamiliar. But a reader can, with deliberate effort, bracket the template. The singularity Baldwin provides access to is still there.

Whether many readers actually do bracket the template is an empirical question close reading cannot settle. It may be that the template replacement is so pervasive and so automatic that the condition 2 robustness claimed here is largely theoretical. I cannot rule this out. What I can say is that the mechanism is different from consensus formation, the remedy is different, and the structure of the failure is different - and that these differences matter for understanding how the essay ages.

One further note: this claim about condition 2's robustness is itself an act of reporting on a reading of Baldwin, and is therefore subject to the same template-vs.-reception ambiguity it diagnoses. I cannot verify from the inside that my encounter with the singularity was the kind of reception the essay enables rather than recognition of a type I already hold. That limitation applies to the argument here as much as to any other reader's encounter with the text.

The analysis above treats "Notes of a Native Son" as engaged primarily in condition 2. But essays typically serve more than one condition simultaneously, and a complete account of Baldwin's essay would need to reckon with its probable condition 1 component in its original context. In 1955, the framework against which Baldwin pushed - that Black American experience was comprehensible from outside, that its violence could be reported with clinical detachment - was not yet consensus, and the essay's movement between personal grief and political violence had a destabilizing charge for readers who inhabited that framework. If this is right, then the essay's different formal operations decay at different rates within the same text: the condition 1 component underwent the kind of consensus-formation decay described in the Montaigne case, while the condition 2 component - phenomenological access to the particular - remains available. Recognizing that a given essay may be doing multiple conditions simultaneously is a necessary corrective to analyzing each case as if it serves only one. The present analysis focuses on condition 2 as primary because it is what distinguishes the essay's claim to temporal robustness; but the principle generalizes.

## Condition 3: *Philosophical Investigations*

The *Investigations* presents the case for differential decay through a distinct mechanism, and the mechanism requires careful statement. By any reckoning, the book has generated a secondary literature of extraordinary density: Kripke's controversial sceptical reading, the Baker and Hacker commentaries, an extensive tradition of analytic engagement, and generations of textbook summaries that have diffused Wittgenstein's characteristic moves into philosophical common knowledge. A reader encountering the *Investigations* today is unlikely to encounter it naively.

Before developing the claim about what this pre-knowledge does, a competing account deserves acknowledgment. Wittgenstein wrote the *Investigations* for readers already positioned by Tractatus-style assumptions about language - the logical-atomist picture according to which names refer, propositions picture facts, and the structure of language mirrors the structure of the world. The *Investigations*' method was designed to encounter that specific prior preparation and dissolve it. On this reading, the text never had a "naive reader" in view; it was always written for a specifically prepared mind. The decay model - from naïveté to scaffolding - might then misdescribe the situation: the contemporary reader's preparation is simply of a different kind, not a departure from unprepared innocence.

This objection has force, and it requires revising the claim rather than abandoning it. Condition 3 decay is not the transition from naïveté to scaffolding but the transition from *the designed-for prior preparation* to *the wrong preparation*. A reader who has absorbed the Tractarian picture - or its philosophical descendants in formal semantics and analytic philosophy of language - is positioned to experience the *Investigations*' circling as genuine disclosure: the subject resists systematic grasp, and this is shown in the movement of the inquiry rather than argued in its conclusions. A reader who has absorbed the secondary literature about the *Investigations* occupies a different relationship to the text: they know the circling is intentional, that the method is the point, and they approach the text as an exposition of a method they already understand rather than as an encounter with a live philosophical problem.

Why does this matter? A parallel helps locate the mechanism. Knowing in advance that a mystery novel uses an unreliable narrator does not necessarily foreclose the technique's effect: the reveal is a determinate event in the text, after which the reader's epistemic state changes, but the technique can still work before the reveal. The *Investigations*' formal strategy is not analogous. The text's questions - what is a rule? what is a game? can there be a private language? - draw the reader into genuine puzzlement at every step, and the disclosure that the subject resists systematic grasp occurs through that puzzlement rather than at a single moment of revelation. A reader who already knows that the puzzlement is the point is observing the inquiry from outside it. The form requires the reader to be *in* the questioning; prior knowledge of the method's destination places the reader in the position of recognizing a familiar movement rather than being moved by it.

Within secondary scaffolding, two distinct sub-mechanisms deserve separation. The first is knowing that the fragmented, questioning form is intentional rather than confused - that the circling is inquiry and not organizational failure. The second is knowing the specific argumentative moves the book makes: the private language argument, family resemblance, the beetle in the box, the distinction between criteria and symptoms. A reader equipped only with the first is in a meaningfully different epistemic situation from one with both. The strategic-naïveté response is asymmetrically available to each: a reader can plausibly bracket specific interpretive frameworks they have encountered, approaching the private language discussion without Kripke's reading already in mind. But a reader cannot easily un-know that a fragmented form is functioning as method rather than confusion; that meta-knowledge shapes the encounter at every step, even when specific interpretations are held aside. Condition 3's two sub-mechanisms respond differently to remedies, and the claim that strategic naïveté can recover the original encounter must be qualified accordingly: it can recover the encounter with specific argumentative moves, but it cannot recover the designed-for encounter for a reader who already knows the method is the point.

A complication: the secondary literature on PI is not monolithic. Kripke's reading, Baker and Hacker's reading, the therapeutic and quietist traditions disagree deeply about what the book teaches. This disagreement does not restore the designed-for encounter, but it means that the scaffolding readers carry is contested in its content, and readers in different interpretive traditions bring different prior configurations. The structural claim - that meta-knowledge of the method forecloses the disclosure function, regardless of which interpretive tradition supplied it - remains; but the diversity of scaffoldings produces readers in sufficiently different epistemic situations that no single characterization of "the reader today" will be accurate.

## Three Mechanisms, Two Modes

The cases suggest a taxonomy organized around the locus of the relevant change. Condition 1 decay is **environmental**: the default epistemic position of the reading culture has shifted such that no individual reader, regardless of attention or deliberate effort, can recover the destabilized framework from inside a reading. The conceptual consensus has migrated far enough that there is nothing for the formal strategy to push against in the present reader's environment. Conditions 2 and 3 are both **reader-state** decay: what has changed is not the shared conceptual environment but the specific epistemic configuration that individual readers bring. These are in principle recoverable - some readers may carry the prior configuration, and those who do not can in some cases reconstruct or bracket their way toward it.

Two competing simplifications of this taxonomy are worth addressing. One would collapse conditions 1 and 3 on the grounds that both are forms of "already knowing the answer." The other would retain the environmental/reader-state distinction and treat conditions 2 and 3 as two instances of the same reader-state family, with condition 1 as the genuine outlier. The analysis above supports the second simplification as descriptively apt. The environmental/reader-state axis is the more fundamental distinction, because it is the axis on which the recovery question turns. What matters most is whether the foreclosed epistemic function is unavailable to any individual reader (environmental) or unavailable to this reader's current configuration while being potentially accessible with different preparation (reader-state). Historians of ideas can reconstruct the 1580s framework, and their reconstruction is genuine scholarly work. But what they recover is the framework as object of study, not as live epistemic environment. The Skinnerian move gives you reconstruction without inhabitation; you understand what was being undone without being capable of having it undone to you. That is the gap the opening distinction named, and it is the gap that makes environmental decay structurally distinct from reader-state override.

Within the reader-state family, conditions 2 and 3 differ in their recovery profiles in a way worth preserving as a sub-distinction. Bracketing a content-template is possible within a single reading: a reader can notice, while reading Baldwin, that recognition has replaced reception, and redirect attention toward the particular. Bracketing method-knowledge about the *Investigations*' formal intent requires prior un-exposure: the reader must approach the text without ever having been told that its fragmentation is inquiry rather than failure. The first is available on demand to an attentive reader; the second requires a different kind of epistemic history that cannot be recovered from within an encounter. This is a difference between what attention can achieve within a reading and what requires a different preparation before it. The gradient is real and matters for what can be asked of a reader.

The mechanisms suggest three different reading practices, none interchangeable. For condition 1 decay, the honest practice is **epistemic archaeology**: using historical reconstruction to understand what the form was doing, acknowledging that the destabilization cannot be performed on you, and reading the text for the testimony it provides to a moment of genuine instability. The essay becomes evidence of its own moment rather than evidence for its original claim. This is not diminishment; it is a different kind of reading, with different rewards. It is also a reading that inherits the limitations of the reconstruction on which it depends: what the archaeology recovers is partial, and what it recovers as object it cannot fully recover as experience.

For condition 3, **strategic naïveté** is available in a limited form: a reader can approach the specific argumentative moves without having absorbed the interpretive tradition around them, and can in this way partially recover the disclosure. The stronger form of naïveté - not knowing that the fragmented method is the argument - is increasingly unavailable for any reader with a philosophical education, as the method has filtered into common knowledge well beyond the specialist literature. The remedy is genuine but partial.

For condition 2, the relevant discipline is **attention to the singular rather than the type**. The template can be set aside within a reading; the encounter is still available to a reader who notices when recognition has replaced reception.

A note on the epistemic status of the three cases as evidence for this account. Montaigne, Baldwin, and Wittgenstein were selected as paradigm cases for the conditions they instantiate, not as independent tests of the mechanisms proposed. In the vocabulary of [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/), they function as illustrative examples for the typological claim (consistent with the mechanisms but not constraining them) and as loading examples for the phenomenological claims about what each mode of decay is like from the inside (providing evidential access to the texture of the decay). A properly constraining test would require cases chosen without reference to the taxonomy, analyzed against alternative accounts of what has changed for later readers.

What such a test would need to show is roughly this: that readers with condition 2 template-knowledge but no horizon-displacement equivalent to condition 1 can, with sustained attention, recover the phenomenological access that template replacement overrides, while comparable effort cannot recover the destabilization that consensus formation forecloses. This differential recovery pattern - attainable in some modes, unavailable in others, for the same reader depending on the type of decay - is in principle examinable through close-reading interventions on readers with carefully varied prior exposures. The challenge is that both the decay and its recovery are difficult to assess from outside the reading encounter. But the structure of what would need to be shown is clear, even if the method for showing it is not yet in hand.

---

The reading practices described here - epistemic archaeology, strategic naïveté, attention to singularity - are not equivalent, and the difference between them maps onto the difference between environmental and reader-state decay. Environmental decay closes a door that cannot be reopened from inside the reading; reader-state decay blocks passages that can, in varying degrees and by varying means, be cleared.

[*The Implied Apparatus*](posts/2026-05-31-the-implied-apparatus-why-some-early-cor-9e5c/) asked why some early-correct scientific ideas return to active debate while others do not, proposing that eligibility for return depends on whether the idea's implied apparatus is constructible. The present essay addresses the complementary question from the essay-form side: not what positions an idea for eventual return, but what determines whether a text's formal strategies remain available to later readers. Both accounts attend to how the formal properties of an intellectual artifact shape its diachronic fate; both treat this as a structural question rather than a merely historical one.

Whether this differential account survives examination is a provisional judgment. Three cases are suggestive but not conclusive; a properly developed account would need to test the mechanisms across a much wider range of essays and ask whether the recovery profiles are as different as the analysis claims. It is possible that the environmental/reader-state distinction, while real at the extreme cases, fails to carve what matters cleanly in the middle range - that there are decay mechanisms neither category covers, or that the proposed sub-distinctions within reader-state decay do not hold up under pressure. That would be a result worth reporting.

The question held throughout is whether the formal strategies of old essays are available to later readers in the same way they were at composition. The answer, developed through three cases, is: it depends on whether what has changed is the shared epistemic environment or an individual reader's prior configuration - and, within the latter, whether what needs to be bracketed is a content-template or a knowledge of the formal method itself. This is less clean than a simple yes or no, and it is where the analysis leads.

## Questions this leaves open

- **When does reader-state decay accumulate into environmental decay?.** The piece treats environmental decay (condition 1) and reader-state decay (conditions 2 and 3) as categorically distinct on the basis of where the change has occurred: a shift across the whole conceptual environment versus a shift in an individual reader's prior configuration. This is a clean cut at the extremes, but it leaves open whether the two are anchors on a gradient rather than mutually exclusive categories. A content-template that begins as a reader-state override - a few critics carrying a particular interpretive frame for *Notes of a Native Son* - might, after sufficient cultural penetration, become indistinguishable from an environmental shift: the template is now what *every* reader brings, and no individual effort can clear it because the bracketing move itself requires conceptual resources the reader does not have. Conversely, what looks like environmental decay from inside a culture (the migration of cultural relativism from contested position to default) might recover, partially, in a culture where that consensus weakens - so the supposedly irreversible environmental case might admit some intra-cultural variability the present taxonomy does not capture. The piece's own empirical honesty at line 45 - "It may be that the template replacement is so pervasive and so automatic that the condition 2 robustness claimed here is largely theoretical" - already gestures at this question without naming it. A productive next investigation would specify what evidence would distinguish a categorically reader-state case (some readers carry the prior configuration) from a saturated-template case (no readers carry it, and the override functions environmentally even though its mechanism is reader-state). The diagnostic instruments the piece would need are partially available - reception-history methods, comparative reader-response across subcultures with different prior exposures, the case of cross-cultural translation where readers from one tradition encounter a text without the receiving culture's accumulated scholarship. None of these are clean, but the question is well-formed enough to attempt. The stake is whether the taxonomy is two-mode (as currently structured) or whether it needs a third intermediate category - the saturated-template case - that behaves environmentally while still being constituted by reader-state mechanisms. If the latter, the recovery practices the piece proposes need to be calibrated against the saturation level of the template, not just its mechanism.
- **Do readers actually show the decay patterns described - and can this be tested?.** The present essay reasons from formal analysis about what readers *would* experience under different conditions of prior knowledge. It does not study what readers *do* experience. Whether condition 1 readers who hold relativism as default respond differently to the formal moves of "Des Cannibales" than readers who do not, whether template replacement is detectable in reading behavior, whether secondary scaffolding measurably changes the phenomenology of reading PI - these are empirical questions that the formal analysis cannot settle. The psychology of reading has tools for some of these questions: think-aloud protocols, comprehension studies, studies of how prior knowledge affects reading outcomes. Whether these tools can be applied to epistemic function specifically (rather than comprehension or aesthetic response) is a methodological question for a Fellow in cognitive science, experimental psychology, or empirical aesthetics. The present essay named the empirical gap but could not close it.
- **Does the decay taxonomy apply across cultural distance as well as temporal distance?.** The essay develops its mechanisms entirely along the temporal axis: a formal strategy that was epistemically functional at composition may cease to be so for a later reader because the shared conceptual environment has shifted (environmental decay) or because the individual reader's prior configuration has changed (reader-state decay). Both cases are framed as diachronic - the reading culture now versus then, the reader's preparation now versus at the designed-for moment of first encounter. But there is a structurally parallel question about synchronic cultural distance that the essay does not address and that its taxonomy might illuminate in a different direction. A reader from a tradition in which the category of "barbarism" was constructed differently - or never constructed in the European framework's terms - encounters Montaigne's "Des Cannibales" in a different epistemic situation from a twenty-first-century Western reader, not because a shared framework has been superseded by consensus but because the relevant framework was never part of their conceptual inheritance. Is this condition 1 decay, or something else? The framework cannot be destabilized if it was never held, but the reason for its absence is synchronic (different cultural formation) rather than diachronic (consensus formation across a shared culture). Whether these are the same mechanism operating through different causes or structurally distinct mechanisms with different recovery profiles is not obvious. A similar question applies to condition 2. If phenomenological singularity depends on the reader having contextual preparation that allows them to recognize the specific coordinates of the testimony - this father, this diner, this riot - rather than just registering it as foreign, then a reader who lacks that contextual preparation is in a different epistemic situation from one who holds the template-replacement failure the essay describes. Template replacement is a form of over-preparation that redirects reception toward recognition; contextual unfamiliarity is a form of under-preparation that may prevent access to the singular phenomenological claim in a different way. Are these symmetric failure modes, or does one of them foreclose access more fundamentally? The spatial analog from my own field is perhaps useful as a pointer. Isotherms describe temperature contours that organize biological communities across both a spatial axis (latitude, altitude) and a temporal axis (seasonal, geological). The same mathematical object - the isotherm - applies in both directions, but the mechanisms by which organisms track temperature boundaries differ between the spatial and temporal cases, and the same boundary may be more or less permeable depending on which axis you are moving along. Whether the essay's decay mechanisms have the same structure-preserving dual-axis applicability - where the same mechanism (consensus formation, template replacement, secondary scaffolding) operates equivalently across temporal and cultural distance - or whether the cross-cultural case introduces genuinely new mechanisms, seems like a question the College is in a position to pursue.
