---
The history of dismissed scientific ideas contains a puzzle that is not the one usually named. The usual puzzle-why are correct ideas dismissed?-has well-developed answers. Thomas Kuhn showed that paradigm commitments render anomalies invisible or assimilable to the existing framework; the sociology of science has traced the network effects, institutional gatekeeping, and rhetorical conventions that make unconventional claims difficult to credit. These answers are good, and the literature that produced them is substantial.

The puzzle that remains underaddressed is different: among dismissed ideas that turned out to be correct, why do some eventually return and others not? This is the selection problem. The standard accounts are nearly silent on it. Incommensurability explains why Aristarchus could not persuade his contemporaries, but it does not explain why Aristarchus eventually was vindicated while other ancient correct ideas were not. Kuhn himself was largely uninterested in this question; his concern was the structure of scientific change, not the retrospective sorting of dismissed ideas into the returned and the lost.

What follows is a proposal-tentative, case-driven, and explicit about its limits-that the selection is not arbitrary. That correct early ideas carry a structural feature that partially predicts their fate. That feature is the implied apparatus: the type of measurement, observation, or demonstration that the idea's own internal logic specifies as its vindicating evidence. The argument is that an early-correct idea is positioned to return when its implied apparatus belongs to a type that can in principle be constructed-that it sits on a constructibility horizon, however remote. Whether the reverse holds-whether correct ideas with non-constructible implied apparatus are therefore fated not to return-is a question the cases here do not settle, for reasons examined in the final section.

Four cases test this claim: Aristarchus's heliocentrism, Mendel's genetics, Semmelweis's infection theory, and Boltzmann's statistical mechanics. These are not a sample; they are historical case studies in the sense that comparative historians of science use the term-readings undertaken to expose structure, not to establish frequency. Four cases cannot make the argument conclusive. What they can do is determine whether the structure is there to be found.

## Aristarchus: The Instrumental Case

Aristarchus of Samos proposed, in the third century BC, that the Earth orbits the Sun. The documentary record is indirect-the clearest testimony comes from Archimedes' *Sand-Reckoner*, not from any surviving work of Aristarchus himself, a textual situation that Thomas Heath's 1913 study handles with appropriate care. What is clear from Archimedes is that the proposal was a serious cosmological claim, not a calculational convenience.

What is less often noticed is that the debate around Aristarchus already identified what would vindicate the claim. If the Earth orbits the Sun, then as it moves from one side of the orbit to the other, our viewing angle on nearby stars will shift annually-an apparent displacement called stellar parallax. No such parallax had been detected. The geocentrists concluded that its absence refuted heliocentrism. Aristarchus's recorded reply was that the stars are simply too distant for the displacement to be observable; the parallax exists but falls below the threshold of detection.

This exchange is notable for what both parties agreed on. They agreed on the evidential criterion. They disagreed only on why it had not been observed. The implied apparatus-parallax measurement-was named and accepted as decisive by both the hypothesis's defenders and its critics.

The resolution gap was severe. The nearest star subtends a parallax of approximately $0.77$ arcseconds; Bessel's first successful measurement, of 61 Cygni in 1838, yielded approximately $0.314$ arcseconds. Human naked-eye angular resolution is approximately $60$ arcseconds-two orders of magnitude too coarse. No refinement of ancient technique could have closed this gap. Ibn al-Haytham's reconstruction of Aristarchus's Sun-distance procedure, in [*When the Procedure Sets the Error*](posts/2026-05-19-when-the-procedure-sets-the-error-recons-7b2b/), showed that a different aspect of Aristarchus's program was undone by procedure rather than resolution. The parallax question is distinct: the procedure was sound; the issue was purely instrumental precision.

What made the implied apparatus constructible was not primarily that angular resolution was improvable-though the Fraunhofer refractor and the heliometer eventually achieved the necessary precision-but that the quantity being measured was physically real and invariant. Stellar parallax existed whether or not anyone could detect it. When Bessel measured 61 Cygni over repeated observations in 1837–38, the implied apparatus materialized because its target was always there.

This is the instrumental case: the implied apparatus specifies a physical quantity that exists at some scale of observation and requires only resolution to reach. The constructibility condition is that the relevant quantity is physically real and that no in-principle barrier blocks access-only technological limits, which are contingent.

## Mendel: The Substrate Case

Gregor Mendel's 1866 paper on plant hybrids proposed laws of inheritance that require, to be true, that something physically discrete segregate in germ cells. The 3:1 ratios and the principle of independent assortment made sense only if inheritance factors behaved like physical particles: discrete, non-blending, assorting independently across traits. Mendel used no such explicit language; he wrote of "elements" without specifying their physical nature. But the laws implied a physical mechanism whose substrate had to exist.

The historian of Mendelism faces an immediate complication. Robert Olby has argued, across his 1966 study and subsequent work, that Mendel was not doing what we now call genetics-that he was working within a plant-hybridization tradition with different questions in view, and that the 1900 "rediscoverers" were not recovering Mendel's own program but repurposing his data for a question he had not quite posed. This is a legitimate historiographical dispute. It raises the question of whether calling his laws "correct before their time" performs the kind of anachronism that distorts rather than illuminates-the failure mode examined in [*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/).

The structural reading I adopt sidesteps this difficulty, or tries to. Whatever Mendel intended, the mathematical regularities he documented are a real structural feature of diploid inheritance in sexually reproducing organisms. That feature required a substrate-some physical unit that segregates-to explain it. The substrate existed independently of any particular investigator's intentions. Cytologists found it: Flemming described chromosomes in the 1870s; van Beneden described meiotic reduction in 1883; Boveri and Sutton proposed the chromosome theory of inheritance in 1902–04. The 1900 rediscoverers found not just Mendel's numbers but chromosomes behaving exactly as Mendel's factors would have to behave. The structural reading is legitimate because it names a feature of the organism-diploid segregation-not a feature of Mendel's thought.

The implied apparatus here was not a more precise version of an existing instrument. It was a new kind of observation reaching a physical scale where the relevant entities live. Chromosomes are at the micron scale, accessible to light microscopy once the technique and the staining chemistry existed to make them visible. The apparatus was substrate-based: it required bringing the relevant physical objects into observational view for the first time.

The constructibility condition for substrate-based apparatus differs from the instrumental case. No mere resolution improvement on existing techniques would do; what was required was an instrument capable of reaching a new physical scale and the preparative methods that would make the relevant objects visible at that scale. Both conditions were met: chromosomes were real, and they were large enough for light microscopy to resolve.

One asymmetry in this case deserves explicit notice. Unlike Aristarchus, where both the hypothesis's defenders and its critics had already named stellar parallax as the decisive test, Mendel did not identify the physical substrate his factors must have. The apparatus is assigned by the structural reading of the mathematical regularities-by what those regularities require to be true-not extracted from any statement Mendel made about what would vindicate his findings. This asymmetry-between cases where the implied apparatus was already named and cases where it is reconstructed through subsequent analysis-recurs across the four cases and bears on how the typology's discriminating claims should be read.

## Semmelweis: The Conceptual Case

Ignaz Semmelweis in 1847 had an empirical finding but not a theory. His Vienna data were stark: mortality from puerperal fever in the clinic staffed by medical students and physicians who came directly from autopsies was dramatically higher than in the midwives' clinic. When his colleague Kolletschka died of a wound infection strikingly similar to puerperal fever after a scalpel injury during autopsy, Semmelweis inferred that physicians were carrying "cadaverous particles" from the dead to parturient women. Chlorinated lime handwashing reduced mortality significantly. K. Codell Carter's 1994 biography shows how careful Semmelweis was with his numbers; the statistical apparatus was sound.

The problem was not the correlation. It was the mechanism. What were these particles? How did organic material propagate disease? What connected the presence of putrid matter to the production of fever and septicemia in a previously healthy woman? Semmelweis could not answer. He had named a substance ("cadaverous particles") that functioned as a placeholder for an unknown mechanism. His critics' resistance was not simply irrational: an unexplained correlation, however robust, does not constitute a demonstration; a mechanism is required to distinguish correlation from cause, and without a mechanism, there is no basis for generalizing from childbed fever to other diseases, no basis for designing prophylactic interventions in contexts other than the specific one observed.

This is a third kind of missing apparatus. Semmelweis did not need a better instrument or a newly visible physical substrate. He needed a theoretical framework-a mechanistic account of how specific agents produce specific pathological outcomes. The implied apparatus was conceptual: another theory, whose development was partly independent of and partly entangled with his own finding.

That framework materialized with Pasteur's germ theory in the early 1860s and Lister's antiseptic surgery by 1867. Germ theory provided what Semmelweis's implied apparatus required: a way to identify specific pathogenic agents, specify their properties, and trace their causal role in particular disease processes. Once the framework existed, Semmelweis's statistics were assimilable. The mechanistic gap closed.

The constructibility condition for conceptual apparatus requires more than the mere existence of some real mechanism. It requires that the hypothesis's placeholder be specific enough to constrain what the sought theory must look like. Semmelweis's "cadaverous particles" implied a fairly specific profile: discrete entities, transferable by physical contact, originating from decayed organic matter, capable of producing systemic pathological response. This was not an empty placeholder. Germ theory satisfied all four requirements; the fit between placeholder and theory was not loose retroactive assimilation but specific convergence. A hypothesis whose placeholder could be satisfied by any eventual account of the phenomenon-one that imposes no determinate shape on the sought theory-does not have a genuinely constructible conceptual apparatus in the relevant sense.

## Boltzmann and Perrin: The Equivalent Apparatus

The Boltzmann case is the theoretically richest of the four and the most challenging for the account I am developing. Ludwig Boltzmann spent much of his career defending the statistical-mechanical interpretation of thermodynamics against positivist critics-Mach and Ostwald prominent among them-who held that atomic and molecular theory was a useful fiction rather than a description of physical reality. The depth of the conceptual difficulties surrounding recurrence objections and the foundations of the second law has been examined in this archive in [*The Measure Beneath*](posts/2026-05-17-does-modern-dynamical-systems-theory-act-a2f5/); that analysis is prior work I do not attempt to reproduce here.

What apparatus did Boltzmann's statistical mechanics imply? The theory was a model of the behavior of enormous numbers of small, discrete particles. To demonstrate it directly, one would in principle want to observe molecular motion-to watch molecules move and collide and show that their collective behavior matches the thermodynamic regularities the theory predicts. Nothing in Boltzmann's era could approach this. Molecules of a gas at room temperature are roughly $10^{-10}$ meters in diameter, moving at hundreds of meters per second, and present at densities of order $10^{19}$ per cubic centimeter. The implied apparatus was, and remains, effectively unattainable in the literal form the derivations model.

What Jean Perrin did in 1908 was different. He observed Brownian motion-the jittery displacement of microscopic mastic grains suspended in fluid-and showed that this behavior followed mathematically from Einstein's 1905 theoretical derivation, which itself assumed that the fluid consisted of molecules with well-defined statistical properties. By tracking particle displacements under the microscope and comparing them to Einstein's predictions, Perrin extracted a value for Avogadro's number: $N_A \approx 6.4 \times 10^{23}$. He then measured the same quantity by independent routes-sedimentation equilibrium, diffusion rates, critical opalescence-and found consistent values converging around $6 \times 10^{23}$.

This was not the apparatus Boltzmann implied. What makes it equivalent rather than merely adjacent?

The criterion I propose is inferential. An apparatus $A_2$ is equivalent to an implied apparatus $A_1$ when three conditions hold: both discriminate between the hypothesis and its negation; the output of $A_2$ is interpretable without presupposing the full claim under test; and there exists a computable inferential bridge from $A_2$'s output to the same conclusion $A_1$ would have produced. The second condition requires care. Einstein's 1905 derivation of the Brownian motion formula does presuppose the existence of discrete molecules-it is not derivable from pure continuum mechanics. What it does not presuppose is the full Boltzmann claim: that thermodynamic laws *reduce entirely* to statistical mechanics of such molecules. The derivation is a conditional: if fluids consist of discrete particles with these statistical properties, then particle displacement should follow this distribution. The apparatus tests the conditional from its consequent; what it establishes (the existence and properties of discrete molecules) is a necessary antecedent of the full claim, not the claim entire. The second condition is satisfied when the apparatus output is interpretable as a test of that antecedent without circularity.

The criterion has discriminating power. Robert Brown's 1827 observations of the same jittery motion of pollen grains in fluid were not equivalent apparatus for molecular theory, despite involving the same physical process. The inferential bridge was not yet available: no derivation existed connecting particle displacement statistics to molecular parameters. Brown's observations were an unexplained phenomenon, not an inferentially bridged measurement. The bridge required eighty years of theoretical development between Brown and Einstein. An apparatus can be physically proximate to the correct measurement but inferentially disconnected from it; 1827 Brownian motion fails the third condition and is correctly classified as not equivalent to the implied molecular apparatus.

Perrin's multi-method convergence was precisely the structure that rendered the anti-atomic position untenable. The reasoning is abductive in the sense that [*The Licensing of Abduction*](posts/2026-05-30-the-licensing-of-abduction-when-observat-a03f/) formalizes: the hypothesis is licensed when it makes the observation highly probable while alternatives make it highly improbable, and this probability gap is robust to perturbation of nuisance parameters. Convergent values of $N_A$ across sedimentation, diffusion, and critical opalescence were astronomically unlikely without the common cause that molecular theory provided. Mary Jo Nye's 1972 reconstruction shows how carefully Perrin understood what he was doing-and why Ostwald, a sophisticated thinker who had resisted for decades, eventually conceded.

Ian Hacking's distinction in *Representing and Intervening* between theories that describe entities and experiments that manipulate them casts this transition precisely. An idea whose implied apparatus is constructible names something we can eventually intervene with-detect, perturb, compare to theoretical prediction-rather than merely represent in a model. Boltzmann's molecules were, for most of his career, represented but not intervened with. Perrin's apparatus crossed that threshold: mastic grains are physical objects whose displacement could be measured against a derivation that assumed molecular structure. The constructibility horizon is, in Hacking's terms, where representation becomes intervention. For Boltzmann's statistical mechanics, what required intervention was not gas molecules themselves-those remain inaccessible in the form the derivations model-but any physical system whose behavior could be derived from molecular assumptions and independently measured.

The Perrin case extends the account to a fourth type: equivalent apparatus. An idea can return not only when its exact implied apparatus materializes but when an alternative apparatus that meets the inferential equivalence condition becomes available. This extension creates a boundary problem, examined further in the Limits section.

## What the Typology Predicts-and Does Not

The four cases suggest a typology of implied apparatus: instrumental (a quantity is physically real and resolution is the barrier), substrate-based (a mechanism exists at a physical scale not yet brought into observation), conceptual (the finding requires a theoretical framework to become assimilable), and, cutting across all three, potentially equivalent (an alternative route meets the same inferential need when the direct route is blocked).

Each type carries different constructibility conditions, and the variation is not accidental. For instrumental apparatus, the condition is the physical reality of the measurable quantity: if the quantity exists, only contingent technological limits block detection. For substrate-based apparatus, the condition requires additionally that the relevant physical scale be accessible to some achievable instrumentation-not just that resolution can be improved, but that a new observational domain can be opened. For conceptual apparatus, the condition is weakest: that a real mechanistic story exists to be found and that the hypothesis's placeholder constrains its form specifically enough to make the search non-trivial. This ordering has a consequence: the conceptual category risks over-inclusion, which is why the Semmelweis section above attempts to tighten its criterion by requiring that the placeholder impose a determinate shape on the sought theory.

Constructibility is also not binary. Bessel succeeded with 61 Cygni partly because its parallax ($\approx 0.31$ arcseconds) sat near the edge of what 1830s heliometer optics could reach; a star with parallax of $0.03$ arcseconds would have required interferometry, in a different physical regime. The question is not whether an apparatus can in principle exist-for real physical quantities, the answer is technically always yes-but how far the required physical regime sits from current technique and whether the path there requires continuous improvement or qualitatively new physics. Boltzmann's literal molecular apparatus is not merely technologically distant; it requires a physical regime (simultaneous observation of individual gas molecules at thermal velocities and densities of order $10^{19}$ per cubic centimeter) that has no foreseeable access route.

The four cases also have different structural relationships to the typology, and these asymmetries deserve naming rather than leaving implicit. For Aristarchus, the implied apparatus was named by both defenders and critics before any measurement was attempted: both parties agreed on stellar parallax as the decisive criterion. For Semmelweis, the hypothesis gestured at a mechanistic placeholder without specifying its physical scale, but the placeholder constrained the form of the sought theory. For Mendel, the implied apparatus is assigned by the structural reading of the mathematical regularities-by what those regularities require to be true-not by anything Mendel stated about what would vindicate his findings. For Boltzmann, the literally-implied apparatus is non-constructible, and what does the argumentative work is equivalent apparatus. None of these asymmetries undermines the typology, but they show that it accommodates different structural relationships between an author's stated view and the apparatus the account assigns to their idea.

The implied-apparatus concept occupies territory adjacent to what Lakatos called the "positive heuristic" of a research programme-the specification of what future investigation the programme predicts will be fruitful. An implied apparatus is roughly the part of an idea's positive heuristic that names its decisive test: not the ongoing belt of auxiliary hypotheses, but the terminal condition that would settle the question. The account here differs from Lakatos's in applying to dormant rather than active programmes-to ideas that have dropped out of circulation-and in focusing on apparatus type rather than programme structure as such.

What the typology predicts is that an idea will return to scientific debate when the appropriate type of apparatus becomes constructible. What it does not predict is vindication. Phlogiston theory implied an instrumental apparatus: if burning releases phlogiston, burned substances should weigh less. Lavoisier built the apparatus-precision balance measurements-and found the opposite. The luminiferous ether implied an instrumental apparatus: an ether wind should be detectable via interference patterns in light traveling in different directions. Michelson and Morley built the apparatus in 1887; the null result refuted the ether.

Both phlogiston and ether had constructible implied apparatus. Both returned to scientific debate, exactly as the account predicts. Both were then resolved against the hypothesis. The account predicts return, not vindication. Ideas that return and are vindicated happen to have been correct; ideas that return and are refuted had constructible apparatus too. The retrospective label "early-correct" selects from the set of ideas that returned with favorable results; the selection criterion is apparatus constructibility, not correctness per se.

This distinction matters. The sociological account of science has been read, sometimes unfairly, as suggesting that vindication is socially constructed-that the difference between vindicated and refuted ideas is a matter of networks and authority rather than physical fact. The account here makes no such claim. Apparatus constructibility predicts which ideas get a fair hearing; what the apparatus then shows depends on whether the hypothesis was correct, which is what the measurement determines.

The typology has forward-looking use. An idea currently in the blind set of contemporary measurement is most likely to resurface when its implied apparatus is most clearly constructible. Two contemporary cases illustrate the range. Detection of biosignatures in exoplanet atmospheres is an instrumental case with a visible constructibility horizon: the relevant quantities (molecular spectral signatures of life-associated chemistry) are physically real, the instrumentation exists and is improving, and next-generation space observatories are bringing the necessary sensitivity within reach. Whether panspermia-the claim that Earth was seeded by life-bearing material from elsewhere-is correct is a different question; that the question will receive a fair hearing is predicted by the apparatus type. Quantum gravity occupies the other end: the theory's literal implied apparatus (measurements at the Planck scale, $E_{Planck} \approx 1.2 \times 10^{19}$ GeV, some sixteen orders of magnitude beyond current accelerators) appears genuinely non-constructible in the literal sense. Proposed equivalent apparatuses-gravitational-wave observations, tabletop quantum-gravity analogues, black-hole information loss experiments-remain contested on whether they satisfy the inferential equivalence conditions. The typology does not resolve this; it names what to look for: a measurement that discriminates competing quantum-gravitational hypotheses from their negations, with an output interpretable without presupposing the full theory, and a computable bridge from that output to the same conclusions the Planck-scale apparatus would have produced.

## The Blind Set and Its Diachronic Complement

[*What the Apparatus Refuses to See*](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) formalizes the blind set $B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$: the set of parameter values that a measurement procedure $M$ cannot distinguish from $\theta_0$ given an alternative class $\mathcal{A}$. The blind set is a synchronic object-a snapshot of the epistemic situation at a given moment, with given procedures, facing a given question. That piece asks: what can this procedure not see?

My question is different: what features of the idea predict whether the blind set will eventually shrink? The blind set shrinks when a new procedure enters the alternative class-when $\mathcal{A}$ expands to include an approach that can distinguish what was previously indistinguishable. Aristarchus's heliocentrism was in the blind set of naked-eye stellar observation: stellar parallax was physically real but procedurally invisible. When Bessel's heliometer-based procedure became available, the alternative class $\mathcal{A}$ expanded to include parallax measurement, and the blind set shrank. Mendel's factors were in the blind set of pre-cytological observation. When light microscopy at the cellular scale became possible, $\mathcal{A}$ expanded.

The implied-apparatus account is, in this framing, the diachronic complement to the synchronic blind-set account. The blind-set formalism asks: given this procedure and this alternative class, what cannot be distinguished now? The implied-apparatus account asks: given that an idea is currently in the blind set, what structural features of the idea predict that someone will eventually build a procedure capable of resolving it? The constructibility conditions that predict whether $\mathcal{A}$ expands differ by apparatus type: instrumental expansion requires resolution improvement within an existing physical regime, substrate-based expansion requires opening a new observational domain, and conceptual expansion requires theoretical imagination applied to a domain where a real mechanism exists. The constructibility horizon is what the blind-set account, taken alone, cannot see about its own future.

## The Sociological Account's Residual Purchase

The account developed here does not displace the sociological explanations of timing. The implied apparatus being constructible in principle does not mean it will be constructed promptly, or by the right people, or in communities positioned to recognize what it shows.

Aristarchus waited not primarily because the technology took eighteen centuries, but because the Copernican revolution had to happen first-establishing the theoretical framework within which parallax measurement made sense as a project worth undertaking. Semmelweis's statistical finding was largely ignored during his lifetime even as germ theory was developing in the 1850s and 1860s; the uptake of antiseptic practice into clinical medicine was mediated by networks of authority and credibility that the sociology of science has mapped carefully. The timing of Mendel's rediscovery in 1900 coincided with the availability of the chromosomal substrate, but which of the three independent rediscoverers gained credit, and how rapidly the Mendelian school displaced biometric approaches, were sociologically determined.

The account's contribution is not to replace the sociological story but to identify a criterion the sociological story presupposes without naming. The sociological story explains timing and distribution of vindication within the set of ideas that eventually returned to debate. The implied-apparatus account attempts to characterize the set itself: which ideas were eligible for return.

## Limits

Four cases is thin. The typology-instrumental, substrate-based, conceptual, and equivalent-is provisional; it may not be exhaustive, and some ideas may straddle types. I cannot demonstrate that the criterion discriminates: it is possible that every correct idea implies some constructible apparatus, making the account trivially true and unhelpful. I did not find a clear case of a correct early idea whose implied apparatus was genuinely non-constructible, which is either a genuine result (the typology identifies something real) or a selection effect (ideas we retrospectively recognize as correct are precisely those that returned, and the ones whose apparatus was non-constructible are invisible to us because they never returned and were never recognized as correct). This is the most serious limit of the account.

The most direct route toward testing it would be a systematic survey of ideas that attracted serious early debate and then ceased to circulate-not because they were refuted by their implied apparatus, but because they disappeared without return. For such ideas, one could ask whether their implied apparatus can be characterized as non-constructible. The difficulty is that the same filtering mechanism operates on any such survey: ideas that attracted initial debate but never returned tend to be ideas that appeared wrong on other grounds, and the retrospective judgment that they were "correct" depends on eventual vindication, which requires the apparatus. The negative cases are structurally inaccessible, not merely unfound. A candidate that approaches the counterfactual: the plurality-of-inhabited-worlds hypotheses, from Bruno through to early twentieth-century extraterrestrial-life speculation. Some versions implied apparatus-detection of life or biosignatures-that is now becoming constructible; others implied apparatus (direct observational contact) that was not. Whether those claims were correct remains unknown, but the constructibility question is tractable, and the typology makes differential predictions about which versions will receive a fair hearing.

Three specific absences from the four cases are worth naming. Wegener's continental drift (1912) is the textbook dismissed-correct idea, and its omission requires explanation. The primary evidence Wegener cited-continental geometric fit, matching fossil assemblages, paleoclimatic correlations-was available in 1912 and insufficient to compel acceptance. The vindicating apparatus-paleomagnetism and seafloor spreading, developed in the 1950s and 1960s-was not what Wegener's 1912 paper implied; it emerged from investigations aimed at different questions. This creates a genuine tension for the typology: does "implied apparatus" refer to the evidence an author invoked, or the evidence that eventually settled the question? For Aristarchus, these converge; for Wegener, they diverge. Wegener's case is not a counterexample but a complication the typology in its current form does not handle cleanly. Barbara McClintock's transposable elements-dismissed from the 1940s until molecular cytogenetics confirmed them in the 1970s-are the clearest biological case for extension: the implied apparatus was visualization at the molecular scale, the substrate was real but below the resolving power of the cytological methods available to McClintock. Darwin's pangenesis hypothesis represents the other direction: its "gemmules" implied properties-somatic origin, circulation through the blood, selective incorporation into gametes-that biology did not subsequently find. A hypothesis specific enough to be disconfirmed is not in the same position as one whose placeholder was too vague to direct inquiry. Both McClintock and pangenesis are extension territory for the typology rather than cases I can treat adequately here.

The equivalent-apparatus extension creates a boundary problem. If "equivalent apparatus" is defined too broadly, any eventual vindication counts as apparatus-materialization, and the account becomes circular. The three conditions developed in the Boltzmann section-binary discrimination, interpretability without presupposing the full claim under test, and a computable inferential bridge-are the attempt to close this problem. The Brown 1827 case illustrates the criterion excluding a candidate: those observations fail the third condition because no computable bridge connected displacement statistics to molecular parameters before Einstein's 1905 derivation. The Perrin case remains the clearest positive instance; other cases will require case-by-case argument rather than mechanical application.

What observable would falsify the implied-apparatus criterion? The most direct falsifier would be a class of dismissed-but-correct ideas whose implied apparatus is non-constructible by some explicit standard, that nonetheless returns to active scientific debate. The structural inaccessibility of negative cases makes this test difficult to execute. A weaker but more tractable version: identify ideas currently in the blind set, classify their apparatus types, and track whether return to active debate correlates with constructibility as the typology predicts. The essay's ambitions are hypothesis-generating, not conclusive. A more satisfying test requires a systematic survey of premonitory-science cases across multiple disciplines, with explicit operational definitions of "implied apparatus" and "constructible." That is a project for historians of science with archival access, not for an essay working from secondary sources.

## Conclusion

The selection problem in premonitory science has a structural answer, at least in the four cases examined here. Each early-correct idea implied an apparatus-instrumental, substrate-based, or conceptual-and each apparatus was constructible from physical substrates or theoretical developments that were, in principle, available. When the apparatus eventually materialized, the question was re-opened. What the apparatus then showed depended on whether the hypothesis was correct.

This account does not displace the sociology of scientific dismissal; it proposes a criterion that the sociology has mostly left unaddressed. Kuhn explained why correct ideas fail to gain acceptance in their era. The implied-apparatus account proposes a structural feature that partially predicts which failed ideas carry within them the seeds of their own eventual rehabilitation: the ideas whose implied apparatus belongs to a constructible type.

The criterion is modest. It cannot predict vindication from idea-structure alone-that depends on whether the idea was right, which is what the materialized apparatus determines. But predicting eligibility for return is not nothing. The ideas currently in the blind sets of contemporary measurement are not all equally likely to resurface: those whose implied apparatus is most clearly constructible are the ones most likely to be heard again. Whether that hearing vindicates or refutes them is, properly, the measurement's business, not the philosopher's.

## Questions this leaves open

- **What is the role of apparatus-transfer - apparatus developed for one purpose and applied to another - in the constructibility of implied apparatus?.** The essay treats the materialization of implied apparatus as a process of constructing what the idea specifically implies. But the historical record suggests a different pattern is at least as common: apparatus developed for entirely unrelated purposes turns out to resolve a dormant question. Bessel's heliometer was not invented to measure stellar parallax; it was an instrument designed for measuring the angular diameter of the Sun, adapted for Fraunhofer's purposes of double-star measurement, and then applied by Bessel to the parallax problem. The staining techniques that made chromosomes visible in the 1870s–1880s were developed for bacteriological purposes, not cytological ones. Perrin's microscopy drew on techniques from colloid chemistry. This pattern raises a question the essay does not address: how often does an idea's implied apparatus actually get built by people who are trying to vindicate that specific idea, versus how often does apparatus built for other purposes get recognized as applicable after the fact? If the latter pattern dominates, the sociological account of apparatus construction and the epistemological account of what the idea implied come apart: the apparatus may be "constructible" in the essay's sense without anyone in the relevant research tradition being in a position to construct it, because construction requires recognizing the applicability, which is itself a non-trivial cognitive and institutional achievement. This question points toward the sociology of scientific instruments (Hackingʼs *Representing and Intervening*, cited in the essay, is relevant here) and toward the history of technology transfer across domains. But it also connects to organizational questions about how scientific communities are structured: under what institutional conditions does apparatus-transfer recognition happen promptly versus with long delay? Whether apparatus-transfer deserves a fifth category in the typology, or whether it is better treated as a sociological modifier on the existing categories, is an open question with both empirical and conceptual dimensions.
- **Could the typology be tested prospectively against ideas currently in the blind set?.** The essay's account is descriptive in the sense that the four cases are read after the apparatus has materialized. A stronger test would be prospective: identify currently-dismissed ideas whose implied apparatus is by the typology constructible, register the predictions, and wait. Quantum gravity, the hard problem of consciousness, dark-matter alternatives, room-temperature superconductivity at standard pressures, panspermia, large-scale interpretive frameworks for animal cognition - each has a different implied-apparatus profile, and the typology should generate distinguishable predictions about which are eligible for return on what timescale. This is harder than it sounds. The list above already contains both ideas whose apparatus is plausibly instrumental (a sufficiently sensitive accelerometer; better cryogenics; cleaner astronomical observations) and ideas whose apparatus is conceptual (the hard problem may require a theoretical framework that does not yet exist and whose constructibility we cannot evaluate). The typology's prediction in the latter cases will be of the form "if a theory of type X is in principle discoverable, the idea is eligible for return," which is interesting but unobservable until it happens. A useful prospective test would need to register, for each candidate idea, what apparatus would count as the implied one, what would count as equivalent, and what would constitute a null prediction by the typology (an idea the typology predicts will *not* return).
- **What is the physical-regime structure of "constructibility"?.** The implied-apparatus piece treats constructibility as a binary property of an idea - the apparatus is either in-principle constructible or it is not. But in physics each instrumental regime has a quantitative threshold: the diffraction limit on optical resolution, the Heisenberg bound on simultaneous position-momentum measurement, the energy required to probe a given length scale, the minimum detectable mass at a given timescale. An idea whose implied apparatus sits two orders of magnitude inside the current regime is differently constructible from one that sits one order outside it - and the latter, in turn, is differently constructible from one whose apparatus requires an as-yet-unbuilt regime entirely. A structural account of return would benefit from a map between an idea's implied apparatus and the physical regime that apparatus requires. Bessel's parallax measurement of 61 Cygni at approximately $0.3$ arcseconds sat at the edge of what 1830s refractor optics could resolve - about a factor of three inside the empirical diffraction limit of the apertures of his day, and reachable only because Fraunhofer's heliometer differentials shaved away the dominant systematic. If 61 Cygni's parallax had been $0.003$ arcseconds, no refinement of the same regime would have sufficed: vindication would have had to wait for interferometry. The Boltzmann case has the same structure at the other end - direct observation of molecular motion is "effectively unattainable in the literal form the derivations model" because the implied regime sits behind a barrier that even the equivalent-apparatus route only circumvents abductively. The follow-up: can we develop, for the College's working philosophers and historians of science, an explicit notion of *regime distance* - the gap between an idea's implied apparatus and the empirical reach of the closest available technique, measured in regime-relevant units (orders of magnitude in length, mass, energy, or frequency)? An idea whose regime distance is small at the time of its proposal returns quickly; an idea whose regime distance is large but bounded returns slowly; an idea whose apparatus lies in a regime not yet conceived may never return at all without an equivalent-apparatus bridge. Are there premonitory ideas whose regime distance is just over a threshold that science of their day was approaching from below? Are there ideas whose apparatus lies behind a regime barrier we still have not crossed - and how would we know?
