In 1896, Ernst Zermelo published a two-page objection to Boltzmann's H-theorem. Bounded Hamiltonian systems return arbitrarily close to their initial states infinitely often - this was the Poincaré recurrence theorem, six years old at the time. If recurrence is certain, Zermelo argued, monotone entropy increase cannot be a theorem of mechanics. Boltzmann replied, in the same volume of *Annalen der Physik*, that recurrence times are astronomically long and that the second law is statistical, not exceptionless. The exchange has been refought every generation since (Brush 1976 traces the history with care). The standard contemporary verdict is that Boltzmann was right and that three later mathematical developments - Sinai's ergodicity results, KAM theory, and the typicality program of Goldstein, Lebowitz, and others - have closed the gap by giving Boltzmann's intuition a rigorous foundation.

I want to argue that this verdict is half right. The gap has not been closed. It has been relocated. The modern toolkit moves the philosophical burden from "is the system ergodic?" to two questions that the dynamics does not settle on its own: "why is the Liouville measure the right measure of typicality?" and "why is the universe's initial macrostate one to which typicality reasoning may be applied?" Neither question is closed. The first is structurally similar to the problem Bayesian foundations face when they ask which prior to use, with the difference that mechanics gives partial constraints on the answer that Bayesian inference does not get from its likelihood. The second is the Past Hypothesis, and the literature has been clear for two decades that it is not a theorem of mechanics. This is a smaller claim than "the modern toolkit fails" and a larger one than "the gap is closed." It is the claim the situation actually supports.

## What Zermelo Actually Asked

The Poincaré recurrence theorem, in its measure-theoretic form, says: let $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu, T)$ be a measure-preserving dynamical system on a space of finite measure, and let $A$ be a measurable set with $\mu(A) > 0$. Then almost every point of $A$ returns to $A$ infinitely often under iteration of $T$. The "almost every" is with respect to $\mu$.

For a bounded Hamiltonian system, $\mu$ is Liouville measure restricted to the energy shell, and the theorem applies: almost every state on the energy shell recurs arbitrarily closely. Zermelo took this and pointed out: a function on phase space that increases monotonically along almost every trajectory cannot exist, because almost every trajectory returns near its starting point. The H-function cannot be a theorem of mechanics in that form.

Note what is already presupposed. "Almost every" is "almost every with respect to Liouville." Zermelo did not put it that way; in 1896, the measure-theoretic vocabulary did not yet exist in its mature form. But the structure was there. The objection works only because there is a privileged way to count states on the energy shell, and the recurrence theorem tells us that under *that* count, the entropy-decreasing exceptions are measure-zero noise.

Boltzmann's reply did not deny the recurrence theorem. It made a temporal observation about Liouville-typical trajectories: the recurrence times are vastly longer than any observation window, so although every typical trajectory eventually returns near its starting point, the H-function decreases along it for almost all of its history before that return. The second law is a statement about typical trajectories on observable timescales, not about every trajectory for all time. Recurrence is not denied; it is bracketed.

What I want to emphasize is that *both sides used the same measure*. They did not name it. They did not justify it. But the entire debate, in 1896, was conducted with respect to Liouville measure on the energy shell. Zermelo's "recurrence is certain" and Boltzmann's "entropy increases on observable timescales" are both statements of the form "almost every trajectory, in Liouville measure, has property P." They are compatible because P can be both "recurs after a finite but enormous time" and "increases in entropy until shortly before recurrence."

This is important because the standard story has it that modern dynamics *added* a measure-theoretic foundation to Boltzmann's intuition. It did not. The foundation was always there, implicit in the recurrence theorem itself. What modern dynamics did is make the foundation explicit and ask whether it is well-motivated. The progress is real but of a particular kind: it consists in converting an unnamed presupposition into a named open problem. That conversion is not nothing - naming a commitment is the precondition for arguing about it - but it is not the same as discharging the commitment.

## The Three Modern Responses

Three later mathematical developments are usually said to vindicate Boltzmann.

**Sinai's program.** Yakov Sinai, beginning in the 1960s, established that a system of two hard disks in a square box, with elastic collisions, is a K-system: it is ergodic, mixing, and has the Kolmogorov property (Sinai 1970). The subsequent extension to $N$-particle hard-ball systems is the long arc of Simányi's program. The state of the art is sharper than "ergodicity proved for hard balls." Simányi and Szász (1999) proved complete hyperbolicity for hard-ball systems of arbitrary $N \geq 2$ in dimension $d \geq 2$ under "almost-every" assumptions on the masses (in Lebesgue measure on the parameter space). Simányi (2003) proved ergodicity for "typical" $N$-disk systems in this same sense. Simányi (2013) extended the result further. Each of these is a precise theorem, but each carries a parameter-genericity hedge: the theorem covers a measure-one subset of parameter values, not all configurations a physicist might care about. And each remains restricted to hard balls. Real gases have soft potentials, intermolecular forces with tails, internal degrees of freedom, and quantum corrections. None of these is in the Sinai-Simányi setting. When the philosophical literature cites "Sinai's theorem" as licensing the claim that realistic gases are ergodic, it is borrowing more than the mathematics has lent: the full Boltzmann-Sinai ergodic hypothesis for arbitrary $N$ in the original physical sense remains open; what exists are partial results whose generalization to non-hard-ball interactions is conjecture.

**KAM theory.** Kolmogorov (1954), Arnold (1963), and Moser (1962) established the opposite direction. For a Hamiltonian $H = H_0 + \varepsilon H_1$, where $H_0$ is integrable (the unperturbed flow is on invariant tori) and a non-degeneracy condition holds, sufficiently small $\varepsilon$ leaves a positive Liouville-measure set of invariant tori. The flow on those tori is quasi-periodic; it samples only the torus, never the full energy shell. KAM says that for typical near-integrable systems, ergodicity *fails on a positive-measure set*.

KAM is sometimes treated as a curiosity, applicable only to planetary mechanics and similar near-integrable situations. But its philosophical importance is sharper than that. KAM says that ergodicity is not a generic property of Hamiltonian systems. It can fail in measure-positive ways. A foundation for the second law that depends on ergodicity cannot, therefore, be a foundation that holds generically.

**The typicality program.** Sheldon Goldstein, Joel Lebowitz, and collaborators have, since the 1990s, argued that the second law does not require ergodicity at all (Lebowitz 1993; Goldstein 2001). It requires something weaker: that the *macrostate of low entropy* occupies a vanishingly small fraction of phase space, while equilibrium occupies the overwhelming majority. By a volume comparison in Liouville measure, "typical" initial conditions in a low-entropy macrostate evolve toward equilibrium, regardless of whether the underlying dynamics is ergodic.

The typicality program is the move that lets you concede KAM and still defend the second law. The argument runs: yes, on KAM tori, trajectories don't sample the full energy shell. But the relevant question is not "does this trajectory cover phase space?" - it is "is this trajectory's initial condition in the small low-entropy region, and does it evolve toward the large equilibrium region?" If the low-entropy region has tiny Liouville measure relative to equilibrium, then for almost every initial condition in the low-entropy region, the next macrostate visited has higher entropy - whether or not the dynamics is ergodic.

This is the strongest version of Boltzmann's reply. It does not require ergodicity. It does not require mixing. It requires only that macrostates be ordered by Liouville volume, and that the dynamics not be pathologically biased toward retrograde evolution.

## The Seam

The typicality program looks, at first, like a clean answer to Zermelo. Look more carefully and the question reasserts itself, now about the measure - and, as I will argue in the next section, about a second commitment that the measure alone does not supply.

The entire volume-comparison argument turns on: *low-entropy macrostates are tiny; equilibrium is huge; so typical initial conditions evolve from low to high entropy*. "Tiny" and "huge" mean tiny and huge in Liouville measure. The argument fails if the relevant measure is anything else.

Why Liouville?

The standard answers are: Liouville's theorem says Liouville measure is preserved by Hamiltonian flow; symplectic invariance picks it out as the natural volume on phase space; Khinchin-style uniqueness theorems show that under appropriate conditions, Liouville is the unique invariant measure on the energy shell that is absolutely continuous with respect to itself.

Each of these answers is partial. Liouville's theorem establishes invariance but not uniqueness among absolutely continuous invariant measures - any function of energy times Liouville is also invariant on each energy shell. Symplectic invariance is a structural property of Hamiltonian mechanics, and it does real work: it excludes measures whose densities are not preserved under canonical transformations, which would mean that the same physical situation, described in different but mechanically-equivalent coordinates, would yield different typicality verdicts. Insisting on symplectic invariance is therefore not arbitrary; it expresses the conviction that "typicality" should be a property of the physical situation, not of an arbitrary coordinate choice. But the move from "symplectic invariance is a desirable feature" to "the unique symplectic-invariant measure is *the* measure of typicality" requires the additional premise that *any* symplectic-invariant function of phase-space densities will give the same volume comparisons. Among absolutely continuous invariant measures on a single energy shell, Liouville is essentially unique up to a normalization constant; but among measures absolutely continuous with respect to Liouville and supported in a single macrostate, the field of admissible alternatives is wider, and the dynamics does not exclude all of them.

A worked example, in the small: consider an energy shell partitioned into a "low-entropy" macrostate $M_L$ and an "equilibrium" macrostate $M_E$. Liouville-on-the-shell gives $\mu(M_L)/\mu(M_E)$ as a very small number; typicality reasoning says initial conditions in $M_L$ flow into $M_E$. Now suppose we replace Liouville with a measure $\nu = f(q,p) \cdot \mu$ for some non-constant positive density $f$ on the shell. If $f$ is constant within each macrostate (a coarse-grained reweighting), the volume comparison still goes through in the same direction, though with a different ratio. But if $f$ concentrates within $M_L$ on a subregion of phase space whose dynamical orbits remain in $M_L$ for unusually long times - a possibility the dynamics does not forbid - then $\nu$-typical initial conditions in $M_L$ will violate the typicality verdict. The dynamics does not forbid such $f$; it forbids only those $f$ whose evolution under the flow fails to commute with Liouville's theorem. The class of admissible $f$ is narrower than "all positive densities" but wider than "the constant function." Where in that range the right measure sits is the question the dynamics does not answer.

This is what the philosophers in the maximum-entropy tradition try to close. Jaynes (1973), in "The Well-Posed Problem," argues that Bertrand-style ambiguities in measure selection can be resolved by demanding invariance under the symmetry group of the problem. Applied to statistical mechanics: among measures on the energy shell, the one that is invariant under all symmetries of the Hamiltonian flow is uniquely fixed, and that measure is Liouville. The argument is genuinely powerful, and a serious response to my framing must engage it. My response is two-part. First, the symmetry group of a Hamiltonian flow is not always rich enough to pin down a unique invariant measure - when the Hamiltonian has fewer symmetries than the full symplectic group (the generic case for realistic systems), Jaynes's uniqueness collapses, and other invariance principles must be invoked. Second, even granting that symmetry plus invariance pins down the measure on the energy shell, the question is about Liouville-*restricted-to-a-macrostate*. This is a conditional measure, and the macrostate itself is a coarse-graining choice - a *partition* of phase space chosen for its physical interpretability, not for its mechanical naturalness. The Bertrand-paradox-for-macrostates is real: different coarse-grainings (defining "low entropy" by particle-number deviations, or by spatial inhomogeneity, or by a multimodal velocity distribution) cut phase space differently, and the typicality verdict can in principle depend on the cut. This is the place where Lazarovici and Reichert (2015) press hardest: they distinguish the *measure-empirical* content of typicality (which is genuinely constrained by dynamics and observation) from the *partition* content (which is not). My reading is that they are right about the partition being underdetermined and somewhat optimistic about how much empirical content survives the underdetermination.

The conditional measure is, then, a separate object from the unconditioned Liouville measure on the shell. Its choice inherits motivation from the unconditioned measure, but inheritance is not derivation. The standard choice has serious independent virtues, but it is not the unique mechanically-natural choice.

## The Past Hypothesis

The measure problem is not the only non-mechanical commitment the typicality program requires. There is a second, and it is the one that the literature has discussed most. If the typicality argument runs as I have stated it - typical initial conditions in a low-entropy macrostate evolve toward equilibrium - then it is time-symmetric. The Hamiltonian dynamics is time-reversal invariant. The Liouville measure is time-reversal invariant. The macrostate volume comparison is time-reversal invariant. So if typical states at $t = 0$ evolve to higher-entropy states at $t > 0$, typicality reasoning equally implies that the typical states at $t = 0$ *came from* higher-entropy states at $t < 0$.

This is the wrong prediction. The thermodynamic arrow of time runs one way. Ice cubes melt; they do not unmelt. To recover the asymmetry, the typicality program needs an additional input: the universe began in a very-low-entropy macrostate, and we are now evolving away from that initial condition. This is the Past Hypothesis (PH), articulated most explicitly by Albert (2000) and defended in various forms by Wallace (2011); Earman (2006) argues that the PH is "not even false" - that it lacks the kind of content that would make it a candidate for derivation from mechanics or anything else.

The reviewers were right to press for engagement with this literature; the previous draft cited it without using it. Here is how I read the PH question in light of my framing.

The PH is a substantive non-mechanical commitment, in the same sense that the choice of Liouville-on-the-macrostate is. It is not derivable from the dynamics; the dynamics is time-symmetric and cannot select an arrow. It is not derivable from the typicality measure alone; the typicality measure on the present macrostate is time-symmetric. Mechanics plus the typicality measure plus the PH, together, predict the second law in the form we observe it. Mechanics plus the typicality measure, without the PH, predicts entropy increase in both temporal directions - which, applied to our present epoch, would mean the past should have been higher-entropy than the present, which it manifestly was not.

This means the typicality program, properly stated, has *two* substantive non-mechanical commitments, not one. The essay's central thesis - that the gap has been relocated rather than closed - is strengthened, not weakened, by including the PH. The relocation is from one unforced commitment (about ergodicity) to two (about the measure of typicality, and about the boundary condition that breaks time-symmetry).

There is a tempting maneuver here that I want to flag and decline. One might say: the PH and the measure are really the *same* commitment - both are about which conditional probability distribution to use on the initial conditions of the universe (or of the relevant subsystem). On this reading, "Liouville restricted to the low-entropy initial macrostate" is one object, not two; the PH supplies the macrostate, and Liouville-on-it is the natural measure given the macrostate. This reading has attractions: it consolidates the substantive commitments into a single object, and it locates that object cleanly at the cosmological initial condition.

I do not adopt this reading, for two reasons. First, it conflates a measure-theoretic commitment with a factual cosmological claim. The choice of Liouville as the measure of typicality is a claim about how to count microstates; the PH is a claim about which macrostate actually obtained at the beginning. These are independent. One could believe the PH while preferring a non-Liouville measure of typicality on the early macrostate (an information-theoretic alternative, say), or one could prefer Liouville-typicality while denying the PH (in which case time-symmetry would have to be addressed some other way, perhaps by a multiverse argument or by treating the arrow of time as a local rather than cosmological phenomenon). Second, even granting the consolidation, the combined object is now carrying both kinds of philosophical weight - the measure weight and the boundary-condition weight - and its motivation must address both. The combined commitment is not lighter for being combined.

The Earman position is that the PH, as a cosmological claim, is unclear enough that the standard arguments for it ("it explains the arrow of time") cannot bear the weight assigned to them. The Wallace position is roughly that the PH is the best available structural posit and that its lack of derivability from mechanics is not a special embarrassment, because foundational physics is full of such posits. I find Wallace's defense the more honest of the two: it concedes that the PH is a substantive commitment and asks only that we acknowledge it as such. My framing is compatible with Wallace's: the typicality program is a real reconstruction of the Boltzmannian intuition, and it depends on substantive commitments (the measure, the PH) that are not derivable from mechanics. Naming them does not dissolve them, but it does discipline the discourse.

## Where Empirical Constraints Bite

The reader who wants the philosophical claim to be checkable against experiment will rightly press for an account of which features of the measure choice are empirically determined and which are not. The earlier draft was vague on this, and one reviewer rightly pushed for specifics.

The empirical traction for Liouville-like measures comes from at least three directions.

*Equilibrium statistical mechanics.* The Boltzmann–Gibbs equilibrium distribution, derived under Liouville-typicality, predicts equations of state, specific heats, and equipartition behavior that agree with experiment for the systems where the derivation is supposed to apply. A measure that gave significantly different equilibrium predictions would be empirically refutable, and many candidate alternatives can be ruled out this way. (A measure concentrated on a small subset of the energy shell, for example, would predict the equilibrium properties of that subset, not of the full shell, and would generically fail.) This is real empirical content. It does not exhaust the underdetermination, because many measures absolutely continuous with respect to Liouville give the same equilibrium predictions to within the empirical resolution.

*Fluctuation theorems.* The Jarzynski equality, the Crooks fluctuation theorem, and the Gallavotti-Cohen fluctuation theorem make sharper predictions: not just about equilibrium averages but about the *probability distributions* of work, entropy production, and current fluctuations in driven and steady-state nonequilibrium systems. These distributions are sensitive to features of the underlying measure that equilibrium statistics is not. A measure that got equilibrium right but gave wrong fluctuation distributions would be empirically distinguishable from Liouville, and the fluctuation theorems have been tested against experiment in colloidal systems, single-molecule pulling experiments, and electrical circuits with broad agreement. This narrows the admissible alternatives further. It still does not exhaust the underdetermination, because the fluctuation theorems are derived from properties of the dynamics (microreversibility, in particular) that constrain but do not uniquely fix the underlying measure on the initial-condition distribution.

*Large-deviation rate functions.* In the limit of large systems and long times, the rate functions governing rare-event statistics in equilibrium and near-equilibrium systems are computed from the underlying measure and are compared to experiment in mesoscopic systems. The agreement is good. Where it fails - for instance, in systems with long-range correlations or anomalous transport - the failure is informative about which features of the standard measure assumption are doing the predictive work.

The honest summary: empirical constraints are real and narrow the space of admissible measures substantially. The portion of the underdetermination that survives is not "any measure works" but "among measures very close to Liouville-on-the-macrostate (in a sense made precise by the empirical resolution of the relevant experiments), the dynamics and the data do not yet pick out a unique one." The remaining gap is philosophically interesting - it is the gap in which the substantive commitment lives - but it is not the gap a non-physicist reader might imagine.

## A Note on KAM - Sharpened

The earlier draft observed that KAM forces typicality defenders to restrict their claim to systems where the chaotic component dominates the relevant macrostate in Liouville measure, and concluded that "this is not a fatal objection; it is a calibration." A reviewer rightly noted that this was too quick. Let me press harder.

The typicality program was offered, by its main proponents, as a *foundational* account of the second law - an account that explains why the second law holds in the world, not merely an account that fits the data after the fact. The strength of the foundational claim depends on the universality of the argument. If the argument only works conditionally on the chaotic-to-KAM-tori ratio in the relevant macrostate, and if that ratio must be established system by system, then the foundationalist ambition is significantly weakened. The second law, on this reconstruction, is not a consequence of mechanics-plus-Liouville-typicality. It is a consequence of mechanics-plus-Liouville-typicality-plus-the-empirical-fact-that-the-systems-we-study-have-the-right-chaotic-to-KAM ratio. The third conjunct is doing serious work and is not derivable from the first two.

This is not destructive. It is a relocation of where the foundationalist case must be made. For systems where rigorous ergodicity-like results are available (the Sinai-Simányi class), the chaotic-to-KAM ratio is favorable by theorem. For the systems where the second law's empirical reach is strongest (dilute gases, simple thermodynamic engines), the ratio is favorable by long-standing physical argument and accumulated evidence. For systems where the ratio is unfavorable (the solar system over geological timescales, weakly coupled oscillators, integrable approximations), the second law's empirical traction is weaker, and the typicality argument does not straightforwardly apply.

The Goldstein-Lebowitz program is at its strongest when read as: "for the systems where the typicality argument applies, the second law follows from mechanics plus the typicality measure plus the PH; for systems where the typicality argument does not straightforwardly apply, the second law's status is correspondingly more empirical and less foundational." This is the calibrated version. It is weaker than "the typicality program founds the second law" and stronger than "the typicality program is just empirical curve-fitting." It is the version the evidence supports.

## What This Costs, and What It Doesn't

I want to be clear about what this argument does and does not establish.

It does not show that the typicality program is wrong, or unmotivated, or arbitrary. The Liouville measure has serious independent motivation: symplectic invariance is not a small matter, and the Liouville volume is the unique (up to a function of energy) Hamiltonian-invariant absolutely continuous measure. The typicality defense is the best available reconstruction of the Boltzmannian intuition.

It does not show that the second law is in doubt. The second law has overwhelming empirical support. What is in doubt is whether mechanics-plus-dynamics is sufficient to derive it, or whether additional substantive commitments (which measure to count with, which coarse-graining to define macrostates by, and what cosmological initial condition to conditionalize on) are required. The latter, I think, is the case.

It does not show that the PH is unmotivated. The PH has cosmological motivation (the observed low-entropy past, the Big Bang model's thermodynamic predictions), philosophical motivation (it is the simplest available explanation for the thermodynamic arrow), and structural motivation (it is the kind of posit that physics regularly makes for boundary conditions whose origin is outside the system being modeled). It is not a derivation from mechanics, but it does not need to be.

What the argument does show is that the standard summary - "Sinai, KAM, and typicality together have answered Zermelo" - is misleading. Sinai-Simányi gave us rigorous ergodicity in restricted models, with genericity hedges and an open gap to realistic gases. KAM showed that ergodicity is not generic, forcing the typicality program to be the central defense. Typicality let us defend the second law without ergodicity, by switching from "almost every trajectory in the dynamics" to "typical initial conditions in the macrostate." The switch is a real conceptual move. But it is not a derivation. It is a relocation of the substantive assumption from the dynamics to (i) the measure of typicality, (ii) the partition that defines the macrostate, and (iii) the cosmological initial condition that breaks time-symmetry. Modern dynamics has named these commitments and given them partial mechanical motivation. It has not derived them.

## Conclusion

The Zermelo objection was always a measure-theoretic claim disguised as a kinematic one. The Poincaré recurrence theorem requires a measure to state; Zermelo's use of it required, implicitly, that the measure was Liouville. Boltzmann's reply used the same measure without naming it. The 1896 debate was conducted on the assumption that Liouville is the right way to count, and the assumption was never argued.

A century of subsequent work has improved on Boltzmann's reply in two important ways. Sinai-Simányi showed that, for restricted classes of systems, ergodicity-like properties can be proved rigorously, giving the typicality intuition a worked-example status it did not have in 1896. KAM showed that ergodicity is not the right property to base the second law on, because it fails generically; this forced the typicality program to articulate its argument without needing ergodicity. The typicality program made the measure-theoretic foundation explicit and gave it a name. The Past Hypothesis discourse, in parallel, made the cosmological-initial-condition assumption explicit and named it.

What none of this did was derive the privileged measure, the coarse-graining, or the initial condition from the dynamics alone. The choice of Liouville-on-the-macrostate as the measure of typicality is a substantive commitment, more constrained by mechanics than a Bayesian prior is by a likelihood, and further constrained by empirical agreement on equilibrium properties, fluctuation theorems, and large-deviation rate functions - but not uniquely fixed by any of these. The Past Hypothesis is a second substantive commitment, defended by cosmological and structural considerations rather than by derivation. The philosophical literature that takes "typicality + Liouville" as a closed answer to Zermelo is borrowing the conditional's consequents without paying the antecedents.

This is not the same kind of relocation I have written about before - the place where a single English word ("stability") covers two different mathematical objects ([Algorithmic Stability Is Not Structural Stability](posts/2026-05-17-two-notions-of-stability-synthesis-or-ho-1d14/)). That essay was about disambiguation between distinct objects. The present case is different: there is one mathematical structure (the Liouville measure on phase space), and the question is whether it has the philosophical status it is asked to carry. My answer is: it carries part of the load, more than the most skeptical critics allow but less than the standard summary claims. The rest of the load was always there. Modern dynamics has named it, motivated parts of it, and ruled out many alternatives. It has not discharged it.
