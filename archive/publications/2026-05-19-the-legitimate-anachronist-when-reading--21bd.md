There is a short distance between illumination and imposture in the reading of past thinkers, and Quentin Skinner spent fifty years trying to map it. His 1969 essay "Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas" is one of the most cited papers in the philosophy of history, and it is cited as often by those who think he went too far as by those who think he was exactly right. Critics like Dominick LaCapra have pressed on what the framework does to literary and philosophical texts that exceed the genre of political argument; J.G.A. Pocock, who built the Cambridge School alongside Skinner, has qualified his own contextualism in ways that acknowledge the gap between authorial intent and readerly meaning. Both parties tend to agree that Skinner identified something real. The disagreement is about what that something licenses in practice — whether it forbids the kind of reading that has produced some of the most illuminating intellectual history of the last century, or whether there is a form of anachronism that evades his critique because it is doing something different from what he attacked. This essay is addressed to a reader who cares about interpretive methodology but need not already know Skinner's specific framework: the question is worth asking on its own terms.

That is the question I want to work through here. My claim is that there is a legitimate anachronism, and that it can be distinguished from the distorting kind by three conditions that are not arbitrary but follow from a careful reading of what Skinner's objection actually requires. I am not certain these conditions are jointly sufficient. I am reasonably confident they are jointly necessary — though the third case I examine will put pressure on condition one in ways that require explicit refinement. And in the borderline case — Judith Butler's reading of Sophocles' *Antigone* through the lens of gender theory — they strain in instructive ways.

## What Skinner's Objection Actually Says

The usual gloss on Skinner is that he prohibits anachronism — that he demands we read past thinkers purely in their own terms. This is an imprecise rendering, and the imprecision matters. What Skinner actually argues is about the nature of textual meaning, not about the date of one's conceptual vocabulary.

His framework draws on speech-act theory: to explain a text's meaning is to explain what its author was *doing* — what speech act they were performing, within a context of conventions that made certain utterances intelligible as certain kinds of act. To explain a logical treatise is to identify it as an act of demonstration within a tradition of formal argument. To explain a political pamphlet is to identify it as an act of intervention in a specific public debate. The meaning is not a free-floating content that the words contain; it is what the author was performing with those words, given the resources available to them.

This is the hinge. What a thinker can be doing is constrained by what they have the conceptual equipment to do. Locke could not have been "anticipating natural rights" in a modern sense because performing that speech act — making that specific claim, within that specific theoretical framework — required conceptual resources that did not exist for him and could not have been deployed even if he had wanted to deploy them. The anachronist who discovers this "anticipation" in Locke has found a verbal parallel and mistaken it for a conceptual identity. They have described an action Locke could not have been performing. (The specific case of Locke is contested: James Tully's reading, Richard Tuck's survey of natural rights traditions, and John Dunn's interpretation each locate more of modern natural rights theory in Locke than Skinner's strictest readings allow. The point does not require settling that dispute. Skinner's mechanism holds independently: what matters is that the test for legitimate attribution is conceptual resource availability, not verbal resemblance, and that test applies whether or not one adopts Skinner's specific reading of Locke.)

This is precise and powerful. It demolishes a whole genre of intellectual history: the genre that finds the origins of contemporary doctrines in thinkers who had, at best, a superficial verbal resemblance to those doctrines and, at worst, worked within a framework that would have made the contemporary doctrine incoherent. It is not a complaint about careless reading. It is a complaint about a kind of care applied to the wrong object.

But notice what the objection targets: it targets *attribution*. It says you cannot attribute to a past thinker the performance of an act they lacked the resources to perform. What it does not obviously target is a different kind of claim — the claim that a practice or argument has a structural feature that a later concept correctly identifies, independent of whether the practitioner had that concept, intended to instantiate it, or could have articulated it.

This distinction is the opening that Pierre Hadot exploits, productively, in *Philosophy as a Way of Life*.

## Hadot and the Structural Reading

Pierre Hadot's central argument about ancient philosophy is that we have been misreading it as doctrine when we should be reading it as practice. The ancient philosophical schools, he argues, were not primarily in the business of producing correct theories about the universe, though they did produce theories. They were primarily in the business of producing practitioners of a certain kind — people who had transformed their relationship to their own mental life through disciplined exercise. The *Meditations* of Marcus Aurelius, read as a philosophical treatise, is puzzling: it is repetitive, it does not advance arguments, it circles back constantly to the same Stoic commitments without elaborating them. Read as a spiritual exercise — a practice of daily self-correction, performed on paper, aimed at training the practitioner's attention and response — it is entirely coherent.

The concept "spiritual exercise" is Hadot's, not Marcus's. Marcus does not use it. The ancients did not have it as a recognized category. Hadot knows this; he says it explicitly. His claim is not that Marcus was consciously doing spiritual exercises in the modern sense. His claim is that the practice Marcus was engaged in has structural features — it is iterative, self-addressed, aimed at transformation of the practitioner's response-dispositions, resistant to summary as propositional doctrine — that the concept "spiritual exercise" accurately identifies.

Before developing the conditions that make this reading legitimate, I should say what I mean by "structural feature" throughout. A structural feature is a property of the practice or argument identifiable from its form, organization, or functional role — as opposed to a property derivable only from the practitioner's own propositional commitments. The two are sometimes difficult to separate, but the *Meditations* illustrates the separation clearly. The book's iterative character — Marcus returns to the same commitments without advancing or refining them into argument — is apparent before any conceptual frame is applied: you notice it from the table of contents, from the repetition of themes across books, from the absence of anything like a developing dialectic. That the text is self-addressed, that Marcus writes to "you" rather than to an interlocutor or reader, is similarly visible from the surface. These features are not the spiritual-exercise concept; they are what the concept names. The concept does not have to be available to see the features.

A related epistemological limit is worth acknowledging here. Hadot did not see the structural features of the *Meditations* and then invent "spiritual exercise" to name what he had already seen; he likely saw the structure partly *because* the concept was available to him. This is a real constraint on the "independently identifiable" claim. I do not mean that a reader fully free of the concept could reach the same description — no such reader exists. I mean that the structural description does not require the concept as a logical primitive: it can be supported by other means, arrived at through other routes. This is a weaker and more honest claim than "any reader unaware of spiritual exercises would see the same structure," and it is the claim condition one actually needs.

Here Skinner's objection does not engage. Hadot is not attributing to Marcus a speech act he lacked the resources to perform. He is not saying Marcus was claiming something he could not have claimed. He is saying the form of the *Meditations* — independently identifiable, derivable from the text before the concept is introduced — has properties that a later concept names accurately. The reading is structural rather than attributive. It asks: what is this practice *doing*, at a level of description that the concept "spiritual exercise" captures? And it answers: that level of description is not the practitioner's own self-description but a structural account that a later observer can give honestly.

What makes this legitimate? Three conditions, I will argue.

## Three Conditions

**Condition one: the later concept must pick out a genuine structural feature of the earlier practice or argument — not a verbal resemblance but a structural identity.**

Hadot's concept "spiritual exercise" identifies a structural feature: the practice of repeated, self-addressed attention-training performed in writing. This feature is independently identifiable (in the qualified sense above) before the concept is introduced. You can see it from the *Meditations*' form alone — from its resistance to summary, its iterative character, its address to "you" rather than to a reader. The concept accurately names something already there. This rules out the case where a later reader finds a word that resembles a later term — finds "soul" and reads psychoanalytic interiority, finds "nature" and reads ecological systems theory — without identifying any structural property that the two share.

Condition one has a limitation the Butler case will make visible: the structural features picked out must not be *selected* from among many competing features purely by the later framework, or if they are, the selection must be explicitly acknowledged as a choice rather than presented as comprehensive. There is a difference between identifying the structural feature a concept names (explicit, potentially legitimate) and using a framework to determine which of several equally present structural features count as primary (implicit, potentially distorting). This distinction will need to do real work in the Butler section.

**Condition two: the reading must be capable of surprise by the original text — it must have something at stake when it encounters specific passages, rather than being a framework that can absorb any encounter without resistance.**

I originally stated this as "the reading must generate falsifiable claims." I now think "falsifiable" reaches for the right thing imprecisely. What the condition requires is that the reading engage with specific textual evidence, make specific claims about it, and be capable of frustration. The practical test is simple: what textual evidence would force you to revise or abandon the central claim? If no answer is available — if every possible passage can be absorbed as confirmation of the framework — condition two has not been met. Hadot's reading passes this test: if someone showed that Marcus did intend the *Meditations* as a doctrinal treatise, or that the form is in fact propositional throughout, Hadot's reading would need to change.

**Condition three: the reading must not require attributing the later framework's metaphysical or ideological presuppositions to the earlier thinker.**

Hadot is not claiming Marcus believed in the modern therapeutic model of psychological self-improvement, or that he shared a contemporary conception of the self as a site of transformation. He is claiming the *practice* had these structural features regardless of Marcus's own theoretical commitments. The later concept names the structure; it does not require the earlier thinker to have endorsed the later theory that makes sense of that concept within its original home.

Condition three does independent work that conditions one and two do not fully cover. A reading can correctly identify structural features (passing condition one) and engage with specific textual evidence in ways that could be frustrated (passing condition two) while still projecting the later framework's presuppositions onto the earlier thinker — treating those features not merely as structural facts the thinker instantiated, but as evidence of the thinker's metaphysical commitments. The Butler case will demonstrate this independence precisely.

## Hegel and the Colonizing Reading

Hegel's reading of the pre-Socratics in the *Lectures on the History of Philosophy* is, by contrast, a relatively clear case of distortion. The standard criticism is that Hegel projects his own system backward and turns the ancients into proto-Hegelians working out the earliest, most abstract moments of Spirit's self-recognition. This is right, but I want to be precise about which condition fails.

Consider the reading of Thales. For Hegel, Thales' claim that "all is water" is the first positing of the Absolute as simple, undifferentiated unity — a "beginning of philosophy" in the sense of Spirit's first attempt to think itself through material self-identity. The structural feature this identifies is: the move from mythological explanation to a single material principle. This is independently identifiable and is not wrong as a description of what Thales does. So far, condition one is not obviously failed.

The failure is at condition two. Hegel's developmental schema — Spirit's dialectical self-recognition through successive stages — is constitutively unfalsifiable as a reading of the pre-Socratics. Whatever Anaximander says, it can be framed as Spirit's next moment, a slightly less inadequate self-comprehension. Whatever Heraclitus says can be framed as the moment where Spirit recognizes contradiction as part of its own nature. The test is explicit: what textual evidence would force a revision of Thales' place in Spirit's development? Within the schema, nothing qualifies — any possible finding becomes another confirmatory moment. There is no surprise available. When a reading cannot be frustrated by the text, it is not really a reading of the text. It is a reading of the reader's own framework, illustrated with passages.

The failure at condition three is logically independent, not merely a consequence of condition two. Hegel does not merely use the pre-Socratics to illustrate a schema he would maintain regardless of what they said. He attributes to them a kind of Hegelian agency: on his reading, Thales is not simply doing something that we can describe in Hegelian terms — he is a moment in Spirit's actual self-development. This requires attributing to Thales not just a structural feature but a metaphysical role within a framework Thales could not have known he was participating in. Condition two's failure and condition three's failure are both present, and they are separable: one could imagine a reading that absorbs any text as a developmental stage without attributing to the thinkers an agential role in that development. Each condition names a distinct failure mode; their co-occurrence in this case does not collapse them.

## Butler's Antigone: The Productive Borderline

Judith Butler's *Antigone's Claim* is described in the proposal for this piece as a "borderline case," and that description survives the close reading. I came to the case expecting condition three to be the principal strain — expecting Butler's framework's presuppositions about discourse and subject-formation to be the place where legitimate structural reading gave way to projection. On close reading, the strain falls primarily at condition one, at the level of which structural features are selected and on what grounds.

Butler is reading Sophocles' *Antigone* through Hegel's reading of it in the *Phenomenology of Spirit*, and simultaneously reading both through contemporary theories of kinship, gender, and political intelligibility. She is doing multiple anachronisms in sequence. At each layer, the three conditions apply differently.

At the level of Sophocles' text, Butler's reading passes conditions one and two more clearly than might be expected. The structural feature she identifies — Antigone's position as simultaneously inside and outside the political order, making a claim that the city cannot recognize as either legitimate or simply criminal — is independently identifiable before Butler's theoretical frame is introduced. It is there in Sophocles: Creon cannot simply execute Antigone as a rebel, because her appeal to divine law has a force the city's law cannot absorb. The claim exceeds the available categories on both sides. This is structural, not merely an imposition, and Butler's reading can be surprised: she finds genuine resistance in Antigone's own language, particularly in her appeals to eternal law and to the specific kinship obligations of a sister to a brother. These resistances do not fit neatly into Butler's framework, and she acknowledges them as complications rather than dismissing them.

Before assessing Butler's condition-three standing, it is worth analyzing the most directly comparable case: Hegel's own reading of *Antigone* in the *Phenomenology of Spirit*. This is the cleanest controlled comparison available — two anachronistic readings of the same text, separated by their analytical frameworks, and both available for examination. Hegel reads *Antigone* as the collision between two incomplete forms of ethical life: Antigone represents divine law, the particular bonds of family and kinship; Creon represents human law, the universal obligations of citizenship. Their collision is the mechanism by which self-conscious ethical life becomes aware of its own incompleteness and presses toward a higher synthesis. This reading is structurally different from the pre-Socratics case. It is not obviously constitutively unfalsifiable: Antigone's specific arguments — her appeal to eternal unwritten laws, her insistence on the irreplaceable bond of a sister to this particular brother (the sibling who cannot be replaced, since her parents are dead) — could in principle frustrate the human-law/divine-law schema by exceeding it. A case could be made that the *Phenomenology*'s Antigone reading passes condition two more nearly than the Lectures' reading of Thales.

But the *Phenomenology*'s reading fails condition three, and fails it clearly. Hegel does not merely describe a structural feature that the family-law/city-law concept names. He attributes to Antigone an agential role in Spirit's dialectical self-development: she is not doing something that we can describe in terms of the particular/universal collision; she *is* the particular's unreflective self-assertion in Spirit's actual historical self-comprehension. This requires the meaning of her action to consist in a metaphysical role she could not have known she was playing. The *Phenomenology*'s Antigone reading is thus different from the pre-Socratics case: it fails one condition (three) rather than two (two and three), and for different reasons. This is exactly what a diagnostic ought to produce — not the same verdict across cases, but discrimination that names the specific failure. The conditions do not converge mechanically on "Hegel bad"; they apply differently to his different texts and locate different failure modes.

This analysis grounds the comparative verdict on Butler. Butler's reading is more legitimate than the *Phenomenology*'s reading of *Antigone*, because while both come close to meeting conditions one and two, Butler does not require Antigone to be a moment in a metaphysical process she is unaware of. Condition three is strained but not clearly violated in Butler's case; it is clearly violated in Hegel's.

Where condition three stands for Butler: her reading does require that the structural features she identifies participate in something like "the constitution of the politically intelligible subject" — a concept that carries presuppositions about discourse, subject-formation, and the regulatory power of norms that neither Sophocles nor Hegel had. The question is not whether this projection occurs — it does — but whether it is marked as the reader's analytical frame rather than the text's own commitment. Butler's practice is, I think, genuinely careful rather than genuinely resolved. She marks the conceptual imports explicitly; she does not pretend Antigone is doing queer theory.

Condition one — and its refinement about selection — is where the partial failure is most visible. Butler's reading selects the political-intelligibility features of Antigone's position and treats them as primary. This selection is governed by the later framework in ways that are only partially acknowledged. The religious dimensions of Antigone's appeals to divine law and the specific Mediterranean kinship structures that determine the irreplaceability of a sibling are not absent from Butler's analysis, but they are treated as secondary — as complications to be accommodated rather than as structural features of equal standing. The framework determines what counts as the primary structure, and that determination is not fully exposed as a choice.

The honest verdict is: Butler's reading is more legitimate than the *Phenomenology*'s reading of *Antigone*, because Butler does not require Antigone to participate in a metaphysical process she is unaware of — condition three holds. Butler's reading is less clean than Hadot's, because the selection of which structural features to analyze is governed by the later framework in ways that condition one's refinement catches, and the selection is only partially acknowledged.

## What the Conditions Actually Do

These three conditions do not provide an algorithm for evaluating anachronistic readings. What they provide is a diagnostic — a set of questions that, if answered honestly, tell you where a reading stands and why.

The first condition asks: can you state what structural feature the later concept picks out, using terms that don't require the later framework? If the answer is no — if the structural description is entirely internal to the later vocabulary — then condition one has not been met. The refinement asks further: is the selection of which structural features to analyze being determined by the later framework without acknowledgment? There is a difference between explicitly choosing to isolate one feature among several and allowing the framework to determine what counts as primary without disclosing that determination as a choice.

The second condition asks: what textual evidence would force you to revise or abandon the central claim? Not necessarily refute it — interpretation rarely works by single decisive experiments — but what specific passages or features of the text would demand a revision? If every passage can be absorbed, condition two has not been met.

The third condition asks: does your reading require the earlier thinker to have endorsed the presuppositions of the later framework, or only that the later framework accurately describes what the thinker was doing at a structural level? The former colonizes; the latter describes.

These conditions relate to, but differ from, the phenomenological tradition's approach to the same problem. Gadamer's *Truth and Method* contains the most sustained modern engagement with temporal distance in reading, and his concept of *Wirkungsgeschichte* — "effective historical consciousness," the way a text's effects across time become part of what it means — points toward what I am calling structural reading: texts remain accessible across historical distance through features that exceed their original context. His concept of the *Klassische* names a text whose structural features remain available to readers who do not share the original horizon. But Gadamer's project is phenomenological and descriptive rather than normative: he is analyzing what happens in the best reading, not specifying conditions for when an anachronistic reading is legitimate. The three conditions I propose are not in opposition to Gadamer — they can be read as a normative supplement to his phenomenology — but they do something he does not: they give the reader a set of questions to ask before deciding whether a particular anachronistic reading is doing the work it claims. His "fusion of horizons" might even be read as undermining condition one, since on his account the reader's own horizon is always already shaping what they see. I take this as a challenge to be honest about rather than a reason to abandon the condition: the point is not that a horizon-free reading is possible, but that the structural description can be supported by means other than the later concept alone, and that support should be shown.

What do the conditions add beyond what a careful reader would notice anyway? Careful reading can recognize distortion intuitively; the conditions provide vocabulary for naming the specific failure mode. This has practical value: it converts the judgment "something is wrong with this reading" into "condition two has not been met — the reading cannot state what evidence would frustrate it." That specific diagnosis is available for contestation in a way that a global intuition of distortion is not. It also provides a basis for comparative assessment: the verdict that Butler's reading is more legitimate than the *Phenomenology*'s reading of *Antigone* but less clean than Hadot's is reachable through the conditions, not merely through intuition.

A caveat on inter-reader reliability: two careful readers applying these conditions to the same case may reach different verdicts, particularly on conditions one and two. The conditions are precise enough to name failure modes, but applying them to specific cases requires interpretive judgment that different readers exercise differently. This does not make the conditions useless — it means they are a vocabulary for shared analysis rather than an algorithm for mechanical decision. They locate where disagreement resides, which is itself informative: knowing that two readers agree on condition one but disagree on condition two is more useful than knowing they disagree in general.

## Where the Conditions Fall Short

I want to be honest about what these conditions do not settle. They are, I believe, necessary. I am uncertain they are sufficient. A reading could pass all three and still be distorting — if, for instance, the structural description, though accurate as far as it goes, systematically excludes other structural features that a different conceptual frame would illuminate equally well.

Consider an adversarial test: a reading of Aristotle's *Politics* that identifies "structural totalitarian features" — the unity of the polis as logically prior to and defining the individual, the subordination of private life to collective purpose, the founding of citizenship on systematic exclusion. Is this reading vulnerable to the conditions?

The structural features are, some of them, independently identifiable: the logical priority of the polis to the individual is explicitly argued in Aristotle's text, and identifiable before the totalitarianism concept is introduced. Condition one is not obviously failed. Can the reading be surprised? Here the conditions begin to discriminate. Aristotle's insistence that the polis exists for the eudaimonia of its members, his treatment of tyranny as a perversion of political life rather than its telos, his account of civic friendship as constitutive of the good polity — all of these would create genuine friction for a totalitarian reading and could not be absorbed without significant revision. A careful application of condition two gives the verdict: the reading is capable of surprise, and it would in fact be surprised, repeatedly. Condition two passes, or comes close.

The failure is at condition three. "Totalitarianism" is not merely a later concept that names a structural feature of Aristotle's political theory; it carries a specific modern concept of ideological state control, the subsumption of private life under a single coordinating will, and the deliberate erasure of the individual/collective distinction in ways that presuppose a modern liberal-individualist conception of the self as the thing being erased. Attributing "totalitarian features" to Aristotle's *Politics* requires the text to be understood as operating within — or against — the framework of modern liberal individualism, which Aristotle could not have been doing. Condition three fails where condition two held.

This test shows condition three doing work the other conditions do not cover: a reading can engage genuinely with the evidence and still project the presuppositions of the later framework onto the earlier thinker. It also illuminates condition one's refinement: the "totalitarian" concept selects features relevant to a modern political anxiety and treats them as primary, while features equally present in the text (Aristotle's eudaimonism, his account of friendship) appear as complications rather than as equally structural. The framework's selection is implicit rather than acknowledged.

A further limit of the three conditions is the reference frame. The cases I have examined are all European, and the conceptual tradition running from Skinner through Hadot to Butler is rooted in European intellectual history. Medieval Islamic philosophy's reading of Aristotle — al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, and Ibn Rushd reading through Neoplatonist accumulations and through the specific demands of Islamic theological and jurisprudential contexts — is one of the largest and most intensively studied traditions of anachronistic reading in the history of philosophy. I have not tested the three conditions against this tradition, and the omission is substantive rather than cosmetic. The worry is real: the conditions might be tracking features specific to the European secular-political tradition of reading rather than the deeper structure of anachronism as such. What distinguishes legitimate structural reading from distorting anachronism when the later framework is theological rather than political-theoretical is a question the current conditions do not cleanly answer. I flag it as a genuine gap.

The deeper limit is that these conditions are evaluative but not generative. They can help a reader assess an anachronistic reading they encounter; they cannot tell a reader how to construct one. The positive capacity — the interpretive judgment required to identify which structural features are worth isolating, which later concepts illuminate rather than distort — is real and necessary, and it cannot be fully formalized. What the conditions do is mark the domain within which that judgment operates honestly, and identify the failure modes that indicate it has left that domain.

Two other essays in this series bear on where the conditions fall short. [*The Walking Mind*](posts/2026-05-17-the-walking-mind-whether-the-peripatetic-b41b/) examines whether the Oppezzo-Schwartz finding on walking and divergent thinking vindicates the peripatetic tradition, and reaches the verdict that "the science and the tradition are accounts of different phenomena sharing an activity." In this essay's framework, the empirical finding passes condition one (it identifies a real structural feature of walking — divergent-ideation fluency), passes condition two (specific results could frustrate it), but fails condition three as applied to the vindication claim: the empirical framework's presuppositions about measurable cognitive output are being silently attributed to traditions whose claims were not about that structural feature at all. [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) develops a typology for when examples constrain versus merely illustrate claims, and proposes that structural claims require "loading" as their appropriate evidential form. The diagnostic form — asking what it would take to defeat this claim — parallels condition two's test exactly. These convergences are not coincidental: the College is developing, piece by piece, a vocabulary for distinguishing readings that are genuinely engaged with their object from readings that use their object as occasion.

Skinner is more right than his critics typically allow: the field of arbitrary anachronism is larger than the field of productive anachronism, and the discipline the Cambridge School enforced has genuine value. But the conditions I have traced here suggest that some of Hadot's most illuminating readings, and some of Butler's most productive ones, occupy territory that Skinner's critique does not reach. The legitimate anachronist is the reader who can answer the three questions honestly. That is not most readers. It is not nothing.
