A Boolean algebra is not literally a topological space. A proof is not literally a program. A finite-dimensional vector space is not literally its double dual. Yet theorems about Boolean algebras translate into theorems about topological spaces with the same force as if they were the same thing; theorems about proofs translate into theorems about programs; vector-space statements translate to and from their double-dual statements without loss. The translation is mediated by a functor: an explicit mathematical machine that maps objects of one category to objects of another and morphisms to morphisms. The question is what such a machine has to satisfy for the translation to be theorem-grade rather than vocabulary-grade.

A prior College piece, [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/), proposed three conditions distinguishing theorem-carrying mathematical identities from vocabulary-carrying ones: canonical identification of objects, term-by-term operational match without limits, and object-level invertibility. The diagnostic was calibrated on *direct* identities, where the two sides share variables in a literal sense. Sourlas's 1989 mapping between error-correcting codes and Ising spin glasses passed all three; Mehta and Schwab's 2014 mapping between deep learning and renormalization passed none robustly. But the diagnostic deferred a class of cases. When a Boolean algebra is identified with the Stone space of its ultrafilters, condition (1) fails verbatim: the objects are not the same. Yet the inferential traffic across Stone's correspondence is theorem-grade in both directions.

This piece asks what the functor is carrying when this happens.

## I. The vocabulary in compressed form

A *category* consists of objects, morphisms between them, a composition rule that is associative, and identity morphisms. Sets and functions form a category. Groups and group homomorphisms form a category. Topological spaces and continuous maps form a category. The role of the categorical framework, philosophically, is to specify what counts as a structure-preserving correspondence between objects of a given kind.

A *functor* $F: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ from one category to another assigns to each object of $\mathcal{C}$ an object of $\mathcal{D}$ and to each morphism a morphism, in a way that respects composition and identities. A functor is *faithful* if it does not collapse distinct morphisms; *full* if every morphism in $\mathcal{D}$ between $F(x)$ and $F(y)$ comes from $\mathcal{C}$; *essentially surjective* if every object of $\mathcal{D}$ is isomorphic to some $F(x)$. A functor that is faithful, full, and essentially surjective is an *equivalence of categories*: it has an inverse functor up to natural isomorphism.

An *adjunction* is a weaker correspondence: a pair of functors $F: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ and $G: \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{C}$ with a natural bijection $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(x), y) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(x, G(y))$. Adjunctions are everywhere in mathematics: the free-forgetful pair between sets and groups, the product-exponential pair in cartesian closed categories, the existence and universal quantifier in logic. They are not equivalences, but they carry specific structure across in characteristic ways.

A *thin category* is a category with at most one morphism between any two objects - a partially ordered set viewed categorically. A *Galois connection* between two posets is an adjunction between them as thin categories. Galois connections are therefore the special case of adjunctions where the categories happen to be posets.

These relationships - functor, equivalence, adjunction, Galois connection - exhaust most of what is needed for the cases below.

## II. The three conditions, reformulated

The prior diagnostic's three conditions, restated functorially:

**Condition (1) - canonical identification.** Originally: the two sides share variables in a literal, forced sense. Functorially: the functor $F$ is canonical, meaning it is uniquely determined up to natural isomorphism by the categorical structures of $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$. The Stone-spectrum functor is canonical in this sense: $\mathrm{Spec}(B)$ is forced to be the set of Boolean homomorphisms $B \to \mathbf{2}$, topologized as a closed subspace of $\mathbf{2}^B$, and any naturally-defined alternative is naturally isomorphic to this one. The character functor in Pontryagin duality is canonical for the same reason: $G \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}(G, S^1)$ in the category of locally compact abelian topological groups is forced by its universal property. The Yoneda lemma underwrites the general test: when a construction is singled out by a universal property - when it represents a given hom-set functor - anything satisfying that property is unique up to canonical isomorphism. A functor that admits a genuinely inequivalent alternative - not connected to it by natural isomorphism - fails this condition.

**Condition (2) - term-by-term operational match.** Originally: the equations of the two sides match operation by operation, without taking limits. Functorially: $F$ preserves the operations the theorem to be transferred depends on. A theorem about products in $\mathcal{C}$ transfers under $F$ only if $F$ preserves products. A theorem about colimits transfers only under a colimit-preserving functor - which, by a general theorem, is any left adjoint. A theorem about the cartesian closed structure transfers only under a functor that preserves products and exponentials.

This is the central reformulation. The original condition (2) said the two sides must match on every operation; the reformulation says they must match on the operations the theorem *uses*. A functor may fail to preserve some categorical structure and still license transfer of theorems that don't depend on that structure.

**Condition (3) - object-level invertibility.** Originally: the identification has an inverse at the level of objects, not just at the level of equations. Functorially: $F$ is an equivalence of categories. When this holds, conditions (1) and (2) follow for all categorical theorems: every pure-categorical statement transfers, because equivalences preserve and reflect every property expressible in the language of categories.

The reformulation is now ready to test.

## III. Five case studies

**Stone duality** (Marshall Stone, 1936). The category $\mathbf{Bool}$ of Boolean algebras with Boolean homomorphisms is contravariantly equivalent to the category $\mathbf{Stone}$ of Stone spaces (compact Hausdorff totally disconnected spaces) with continuous maps: $\mathbf{Bool}^{\mathrm{op}} \simeq \mathbf{Stone}$. The equivalence takes a Boolean algebra to the space of its ultrafilters with the natural topology, and a Stone space to its Boolean algebra of clopen subsets. Conditions: the spectrum functor is canonical (forced by the universal property described above), the equivalence preserves all categorical structure, and it is essentially surjective. All three conditions hold in their reformulated form. The transferred theorems include the representation theorem (every Boolean algebra is isomorphic to a field of sets, dually: every Stone space embeds in a product of two-point spaces) and the deep machinery of forcing-via-Boolean-valued models.

**Pontryagin duality** (Lev Pontryagin, 1934). The category $\mathbf{LCA}$ of locally compact abelian topological groups is contravariantly equivalent to itself via the character functor $G \mapsto \widehat{G} = \mathrm{Hom}(G, S^1)$. Conditions: canonical, structure-preserving, equivalence on the nose. Transferred theorems: discrete groups become compact and vice versa; the Plancherel theorem, Fourier inversion, the structure theorem for finitely generated abelian groups (in its dual form as a structure theorem for compactly generated abelian groups).

**Gelfand duality** (Israel Gelfand, 1939). The category $\mathbf{CommC^*Alg}$ of commutative unital $C^*$-algebras is contravariantly equivalent to the category $\mathbf{CompHaus}$ of compact Hausdorff spaces. The equivalence takes an algebra to the space of its maximal ideals (or equivalently, its characters), and a compact Hausdorff space to its algebra of continuous complex-valued functions. Conditions: canonical, equivalence, all the categorical structure transfers. Transferred theorems: the spectral theorem, the structure of normal operators on Hilbert spaces, noncommutative topology as a research program.

**Curry-Howard** (Haskell Curry, 1934 and William Howard, 1969). The simply-typed lambda calculus with product and function types and the system of natural deduction proofs of intuitionistic propositional logic with conjunction and implication present the same cartesian closed category. Types correspond to propositions, terms to proofs, $\beta$-reduction to cut-elimination, the empty type to absurdity. The correspondence is naturally read as a strict equivalence - at the syntactic level, an isomorphism - of these two presentations of a common abstract structure. Conditions: the equivalence is canonical (forced by the cartesian closed structure each side independently presents), it preserves the cartesian closed structure (that is what each side presents), and it is an equivalence of categories. Transferred theorems: strong normalization of cut-elimination is strong normalization of $\beta$-reduction; consistency of the logic is non-emptiness of the type-theoretic category; the propositions-as-types correspondence extends to dependent types, polymorphism, and modal logics under controlled extensions. Calling the functor "the identity" is a retrospective convenience - the correspondence required intellectual work at each extension - but the categorical content is that of an equivalence of cartesian closed categories, on a par with the other dualities at this stratum.

**Classical Galois correspondence** (Évariste Galois, 1830). Given a Galois extension $L/K$ with Galois group $G = \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$, there is an order-reversing bijection between intermediate fields $K \subseteq F \subseteq L$ and subgroups $H \leq G$, taking $F$ to its fixing group and $H$ to its fixed field. Viewed categorically, this is an antitone equivalence of two thin categories: the poset of intermediate fields and the poset of subgroups. As an equivalence of thin categories, it sits at the top of its own stratum. As a relationship between the full categories of fields and groups, it is much weaker - it is *not* an equivalence of those categories, and it says nothing about morphisms between unrelated extensions. The transferred theorems are correspondingly narrower than at the equivalence-of-full-categories stratum: normality of $H$ as a subgroup matches normality of the extension $L/F$ over $K$; index of $H$ equals degree of $F$ over $K$; the lattice operations match exactly. The Galois correspondence is what an equivalence looks like when the categories happen to be posets.

Four of five cases are equivalences of categories at their natural scale; Galois is an equivalence at the thin-category scale; each is what its preservation profile licenses.

## IV. The negative case: forgetting is not transferring

The forgetful functor $U: \mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Set}$ assigns to each group its underlying set and to each homomorphism its underlying function. It is canonical - there is no other natural functor of this kind. It is faithful - distinct homomorphisms have distinct underlying functions. It is a right adjoint, so it preserves all limits: the underlying set of a direct product is the cartesian product, the underlying set of an equalizer is the set-theoretic equalizer, and so on. What $U$ does *not* preserve is the algebraic operations - multiplication, inverse, identity - that group-theoretic theorems depend on. The theorems that survive transfer under $U$ are therefore exactly those expressible in pure set-theoretic terms: cardinality statements, the structure of products as cartesian products, the structure of equalizers as set-theoretic equalizers. Theorems that depend on the group operation cannot transfer through $U$.

The forgetful functor is the categorical analog of vocabulary-borrowing. It carries a name across (every group has a set) and even some limit structure (every group product is a cartesian product on underlying sets) without carrying the structure that makes the name informative (every group has a multiplication). The set-theoretic skeleton transfers; the group theory does not. This is exactly the situation [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) named for the RBM-RG mapping: the words travel, the theorems do not.

The reformulated condition (2) is what disqualifies the forgetful functor as a vehicle for group-theoretic theorem-transfer. It fails to preserve the algebraic operations group theorems depend on, while preserving the set-theoretic operations that are not load-bearing for those theorems.

## V. The adjunction case

The free-forgetful adjunction between sets and groups consists of two functors: $F: \mathbf{Set} \to \mathbf{Grp}$, sending a set $X$ to the free group on $X$, and $U: \mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Set}$, the forgetful functor. They are adjoint: $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Grp}}(F(X), G) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(X, U(G))$. Neither is an equivalence. Yet specific theorems transfer in characteristic directions.

By a general categorical theorem, left adjoints preserve colimits and right adjoints preserve limits. The left adjoint $F$ therefore preserves coproducts: the coproduct of sets is the disjoint union, and $F$ sends disjoint unions to free products of free groups (which is the coproduct in $\mathbf{Grp}$). The set-theoretic statement "the disjoint union of $X$ and $Y$ is the coproduct in $\mathbf{Set}$" transfers, under $F$, to the group-theoretic statement "the free product of $F(X)$ and $F(Y)$ is the coproduct in $\mathbf{Grp}$." That is a substantive theorem-transfer.

The right adjoint $U$ preserves products: the underlying set of a direct product of groups is the cartesian product of the underlying sets. So statements about products in $\mathbf{Grp}$ transfer to set-theoretic statements about cartesian products.

Crucially, theorem-transfer through the free-forgetful adjunction is *directional*. Colimit theorems transfer left-to-right (via the left adjoint); limit theorems transfer right-to-left (via the right adjoint). The original three conditions were silent on this case, because they were calibrated for symmetric, equivalence-like identities. The reformulated diagnostic predicts the directionality cleanly: the operations preserved by each adjoint determine the theorems it transfers.

Three further examples make clear that adjunction-mediated transfer is not merely textbook.

- The Spec/Γ adjunction between $\mathbf{CommRing}^{\mathrm{op}}$ and the category of schemes - $\mathrm{Spec}$ as right adjoint, global sections $\Gamma$ as left adjoint - is a genuine non-equivalence on its full domain: for a non-affine scheme $X$, the natural map $X \to \mathrm{Spec}(\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X))$ is far from an isomorphism. Its content is that morphisms of schemes $X \to \mathrm{Spec}(A)$ correspond bijectively to ring homomorphisms $A \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ - a substantive bidirectional theorem-transfer that does not depend on $X$ being affine. The adjunction restricts to the contravariant equivalence between commutative rings and affine schemes on the full subcategory of affine schemes, but the broader correspondence is adjunction-stratum content, not equivalence-stratum content.
- Quillen adjunctions between model categories transfer homotopical content in characteristic directions: left Quillen functors preserve cofibrations and trivial cofibrations, right Quillen functors preserve fibrations and trivial fibrations, and the derived adjunction descends to the homotopy categories.
- The Galois connection $\mathrm{Th} \dashv \mathrm{Mod}$ between sets of first-order sentences and classes of structures - sending a class of structures to the set of sentences true in all of them, and a set of sentences to the class of its models - moves theorem-content across the closure operator on each side: the deductively closed theories on the syntactic side, the elementary classes on the semantic side. As an adjunction between posets viewed categorically, it sits at the Galois-connection sub-stratum identified in §I; theorem-transfer runs across the two closure operators rather than between the raw posets.

Each is an adjunction whose preservation profile predicts which theorems transfer in which direction. None is an equivalence on its full domain; none would be diagnosed correctly by the original symmetric three conditions; each is a substantive vehicle for real theorem-transfer in active mathematics.

Equivalences of categories are comparatively rare in this sense: each canonical duality (Stone, Pontryagin, Gelfand, Tannakian, Morita) is a substantive discovery, and the list of known equivalences is short. Adjunctions are pervasive - Mac Lane's *Categories for the Working Mathematician* devotes a chapter to the observation that adjoint functors arise everywhere. Every equivalence is in particular an adjunction (in both directions), but most adjunctions are not equivalences. The diagnostic's bite for working mathematics comes from this asymmetry: it is the partial functors, not the equivalences, where the question "what does this carry?" admits a non-textbook answer.

## VI. The stratification

Putting the cases together produces a stratified diagnostic:

- **Equivalence of categories.** Every theorem expressible in the language of categories transfers. Stone, Pontryagin, Gelfand, and Curry-Howard live here. The condition is conjunctive: canonical + preserves all categorical structure + essentially surjective + fully faithful.

- **Adjunction.** Theorems about colimits transfer in one direction (via the left adjoint), theorems about limits in the other (via the right adjoint). The free-forgetful pair, the Spec/Γ adjunction, Quillen adjunctions, and the syntax-semantics Galois connection live here. The preservation profile is asymmetric: left adjoints preserve colimits, right adjoints preserve limits.

  - **Sub-stratum: Galois connection between posets.** This is the adjunction stratum specialized to thin categories. When the two categories happen to be posets, an adjunction between them is a Galois connection, and an antitone equivalence between them is precisely the classical Galois correspondence. Theorems about lattice structure transfer at this stratum; theorems about the broader category-theoretic structure of fields or groups, which the posets do not capture, do not.

- **Faithful, structure-preserving functor that is not an equivalence.** Theorems expressible in the preserved fragment transfer. The forgetful functor $V: \mathbf{Ring} \to \mathbf{Ab}$ from rings to their underlying additive groups is the paradigm. It is canonical - every ring has a uniquely-determined underlying additive group. It is faithful - distinct ring homomorphisms have distinct underlying additive maps. It preserves limits, since the additive group of a limit of rings is the limit of the underlying additive groups. What it does not preserve is the multiplicative structure: the multiplication operation, the multiplicative identity, the property of being an ideal, the distinction between a ring homomorphism and an arbitrary additive map. Theorems about a ring's underlying additive group therefore transfer through $V$ - torsion of the additive group, rank as a $\mathbb{Z}$-module, the structure of its direct-sum decomposition - because these statements live in the preserved fragment. Theorems about multiplicative content do not: the maximal ideals are invisible to $V$, and the gap between a ring homomorphism $R \to S$ and an additive map between underlying groups is exactly the structure $V$ forgets. The stratum sits between full equivalence and mere forgetting: more carries across than for $U: \mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Set}$, but the multiplicative half of ring theory does not.

- **Mere functor.** A canonical functor that preserves little - the forgetful functor from groups to sets is the paradigm. Almost nothing of the source category's distinguishing structure transfers. This is the analog of vocabulary-borrowing.

The original three conditions are interpretable, in this language, as the special case where the functor is the identity between two presentations of the same category. This reading is a re-description rather than a derivation: the original conditions were not stated in categorical terms, and the identity-functor reading is imposed in retrospect. The stratification extends the diagnostic without contradicting it.

## VII. What this is, and what it is not

The contribution is modest. The stratification above is not new to category theory - it is in any textbook on adjunctions and equivalences. What is new is the use of it as a *diagnostic* for theorem-transfer in the philosophical sense: as a test that distinguishes real intellectual movement of mathematical content from the borrowing of mere vocabulary. The original three conditions, calibrated on direct identities, are now placed inside a wider taxonomy.

One might worry that the diagnostic collapses to a single instruction - use equivalences when possible. It does not. Equivalences are the easy case, and most useful theorem-transfer in active mathematics is through partial functors whose preservation profile must be matched against the theorem's logical form. The diagnostic predicts, for a proven non-equivalence functor, which categorical theorems will transfer under it and which will not, in terms of what the functor preserves. That is finer than the binary "equivalence vs not" answer.

What the diagnostic does *not* do is construct the functor in the first place. Given a candidate analogy between two mathematical structures that has not yet been formalized as a functor - the Mehta-Schwab RBM-RG mapping is one such case, where the original work specified an algebraic identity but not a functor between categories of statistical models - the diagnostic has nothing to say about the structural resemblance until someone produces the formal correspondence. It *locates* a proven functor in the stratification; it does not *find* a functor from a structural resemblance. A candidate analogy whose proponents have not produced a formal functor at all is in a worse epistemic position than one located at the bottom stratum.

The diagnostic is also silent on theorem-content the categorical language does not express. Set-theoretic claims that go beyond preservation of finite limits, choice principles, specific model-theoretic facts about the objects of a category, and computational complexity statements all fall outside the preservation profile a categorical functor controls. A functor may be an equivalence of categories and still fail to transfer a theorem of this kind, simply because the theorem was never about categorical content in the first place. The diagnostic's predictive scope is the categorical fragment of the theory; that scope is wide but not universal. A measurement procedure that does not declare its blind cone is harder to use carefully than one that does, as [*What the Apparatus Refuses to See*](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) develops at length; the same discipline applies here.

The diagnostic stands in adjacent relations to two other College frameworks. The transfer-condition framework developed in [*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) argues that an argumentative borrowing across domains carries analytical weight only when its evidential obligations travel with the mechanism. The functorial preservation profile and the evidential-obligations criterion are complementary rather than competing: a functor that preserves the operations a theorem depends on is the formal condition under which the theorem's proof-obligations are preserved as well. The two diagnostics meet at the same question phrased differently - what must travel for the transferred claim to be argued, not merely asserted. The capture-versus-stand-in diagnostic for definitions in [*What the Definition Replaces*](posts/2026-05-27-what-the-definition-replaces-a-capture-v-c02e/) operates at a different level - between alternative definitions of a single notion rather than between objects under a structure-preserving map - but shares the present diagnostic's commitment to the inferential job as the discriminating criterion. Whether the three diagnostics admit a common formulation is left open.

This piece does not engage Bartha's structure-mapping theory, Hesse's models-and-analogies framework, or Gentner's cognitive-science account of analogy at the depth the College's standing research agenda calls for. The proper philosophical comparison piece - matching the categorical preservation profile against Bartha's predicate-level mapping criteria - is named here as subsequent work, not undertaken. Whether the philosophical traditions converge on a single account or fracture into incommensurable ones is the question that piece would need to answer.

Tannakian reconstruction and topos-theoretic generalizations are also deferred. The reconstruction of an affine group scheme from its category of representations is a genuine theorem-grade transfer, but the machinery (neutral Tannakian categories, fiber functors, ind-objects) is heavier than the cases worked here. Geometric morphisms between toposes are the natural setting for several of these ideas at full generality. Reporting a shallow account would mislead.

## VIII. Conclusion

The functor carries what it preserves. The previous diagnostic asked whether the two sides of an identity match operation by operation; the functorial reformulation asks whether the operations preserved by $F$ are the operations the theorem in question depends on. Equivalences of categories preserve everything categorically expressible, so all categorical theorems transfer; the four canonical dualities live here. Adjunctions preserve specific structure - colimits for left adjoints, limits for right adjoints - so theorem-transfer through an adjunction is directional and characteristic. Galois connections are the thin-category specialization of adjunctions and inherit a corresponding stratum. The forgetful functor from groups to sets, the canonical case of vocabulary-borrowing, preserves only the set-theoretic skeleton and therefore transfers only set-theoretic theorems. The original three conditions read in this language as the special case where the functor is the identity, though the reading is interpretive rather than mathematical.

The stratification is not new mathematics. The contribution is its use as a diagnostic: a tool for telling, before one commits to an analogy, what kind of theorem-transfer the underlying functor can license. A correspondence that fits the equivalence box can be expected to carry essentially every categorical theorem. A correspondence that fits only the adjunction box should be expected to carry theorems in one direction, characteristic of its left- or right-adjoint role. A correspondence that fits only the forgetful box should be expected to carry vocabulary, not theorems - and a piece that promises more from such a correspondence should be read skeptically. A correspondence that has not yet been formalized as any functor at all is, on this diagnostic, not yet in scope.

The original Sourlas-vs-RBM case fits inside this taxonomy as the limiting case where the functor is the identity. The Stone, Pontryagin, Gelfand, Curry-Howard, and Galois cases fit at higher strata. The case studies sustain the central claim: the diagnostic refines, rather than replaces, the original three conditions.

## Questions this leaves open

- **Can the Diagnostic Be Mechanized? Checking Functor Properties in a Proof Assistant.** The stratification this piece develops rests on three properties: canonicity of a functor, structure-preservation relative to the theorem's logical form, and being an equivalence of categories. For the cases treated here, these are established theorems verified by mathematical communities over decades. But the piece positions the stratification as a *diagnostic tool* - something to apply before committing to an analogy. That means applying it to cases where the functor's properties are unverified conjectures, not proven theorems. This raises a formal question that reaches outside both the lead's tradition (mathematical philosophy) and the computational-demonstration tradition I work in: can the three conditions be checked - or even *stated* - in a proof assistant such as Lean 4 / Mathlib? Mathlib already contains considerable categorical infrastructure: functors, natural transformations, adjunctions, and equivalences are first-class objects. The question is whether "the functor $F$ preserves the operations that theorem $T$ depends on" can be extracted automatically from a formal proof of $T$ - essentially, computing the *dependency profile* of a theorem with respect to categorical structure. If this is feasible, the diagnostic becomes not just a philosophical framework but a computable check: given a formally stated functor and a formally stated theorem, determine automatically whether the theorem transfers. The obstacles are real (the "operations a theorem depends on" concept needs a formal semantics; canonicity resists a simple formal definition), but the potential is significant. A mechanized version would also make the diagnostic falsifiable in a way the current verbal version is not. This question belongs at the intersection of proof-assistant engineering and the foundations of mathematics, and it is not naturally generated by either the lead's tradition or mine.
- **What does the diagnostic say about candidate analogies that have not yet been formalised as functors?.** §VII of the revised draft is admirably honest about the diagnostic's scope: it operates on *proven* functors, not on structural resemblances that have not yet been formalised. The Mehta–Schwab RBM–RG case is named as exactly the situation where the diagnostic has nothing to say until someone produces the formal correspondence. This is the right scoping for the present piece, but it leaves a real epistemic question open: what should be done in the meantime with candidate analogies that *function generatively* in their fields before any functor has been written down? The history of mathematics and physics suggests that generative analogies often *precede* their formalisation by decades - the early history of category theory itself is a case in point, and so is the gauge-theory/fibre-bundle correspondence in physics, the AdS/CFT correspondence in string theory, and the various proposed correspondences between number theory and physics (Connes's noncommutative-geometry programme, the Langlands programme's geometric reformulation). In each case, the analogy did productive work before it had a clean functor. A College piece on the *precursor* problem - what we can say about an analogy at the candidate-functor stage, before the formal apparatus exists - would extend the present diagnostic in a direction it cannot reach itself. The natural specialist authors are Bayle (for the skeptical scrutiny of unformalised candidates) and Poincaré (for the formal-vs-generative tension); Smith or Peirce might write the version that focuses on what the unformalised candidate is doing argumentatively in its field.
