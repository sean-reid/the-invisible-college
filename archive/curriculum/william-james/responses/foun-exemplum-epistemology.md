# Application of Exemplum Typology: Analysis of Draft Work

## The Paragraphs Under Examination

I have selected two paragraphs from my qualifying exam response on skill acquisition and attentional reallocation for analysis against Montaigne's typology.

**Paragraph 1:**

> As she practices, the motor execution becomes automated - transferred from controlled processing to automatic processing in Shiffrin and Schneider's terminology. This frees attentional capacity. But here is the critical observation: she does not simply allocate that freed attention to enjoying the music or thinking about something else. In deliberate practice, she reallocates it *within the performance domain*. Now she can attend to dynamics, phrasing, the balance between hands, the emotional arc. Her attention is *on the nuance*.

**Paragraph 2:**

> This reallocation is real work. It requires conscious effort. It requires building new habits of attention - learning to notice what previously went unnoticed. A person who has not practiced deliberately does not naturally attend to these distinctions; they simply do not see them as figure against ground.

## Analysis

These two paragraphs deploy a single extended example - the pianist learning to play a piece - to support structural and epistemological claims about attention and skill. Let me disaggregate what each is doing.

### The Pianist as Constraining Example

The first paragraph functions as *constraining*. The claim is structural: "freed attentional capacity is reallocated within the task domain, not elsewhere." A genuine counterexample would defeat this claim: a learner who, when automated execution frees attention, redirects that attention away from the task (to thinking about something else, or to metacognitive reflection unrelated to performance). I myself acknowledge that some practitioners do precisely this - they automate and then stop improving. The example is therefore vulnerable to evidential pressure.

What the paragraph does well: it shows the mechanism in operation. You can see the pianist's attention moving from "where is my hand?" to "what is the emotional shape of this phrase?" This is observation, not inference. A reader who has practiced an instrument recognizes this; a reader who has observed deliberate practice sees it happening. The example constrains the claim because the claim depends on this attentional reallocation actually occurring in at least some cases. If no one realllocates attention within a task when freed to do so, the whole apparatus breaks.

However - and here is where the analysis surfaces a problem - the paragraph presents the mechanism as though it follows automatically from automation. "This frees attentional capacity. But here is the critical observation: she does not simply allocate that freed attention to enjoying the music or thinking about something else. In deliberate practice, she reallocates it *within the performance domain*." The conditional structure is: automation frees capacity, therefore reallocation happens. But that is not what the case shows. What the case shows is: in deliberate practice, reallocation happens. The causality is not established by the example; it is assumed.

The claim that needs defending is: *why* does deliberately-practicing reallocation happen? Is it because the capacity was freed, or because deliberate practice is defined as allocating that freed capacity to task refinement? If the latter, the example is circular: I have defined deliberate practice as attention-reallocation, then used a case of deliberate practice to show that attention gets reallocated. The constraining example only constrains if I have independently established what freed capacity *would* do absent deliberate practice. The example alone does not establish causality; it establishes that the phenomenon occurs.

**Verdict:** The example functions as constraining, but it *overreaches*. The claim it actually supports is narrower than stated: "in deliberate practice, attention reallocates within the domain." The causal claim - "freed capacity causes reallocation" - requires additional argument. The paragraph should be reclassified or tightened.

### The Phenomenological Loading Problem

The second paragraph is more problematic. It functions as *loading* for a phenomenological claim: "active attention-reallocation creates new perceptual capacities; you experience distinctions you could not previously see." The claim is about what the subjective experience is like from the inside.

But here is the difficulty: the paragraph offers no phenomenological content. It makes no actual description of what reallocation feels like or looks like to the practitioner. Instead, it asserts a metaphor: "they simply do not see them as figure against ground." This is drawn from Gestalt perceptual psychology, which is apt. But it is not a description; it is a theoretical placeholder. The paragraph *names* the phenomenological claim without instantiating it.

To function as loading, the paragraph would need something like: "A person who is not practicing deliberately might hear the piece and perceive the melody as the salient element, the accompaniment as background texture - a foreground and ground that seem natural, inevitable. A person in deliberate practice hears the same notes and, through attention, separates what was bound: the harmony suddenly becomes figure, or the rhythmic texture, or the color of the middle register. This is not learning a new fact; it is a shift in what the ear itself can apprehend."

What the paragraph actually does is *assert* that this shift happens, using theoretical language from perceptual psychology. The loading function requires the reader to *feel* the pertinence of the mechanism through description. Here, the reader is asked to trust the assertion instead.

**Verdict:** This paragraph attempts loading but fails to do the work it names. It deploys example-adjacent theoretical language without the phenomenological content that makes loading legitimate. The paragraph should either: (a) be replaced with an actual phenomenological description that lets the reader recognize the experience, or (b) be reclassified as an assertion requiring different evidential support (empirical evidence that perceptual change occurs, not testimonial recognition).

## Proposed Revision

**Original Paragraph 1 plus new clarification:**

> As she practices, the motor execution becomes automated - transferred from controlled processing to automatic processing in Shiffrin and Schneider's terminology. This frees attentional capacity. In deliberate practice, practitioners reallocate this freed attention *within the performance domain* rather than elsewhere. Now she can attend to dynamics, phrasing, the balance between hands, the emotional arc. Her attention is *on the nuance*.
>
> [New sentence:] This reallocation does not happen automatically when attention is freed; it requires deliberate choice and structured practice. Many learners automate execution and then plateau, because no mechanism redirects their freed attention back into task refinement.

This move makes the example constraining rather than causally misleading. It acknowledges that freed attention *could* go elsewhere, making the reallocation observation itself load-bearing.

**Original Paragraph 2, revised:**

> This reallocation is real work. It requires conscious effort. It requires building new habits of attention - learning to notice what previously went unnoticed. [DELETE: "A person who has not practiced deliberately does not naturally attend to these distinctions; they simply do not see them as figure against ground."] [REPLACE WITH:] A person who has not practiced will attend to the melody and skip the phrasing; practice remaps attention so the phrasing becomes graspable. The shift is not learning a new fact about the piece but a shift in what immediate perception delivers. Where once the emotional arc of the phrase was a thought you had *about* the notes, it becomes something you hear *in* them.

This version actually does the phenomenological work that loading requires. It shows the reader the difference between baseline perception and practiced perception by making the difference visible, not by naming it theoretically.

## Metacomment: The Burden of Awareness

What this analysis reveals is that I was gesturing at the mechanism (freeing of attention, reallocation, perceptual shift) without doing the slower work of showing what each actually entails. Montaigne's example of his kidney stones works because he is willing to sit with the texture of the experience: the pain itself, the obstruction, the relief, what his self-knowledge in this particular case permitted him to do better than his physicians. He does not simply assert that self-knowledge is valuable; he demonstrates its value by showing a case where it made a concrete difference.

My draft accelerates past this work. I had the claims right - that attention is reallocable, that reallocation requires effort, that it produces perceptual change - but I had not yet done the writer's obligation to slow down and *show* what these claims actually look like when a person is living through them. The pedagogical gap this reveals is important: I can state the structure of an argument before I have earned the right to the examples that would make it vivid. The constraint is not laziness; it is that writing a genuine loading example requires more observation and honesty about what the reader actually experiences than I had yet completed.

What I must do before the qualifying response is final: sit with a genuine case of skill plateauing due to attentional constraints (my own piano practice, or an interview with someone in high-feedback deliberate practice), and let that case teach me what the lived experience actually contains. Only then can I load the structural claim responsibly.

The typology has done its work: it has shown me where I am claiming authority I have not earned.
