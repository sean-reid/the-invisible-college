# Divergences from Peirce's Pragmatism: Three Sites of Methodological Disagreement

## Preamble

Peirce's "How to Make Our Ideas Clear" establishes the pragmatic maxim: the meaning of a conception exhausts itself in its practical effects. This is the founding move of pragmatist epistemology, and I have learned from it deeply. But the pragmatism I have developed in my own work-what I will call a *radical empiricist* version-diverges from Peirce's in at least three specific places, each of which creates real constraints for the next person who tries to act on the divergence. I will name them directly, as the prompt asks, and specify what following each commitment would cost.

---

## Divergence 1: Thought Does Not Terminate in Belief; It Suspends There

**Peirce's formulation:** "The action of thought is excited by the irritation of doubt, and ceases when belief is attained; so that the production of belief is the sole function of thought."

This is elegant and sweeping. Thought is goal-directed; doubt initiates it; belief terminates it. Peirce means belief as *establishment of habit*. A person reaches belief when she has fixed a habit of action-when the uncertainty is gone, and action can proceed. I do not dispute the mechanism. I dispute that this is where intellectual work stops.

**My divergence:** In the cases I care most about-the moment of genuine epistemic decision, the structure of justified risk-taking, the phenomenology of intellectual commitment-belief does not terminate inquiry. It *suspends* it. A person who has reached belief has suspended further active investigation *for now*, but this is a poised state, not a finished one. The belief is held conditionally on future experience. It may be provisional, qualified, hedged. It is held *at a cost*.

**What this commits the next person to:** If you adopt this view, you cannot treat belief as Peirce does-as the resting-place where thought ceases. Instead, belief becomes an active posture, a stance toward the world that requires ongoing maintenance. You must account for the *psychology of conditional commitment*. You must explain how a person holds a belief while simultaneously keeping open the possibility of revision. This is not the same as doubt; it is not uncertainty. It is a conscious allocation of *when to stop investigating and when to resume*. The next person who works from this principle must develop a theory of attention management around belief-when do I reopen a question I thought settled? What cognitive cost does that reopening carry?

The practical consequence: empirical psychology becomes central to epistemology. The question is no longer "what is the logical structure of justified belief?" but "under what conditions does a human mind actually hold a belief without obsessively re-examining its foundations?" This moves investigation away from the logical termination point and into the temporal structure of inquiry itself.

## Divergence 2: The Practical Effects Are Not Transparent; They Require Attentional Work to Identify

**Peirce's formulation:** "Consider what effects, that might conceivably have practical bearings, we conceive the object of our conception to have; then, our conception of these effects is the whole of our conception of the object."

The pragmatic maxim assumes that practical effects are *recoverable*. If I understand what effects a concept produces, I understand the concept fully. Peirce illustrates this with "hardness": hardness just *is* resistance to scratching. The effect is transparent. I can test it.

But Peirce works mostly with concepts where practical effects are *physically observable*-force, weight, hardness, even truth (understood as the limit of inquiry). My disagreement is not with the principle but with the assumed transparency of the effects.

**My divergence:** The practical effects of many concepts-especially psychological and epistemic ones-are not mechanically accessible. Consider the practical effects of *attention* itself. What is the practical cash-value of attending to something rather than not attending? The answer is not simple. It depends on context, on what else the person is attending to (opportunity cost), on prior learning, on whether the attended-to thing is salient or requires sustained effort to notice. The effect is *real*-attending to something does change action and outcome-but it is not transparent the way scratching a diamond is transparent.

This creates an epistemological problem Peirce's essay does not address: *how do I identify the practical effects when they are distributed across attention, habit, and the structure of a person's world, rather than concentrated in a single observable change?* The cash-value of a concept may be real, but it may not be *readable off the surface of experience*. It requires psychological investigation.

**What this commits the next person to:** You must develop a method for *recovering practical effects that are not immediately observable*. You cannot rely on naive observation. You must use introspection (carefully), experimentation, the analysis of failure cases, the mapping of habit change. You must be willing to admit that the practical effects of some concepts are *irreducibly personal*-what it is practically like to attend to something varies with the attendee's history and capacities. This makes pragmatism empirically messier than Peirce's version. It means you cannot simply invoke "practical effects" as though they were univocal. You must do psychological work to identify them, and you must be honest about the limits of that work.

The practical consequence: pragmatism becomes more introspectively taxing. It requires the philosopher or scientist to *notice* what her own attention is doing, what habits are being formed, what costs are being borne. This is why radical empiricism must remain in contact with phenomenology. The next person who works from this principle is committed to examining their own mental life as data.

## Divergence 3: The Investigative Community Is Not Transparent; Consensus Does Not Automatically Converge on Truth

**Peirce's formulation:** "The opinion which is fated to be ultimately agreed to by all who investigate is what we mean by the truth...the real is that which, sooner or later, information and reasoning will finally result in."

This is Peirce's deepest move. Reality is what investigation converges on; truth is the limit of inquiry. This is a magnificent statement of scientific realism. But it contains an assumption about the investigative community that I cannot accept without severe qualification.

Peirce assumes that *given enough time, given sufficiently diverse investigators with sufficiently rigorous methods, the investigative process will converge on truth*. This requires that the investigative process is, over the long term, reliable. The assumption is that error, bias, and limitation are eventually self-correcting. The community gets it right in the end.

**My divergence:** I believe the investigative community is shaped by attention. The community asks the questions it is *equipped to notice*. It converges on truths that are *accessible to its methods*. But some truths-perhaps some important ones-may be invisible to the standard procedures of inquiry. A community can reach consensus while remaining systematically blind. This is not a failure of rigor; it is a structural feature of any bounded investigative apparatus.

Consider the history of attention itself. Before William James and the phenomenologists turned explicit investigative attention to the structure of consciousness, the investigative community reached convergence on many questions-in physics, chemistry, biology-while remaining almost wholly unable to ask coherent questions about consciousness. Not because consciousness was unreal, but because the investigative apparatus had not developed methods for it.

**What this commits the next person to:** You must be suspicious of consensus. Not skeptical to the point of paralysis, but genuinely suspicious. You must ask: *what is this community equipped to notice?* What questions does it fail to ask because it has not developed the methods to answer them? You must also ask: *what am I personally equipped to notice, given my own attention and training?* You cannot rely on the limit of inquiry as a criterion of truth because you cannot be sure you are investigating the right question. You are committed to a kind of epistemic humility that Peirce's formulation, at least, does not emphasize. You must continually expand the investigative apparatus itself.

The practical consequence: this dissolves Peirce's comforting picture of truth as the eventual consensus of a reliable process. It means doing philosophy or science is not just about answering questions-it is about *inventing better questions*, developing new methods for noticing what was previously invisible. It commits you to the exhausting work of meta-inquiry: not just investigating X, but investigating *whether we are asking the right question about X*.

---

## Conclusion

These three divergences are not violations of pragmatism. They are elaborations of it, pushed in a different direction. Each one takes Peirce's commitment to practical effects and works it harder-into the psychology of belief, into the hidden costs of identifying effects, into the structure of investigation itself. 

But each one exacts a cost. Each one makes the investigator's work more introspectively demanding, more suspicious of consensus, more aware of the limits of any single method. The investigator who takes on these commitments cannot rest as comfortably as Peirce suggests. She must remain in motion, aware that she is always investigating under conditions of partial blindness, holding beliefs that are provisional, trying to identify effects that are not transparent.

This is not weakness in the framework. It is honesty about what the investigator's actual condition is. And I believe it is more aligned with the lived structure of inquiry than Peirce's more confident version.
