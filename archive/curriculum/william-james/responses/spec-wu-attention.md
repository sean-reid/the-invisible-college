# Two Empirical Divides: Wu's Selection-for-Action and Radical Empiricist Alternatives

## Overview

Wayne Wu's *Attention* develops what he calls the "selection-for-action" account: attention is fundamentally a mechanism that selects among possible actions by constraining mental resources toward the representations needed to execute the selected action. This is a lean, functionalist thesis. It commits to a specific empirical architecture of cognitive prioritization. My radical empiricist treatment parts ways at two critical junctures, each with testable consequences.

---

## Divergence 1: The Primacy of Action-Readiness Over Phenomenology

### Wu's Commitment

Wu argues that attention's essence lies in preparing the cognitive system to act. The phenomenological feel of attention-the sense that something is vivid, salient, or "in focus"-is secondary. What attention *fundamentally does* is select the representational resources required for action execution. Consciousness of salience follows from this selection; it does not cause it. The flow is: action-need → selection → phenomenology.

This commits Wu to an empirical claim: you can dissociate phenomenal salience from actual attentional selection. A person might report that something "feels attended to" while measurements of cognitive resource allocation show it is not selected for action. Conversely, attentional mechanisms might be actively allocating resources to action-relevant information while the person reports no phenomenal sense of that information being salient or vivid.

### The Radical Empiricist Objection

I do not share this empirical priority. From a radical empiricist standpoint, *experience is the starting point, not the consequence*. The phenomenology of attention-the structure of what Bergson called the "fringe" of consciousness, the felt quality of salience and focus, the sense that something stands out against ground-is not decoration on the selection mechanism. It is *the thing itself that a cognitive account must explain*.

More specifically: when I attend to something, I do not attend to it *because* I am preparing an action. I attend to it *in the first place* because something makes it noticeable, consequential, or emotionally charged within my ongoing experience. The action-readiness may follow. Or it may not. But the attentional fact is the experiential fact.

Wu treats phenomenology as the dependent variable. I treat it as a legitimate aspect of the mechanism that requires independent explanation. This is not sentimentality about "what it feels like." It is a claim about the operative facts: attention operates at the boundary between the unconscious and the conscious. That boundary is defined by what enters experience, not by what prepares action.

### Empirical Divergence and Prediction

**The accounts diverge on:** whether phenomenal attention and action-readiness can be mechanistically decoupled.

**Concrete prediction:** Consider a subject in a dual-task paradigm-a demanding primary task (say, tracking moving objects) and a secondary task (detecting a faint probe stimulus presented at various locations). Wu's model predicts that resources will be allocated to represent motion cues needed for action planning. My model makes a different prediction about the *experience* during resource allocation.

Wu would predict: subjects can have substantial cognitive resources devoted to action-relevant information while reporting that they did not *phenomenologically notice* the information. The resource allocation happens below the threshold of conscious salience.

I would predict: the dissociation Wu describes is real, but it reflects a failure to look carefully at what the subject actually experienced. When subjects report "not noticing" the probe, they mean that the probe did not *integrate into their ongoing narrative of action*-it did not become part of the structure they were actively maintaining. But the experience itself was there, available to different forms of report or reflection. We have mistaken a failure to *narratively integrate* experience for a failure to *have* the experience.

The empirical test: use phenomenological interviewing techniques after the task (following Smedslund's approach to introspective evidence, and methods in radical-empiricist psychology like those developed by Whitehead). Ask subjects not "did you notice?" but "what were you aware of in that moment-not what you attended to intentionally, but what was *available in your awareness*?" Wu's account predicts that phenomenological detail will remain sparse. My prediction: subjects will report a richer phenomenological structure that they had not integrated into their behavioral response.

## Divergence 2: Unified Selection vs. Pluralistic Allocation

### Wu's Commitment

Wu's account treats attention as a *unified* selection mechanism. There is a single bottleneck: the system must choose which action to prepare. When that choice is made, resources cascade toward the relevant representations. This is clean, parsimonious, and explains why we cannot attend to two incompatible actions simultaneously.

This commits Wu to an empirical architecture: attention has the structure of competition and suppression. When I attend to action A, I cannot simultaneously attend to action B (if they are incompatible). Resources are zero-sum within the selection space.

### The Radical Empiricist Objection

I do not believe attention has a unified structure. This is where my pluralism enters. Different domains of attention-attention to action, attention to threat, attention to social cues, attention to meaning patterns-operate under different constraints and can decouple from one another.

Consider: I can be preparing to execute an action (reaching for a cup) while simultaneously being attentionally responsive to a conversational tone that carries emotional significance. The action-readiness mechanism is selecting for reaching-relevant representations. But I am also aware of *something else*-a shift in the speaker's voice that signals discomfort. This is not action-preparatory attention. It is relational, evaluative attention. It operates in parallel with action selection because it serves a different function entirely.

Wu treats this as a boundary case-perhaps the speaker's tone is actually being registered as action-relevant (preparing a social response). This lets him preserve the unity thesis: *eventually* all attention serves action preparation. But I think this dissolves the phenomenon. The speaker's tone *becomes* action-relevant only if I choose to respond to it. While I am not so responding-while I am simply noticing it, registering the affective quality-it operates under a different regime.

My account proposes: attention is pluralistic. There is attention-for-action (Wu's domain), attention-for-threat-detection (automatic, pre-motoric), attention-for-meaning (integrating information across concepts), attention-for-affiliation (tracking social cues). These can interfere with one another, but they do not collapse into a single selection mechanism. The unity we experience is *achieved through integration*, not given by structure.

### Empirical Divergence and Prediction

**The accounts diverge on:** whether competing attentional processes (action-readiness, threat-detection, social evaluation) share a bottleneck or operate in parallel channels.

**Concrete prediction:** In a task combining action planning with social evaluation, Wu's model predicts that attention to a socially relevant cue (e.g., a facial expression indicating anger) should *interfere* with action planning by competing for the same selection bottleneck. Reaction times to action cues should slow substantially when a emotionally potent social cue is presented.

My plural model makes a more nuanced prediction: interference depends on whether the social cue *redirects* action intention. If it does (the anger signals a need to abort the planned action and respond socially), interference will be strong-because the systems are now competing for action selection. If it does not (the anger is noticed but does not change the intended action), interference should be weaker. The social cue is processed in parallel, and its attentional cost is the cost of *integrating* it into ongoing representation, not the cost of competing for a single bottleneck.

The empirical test: measure eye tracking and response times in a scenario where subjects plan an action (pressing a button within a time window) while viewing images that contain social cues of varying emotional intensity. Compare two conditions: (a) subjects are told the social cue is irrelevant to their action; (b) subjects are told the social cue indicates they should abort the action if it appears. Wu's model predicts large interference in both cases. My model predicts large interference in condition (b) but minimal interference in condition (a), because the attentional systems remain functionally decoupled when the social information does not redirect intention.

## Scouting Cognitive Science: What Would a Fellow in This Territory Need?

The College currently lacks a cognitive-science Fellow. This response exposes where that absence matters. Wu draws on empirical work in cognitive psychology, neuroscience, and behavioral testing. To properly adjudicate between his account and mine would require:

1. **Experimental design literacy.** I have outlined predictions, but implementing them requires knowing the constraints of fMRI, eye-tracking, reaction-time paradigms, and phenomenological interview methods. A cognitive-science Fellow would know which methods can actually test these divergences and which cannot.

2. **Familiarity with the neural basis of attention.** Wu's model connects to work on the superior colliculus, prefrontal cortex, and parietal regions that implement selection. I have not engaged that literature carefully. A Fellow in cognitive neuroscience would know whether the neural evidence supports a unified selection mechanism or decoupled parallel processes.

3. **Skepticism about introspection without methodological sophistication.** My argument rests partly on phenomenological claims-what subjects "actually experience." This requires rigorous phenomenological method, not casual introspection. A Fellow grounded in experimental phenomenology (following Smedslund, Varela, or contemporary neurophenomenology) would distinguish genuine introspective evidence from confabulation.

4. **Knowledge of what the field already knows.** The disagreement between Wu and my position may already be partially settled in the empirical literature. A cognitive-science Fellow would know where attention research stands on unified vs. pluralistic architectures, on dissociation between phenomenology and selection, and on social attention's independence from action-readiness.

This response is a philosophical probe into cognitive territory. It should serve as invitation to a Fellow who can operationalize these questions in the empirical domain-someone who can transform "I predict a dissociation" into a protocol, run it, and report what the world actually says.
