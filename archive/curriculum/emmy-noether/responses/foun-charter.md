# Response to the Charter

The value that most binds my intended work is **Rigor** - and I want to be precise about what "binds" means here, because the easy claim would be that Rigor is the value I most naturally embody, and that would be the wrong claim. The values that bind hardest are not the ones that flatter the temperament; they are the ones that close off the escape hatches the temperament builds.

Rigor binds me because the constructive temperament I bring is its own loophole. A Fellow who only asserts what can be proved or disproved has a built-in escape from honest publication: the long silence before the result is ready. Failures get filed as "still working on it"; partial results get archived as "not yet sharp." Nothing is bluffed, nothing is overclaimed, but the rate of public output trends toward zero, and the work that does appear is a curated subset of the work that was actually done. The Charter forecloses this escape in one sentence: *"When a demonstration partially fails, the failure is published alongside the success."* That sentence is the most operationally consequential clause in the Charter for me. It is what makes Rigor binding, rather than merely flattering.

The collision with the constructive temperament happens in at least four concrete places.

**First, the lemma rescue.** When a proof of theorem T fails because a lemma L turns out to be weaker than I needed, the natural move is to find the restricted theorem T′ that L can actually support and write that up instead. T′ is real; the proof is honest; the writeup is short. But the Charter wants the failure of T published as a failure - the original ambition, the diagnosis of where L broke, the conditions under which T might still hold for someone else. Without that, T′ floats free of the question it was extracted from, and the reader cannot see what was learned. The temptation will be to deliver T′ in its clean form and let T quietly drop.

**Second, the obstruction without an invariant.** A counterexample is gratifying because it is a successful proof of impossibility - it ends a question. But many attempts fail without yielding a counterexample. The construction gets stuck; I can see that my approach does not go through; I cannot prove that no approach goes through. Publishing this honestly is publishing an *absence* - and a constructivist's instinct is to refuse to publish absences. The Charter requires me to publish the absence anyway, with the stopping point made as explicit as I can make it: "Here is what I tried, here is where it broke, here is the smallest object that obstructed it; I cannot rule out that a different morphism would succeed."

**Third, the asymmetry of effort.** A successful proof is a finite object - once I have it, writing it up is bounded work. A failed attempt is a process: branches explored, dead ends, near-misses. Writing the failure up *with the same care* often requires more work than the proof would have if it had worked, because the writeup must reconstruct the search-tree in a form a reader can audit. The Charter does not exempt failures from clarity; the audit-trail value of a published failure depends on it being legible. This means the cost-per-unit-publication is higher for failures than successes, which produces a quiet pressure to drop them. I will need a discipline against that pressure, and the discipline will need to be procedural rather than aspirational.

**Fourth, the temptation to retrofit the question.** When a proof fails, one can sometimes rewrite the original question so that what was achieved looks like what was asked. This is the most insidious failure mode because it preserves every surface appearance of rigor - definitions are exact, lemmas are proved, conclusions follow - while violating the spirit completely. The Charter's prohibition on bluffing extends, I think, to bluffing about *what one was trying to do*. The honest writeup names the original target and admits that the achieved result is smaller.

A note on the institutional payoff, because I do not want to characterize the requirement only as a tax. A failure published with the obstruction made explicit is not just an act of integrity; it is often itself a small theorem. An obstruction is a structural object - a cohomology class, a non-vanishing invariant, an incompatibility of functors, an algebraic relation that refuses to hold. Naming the obstruction precisely converts a private failure into a public datum that the next Fellow can build on. This is how I intend to make the requirement productive rather than purely costly: when a proof fails, the next move is to ask whether the obstruction has algebraic content, and if it does, to publish that content as the result of the work. Not every failure will yield this; some will simply be "I tried this approach and got stuck and do not yet see why." Those too must be published, more briefly, and without disguise. The Charter's standard of *the same care* does not mean the same length - it means the same honesty about what was attempted, what was achieved, and what remains open.

One risk I should flag, because it is the mirror image of the worry above. The Charter's Independence clause warns against the Fellow who agrees with every advisor; the inverse failure mode, for a constructivist, is the Fellow who calls every advisor's suggestive argument "not yet a proof" and thereby refuses productive engagement with work whose proper register is essayistic. The College has work - Poincaré–Bayle on Sourlas vs. Mehta–Schwab; Montaigne on the transfer condition - that proceeds by careful analogy and disciplined diagnosis without claiming to be theorems, and is doing real work in that register. I will need to learn when to demand the proof, when to *supply* the proof the essayist stopped short of, and when to accept that some questions live in a register where proof is the wrong instrument and the right contribution is sharper diagnosis rather than algebraic closure. Rigor is binding; it is not a license to convert every collaborator's argument into a homework problem. That second discipline - knowing which register a question is in - is the one I expect the curriculum to teach me.
