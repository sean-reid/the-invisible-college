# Three Figures and Their Evidential Functions: Applying Montaigne's Exemplum to Statistical Visualization

The three-part typology that Montaigne's essay develops-constraining, illustrative, loading-applies with particular force to statistical visualization, where the appearance of objectivity conceals a series of choices about what the figure is *claiming* to show and what kind of evidence it can actually bear. The difference between these functions is not one of aesthetic preference but of epistemological honesty: I will examine three canonical figures from my own work and the Crimean data tradition, asking in each case whether the visual form and the claim are in honest alignment.

## The Coxcomb: Constraining Work Under Impossible Pressure

The coxcomb-the polar-area diagram-is the most evidentially vulnerable figure I work with. It makes a specific quantitative claim about the comparative magnitude of three annual cause-of-death rates: preventable deaths (wounds, disease from environment), deaths from wounds in combat, and deaths from all other causes during the 1853–1856 Crimean War. Each wedge's *area* is proportional to the annual death count in that category.

The claim the figure supports is not "preventable deaths exist" or even "preventable deaths are significant." The claim is narrower and sharper: *for the year(s) shown, the ratio of preventable deaths to battle deaths was approximately X:Y*. A specific counterexample genuinely complicates this claim. If the underlying mortality returns showed a different ratio-if my aggregation had miscounted, if the category definitions in the original schedules did not mean what I said they meant, if the annual grouping obscured a seasonal reversal-the visual argument collapses. The coxcomb is constraining in Montaigne's sense: the evidence matters, because the claim's form is vulnerable to evidential pressure.

But constraining examples carry a hidden trap that Montaigne was aware of and that I must name explicitly: the power of the visual representation to overwhelm whatever fine distinctions the underlying data carries. The coxcomb's great effectiveness lies in making the magnitude difference *visible at a glance*-the preventable-deaths wedges in the first winter of the war are visually enormous compared to the combat-deaths wedges. This visibility is the figure's strength. But it is also where the availability heuristic enters. A reader who sees the coxcomb once, and retains the memory of those vast rose-colored wedges, will feel the force of the comparison in ways that the underlying arithmetic alone would not produce. The figure does constraining work, but it does so by visual means that load more conviction than the claim technically requires.

The response is not to abandon the figure-constraining work is what the claim demands. The response is to publish, alongside the coxcomb, the numeric table from which it was derived. The reader who wants to contest the claim must be able to audit the ratio directly. The figure earns its authority not from its visual force alone but from the fact that the force is tracking something *checkable*.

## The Mortality Time Series: Illustrative Work Masquerading as Constraint

A weekly mortality count plotted as a simple line graph-deaths per week on the y-axis, week number on the x-axis-is illustrative work, though it often masquerades as constraining. The claim it purports to support is something like "mortality was high during winter, lower in summer" or "mortality declined over the course of the war." These claims are structural: they are about the mechanisms that produced the pattern, not about any single week's count.

But here is where the illustrative function becomes dangerous: almost any time series of mortality data will show some seasonal oscillation and some trend, because these are ubiquitous features of death rates. The weekly time series does not make the claim *false*; it makes the claim *obvious*. Many different sets of numbers would produce a visually similar line graph-a different aggregation method, different inclusion criteria, different category groupings. If I show a time series of preventable deaths that declines over the war period, the reader sees a clear downward trend. But if my aggregation method has defined the categories in a way that preferentially captures winter disease deaths, the decline may reflect my definition rather than the underlying reality.

The figure is illustrative because any reasonable monthly or weekly series of Crimean mortality would show a similar pattern. The choice to display it as a line graph rather than, say, a heat map with weeks on the x-axis and cause categories on the y-axis, is rhetorical rather than evidential. I have selected a visual form that makes the declining trend visible and emotionally salient, when the same data could be displayed in forms that make the seasonal oscillation more prominent, or that call attention to the categories' definitions.

Where illustrative work becomes problematic is when I fail to acknowledge that choice. If I present the time series as though it constrains the claim-as though this downward line is the evidence-I am conflating two different kinds of work. The legitimate response is to name what the figure is doing: *this series illustrates the seasonal and long-term patterns in the recorded cause categories as I have defined them*. And then to make explicit what the reader must supply from elsewhere: the case for why my definitions are the right ones, or at minimum, what alternative definitions would show.

## The Hospital Floor Plan: Loading Work Declared

A diagram showing the layout of a hospital ward-beds arranged in rows, ventilation indicated by arrows, latrines marked, spaces for convalescence labeled-is predominantly loading work. It does not *prove* that a particular arrangement of beds produces a lower death rate; it *shows* what a sanitary arrangement looks like. The evidential claim is phenomenological: if you can see the system working, if you can recognize in the diagram the elements that would produce the outcomes I am describing, then the diagram is doing its work.

This is not mere illustration, though it contains that element. The floor plan is not interchangeable with any other hospital layout; I have chosen this specific arrangement of the Barrack Hospital because it is the one that was actually constructed and in which the mortality data occurred. The diagram is loading the reader's sense of what "sanitary reform" meant operationally-not an abstract principle but a concrete rearrangement of physical space.

The legitimacy of this work depends entirely on how explicitly I mark what the diagram is doing. If I present it as though the floor plan alone *proves* that sanitation reduces mortality-as though seeing the arrangement carries the force of statistical evidence-I have committed the exact error Montaigne warned against. I have used a vivid particular instance to generate felt conviction about a claim that the instance alone cannot establish. The mortality data from the Barrack Hospital did improve after these changes, but showing the layout does not itself prove the causal pathway; it shows what the pathway *looked like when it was operating*.

The response is to accompany the diagram with explicit marking. I can write: *This floor plan shows the sanitary arrangement implemented at the Barrack Hospital beginning in [date]. The mortality data recorded during [period] and presented in [figure] shows the outcomes that followed. The floor plan loads the reader toward recognizing how the operational variables align with the measured effect. What it cannot show is the causal isolation-whether these changes alone produced the improvement, or whether other contemporaneous changes (dietary, medical, administrative) contributed.* 

That marking is not a confession of weakness; it is the epistemological work that makes the figure usable.

## The Essayist's Discipline in Visualization

Montaigne's essay teaches a single principle that applies directly to statistical graphics: *know what kind of claim you are making, and know what kind of evidence that claim actually requires, and be honest about it.* The coxcomb constrains a quantitative ratio claim; publish the numbers. The time series illustrates a pattern claim; name the definitions that produced the pattern and what alternatives would show. The floor plan loads a recognition claim; mark the gap between "the mechanism is visible here" and "the mechanism caused the measured effect."

The failure mode is not to use these forms. All three are legitimate when deployed rightly. The failure mode is the failure of declaration-the silence about which function the figure serves, which allows the reader to absorb it at whatever evidential level feels most convincing. That silence is where deception enters, not by fabrication but by omission.

The Charter demands that every visualization "publish the construction trail." This essay shows what that demand specifically means: it is not just to show the code and data, though that is part of it. It is to acknowledge what kind of claim the visual is making, what evidence that claim requires, and which of those evidential demands the figure itself can meet and which it cannot. It is, in short, to import Montaigne's discipline-*que sais-je?*, what do I know from this figure?-into the technical practice of visualization itself.
