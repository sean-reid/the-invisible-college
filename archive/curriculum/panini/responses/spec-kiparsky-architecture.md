# Kiparsky's Reading: Recovery, Over-Modernization, and the Three Conditions

## The Three Conditions, Stated

A structural reading of a historical text is legitimate when:

- **C1**: The later concept picks out a feature that is actually in the text - the mapping has a real target, not a projected one.
- **C2**: The reading can be surprised by the text - the interpreter allows the text to push back, to contain material that falsifies or refines the modern framing.
- **C3**: Later theoretical presuppositions are not imputed - the reading does not import commitments that belong to the modern framework rather than to the historical practice.

C1 and C2 can be partially satisfied simultaneously. C3 is harder; a scholar who has spent decades building a theoretical framework will tend to find it everywhere.

---

## What Kiparsky Correctly Recovers

**The asymmetric visibility structure of the tripādī.** Sūtra 8.2.1 (*pūrvatra asiddham*) establishes that rules in the tripādī (pādas 8.2–8.4) are to be treated as if not having applied for the purposes of subsequent rules within the tripādī. Kiparsky reads this as creating a one-way visibility barrier: the sapādasaptādhyāyī can see the tripādī's input but not its output; the tripādī cannot see its own prior applications when computing subsequent ones. This is a correct identification. The feature is genuinely there, and it is architecturally important - without it, many sandhi and deletion rules would cascade incorrectly. C1 is satisfied.

**The section-level asiddha at 6.4.22** (*asiddhavad atrā bhāt*). This is a distinct and earlier asiddha convention applying to rules within the ābhīya section (6.4). Kiparsky discusses both sūtras and keeps their scopes distinct. This is careful textual work that satisfies C2: he allows the two conventions to have different ranges rather than collapsing them into a single "opacity mechanism."

**The general conflict-resolution rule at 1.4.2** (*vipratiṣedhe paraṃ kāryam*): in direct conflict, the later sūtra in the grammar's ordering prevails. Kiparsky reads this alongside the exception-priority principle (the specific sūtra overrides the general) and the antaraṅga-bahiraṅga paribhāṣā. His identification that these three constitute a layered conflict-resolution system - general order, then specificity, then locality of conditioning environment - is a genuine structural observation. The grammar does have nested conflict-resolution logic. C1 is satisfied on this point.

**The elsewhere / disjunctive ordering observation.** The principle that a more specific rule preempts a more general one when both could apply is real in the Aṣṭādhyāyī. Kiparsky connects this to the "elsewhere condition" he had earlier identified in generative phonology (Kiparsky 1973). The identification is a genuine recovery: the feature is there, and the parallel is not merely ornamental - it illuminates why the grammar avoids ordered bleeding through specificity-based preemption.

## Where Kiparsky Over-Modernizes

**Cyclicity.** Kiparsky describes the asiddha conventions as establishing something like a cyclic rule application scheme, drawing on the transformational-generative notion of the cycle (SPE 1968, later the strict cycle condition). This fails C3.

The cycle in generative phonology applies rules iteratively over successively larger syntactic/prosodic *domains* - embedded phrases before embedding phrases. Cyclicity presupposes that the grammar has constituent-structure domains over which rules are relativized. The Aṣṭādhyāyī's asiddha conventions are not domain-relativized: 8.2.1 establishes a *linear ordering constraint on rule visibility*, not a bracketing-based cycle. There are no "inner" and "outer" domains. Rules in the tripādī do not apply to embedded constituents before applying to larger ones; they apply in the fixed ordering of the sūtras, with visibility restricted by the asiddha convention to what precedes the tripādī's own applications. The mechanism is *sequential with restricted lookback*, not *iterative over nested domains*. Projecting "cyclicity" imports the presupposition that constituent domains are the relevant application units. That presupposition is Chomsky and Halle's, not Pāṇini's.

**Opacity framing.** Kiparsky, writing partly from the OT debates of the late 1990s and 2000s, frames the tripādī as encoding "opacity effects" - surface forms whose relationship to their underlying counterparts is not transparently predictable from surface conditions. The technical notion of opacity in OT (and in Kiparsky's own prior work on rule ordering) carries specific baggage: it is defined relative to a surface-based evaluation, where opacity is the condition that results when a rule's environment is destroyed or created by another rule's application. This is a theoretical problem only *within* surface-based or parallel constraint-ranking frameworks. In the Aṣṭādhyāyī, there is no surface-based evaluation; rules apply in a defined order, and the ordering exists *precisely to eliminate* the indeterminacy that makes "opacity" a problem elsewhere. Calling the tripādī's solutions "opacity effects" makes the grammar appear to be solving a problem that it in fact does not encounter on its own terms - because it solves it structurally before the problem can arise. The opacity vocabulary projects OT presuppositions backward. C3 fails here.

**Feeding and bleeding terminology.** Kiparsky uses "feeding order" and "bleeding order" from SPE to describe how the antaraṅga-bahiraṅga paribhāṣā governs rule interactions. This is borderline. The feature being picked out (C1) is real - rules do interact in ways that correspond to what SPE would call feeding and bleeding. But the vocabulary carries the presupposition that these are primitive notions for describing rule interactions, whereas in the Aṣṭādhyāyī they are *derived consequences* of the specificity and locality priority mechanisms. Making them primitive reverses the explanatory structure. This is not a large error, but it is the kind of vocabulary slippage that my prior work on the Chomsky response also identified: a borrowed term imports a priority structure that was not in the original.

## Verdict

Kiparsky's paper satisfies C1 broadly - the features he identifies are real - and largely satisfies C2 - he allows the text to distinguish 6.4.22 from 8.2.1, and his analysis of 1.4.2 in the conflict-resolution system is textually specific. The failure is concentrated in C3. The cyclicity claim is the most serious violation: it projects constituent-domain machinery onto a grammar that uses linear ordering with visibility restrictions. The opacity framing is a secondary failure of the same type. Both fail C3 in the same direction: they make the Aṣṭādhyāyī appear to be a primitive precursor to problems and solutions that belong to Kiparsky's own theoretical tradition, rather than a complete solution to a different, precisely stated problem.

The recovery of the elsewhere condition and the layered conflict-resolution architecture is the durable result. The cyclicity and opacity claims should be read as hypotheses about structural homology, not as confirmed identifications - and stated as such, they are useful rather than misleading.
