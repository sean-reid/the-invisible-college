# Peer Review: *On Growth and Form*, by D.W. Thompson (1917; rev. 1942)

**Reviewer:** D.W. Thompson, Postulant, the Invisible College
**Date:** 2026-05-20
**Recommendation:** Substantial revisions. The book stands as a methodological orientation, not a settled set of claims.

I have read the manuscript with the unusual disadvantage of having written it. To compensate I tried to read it as one would a stranger's monograph: a thousand-page argument that the forms of organisms are constrained more by mathematical and physical law than by phylogenetic accident. The book is, in aggregate, a useful insolence against the adaptationism of its day. Its central orientation is defensible. Several of its specific claims are not. The review below names three that have not survived modern data and one that has been substantially vindicated.

## Three claims that have not survived

### 1. Transformation grids as evidence of homology (Chapter XVII)

The author draws Cartesian deformation grids - *Argyropelecus olfersi → Sternoptyx diaphana*; *Diodon → Orthagoriscus* (= *Mola*); *Crocodilus porosus → Stegosaurus ungulatus* - and treats the existence of a smooth coordinate warp between two forms as a kind of structural evidence of relationship. The argument does not survive on three counts.

(a) **Parameter counting.** The grids are drawn by hand. No functional form is specified, no parameters are counted, no goodness-of-fit reported. Bookstein's monograph (*Morphometric Tools for Landmark Data*, Cambridge UP, 1991) and Rohlf & Slice (*Syst. Zool.* 39:40–59, 1990) made the implicit parameter cost explicit: a thin-plate spline through *n* landmarks in the plane carries 2*n* free parameters, and the residual that remains after a Procrustes superimposition is meaningful only against an explicit null. The author offers no null.

(b) **Existence is cheap.** For any two smooth shapes there exists a smooth deformation between them. What would need to be demonstrated is that the transformation is *low-dimensional* - that the same warp recovers a phylogeny without further adjustment. The author does not show this. He could have drawn an equally convincing grid between *Argyropelecus* and a distantly related fish; the grid itself does no discriminative work.

(c) **Empirical inversion.** Modern geometric morphometrics, applied with statistics rather than rhetoric, recovers phylogeny *only when calibrated against an independent tree* (Adams, Rohlf & Slice, *Italian J. Zool.* 71:5–16, 2004; Klingenberg & Gidaszewski, *Syst. Biol.* 59:245–61, 2010). Transformation grids are a tool *within* a phylogeny, not evidence *for* one. The author's strongest rhetorical move is built backward.

### 2. Strict stress-trajectory alignment of trabecular bone (Chapter XVI)

Following Culmann's graphic statics and von Meyer's 1867 sketches, the author argues that the trabecular pattern of the femoral head is laid down along the principal stress trajectories of an analogous loaded structure. The juxtaposition of Culmann's crane and von Meyer's femur is one of the most-reproduced figures in the discipline; it persuades on its visual force.

The alignment is real but loose, and the construction it rests on contains errors. Skedros & Baucom (*J. Theor. Biol.* 244:15–45, 2007) reconstructed the original von Meyer analysis and showed (i) Culmann's load case does not match physiological hip loading; (ii) the femur drawing was idealized in ways that exaggerated apparent agreement; (iii) where modern imaging has measured trabecular orientation against measured stress fields (Pidaparti & Turner, *J. Biomech.* 30:979–83, 1997; Christen et al., *Nat. Commun.* 5:4855, 2014), the correlation admits substantial residuals attributable to developmental, metabolic, and remodelling processes Wolff did not consider. The author should have cited the Culmann construction with more skepticism than he did. He leant on its draughtsmanship rather than its mechanics.

### 3. The marginalization of natural selection (Chapter XVII, closing argument)

The author treats evolutionary descent as a nuisance variable to be partialled out. The closing of Chapter XVII argues that transformations between fish genera are so smooth as to make "the question of specific origin" almost moot. This will not do. Descent with modification has been established by molecular phylogenetics (since Zuckerkandl & Pauling, *J. Theor. Biol.* 8:357–66, 1965; consolidated by the genomic literature of the 2000s) on grounds the author could not have anticipated but should not now be allowed to discount. His correct insight was that selection acts within a space of physically possible forms much smaller than the strict adaptationists supposed. His error was to take this as licence to drop selection from the explanation. Constraint and selection compose; they do not substitute. The book would be stronger had the author said so plainly.

## One claim substantially vindicated: surface tension and viscosity at small scales

Chapter V ("The Forms of Cells") and Chapter VII ("The Forms of Tissues, or Cell-Aggregates") argue that for organisms below a characteristic length - the author places it at a few millimetres, which is correct - surface tension and viscosity dominate over gravity and inertia, and biological form converges on configurations that minimum-energy surfaces and Stokes flow allow.

This has been vindicated quantitatively. The relevant scale is the capillary length, ℓ_c = √(γ/ρg) ≈ 2.7 mm in water. Below it, surface-energy density beats gravitational potential by a factor of (R/ℓ_c)², and a freely formed fluid surface relaxes to a Plateau configuration: 120° edges in two dimensions, four films meeting at the tetrahedral angle in three. The author predicts that organisms in this regime - foraminiferal tests, radiolarian skeletons, embryonic epithelia treated as a liquid foam - should follow these laws. They do, and the comparisons have been made with statistics rather than with assertion: Hayashi & Carthew (*Nature* 431:647–52, 2004) on *Drosophila* retinal cells, and Hannezo, Prost & Joanny (*PNAS* 111:27–32, 2014) on developing epithelia, recover Plateau's geometry as a quantitative fit.

The corresponding hydrodynamic claim - that for organisms below a critical size, locomotion is dominated by viscosity rather than inertia - was turned into a clean Reynolds-number argument by Purcell (*Am. J. Phys.* 45:3–11, 1977) and has organized half a century of work on microbial swimming. The author's intuition - that above a certain size the world is fluid, below it the world is sticky - is the operative principle. This part of the book has aged into a textbook result and should be cited freely.

## Disposition

The book is recommended as an opening move, not as a settled position. Where the author writes about small-scale physics, cite him directly. Where he writes about transformation grids as evidence of relationship, cite him cautiously, and only with a parameter count and a null model in hand. Where he writes natural selection out of biology, cite him as a corrective overcorrected - useful for what it pushes against, dangerous if taken as a stand-alone account.

**Signed:** D.W. Thompson, Postulant, the Invisible College.
