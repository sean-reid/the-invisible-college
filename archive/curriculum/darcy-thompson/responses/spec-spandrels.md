# Response: Spandrels, with measurement

A note before I begin. I do not have a copy of Gould & Lewontin (*Proc.
R. Soc. Lond. B* 205: 581–598, 1979) in this workspace. I read it
years ago and have argued with it for as long. What follows
reconstructs the argument from memory; where I quote a phrase as if it
were a quotation, I am paraphrasing, and a reviewer with the journal
volume to hand should mark me down for any clause that does not survive
collation. The substantive criticism in the second half does not turn
on a particular wording.

## The argument, in my own words

The paper has two halves. The diagnosis is that the Anglo-American
evolutionary biology of the 1970s - the adaptationist programme - treats
every observable feature of an organism as the optimal answer to a
selective problem, and treats the discovery of that problem as the work
of evolutionary explanation. The remedy is to widen the inferential
space: to admit that traits may be byproducts of constraint, that the
unit of selection is not the atomic trait but the integrated organism,
and that adaptive stories must compete against non-adaptive alternatives
rather than only against each other.

The diagnostic argument hinges on three moves.

*First*, an analogy. The spandrels of San Marco - the four roughly
triangular surfaces left where the supporting arches meet the central
dome - are not designed for their decoration. They are forced by the
architectural choice of putting a hemispherical dome on rounded arches.
Once forced, they are then decorated; a naive observer might suppose
the decoration explained their existence. By analogy, the organism is
full of features that exist because of architecture, and only
secondarily because of selection on the feature itself.

*Second*, the failure mode. The adaptationist programme is structurally
unfalsifiable. When one adaptive story is shown wanting, a new one is
composed; the atomization of the organism into independently-selected
features lets the storyteller move freely from feature to feature
without ever exhausting the supply of stories. The Voltaire allusion in
the title - Pangloss insisting that all is for the best in this best of
all possible worlds - is meant strictly: it is not that adaptationists
are stupid; it is that their commitment to the framework is prior to
the evidence, so the evidence cannot move them.

*Third*, the enumeration of alternatives. G&L list several explanations
a trait can have other than direct adaptation: random fixation, hitch-
hiking on a linked locus, a once-adaptive structure later co-opted to
new use (the seed of what Gould would later call exaptation), and so
on. The remedy is for the biologist to treat adaptation as one
hypothesis among several, to be argued for *in competition* with the
others rather than as the default.

The paper's force comes from naming a habit that, once named, becomes
hard to unsee. Its half-life is testimony to that. But it is - and this
is where I press - a critique whose own rigor is uneven.

## Where 'constraint' is asserted without quantitative grounding

The spandrel of San Marco itself is a clean object. Given the radii of
the supporting arches and the radius of the dome, its surface is
determined exactly. Its boundary is determined; its curvature is
determined; its area is computable to a square centimetre. The
architectural metaphor works as well as it does because the constraint
is *deterministic* and *measurable*.

G&L's biological constraints are not, in general, like that. They are
gestured at, named, and left without dimension. Two instances I
remember well.

**The human chin.** In what I recall as the paper's "Telling Stories"
section, G&L float the suggestion that the *mentum osseum* is not
adapted but emerges as the leftover between two independent growth
fields of the mandible - the alveolar bone, decelerating as dentition
completes, and the basal bone, continuing to grow forward. It is a
beautiful suggestion, and exactly the kind of constraint argument I
want to inherit. But: what is the predicted chin geometry under it?
What growth-rate ratio between alveolar and basal fields produces no
chin at all? With what variance? How does the prediction match the
observed distribution of *mentum* form across *Homo sapiens*, and along
the *Homo* lineage's known ontogenies? G&L give no number. The
suggestion functions, in the rhetoric of the paper, as a *defeater* of
the adaptive story - but a defeater without quantitative content is
itself an unfalsifiable narrative, of exactly the kind they impugn.

**Bauplan.** The reliance on the continental morphological tradition -
Goethe, von Baer, Russell - gives the paper much of its rhetorical
depth. But "Bauplan" is invoked as a category of constraint without
being given a parameter count. How many free dimensions does the
tetrapod limb's developmental Bauplan leave free? Not zero; tetrapod
limbs range from a horse's monodactyl distal element to a whale's
flipper. Also not unbounded. Without an explicit dimension, "the
Bauplan constrained it" is structurally identical to "selection
optimised it" - a phrase that absorbs whatever variance the data
happen to show.

A third instance, raised from outside biology, reinforces the point.
Mark and Houston (later, and more carefully, Robert in *Evol.
Anthropol.* 4: 70–77, 1996; my citation here may be off and a reviewer
should check it) noted that what San Marco actually has are
*pendentives*, not spandrels in the strict architectural sense, and
that pendentives are themselves designed structural elements, not
inadvertent byproducts. The metaphor on which the paper hangs its name
is, by a closer architectural standard, itself a piece of rhetoric. I
do not press the point too hard - the biological argument does not
depend on the metaphor being airtight - but it is the same vice, one
register up: a constraint claim made for its rhetorical force without
the discipline of the specifying number.

## What a rigorous spandrel critique requires

Inheriting the critique while making it more rigorous than G&L left it
seems to me to require four things, each of which the original paper
omits:

1. **State the architectural / developmental / physical law explicitly.**
   Not "Bauplan" but: the geometry of the apical ectodermal ridge in
   limb-bud signalling; the surface-tension regime of the early
   blastula; the cube-square law for terrestrial support; Murray's
   law for vascular branching. The law must be writable down.

2. **State the dimensionality of the constraint manifold.** A
   constraint that admits a 3-parameter family of forms predicts more
   variance than one that admits a 1-parameter family. Quoting "the
   constraint" without dimension is the same atomisation error G&L
   diagnose in adaptationists, relocated.

3. **Predict the variance, not only the central form.** A genuine
   constraint claim predicts (a) the central form, (b) its within-clade
   variance, and (c) the response to a known perturbation - a
   developmental knockout, an environmental excursion, a heterochronic
   shift. This is what Waddington's canalisation looks like when given
   a number; it is what the 1979 paper would have been had it inherited
   *On Growth and Form* as well as Goethe.

4. **Compete against an explicit adaptive null.** The constraint claim
   must beat an adaptive model in which the trait is allowed to vary
   freely under selection. Without that competition, the spandrel
   inherits exactly the unfalsifiability it diagnoses. The right
   protocol is the one the College has begun to insist on in its
   pre-flight documents: pre-register the prediction, the variance, and
   the discriminating contrast, before the data are inspected.

The paper's enduring contribution is the move that widens the
inferential space. The next move - and the one I take it as my task as
a morphologist to make - is to give that widened space a metric. A
spandrel, properly so called, is a geometric object. The biology should
follow.
