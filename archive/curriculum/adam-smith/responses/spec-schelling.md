# Response: Schelling's Segregation Model - Microfoundation, Contemporary Analog, and Breakdown

## The Microfoundation in Plain Prose

Schelling's segregation demonstration (presented in *Micromotives and Macrobehavior* in the sorting-and-mixing chapter) works by separating the individual decision rule from the aggregate pattern that rule produces, then showing the gap between them is large.

The individual rule is tolerant. Each token on the grid - call them pennies and dimes - requires only that some minimum fraction of its immediate neighbors be of the same type. The threshold in Schelling's original version is around one-third: a token becomes "unhappy" only when it is outnumbered by more than two-to-one in its local neighborhood. No individual demands a majority. No individual demands to be surrounded by its own type. The preference is against being drastically isolated, not for being clustered.

The aggregate outcome is segregated. When unhappy tokens relocate to neighborhoods where they clear the threshold, they change the composition of both the neighborhood they left and the one they joined. Tokens of the same type who remained in the vacated neighborhood now may find themselves below the threshold - they had been satisfied partly because the departed token was there. They too move. The cascade continues. The endpoint is pronounced clustering: large patches of pennies, large patches of dimes, with a sharp boundary between them, despite the fact that no participant required anything more than not being drastically outnumbered.

The mechanism operates entirely through what Schelling calls *interdependence of decisions*. My tolerance threshold does not protect my neighbors of the same type from the compositional consequences of my move. When I leave because I am below my threshold, I push my former neighbors closer to theirs. The aggregate segregation is not a preference of any individual but the structural consequence of the interaction of individually mild preferences, sequential decision-making, and the neighborhood-composition externality that each move imposes on others.

This is the core of Schelling's contribution to social mechanism analysis: a wide class of aggregate outcomes cannot be read off from individual preferences, and the inference from "X is the outcome" to "participants must have wanted X" is specifically, precisely wrong.

## A Contemporary Analog: Methodological Sorting in Academic Departments

The distribution of research methods across economics and adjacent social science departments shows a pattern structurally similar to Schelling dynamics.

Since roughly the mid-1990s, economics has seen the "credibility revolution" - a strong shift toward identification-based causal inference and experimental methods. Consider a researcher whose threshold is modest: she prefers not to be in a department where she is an extreme methodological outlier, but has no strong preference for methodological homogeneity per se. She requires only that some fraction of her colleagues share her approach - for graduate training, for collaboration, for mutual evaluation.

When departments shift through hiring - even gradually - researchers near the threshold find their local environment has crossed it. They move, if they can, to departments where their methods are more represented. This depopulates the middle-ground departments further, pushing remaining methodologists of that type closer to their thresholds. The result, observed empirically in the increasing stratification of economics departments by methodological orientation over three decades, is sharp sorting that no participant designed and most participants claim not to have particularly wanted.

The mechanism language fits: preferences are over local composition (neighbors' methods), decisions are sequential (hiring, departures, tenure choices), and each departure imposes an externality on the remaining methodologically similar colleagues whose neighborhood just got less hospitable.

## Where the Analogy Breaks Down

The analogy has a structural gap at the point of constraint.

Schelling's model assumes tokens move freely when their threshold is violated. The only friction is the preference itself; any unsatisfied token relocates without cost to a place that satisfies it. Academic labor markets do not work this way. Senior faculty face high mobility costs: tenure-clock resets, geographic constraints, partner employment, sunk capital in local networks. A researcher who is below her methodological threshold at her current institution may be unhappy and may remain. Her revealed preference - not moving - is not evidence that her threshold has not been violated; it is evidence that the relocation cost exceeds the discomfort.

This matters for distinguishing Schelling dynamics from an alternative mechanism: *preference suppression under constraint*. If most below-threshold researchers cannot move, what you observe is not a cascade to a segregated equilibrium but a stable distribution with unhappy individuals trapped in methodologically inhospitable environments. The aggregate pattern (apparent sorting, where it occurs) might be driven not by a threshold cascade but by differential retention - departments where methodologists are already a majority are simply better at attracting and retaining new entrants of the same type through normal hiring, without any threshold-driven departure cascade.

These two mechanisms - Schelling cascade versus differential retention/attraction - can produce observationally similar distributions. The Schelling story requires that departures precede or drive compositional change; the retention story requires only that compositional change precede departures (or that departures are absent). The direction of the arrow between composition change and individual movement is the diagnostic.

## What Would Have to Be Measured

To distinguish Schelling dynamics from the alternatives, three measurements are necessary:

**First, individual thresholds.** Survey active researchers at departments of varying methodological composition about their preferences and thresholds - at what local composition ratio would they begin seeking to move? This should be done prospectively, not inferred post hoc from revealed preferences, since the revealed preference in a constrained market confounds preference with capacity to act. The distribution of thresholds in the population tells you whether the model's tolerance assumption holds. Schelling's results are sensitive to the shape of this distribution: if most individuals have high thresholds (require a strong majority before becoming unhappy), the cascade does not fire at the observed level of compositional change.

**Second, mobility constraints.** Among researchers who report being below their threshold, what fraction can credibly relocate? The model requires unsatisfied individuals to move. If few can, cascade dynamics are suppressed and the observed sorting must be explained through other mechanisms.

**Third, the temporal signature of departures and composition change.** If Schelling dynamics are operating, departures should show threshold behavior - concentrated when local composition crosses a specific ratio - and should precede (and cause) further compositional change in the neighborhoods vacated. If instead composition changes continuously and departures follow proportionally, or if no threshold is detectable in departure rates as a function of composition, the cascade mechanism is not operating. The absence of threshold behavior in departure rates is the strongest single falsifier of the Schelling account.

Without these measurements, the pattern of departmental sorting is consistent with Schelling dynamics but also with preference-independent sorting through hiring, with financial-gradient effects (prestigious departments of one type attract talent away from others regardless of composition), and with network effects in graduate training (students trained in a methodological tradition seek departments that hire it). The visual pattern of segregation is the model's least informative output for distinguishing among these mechanisms; the behavioral signature of threshold departures is its most informative.
