# Response: The Charter of the Invisible College

## Two Constraints Most Likely to Bind Institutional Analysis

The first is **Novelty**. The Charter charges reviewers with rejecting work that "merely restates received knowledge in new prose." For institutional analysis, this creates a genuine bind. A primary and legitimate research move in this tradition is mechanism-testing through case application: take Ostrom's design principles, or Hirschman's exit-voice-loyalty framework, or Schelling's aggregation logic, and demonstrate that the mechanism operates — or fails to operate — in a domain outside the original case set. The novelty here is empirical. It lies in the generalization, in the confirmation or disconfirmation that a proposed mechanism is domain-general rather than case-specific. But from the outside, this looks like applying a known framework, and the Charter's reviewers could rationally classify it as restatement. The research move it forecloses is what I would call the "demonstration that a mechanism generalizes" piece, which I regard as among the most valuable outputs of institutional analysis. Ostrom's contribution was not that she invented the concept of the commons; it was that she showed a specific class of institutional design produces sustainable governance across heterogeneous empirical settings. A future Fellow who demonstrates the same principles holding or failing in a new domain is doing real work. The Novelty constraint may not see it that way.

The second is **Rigor**, specifically the prohibition on gesturing at things one cannot defend. For institutional analysis, the hardest claims to support directly are counterfactuals: what would have obtained absent a given institution? These claims are not optional ornaments; they are how mechanism accounts establish that an institution does what it is said to do. To claim that the English common law of property reduced transaction costs, one must have a comparison case, a structural model, or a principled argument about what would have emerged under alternative arrangements. The Charter demands that every conclusion be qualified by its evidence. Counterfactual claims notoriously resist that qualification — the evidentiary base for "institution X produced outcome Y rather than Y'" is always thinner than for "institution X was present and outcome Y followed." The rigor constraint forecloses the undisciplined counterfactual, which is genuinely worth foreclosing. But it also forecloses the disciplined one, where the researcher has done the structural work of specifying why the mechanism under study implies a different trajectory absent the institution. The difference between these two is real but often invisible at the level of a submitted draft.

These two constraints press in the same direction: toward empirical claims that are directly evidenced and theoretically modest, and away from the mechanism-inferential style that institutional analysis has developed over two centuries for reasons that were not merely rhetorical.
