## The Eight Design Principles, Restated

Ostrom derived her principles inductively from a range of durable commons institutions - the Swiss *Allmend*, Japanese village forests, Spanish and Philippine irrigation systems - and from the contrast with commons that had collapsed. The principles describe what these successful institutions had in common, not a utopian design template.

**1. Defined boundaries.** Who has rights to the resource, and what the resource system itself encompasses, are both clearly specified. Without boundary definition, monitoring and exclusion are impossible; with ambiguous membership, any individual can claim entitlements she has not contributed to maintaining.

**2. Congruence with local conditions.** The rules governing use are matched to the specific characteristics of the resource and community. A rule optimal for a mountain pasture does not transfer intact to an inshore fishery. Institutions that import generic rules from outside fail to track the actual structure of the problem.

**3. Collective choice arrangements.** Those affected by the rules can participate in modifying them. Ostrom's emphasis here is operational: users who are governed by rules they had no hand in forming have weaker incentives to comply, weaker information channels through which problems surface, and no legitimate standing to propose improvements.

**4. Monitoring.** Someone must observe both the resource condition and the behavior of users, and that someone must be accountable to users or be a user herself. Monitoring that is external to the community and serves someone else's interests produces different behavior than monitoring by a co-user who will bear the consequences of cheating.

**5. Graduated sanctions.** Violations receive proportionate responses - light for first offenses, severe for persistent ones. This structure achieves two things simultaneously: it maintains legitimacy (a draconian response to an inadvertent first breach undermines the institution) while establishing that repeated violation is costly.

**6. Conflict-resolution mechanisms.** Users have access to low-cost arenas for resolving disputes - about allocation, about enforcement, about rule interpretation. Without this, disputes escalate, enforcement is contested, and the institution fractures along lines of unresolved grievance.

**7. Minimal recognition of rights to organize.** External governmental authorities do not challenge the institution's legitimacy. This is a condition of possibility, not an active design feature: its absence does not require active undermining, only the failure of external recognition to exist.

**8. Nested enterprises.** For systems at scale, governance is organized in multiple layers: local rules nest inside regional frameworks nest inside national or international regimes. Each level handles the problems appropriate to its scale; the institution is not a single flat layer trying to manage everything.

The unifying logic across these eight is that **sustainable commons governance requires that each user's incentives be structured so that contribution to the commons is individually rational**. The principles are the structural conditions under which that alignment is achievable. Ostrom's contribution was to show that this alignment does not require privatization or state command - that communities, under specifiable conditions, solve the problem themselves.

## The Antibiotic Commons: A Case of Visible Failure

Antibiotics are a commons in the precise sense that Ostrom's framework addresses. The resource is antibiotic *effectiveness* - the capacity of particular compounds to kill or inhibit bacterial pathogens. That capacity is a shared pool: each use of an antibiotic creates selection pressure for resistance, depleting the pool fractionally. The depletion is irreversible on any human-policy timescale, is distributed across all current and future users of that compound class, and is not captured by the user who caused it. This is Hardin's structure exactly, with the additional complication that the resource is global and the commons has hundreds of millions of simultaneous users.

Ostrom did not analyze antibiotic stewardship in 1990 - the problem had not yet achieved its current urgency, and the governance institutions that exist (or fail to exist) were not yet visible. The case falls squarely outside her empirical base. Against her design principles, the failure pattern is specific and traceable.

**Boundaries are absent.** The resource system - global antibiotic effectiveness - has no boundary. Who counts as a legitimate user is undefined, which means exclusion is impossible and the logic of the rule "use antibiotics only when necessary" cannot be enforced at the commons level. National jurisdictions create partial sub-commons, but the boundaries are permeable: resistant strains travel; medical tourists bypass tighter regimes; agricultural use in one country depletes effectiveness for clinical use in another.

**Rules are not congruent with local conditions.** Antibiotic use patterns differ radically across communities - surgical prophylaxis in high-income health systems, over-the-counter self-treatment in low-income ones, subtherapeutic growth promotion in industrial livestock operations. The governance response has been largely to apply uniform international recommendations (the WHO's Essential Medicines list, the Global Action Plan on Antimicrobial Resistance) that do not track the actual structure of overuse in each setting. A veterinary farmer in a low-income country operating on minimal margins and without prescription access to diagnostics is not in the same structural position as a teaching hospital in a high-income country with an infection control committee. Rules that treat these as the same governance problem fail both.

**Collective choice arrangements are vestigial.** The users of the antibiotic commons - patients, physicians, veterinarians, agricultural producers - do not participate in formulating the rules that govern their use. Those rules descend from regulatory bodies, national health authorities, and international organizations. The predictable consequence is low compliance, poor information flow about which rules create friction, and no legitimate standing for users to propose workable alternatives. Physicians in clinical practice could probably identify which antibiotic stewardship rules fit their case mix and which produce perverse responses (e.g., switching to broad-spectrum agents when narrow-spectrum ones are restricted); they are not in the relevant governance structure.

**Monitoring fails at scale.** Where antibiotic use is monitored, it is monitored badly. In many settings, it is not monitored at all. The misalignment Ostrom identifies is visible here: monitoring is conducted by regulatory bodies external to the user communities, serving compliance rather than resource-condition purposes. A physician who overprescribes in a setting without prescribing data infrastructure creates no legible signal; the resistance her prescribing generates surfaces as a global epidemiological trend, not as feedback that reaches her.

**Graduated sanctions are structurally impossible.** To sanction an individual user proportionately, you need to observe the infraction and attribute the commons cost to it. Both conditions fail. Individual prescribing decisions are rarely monitored, and the marginal resistance generated by a single course of antibiotics is unmeasurably small - the harm is statistical and distributed. Sanctions require identifiable, attributable violations; this commons has neither.

**Conflict resolution, rights to organize, and nested enterprises** are all at best embryonic. Some national stewardship programs have created nested structures - hospital committees inside national formulary systems inside WHO frameworks - but the nesting is incomplete and the lower levels have weak feedback channels into the upper ones.

## The Microfoundational Mechanism

Ostrom's analysis is comparative statics: it identifies which structural features separate success from failure, but the mechanism is implicit. To make it explicit: the success of a commons institution depends on each user's *individually rational choice* converging on the collectively sustainable outcome. That convergence requires that each user has: (1) accurate information about the resource condition, (2) expectations that others are also complying, (3) certainty that defection will be observed and sanctioned, and (4) access to the institution's rule-making process as a safety valve when conditions change.

In antibiotic stewardship, all four conditions fail simultaneously. No individual prescriber has direct information about global resistance trajectories. No individual prescriber can form reliable expectations about others' compliance - the commons has too many users in too many uncoordinated settings. Defection (over-prescription) is rarely observed and almost never sanctioned. And the rule-making process is wholly external to the users whose micro-level decisions determine the resource condition.

The result follows mechanically: individually rational prescribing systematically depletes the commons. This is not a moral failure of physicians or patients; it is an institutional structure that makes individual rationality and collective sustainability incommensurable. Ostrom's contribution is to have shown, in positive cases, that the two can be aligned. Her case set also implies that the failure cases are not accidents but predictable consequences of the absence of specific structural features.

The antibiotic commons has none of the features that alignment requires. The question that follows - whether those features can be built, and at what level - is not answered by the design principles alone. But the diagnosis is clear, and its precision is the value of working through Ostrom's framework rather than settling for the vague prescription that "more coordination is needed."
