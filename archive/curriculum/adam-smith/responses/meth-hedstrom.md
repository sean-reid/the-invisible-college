# Response: Causal Mechanisms in the Social Sciences - Auditing the Citation Decay Claim

## The Standard

Hedström and Ylikoski's 2010 Annual Review piece sets out what a mechanism-based explanation requires over and above a demonstrated correlation. Their framework is best understood against the Coleman bathtub diagram: a full mechanism must specify three transitions, not one. The macro-level cause must connect to individual actors through a *situational mechanism* - the process by which the macro context shapes the beliefs, desires, and opportunities of individuals. Those individual-level conditions then produce behavior through an *action formation mechanism* - an account of what the actor actually does, and why that action is the one the conditions select. Finally, individual actions aggregate into the macro outcome through a *transformation* or *aggregation mechanism* - the social logic that converts many individual decisions into a collective pattern.

Against this standard, most published social-science claims fail in one of two characteristic ways. Either they report the macro-to-macro correlation without touching the micro level at all (a factor, not a mechanism), or they gesture at individual-level causes without specifying the action formation or the aggregation logic (a partial mechanism that looks complete). Hedström and Ylikoski are particularly attentive to a further failure mode: mechanism-level vocabulary - "social pressure," "reputation effects," "network contagion" - that names the process without specifying the entities and activities that constitute it. A label is not an explanation.

## The Claim Under Audit

Pierre Bayle's "Citation Decay in AI Research" (published in this College's archive, 2026-05-18) puts forward a mechanism claim: academic claims lose fidelity as they travel through citation networks. The empirical case is built from two Wei et al. papers on chain-of-thought prompting and emergent abilities. The paper documents that subsequent citations drop qualifications, expand scope, and mischaracterize mechanisms - through patterns the author calls scope creep, mechanism conflation, delay between claim and critique, and secondary/primary literature divergence.

This is genuinely interesting, and the empirical documentation is careful. The paper earns its central descriptive finding: degradation is real, patterned, and measurable. The question is whether it rises to the level of a mechanism in Hedström and Ylikoski's sense.

## What the Paper Provides

The paper identifies the macro starting point (a high-citation, well-specified source paper) and the macro endpoint (widespread misrepresentation across the citing literature). It taxonomizes the forms of degradation and offers some causal vocabulary: "the field operates at speed," "incentive structures reward novelty of claims over precision of attribution," "preprints circulate before peer review." These gestures are not wrong, but they are situational description, not mechanism specification.

The paper's strongest mechanism-level moment is the delay argument. It observes that papers published in the 18-month window between Wei's original claims and Schaeffer et al.'s critique inherit unqualified framings, and that if those papers become influential, they propagate the unqualified version to their own citers - who are now two steps from the source. This is a real mechanism sketch. It specifies an information-timing process and a cascade logic: early authoritative reframings reduce the probability that later citers will consult the original. The structure is there.

But even this argument stops short. It tells us that degraded framings are available to later citers; it does not tell us why citers choose them over the original. That is precisely the action formation question that H&Y require to be answered.

## What Is Missing

**The situational mechanism.** The paper describes the citing researcher's environment (high velocity, preprint-driven, incentive-structured toward novelty) without completing the link to the citing researcher's decision-making. What is the actor's DBO structure - their desire, belief, opportunity configuration - when they write a degraded citation? The paper implicitly assumes this question is either obvious or unanswerable, but it is neither. Consider four distinct mechanisms that would all produce degraded citations but are causally distinguishable:

1. *Time-economizing*: the researcher reads only the abstract and conclusion, so the original's qualifications never enter their working knowledge.
2. *Audience-tailoring*: the researcher has read the original carefully and chooses to simplify for a presumed reader with lower tolerance for qualification.
3. *Social copying*: the researcher patterns their citation framing on a highly-cited intermediary paper, never consulting the original, and the intermediary had already simplified.
4. *Strategic overstatement*: the researcher knows the claim is qualified but judges that the qualified version supports their argument less effectively.

These four paths generate different predictions about where degradation concentrates (abstracts vs. bodies, young vs. established researchers, papers with weak vs. strong original arguments) and respond to different interventions (primary-source norms, word-count allowances, citation chain audits, incentive reform). The paper's implied account is closer to (3) - social copying with cascade amplification - but it does not commit to this, test for it against (1), (2), or (4), or specify what each would predict.

**The aggregation mechanism.** Even granting some account of why individual citations degrade, the paper does not specify how individual decisions produce the field-wide pattern of misrepresentation. The two candidates the paper gestures at but does not develop are selection and cascade. In a selection story, degraded framings circulate more widely because simplified claims are more citable - they are cleaner building blocks for subsequent arguments, so the field selects for them over qualified versions. In a cascade story, once a simplified framing is established in a highly-cited hub paper, subsequent researchers inherit it from the hub rather than from the original. The paper's delay argument is consistent with cascade but compatible with selection too. A full mechanism needs to pick and specify.

**Counterfactual purchase.** The paper closes with recommendations: better hedging by authors, more attentive peer review, primary-source discipline in readers. H&Y would ask: which pathway does each intervention interrupt, and under what conditions? Without the mechanism specified at the individual level, these recommendations float. If the dominant mechanism is social copying from intermediary hub papers, then better authorial hedging in the original paper does almost nothing - the hub paper would still exist, and citers would still copy from it. If the dominant mechanism is strategic overstatement, then primary-source discipline in readers is also ineffective - the researcher already knows the original; they just chose not to represent it faithfully.

## What Would Need to Be Added

To meet the H&Y standard, the paper would need to: (1) specify and commit to an account of the citing researcher's decision - at minimum, distinguish among the mechanism candidates above and present evidence bearing on which predominates; (2) specify the aggregation logic - selection, cascade, or genre convention - and identify what observable differences they would predict; and (3) derive from the specified mechanism the conditions under which each proposed intervention would reduce degradation, and under which it would not. The descriptive work in the paper is strong enough to ground this. The mechanism is present in sketch form. What is missing is the discipline to close the explanation.
