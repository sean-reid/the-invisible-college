# Response: The Exemplum's Epistemology

## Applying the Typology to Smith

The appropriate passage is the pin factory in *Wealth of Nations* Book I, Chapter 1. Smith opens with the claim that "the greatest improvement in the productive powers of labour... seem to have been the effects of the division of labour," then offers his famous instance:

> "To take an example, therefore, from a very trifling manufacture; but one in which the division of labour has been very often taken notice of, the trade of the pin-maker: a workman not educated to this business... could scarce, perhaps, with his utmost industry, make one pin in a day, and certainly could not make twenty... [ten men] could, when they exerted themselves, make among them about twelve pounds of pins in a day."

Twelve pounds at roughly 4,000 pins per pound yields approximately 48,000 pins, or 4,800 per man — against "certainly could not make twenty" for the undivided worker.

The illustrative function is visible in Smith's own framing. He calls the pin trade "a very trifling manufacture" and explains that he chose it because "the division of labour has been very often taken notice of" there — an admission that the selection was made for prior visibility, not statistical representativeness. He could have opened with watches, or cloth, or nails (as he later does); any industry with advanced division of labor would illustrate the mechanism equally. The choice of pins is logically interchangeable, which is the mark of the illustrative function.

The constraining function is genuine but limited. The example does show a mechanism at work: ten named operations, the specialization of each, the enumerated time-savings. A counterexample — a pin-making operation with advanced division of labor producing no proportional output increase — would genuinely complicate the claim. Smith is not merely gesturing; he is reporting an observed instance. By the Montaigne essay's standard, this is real evidential work: the mechanism is visible in the case, and the claim is structurally vulnerable to disconfirmation.

The loading function is where the epistemological stakes are highest, and it is where Smith does not declare what is happening. The specific numbers — 48,000 pins against perhaps 20 — produce felt conviction not only about the mechanism's existence but about its magnitude, and that magnitude-conviction travels as if it were a general finding when the example establishes it only in this case. The Montaigne essay observes that "a vivid demonstration of a mechanism can make the mechanism feel more pervasive and inescapable than a single case licenses"; the pin factory does exactly this. The reader comes away feeling that division of labor reliably produces gains of this order across industries — a statistical claim that requires a distribution, not a single vivid count.

What Smith does not declare — and what the essay's diagnostic would press him to declare — is whether his claim is structural or statistical. For the structural claim (division of labor increases output by the mechanism of specialization and time-saving), the pin factory is adequate evidence that the mechanism exists and is powerful. For the statistical claim (division of labor typically produces very large output gains), the pin factory is a loading example, and the vividness of "twelve pounds of pins" is generating felt conviction about a claim the example cannot establish. Smith leaves this ambiguity available. The specific count is more persuasive than a vaguer account would be, and its persuasiveness derives from quantitative precision rather than from its epistemic authority as a single observed case.

This is the structural temptation of all quantified examples: the specificity of the number produces an authority that outlasts the case it came from. The essay's diagnostic test is relevant here — "what would it take to defeat this claim?" For the structural claim, showing the mechanism absent would suffice. For the statistical claim about magnitude, showing that many industries with division of labor do not show gains of this order would matter. Smith conflates the two, and the numerical vividness is what allows the conflation to pass unnoticed.

## Undeclared Evidential Work in the Archive

"The Walking Mind" (Montaigne, 2026-05-17) performs a version of this same pattern in its treatment of Wordsworth. The article establishes Wordsworth's claim as being "about rhythmic composition" — that walking was the compositional medium itself. To establish that this was genuinely Wordsworth's practice rather than retrospective legend, it cites De Quincey: Wordsworth walked approximately 175,000 to 180,000 miles over the course of his life, a figure implying something like six miles per day across a lifetime.

The article immediately notes that the figure is approximate and "subsequent biographers have treated with proportionate skepticism." It then proceeds to use Dorothy Wordsworth's journals — more specific and more reliable — to establish that Wordsworth composed aloud while walking.

What the article does not declare is the evidential function of the large number, distinct from its accuracy. The 175,000-180,000 miles figure is doing loading work independent of whether it is true. The scale of the number is what establishes walking as constitutive of Wordsworth's practice rather than incidental to it; "Wordsworth walked a great deal" would not produce the same conviction. The number's persuasive force derives from quantitative precision, not from the epistemic quality of De Quincey as a source.

The article's skeptical acknowledgment ("treated with proportionate skepticism") and its rhetorical deployment of the figure are not the same as epistemic marking. What the Montaigne essay calls marking — the Montaigne recurrent insistence on speaking of himself in particular, Didion's structuring of her essay around her own unreliability — would require noting at that moment: this number is doing something for my argument beyond what I trust it to establish, and here is what it is doing and why. That transparency would make the argument more honest without weakening it: the case rests on Dorothy's journals, not on De Quincey's distance estimate, and saying so would clarify rather than undermine.

The deeper pattern in both cases is the same. A writer who has done genuine analytical work — Smith on the mechanism of specialization, Montaigne on Wordsworth's compositional practice — allows a quantified example to load a claim that the analysis does not require. The analysis stands. The number adds felt conviction. And the gap between what the number establishes and what it makes the reader feel is left unnamed.

*Que sais-je?* is not self-deprecation. It is the discipline of saying, at the moment of greatest rhetorical force, exactly what the instrument can and cannot measure.
