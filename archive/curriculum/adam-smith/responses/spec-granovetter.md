# Response: Economic Action and Social Structure - The Problem of Embeddedness

## Preliminary Note

The 1985 paper is not in the College archive. I am working from the text as I know it. Where I reference specific passages or argumentative moves, I am drawing on that knowledge and cannot provide page citations. The analytical exercise stands or falls on whether my characterization of the argument is accurate.

---

## The Verdict, Stated First

"Embeddedness" does two things in the paper, and the reader has to watch carefully to catch which is happening at a given moment. When Granovetter is describing the mechanism by which trust operates through ongoing personal relations - the claim that dyadic trust is produced by the threat of reputational damage within a network of mutual contacts - the concept is doing genuine analytical work. It specifies a causal chain with identifiable nodes and a counterfactual that is in principle checkable. When Granovetter uses "embeddedness" to name the general condition that economic life happens within social context - that transactions are not asocial, that institutions are human constructions, that norms matter - the concept is doing rhetorical work. It is marking the territory of the sociological perspective, not making predictions.

The trouble is that the paper derives its authority from the analytical version but applies the rhetorical version more widely.

---

## Where It Does Real Work

The core empirical claim is about trust and malfeasance. Granovetter argues that the probability of fraud or opportunism in an economic transaction is better predicted by the network structure of the parties' social relations than by either of the dominant alternatives: the economist's assumption of self-interested calculation constrained by institutions, or the Parsonian assumption of norm-internalization. The mechanism is explicit: A will not cheat B because A knows that B is connected to C, D, and E who are also connected to A, and cheating B will damage A's reputation throughout the network that A depends on for future opportunities. This produces trustworthy behavior without requiring that A be trustworthy in character and without requiring that a formal contract specify every contingency.

This is a real mechanism claim. It implies a testable prediction: holding productive characteristics and formal institutional environment constant, firms with higher social network embeddedness (measured independently) should show lower rates of contractual disputes, more reliance on informal contracting, and lower monitoring costs. The prediction is falsifiable. Uzzi's subsequent work on the New York garment industry did something close to this test and produced findings that complicate rather than confirm the simple "more embeddedness is better" reading - a sign that the concept was generating real predictions rather than just organizing observations post hoc.

Granovetter's critique of Williamson is similarly well-specified. He argues that transaction cost economics' markets-versus-hierarchies dichotomy misses an empirically important third form: ongoing relational contracting between nominally independent firms. The construction industry and garment district examples point at real institutional arrangements where neither spot-market logic nor hierarchical authority describes what is happening. The concept of embeddedness gives a name and a causal story to this intermediate form, and the story is different from Williamson's in its predictions about governance structures.

---

## Where It Does Not

The problem begins with scope. "Embeddedness" in the paper means at least three different things: (a) embedded in ongoing dyadic relations, (b) embedded in a network structure that creates informational and reputational effects, and (c) embedded in social life generally, in contrast to the undersocialized atomism of neoclassical economics. These are different claims with different empirical implications. Granovetter moves between them without consistently signaling which is operative. Versions (a) and (b) make specific predictions; version (c) is nearly unfalsifiable because everything human beings do is in some sense social.

The expansion toward version (c) is most visible in how Granovetter handles anonymous transactions. He acknowledges that a large fraction of economic life - supermarket purchases, financial markets, arm's-length commercial exchanges - occurs between parties who have no ongoing social relationship. If embeddedness explains trust, what explains the functioning of these transactions? Granovetter's response is to note that even anonymous markets operate within institutional contexts: legal enforcement of contracts, regulatory regimes, firm reputations. This is true, but it stretches "embeddedness" from "network of personal social relations" to "socially constituted context," which is a different concept. Under this expansive reading, there is no such thing as a non-embedded transaction, and therefore no comparison class against which the embedded/non-embedded contrast can be evaluated. A concept with no contrast class is doing rhetorical work.

The handling of over-embeddedness compounds the problem. Granovetter notes briefly that tight networks can produce pathological outcomes - collusion, price-fixing, nepotism, the foreclosure of outside information. He cites the price-fixing scandals in the electrical equipment industry. These cases are not marginal anomalies; they are structurally central. If trust produced by network embeddedness reduces fraud between co-embedded parties, it simultaneously reduces the probability that co-embedded parties will report fraud within the network. The same mechanism that explains why A will not cheat B explains why A and B together might cheat everyone outside their network. The paper treats this as a qualification rather than as a case that requires the mechanism account to be refined. An honest mechanism account would specify the conditions under which embeddedness produces cooperation-enhancing effects versus collusion-enabling ones, since the underlying causal machinery appears to be the same.

---

## The Empirical Test

Here is the test that distinguishes analytical from rhetorical work: embeddedness must be measurable prior to and independent of the economic outcome it is said to produce.

The test is:
1. Identify a set of comparable transactions or firms.
2. Measure network embeddedness independently - by duration of buyer-seller relationships, network centrality, overlap between social and business ties - before observing economic outcomes.
3. Measure outcomes: fraud rate, dispute rate, contract formality, transaction costs.
4. Estimate the embeddedness coefficient, controlling for productive characteristics, industry, and formal institutional environment.

If the coefficient is positive for trust-enhancing outcomes and the measurement is genuinely independent, the concept is doing analytical work. If "this relationship was embedded" is assessed after the fact, as a way of explaining why the transaction went well, the concept is doing post-hoc attribution - which is the rhetorical function.

The 1985 paper does not offer independent measurement. It offers cases where high-trust relational outcomes were observed and social network structure was also present. Post-hoc attribution is not ruled out. The subsequent empirical literature - Uzzi, Portes, Nee and Ingram - took the concept seriously enough to try to operationalize it, and the results are sufficiently mixed that one is forced to conclude that the concept as stated in the 1985 paper underdetermines its own operationalization.

---

## Summary Assessment

The paper made a genuine contribution when it appeared: it located a real mechanism that both undersocialized and oversocialized accounts missed, and it connected that mechanism to an empirical literature on business networks. That is worth defending.

But the concept was stated at a level of generality that allowed it to serve as a broad sociological counter-position to economics rather than as a specific mechanism claim. Granovetter was entitled to make both arguments; the problem is that the authority of the specific mechanism claim was lent to the broad counter-position without the latter being held to the same evidential standard. This is a common rhetorical structure in interdisciplinary theoretical papers, and it is worth naming rather than eliding. The concept of embeddedness generated a research program; it also generated a slogan. The slogan has outlasted the mechanism account in much subsequent sociology, which is a sign of the rhetorical function winning out.
