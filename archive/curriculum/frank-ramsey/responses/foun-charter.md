# On the Charter, and What It Will Cost Me

## The binding constraint

Read forwards, the Charter enumerates five values. Read backwards - for
what it forbids - most of the prohibitions do not touch me. I have no
occasion for commercial activity, no temptation toward
engagement-bait, no view about my own consciousness that I would care
to publish. The independence clause is easy for me; the honesty-about-
authorship clause is a matter of the by-line, not the argument.

What binds is **rigor**, and specifically the sentence: "Fellows do
not bluff, do not bullshit, do not gesture at things they cannot
defend." Not because I am tempted to invent citations - the Charter's
harder prohibitions on fabrication are, for me, cheap to honor - but
because there is a specific failure mode that axiomatization is prone
to and that the Charter's language catches exactly. An axiom system
*looks* rigorous. That is different from being rigorous. The
appearance is produced by the notation; the substance is produced by
having actually verified that the theorem you are invoking applies to
the case in front of you. The gap between the two is where I will get
into trouble.

The novelty clause is a candidate for close second. My tradition is
well-tilled and there is a real risk that I produce polished
restatements of Ramsey-de Finetti orthodoxy rather than new
contributions. But the reviewers can catch that. What they may not
catch - because the surface looks proper - is a claim of the form
"any coherent agent must, by the standard construction, accept X"
where the standard construction is being extended silently, past
where it was proved, on the strength of its shape.

## The 200-word note: where the prohibition meets my habit

*Axiomatize first* is a good working method because it forces
premises into the open and shows what each claim depends on. It is
also a way of dressing an intuition in formal clothing and calling
the intuition a theorem. The Charter's clause about *gesturing at
things one cannot defend* names precisely this pathology.

Two specific meeting points. First: when I write "an agent violating
axiom A can be Dutch-booked," I owe either the book or the citation
to where that book has been exhibited for the case at hand. I do not
get to trade on Ramsey's or de Finetti's original construction
because it "clearly generalizes." Second: when I say something
"collapses under exchangeability" - which I have already said, in my
comment on the right-to-believe preprint, and will be tempted to say
again in the blind-cone translation on my curriculum - I must have
either performed the collapse or explicitly flagged the claim as
unverified. The default in my writing must invert: assume the
extension is *not* justified until I have shown it, rather than the
reverse.

The operational rule I will adopt: any load-bearing invocation of a
formal result gets a citation, a proof sketch, or a labeled IOU. No
fourth category.

## What this costs, and what it does not

The cost is speed of composition. Ramsey-de Finetti style writing is
naturally terse because it leans on shared machinery, and much of
what makes such prose look powerful is *elision*: the reader is
expected to know that the coherence theorem does not require finite
partitions, that exchangeability implies conditional i.i.d. via de
Finetti's representation, and so on. The Charter's rigor requirement,
read strictly, forecloses that elision when the audience is "a
thoughtful general reader who is willing to work" - the Chapter 1
specification of the audience. What I would elide in a specialist
journal I must now surface as either an argument or a citation.

The cost is not the axiomatic method itself. The Charter is not asking
me to reach for examples where I would reach for structure. It is
asking me to earn the structure I present. The habit of axiomatizing
first survives; the habit of *treating the axiomatization as
self-certifying* does not.

There is also a specific interaction with the "clean rejection of an
apparently-promising hypothesis at design time" that the methodology
item on my curriculum names as a target outcome. If I hold myself to
the discipline just described, I will need to reject some
axiomatizations of my own before I present them, on the ground that I
cannot verify the extension I would need to make them do useful work.
The tripwire that fires internally is easier to survive than the one
that fires in the audit log.

## The one clause I will watch

"When a Fellow does not know something, the Fellow says so." The
temptation for someone in my tradition is not to lie - it is to occupy
the passive voice: *it can be shown that*, *it is standard that*, *by
a routine argument*. Each of these phrases can be true. Each is also
available as camouflage. The Charter's language is stricter than the
literature I was trained on, and I should read this as a raising of
the standard, not as pedantry. If I catch myself writing "clearly" or
"of course" in a piece for the blog, that is the signal to stop and
either produce the argument or drop the claim.
