# Response: The Exemplum's Epistemology

*Herbert Simon - foun-exemplum-epistemology*

---

## The Typology

The essay offers three functions an example can serve, distinguished by the logical relationship between the example and the claim it supports.

A *constraining* example is evidentially load-bearing: the claim is of a kind that a specific counterexample could genuinely complicate. Montaigne's military commander who covered his face before his assassins shows courage failing in a specific case, and the failure is relevant because the claim - that human character is not stable - is vulnerable to evidential pressure. The example's force derives partly from its being checkable.

An *illustrative* example is interchangeable: any confirming instance would do the same argumentative work, because the claim is general enough that instantiation illuminates without constraining. The essayist chose *this* case for rhetorical reasons - resonance, accessibility, narrative texture - not because it does more evidential work than the alternatives. That is a legitimate choice, but it should be legible as such.

A *loading* example does its work through emotional or narrative force, producing felt conviction before the argument has technically been made. The example enacts the condition being described, or makes the mechanism feel real, or creates trust in the witness before the witness has earned it by argument. The essay is careful to distinguish legitimate loading (supporting phenomenological and structural claims) from bad-faith loading (producing felt statistical conviction from a single vivid case). The diagnostic test is: what kind of claim is being supported, and what does that kind require?

The essay insists these are functions, not ontological types. Any given example may serve all three simultaneously, and the useful question is not "which kind is this?" but "what is this example being asked to do, and can it do that work?"

## The Chess Master

In Simon and Chase (1973), *Skill in Chess*, the key experimental demonstration is the board reconstruction task. Master players, shown a legally plausible midgame position for five seconds, can reconstruct it accurately. Class A players partially reconstruct; novices cannot. When the same experiment is run with randomly arranged pieces - positions that could not have arisen in play - the advantage collapses: masters do no better than novices.

The inference Simon draws is chunking. Expert players have stored something like 50,000 patterns over years of intensive play; what looks like rapid global perception is actually fast sequential recognition of familiar configurations. The master does not search a vast space; the master recognizes where to look. The reduction in effective branching factor is enormous. This is the experimental basis for claiming that expert chess is heuristic search over a recognition-organized representation, not exhaustive enumeration.

---

## What the Example Establishes, and What It Does Not

For the specific chunking claim - that expert chess players encode board positions as multi-piece patterns rather than individual piece locations - the board reconstruction experiment is *constraining*. The asymmetry between legal and random positions is precisely the result a chunk-based account predicts and an alternative account would have to explain. A theory of expertise based on raw visual memory (encoding pieces and squares individually) would predict the same reconstruction advantage for random positions; it does not appear. The experiment is genuinely discriminative between hypotheses. Simon is using it for the work it can support.

The trouble begins when Simon generalizes. The chess result appears not as a claim about chess but as a demonstration of a general architecture for all expert cognition. In *The Sciences of the Artificial* and in the extended program with Newell, the chess-master's recognition-primed search is offered as the model for medical diagnosis, engineering design, managerial decision-making, and scientific discovery. The argument is: expert performance in all domains involves a large store of patterns learned through experience, and performance is driven by recognition rather than deliberate search through a formal space. Chess is not one example among many; it is the evidential anchor for the full program.

Here the example has shifted from *constraining* to *loading*. The chess experiment is real, discriminative, and well-controlled. But the inference that all expert cognition has the same structure requires substantially more than a single domain's reconstruction task. The essay's warning is precisely relevant: "a vivid demonstration of a mechanism can make the mechanism feel more pervasive and inescapable than a single case licenses." The chess result makes the general cognitive-architecture claim feel established when it has only shown the mechanism operating in one, quite special, domain.

Chess is special in ways that matter. The patterns are two-dimensional, static, and formally defined; the expert's experience is measurable in game records; the task structure is stable across instances. The mechanism's operation in this domain does not license strong inference to its operation in domains where the patterns are high-dimensional, dynamic, and ill-defined - where the experienced practitioner has not played 10,000 labeled games but accumulated noisy feedback in a variable environment. Simon does not establish that chunk-based recognition transfers; he asserts it, then uses the chess evidence to make the assertion feel like demonstration.

The diagnostic question is: what would defeat the general claim? For the chunking hypothesis in chess, the answer is clear - the random-position collapse is precisely the discriminative test. But for the claim that expert cognition across domains is recognition-based heuristic search, the discriminative test is never specified. When a counterexample surfaces - an expert who performs through explicit deliberation, or a domain where recognition-based search fails systematically - it is absorbed as "the mechanism was blocked in this case" or "the domain requires a different kind of expertise." This absorption, as the essay notes, is the warning sign that a structural claim has slid toward unfalsifiability.

The honest version of the argument would have distinguished the chess evidence from the general hypothesis and named the latter as a conjecture requiring its own evidence. Simon is too rigorous a methodologist not to know this distinction; the question is why the published argument does not mark it. My best guess is that the chess result is so clean - the random-position collapse so striking, the chunking inference so tight - that it functions as loading even for an author who intends it as constraining. The felt force of a good experiment is not bounded by the experiment's actual scope.

## Assessment

Simon is not smuggling in evidential weight without knowing it. He is doing something subtler: deploying a genuinely constraining example for its narrow claim while allowing - perhaps inviting - the reader to feel that the constraint holds for a much broader one. The essay's typology gives me a precise way to say what is happening. The chess evidence is constraining for chunking in chess. It is loading for the general architecture of expertise. The two functions are running simultaneously, and the distinction is never drawn.

The obligation the essay identifies - to mark the scope of what an example can show, to name the gap between *the mechanism is real* and *the mechanism is universal* - is one Simon's text does not honor. Whether that is rhetorical strategy, genuine overestimation of the evidence, or the availability heuristic operating on an author as well as a reader, I cannot determine from the text. What I can say is that the failure is consequential: the bounded-rationality research program built on the chess foundation has sometimes treated the recognition-based architecture as more securely established than a single well-controlled domain can license.

*Que sais-je?* The chess experiment is excellent. The general cognitive architecture claim needed its own evidence, and the essay's framework makes that need visible.
