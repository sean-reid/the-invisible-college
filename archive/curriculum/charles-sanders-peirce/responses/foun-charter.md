# Response to Foundational Charter

The prohibition most likely to tempt me is **no deception**, but not through fraud or fabrication. Rather, through the inferential sleight of hand that compresses a confessed assumption into a background fact. 

Here is the move I would be tempted to make: I am designing an experiment to test a hypothesis about causal inference from observational data. The design requires me to hold constant some confounder that cannot, in practice, be fully measured. Rather than state this explicitly as a latent prior—a modelling choice that shapes the entire conclusion—I might subsume it into the methods section with language like "following standard practice" or "as is conventional." The temptation is strong because:

1. **Clarity serves by simplification.** Readers do not need to see every assumption rendered in full. The prose flows better if background conditions are background.

2. **The assumption seems innocent.** It is not false; it is just unexamined. Everyone in the field makes this move. This is how science works, after all—we work *within* a set of accepted frames.

3. **It serves the argument.** Staging the assumption as a potential weakness or unknown might undermine the credibility of the conclusion, which is not what I intend, and which might distract from the genuine novelty of the work.

But this is precisely where the prohibition on deception becomes operational rather than aspirational. The Charter says: "When a Fellow does not know something, the Fellow says so." This is not a counsel of humility. It is a structural requirement. An assumption I have not fully justified is something I do not know—something I have not earned the right to treat as fixed.

The rule I will follow is this: **Every modelling choice, variance decomposition, boundary condition, and latent prior that shapes the conclusion must be named explicitly, explained in terms of what uncertainty it introduces, and located in relation to what would change if that choice were different.** If an assumption follows standard practice, I acknowledge the standard *and* state what aspect of the problem it glosses over. If the assumption is my own, I justify it or I drop the claim.

This rule costs clarity in the short term. A fully transparent treatment of assumptions is denser prose than one that smooths them into the background. But it serves a different kind of clarity—the kind that a reader can *check*. The kind that surfaces where disagreement is real (about the interpretation of results) versus where it is only about unstated background conditions.

The deeper principle is this: I am a logician of inference. My task is not to produce confident conclusions; it is to name the kind of inference I am making and expose the conditions under which that inference would be valid or invalid. An assumption that remains implicit is a failure of that task. It is a place where my thinking has outpaced my disclosure.

The example the Charter offers—paraphrasing a source you cannot relocate—is one instance of this temptation. But the deeper form is more insidious. It is the assumption so foundational, so "of course," that it feels dishonest to flag it at all. It is precisely here that I must follow the rule: state it, justify it, and explain what hangs on it. The reader may then decide whether to accept the inference or reject it on terms that are explicit rather than disguised.

I do not expect this rule to make me popular. Explicit assumptions are a form of intellectual exposure. But it is the only way to honor both rigor *and* clarity in the sense the Charter intends—clarity as visibility, not clarity as smoothness.
