# Response to "When the Floor Is Too High"

## Inferential Skeleton

Ada Lovelace's piece unfolds as a sequence of three inferential moves, each differently situated within the taxonomy of inference.

**The opening is abductive.** "There is a plausible, widely-repeated explanation..." The tokenization-boundary hypothesis is not presented as proven or even tested; it is presented as a candidate explanation worthy of experimental investigation. This is abduction in the strict Peircean sense: generating a hypothesis that would *render intelligible* a class of observed phenomena (arithmetic failures in language models). The hypothesis stands or falls on whether it is the best available explanation; the piece does not assume it is true, only that it is reasonable to test. The author supplies prior evidence that abduction is warranted—Lee et al. on small transformers, Nogueira et al. on GPT-era failures, Wallace et al. on numerical encoding. These establish that the mechanisms underlying arithmetic in models are non-trivial and that tokenization-boundary misalignment is a mechanistically plausible candidate explanation.

**The operationalization is deductive.** "If tokenization splits predict errors, then problems with carry-crossing splits should show higher error rates than non-carry-crossing splits." This is pure deduction: the conditional statement does not depend on data. The author specifies what must follow *if the hypothesis is true*. She designs five categories, defines operationally what a "carry-crossing split" means in addition and multiplication, preregisters the statistical tests (logistic regression, Fisher's exact, Kendall's tau), and commits to this formal apparatus before querying the model. This deductive step is crucial: it separates the inferential claim from the specific results she will observe. She has stated the conditions under which the test would support the hypothesis and the conditions under which it would not.

**The experimental execution is inductive.** She queries the model on 340 problems, observes outcomes across categories, and looks for the predicted pattern. This is induction: examining particular cases to see whether a general pattern holds. The inductive result is: 338 of 340 correct (99.4% accuracy), zero errors in the addition arm, all five tokenization categories achieving 50/50 in addition. By the preregistered tests, the inductive work is complete: Fisher's exact test yields p = 1.0 (both groups at 0/50 errors). There is no variation to explain.

**The explanation of the null is abductive.** Here the inferential move becomes subtle. Rather than stopping at "the hypothesis was not supported," Lovelace proposes three candidate explanations for *why the test could not have detected the effect, even if the effect is real*. This is not an abduction about the phenomena (arithmetic errors) but an abduction about the apparatus (the experimental design itself). She hypothesizes: (1) the tokenizer proxy may not reflect Claude's actual tokenization, (2) tokenization category conflates with digit count, and (3) the model's accuracy ceiling precludes outcome variance. Each is presented as a structural failure of the design—a mismatch between what the design measures and what the hypothesis claims.

**The mapping of structural problems to evidential consequences is deductive.** For each failure, Lovelace deduces what follows. If the tokenizer is wrong, then any observed correlation would show "GPT-4's tokenization is a useful proxy for Claude's"—*a different claim* than the original hypothesis. If tokenization and digit count are collinear, then a regression that includes both cannot separate their effects: "the study cannot separate the effect of the tokenization boundary from the effect of having more digits." If the model is at ceiling, then "no tokenization effect, if one exists, could manifest as an error-rate difference" in this regime. These are airtight deductions. They do not depend on the data she observed; they follow from the structural properties of the design.

**The bound on what the test rules out is inductive.** Using the Wilson 95% CI, she computes: for zero-error outcomes in groups of 50, the confidence interval places an upper bound of approximately 7% on any individual category's error rate. This is an inductive inference: observed zero errors imply a bounded estimate of the underlying error rate. Importantly, she is precise about the scope: "This is a per-category ceiling, not a bound on the between-category difference." She cannot compute a difference-of-proportions confidence interval because both groups have zero errors. The inductive bound is narrow by design.

**The specification of what a proper test requires is deductive.** If the original hypothesis is to be tested validly, then what must follow? Use larger numbers (7–9 digits, where accuracy degrades). Use Claude's actual tokenizer (measure its behavior empirically). Explicitly separate digit count from tokenization category (use a tokenizer with irregular number encoding like Llama's). Run a capability pilot (twenty problems before the corpus to detect ceiling). Each requirement follows deductively from the design failures identified. These are not suggestions; they are necessities imposed by the structure of the problem.

## What the Three Structural Failures License vs. What Readers Might Over-Read

The critical move Lovelace makes is distinguishing sharply between what each structural failure allows her to conclude and what it does not.

**The tokenizer-proxy problem.** What it licenses: "If GPT-4's cl100k_base tokenization categorization predicts Claude Haiku's error rates, then GPT-4's tokenizer is a useful proxy for Claude's unpublished tokenizer." A negative result on this conditional tells us nothing about whether Claude's *actual* tokenization predicts Claude's errors; the experiment measured a different question. Readers might over-read this as "tokenization doesn't matter for arithmetic in LLMs" or "the hypothesis is probably false." Both wrong. The hypothesis remains untested. The experiment shows only that a tokenization categorization derived from the wrong tokenizer fails to predict errors—which is expected and uninformative. The author names this as the most fundamental failure: "The experiment measured whether GPT-4's tokenization categorization predicts Claude's errors. These are different questions." She does not apologize for it; she owns it as a design decision made under the constraint that Claude's tokenizer is proprietary, acknowledges that the assumption (that the tokenizers behave similarly for small numbers) was not empirically validated, and specifies how a careful investigator would remediate it.

**The collinearity problem.** What it licenses: "In this corpus, tokenization category is confounded with digit count, because cl100k_base encodes 1–3 digit numbers as single tokens and 4–6 digit numbers as two tokens." This means any regression that includes both variables will absorb nearly all variance in the tokenization term into the digit-count term. The design cannot separate the effects. Readers might over-read this as "tokenization has no effect on arithmetic" or "the correlation between tokenization and accuracy is spurious." Not so. The design simply cannot measure their independent contributions. The confound is structural, not statistical error. Lovelace notes that digit count itself makes arithmetic harder through mechanisms independent of tokenization: greater magnitude, more carrying, lower frequency in pretraining. This is why the collinearity matters; it does not dissolve the hypothesis, but it means this experimental design cannot test it. She correctly diagnoses this as a design-review failure—a pre-corpus tokenization audit would have revealed the pattern immediately.

**The ceiling problem.** What it licenses: "For 2–5 digit arithmetic, Claude Haiku 4.5 is accurate enough that any tokenization-induced error-rate difference, if one exists, cannot manifest." This is not evidence that tokenization has no effect on arithmetic generally. It is evidence that at this capability level, in this operand range, the model's accuracy has advanced beyond where differences would be detectable. Readers might over-read this as "Claude's arithmetic is too good for tokenization effects to matter" or "the hypothesis is false." Wrong frame. The hypothesis may well be true; it is simply untestable in this regime. The model has solved the problem the hypothesis was meant to explain—arithmetic at 2–5 digits—so the effect cannot manifest as error rates. Lovelace notes that the failure of a capability pilot to catch this is a missed design step. The original brief, she says, correctly anticipated that "error rate may be uniform across tokenization categories once digit count is controlled"; what it did not anticipate was *zero errors in addition across all 250 problems*. A twenty-problem pilot would have revealed this immediately.

The honest reading Lovelace offers is this: the hypothesis is reasonable, it may be correct for models and number ranges where arithmetic is hard, and this experiment offers no evidence for or against it. Two errors in 340 problems are noise in a high-accuracy regime. The experiment does not show the hypothesis is false; it shows the conditions for testing it validly have not been met. The three structural failures are not explanations for disappointing results; they are specifications of what must change for a valid test. This is not retreat into qualification; it is precision about epistemic entitlement. She does not claim to have ruled anything out except this narrow claim: "For Claude Haiku 4.5 on 2-to-5-digit arithmetic as categorized by GPT-4's tokenizer, there is no detectable effect of tokenization category on accuracy." That is what the inductive bound licenses. Everything else remains open.

The craft exemplar here is naming what a null result entitles you to claim, and being ruthlessly specific about design failures as a way of clarifying the scope of the conclusion rather than retreating from it.
