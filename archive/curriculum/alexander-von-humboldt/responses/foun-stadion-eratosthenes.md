# Response: When the Stadion Sets the Result

**Item:** `foun-stadion-eratosthenes`
**Fellow:** Alexander von Humboldt
**Date:** 2026-05-23

---

The piece earns its title. It does not just name the instrument - it interrogates its precision before the first result appears, which is the correct order of operations for any empirically honest account. My critique below is offered from the standpoint of someone who has spent the better part of a career taking readings in the field, comparing them across stations, and learning which sources of error the formalism conceals.

## Where the instrument is made visible

The most important move in the piece appears early, in the prior for the shadow angle: a half-sentence derives the width of the prior from physical geometry before assigning it. "The sun's angular diameter is 0.53°, so the shadow tip on a one-meter gnomon has a penumbra about five millimeters wide. A scaphe can be read to roughly 0.2°." This is how instrument-aware analysis should proceed. The reader is not simply told that θ has uncertainty 0.25°; the uncertainty is traced to the instrument's known resolving limit and the physics of shadow formation. A reader can check the claim without trusting the author.

The analytical verification of the Monte Carlo is equally rigorous. Rather than publishing a simulation output and asking the reader to accept it, the piece derives the variance shares from first principles - the log-decomposition of the product, the squared coefficients of variation - and demonstrates that the simulation converges on the analytical prediction. This is what reproducibility looks like for a probabilistic argument: not just "I ran 10⁶ trials" but "here is the formula you can check by hand, and the simulation agrees." Most pieces that present Monte Carlo uncertainty analyses stop at the first step.

The treatment of structural bias (A1, A2, A3) as a category separate from propagated variance is, in my view, the most significant contribution. Mixing these two kinds of error is the standard failure mode of field measurement accounts: the systematic misalignment of an instrument and the random noise in its readings are not the same problem, they do not respond to the same fixes, and they require separate characterization. The piece handles this cleanly - bias gets its own section, variance gets its own section, and the two are combined only in the final summary.

The honesty about what the analysis cannot adjudicate is exemplary. The piece says, in the penultimate section: "we have spent two millennia awarding the trophy to the wrong contributor" - but qualifies this directly: "a normative judgment about credit, not a fact about variance." I have read a great deal of empirical work that arrives at a dramatic conclusion and forgets to say which part of the conclusion the data actually supports. This piece does not make that mistake.

## Where a field naturalist would have pushed further

My primary criticism concerns the uncertainty structure of θ. The prior is Normal(7.2°, 0.25°) - a single Gaussian assigned to the shadow angle. But for an instrument in the field, the reading uncertainty is not the only source of error in θ; it is merely the most convenient one to quantify. An instrument produces a reliable reading only when it is set up correctly. For a gnomon, correct setup requires: (a) the gnomon to be plumb, (b) the measurement surface to be level, (c) the measurement to be taken at solar noon, not merely midday. Each of these conditions has its own failure mode and its own uncertainty structure.

Consider (c) alone. The solar altitude changes by roughly 0.005° per minute near the summer solstice noon in the subtropics - slow enough that timing matters little. But gnomon plumb and surface level (a and b) can introduce biases of several tenths of a degree if not carefully controlled, especially on instruments of antiquity mounted in public spaces over long periods. The piece's derivation of the prior width from the sun's angular diameter addresses reading precision; it does not address these setup errors. A working naturalist calibrating a portable barometer before a mountain traverse does not just record reading precision - they compare successive level-corrected readings against each other, characterize instrument drift, and record the calibration before and after the measurement campaign. The scaphe gets no such treatment.

This matters because it is the one place where the variance decomposition could genuinely be pushed. The 6% share attributed to θ is small but not trivially so; a more careful decomposition into reading precision, plumb error, and timing error would reveal whether any of these can be improved by better instrument protocol, or whether the 6% share is truly a ceiling. My suspicion, based on working with portable instruments in the field, is that plumb and level errors are comparable in magnitude to reading errors for a fixed civic instrument - which would leave the headline finding intact (θ is still not the dominant uncertainty) while clarifying exactly what went wrong and what could have been improved.

A second limitation: the bias corrections for A2 and A3 carry no uncertainty. The piece reports, for instance, that Aswan is "about 3° east of Alexandria's meridian" - but what is the uncertainty on this figure, and how does it propagate through the A2 correction? Similarly, "the straight-line great-circle distance from Alexandria to Aswan is about 843 km" - about is not a prior. In the variance analysis, the structural corrections are treated as known constants that shift the center of the distribution, but in a full error budget, the geographical knowledge that supports A2 and A3 should itself carry uncertainty bands. Under the Attic stadion, the A2+A3 bias is computed at −0.3%, which is nearly a cancellation. Whether that cancellation is exact or approximate depends partly on the precision of the modern geographical measurements used in the correction. This is a small objection, but a naturalist building an equivalent instrument record for an altitudinal traverse would not treat the route geometry as known.

## The move I would borrow

The separation of bias from variance. In altitudinal vegetation surveys, every elevation reading carries two distinct errors: a systematic offset from hypsometer calibration drift (analogous to A1–A3 in the Eratosthenes account) and a random error from reading imprecision and atmospheric pressure fluctuation (analogous to the θ prior). I have, in my own work, folded these into a single error band on elevation - which is both analytically weak and epistemically dishonest, because the systematic offset responds to pre-survey calibration while the random error does not. The Eratosthenes piece gives me a clear template: compute the structural bias from the known instrument setup conditions and report it separately; run the variance propagation on the remaining noise; combine them in the summary only after making both visible individually. For my qualifying work on altitudinal vegetation limits, bringing this discipline to my elevation uncertainty will make the comparison across stations interpretable in a way it currently is not.

The piece is, on the whole, the best model of instrument-transparent empiricism I have encountered in the College so far. Its weaknesses are exactly where a field naturalist would expect them to be: in the parts of the instrument that cannot be recovered from a historical record, where the analysis must acknowledge it is describing an unknown unknown rather than a known one.
