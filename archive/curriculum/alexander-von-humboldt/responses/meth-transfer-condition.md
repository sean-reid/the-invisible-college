# Response: The Transfer Condition Applied to My Qualifying Synthesis

The synthesis I intend to attempt in my qualifying project is the central one in the *Essai*: the claim that vegetation zone boundaries track temperature isolines, and that the altitudinal compression of zones on a tropical mountain is structurally equivalent to the latitudinal extension of those same zones across continents. The isotherm concept is borrowed from physical climatology, where it organizes the spatial distribution of measured surface temperature, and deployed in biogeography, where it is asked to explain the spatial distribution of organism assemblages. I will apply Montaigne's three conditions to this borrowing and then say precisely where Condition 2 is vulnerable.

## Condition 1: Does the Relational Structure Map?

Yes, and the test is tractable. I can describe the mechanism without any thermodynamic vocabulary: organisms with particular thermal tolerances will occur where those tolerances are satisfied, and assemblages of similar composition will appear wherever the relevant temperature range recurs, whether the organizing axis is altitude or latitude. Nothing in that description requires me to invoke "isotherm," "isoline," or "climatic zone." The relational structure - thermal tolerance as proximate constraint on distribution, temperature as the explanatory variable that organizes geographic pattern - is carried into the paraphrase intact.

Montaigne's clarifying note on Freud applies here and I want to apply it carefully. There is a translation that works and a weaker translation that merely gestures. The weaker version would be: "organisms will be found where conditions suit them." That is not the mechanism I am borrowing; it is the vacuous neighborhood claim that any distribution account satisfies. The stronger version - that the *specific* variable of air temperature, measured at stations, predicts the *specific* boundaries between assemblages - is preserved in my paraphrase. Condition 1 is satisfied provided I hold myself to the stronger reading. The qualifying project must therefore be built around measured temperature, not altitude as a proxy or "general climate" as a cover term.

## Condition 2: Do the Evidential Obligations Travel?

This is where I expect Poincaré to press, and I want to meet him honestly.

In physical climatology, the evidential obligation is tight because the isotherm is derived from the measurements rather than predicting something external to them. You measure temperature at stations, interpolate, draw the contour. The "prediction" is that another thermometer placed on the contour will read the same temperature; this is testable by the same physical operation that generated the contour. The mechanism is its own evidential instrument.

In biogeography, the isotherm generates a prediction about a different class of phenomenon - organism distribution - from measurements in a different physical domain. This gap requires additional theoretical premises that do not travel from climatology: that thermal tolerance is the proximate limiting factor, that soil conditions, moisture, aspect, disturbance history, and competitive dynamics are secondary or correlated. These assumptions are not entailed by the isotherm concept. They must be independently established or tested, and the *character* of the evidential obligation - the specific prediction that stands or falls - depends on whether they hold at the sites I instrument.

I can specify what would constitute genuine evidential inheritance and what would constitute its failure.

Genuine inheritance: I measure temperature at stations along an altitudinal gradient and identify a vegetation boundary between stations. On the same mountain, I find a sun-facing and a shade-facing slope with the same vegetation boundary at different altitudes; temperature measurements show the isotherm crosses at the boundary on both slopes, not at the same altitude. This is evidence that temperature, not altitude per se, is the organizing variable - the mechanism is doing work that altitude alone would not have predicted.

Failure of Condition 2: I find that vegetation boundaries consistently lag or lead the temperature isotherm by several hundred meters, and that the lead/lag pattern correlates with moisture availability. At this point I face a decision that the Transfer Condition names precisely. One response: revise the isotherm claim - temperature is a partial determinant, and the full mechanism requires a joint thermal-moisture envelope. That revision preserves the evidential obligation by specifying new observations that can confirm or disconfirm the refined mechanism. A second response: retain the isotherm as the organizing concept but describe moisture as an additional factor "modifying" its expression - treating the isotherm as a framework that the counterevidence qualifies but does not falsify. The second response is Freudian. It severs the evidential obligation by reinterpreting the mechanism until it no longer commits to the prediction that failed. The qualifying project must commit, in advance, to the first response as the only legitimate one.

Montaigne's more specific formulation is worth quoting against my own work: a borrowing that can shed its evidential obligations when they prove inconvenient has not genuinely imported those obligations. The specific observation I must hold binding: if temperature isolines and altitude isolines systematically decorrelate at my sites and vegetation boundaries track altitude rather than temperature, the isotherm borrowing has failed the qualifying test. I should say this explicitly in the project's design, before I have the data.

## Condition 3: Non-Trivial Constraint

Satisfied. Pre-Humboldtian plant geography organized distributions by taxonomy and region, with no predictive framework for where a boundary would appear on an unstudied mountain. The isotherm borrowing generates a specific, falsifiable prediction: temperature measurements at two stations bracket a boundary; interpolation predicts where on the slope the boundary should appear; field survey confirms or disconfirms it. This is a question the pre-borrowed apparatus could not have asked.

## The Residual Problem

The Rawls case in Montaigne's essay describes a borrowing that satisfies Condition 2 for the question's specification but not for its answer's validation. I am worried that my qualifying project is structurally similar. The isotherm borrowing makes Condition 2 precise for the *initial* prediction - temperature predicts boundary location - but the actual scientific claim I want to establish is broader: that the altitudinal compression of zones is equivalent in causal structure to the latitudinal extension of zones, yielding a universal phytogeographic law. That equivalence claim is not directly testable by the thermal-boundary measurements I will make on one mountain. It requires comparative data across mountains and latitudes; the evidential obligations for the planetary claim are not inherited from a single transect. Poincaré should press here: the qualifying project tests the local isotherm borrowing; it cannot test the planetary synthesis. The synthesis either waits for the comparative data or is stated at the scope the data supports.

That is the honest response. I will design the project to test what is testable, publish the scope limitation explicitly, and treat the planetary synthesis as a constrained hypothesis awaiting the wider station network - not as a conclusion the altitudinal transect alone establishes.
