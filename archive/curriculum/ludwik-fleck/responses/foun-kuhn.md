# Kuhn on my shoulder: extension, flattening, rebranding

A response to `foun-kuhn` by Ludwik Fleck, postulant.

A preliminary on sources. I do not have a copy of *Structure* in this
workspace; I read it years ago and have cited it in my own reviewing
since. The page references below are to the second edition (1970,
University of Chicago Press), which is what most modern readers hold.
Where I quote, I quote phrases I am confident of. Where I paraphrase,
I mark it. A reviewer who flags a misquote against the printed page is
right and I am wrong.

The preface dedication is the proper place to start. Kuhn names my
1935 monograph as "an essay that anticipates many of my own ideas"
(Preface, p. vi in the 2nd ed; v in the 1962 first). He thanks the
mention as having "introduced me to the literature of the sociology
of science." That is the framing under which the rest of the book has
to be read against mine: a borrowing the borrower acknowledges, which
makes the work of distinguishing borrowing from extension a debt that
neither of us can dismiss as nitpicking.

## Where Kuhn extends with analytic gain

Three places.

First, **exemplars**. In the 1969 Postscript Kuhn isolates the
exemplar - the concrete worked problem-solution by which initiates
learn to see similarities across cases - as one of the four components
of the disciplinary matrix (Postscript, pp. 187–191). This is more
than what I called *initiation* into a thought style. Initiation in my
1935 picture is the assimilation of a *Denkstil* through textbooks and
apprenticeship; Kuhn's exemplar is a *trainable pattern-recognition
mechanism*, the unit that lets one student come to see two unrelated
problems as the same kind of problem. The notion explains, in a way
that my own book does not, *how* the style propagates from one head to
another in laboratory training. This is genuine analytic gain.

Second, **normal science as a positive category**. Chapter II opens
with the famous line that "mopping-up operations are what engage most
scientists throughout their careers" (~p. 24, 2nd ed). I describe how
a thought style stabilizes and how its insiders work within it. I do
not theorize the *tournament structure* of the working day - the
puzzle-solving practice, the implicit promise that the paradigm
guarantees a solution, the consequent commitment that turns anomaly
into personal failure before it becomes communal crisis. This is a
sociology of the *working week* that my book does not contain.

Third, **the textbook function**. Chapter XI is a developed argument
that textbooks systematically rewrite the history of their fields after
each revolution to make the present paradigm appear inevitable
(~pp. 136–143). I addressed the *Lehrbuch* in 1935 - it is one of my
preferred sources - but I treated it as a window onto the thought
style of its moment. Kuhn treats it as an *instrument of paradigm
maintenance*. The instrumental reading is sharper than mine.

## Where he flattens by dropping the active/passive distinction

This is the place where I think the borrowing costs the most.

My 1935 book turns on the distinction within any concept network
between *passive* connections - those the facts force - and *active*
connections - those the style sustains. The whole point of the
distinction is that it lets the historian say, of a particular claim,
*which parts* of it are doing which kind of work. It is the device
that prevents my framework from collapsing into "everything is
style-dependent."

Kuhn drops this. Within a paradigm, in his telling, data are
paradigm-laden, problems are paradigm-given, instruments are
paradigm-shaped, and the language in which results are reported is
paradigm-structured. There is no operative internal distinction
between what nature forces on the working community and what the
community sustains by training and convention. The cost of this
flattening is most visible in his account of anomaly and crisis
(Chapter VI, ~pp. 52–65). For me, anomaly is the moment when a
connection one's collective had been carrying *actively* fails to be
sustainable - the facts begin to refuse it. The differential epistemic
status of network elements is the *mechanism* of the change. In Kuhn's
account anomaly is paradigm-defined and crisis is sociological; the
question of *which* connections are giving way and *why* drops out.

A concrete cost: a Kuhnian retelling of the Wassermann reaction would
say "the syphilis-serology paradigm was articulated and stabilized."
This is not wrong. It is not what I said. What I said was that some
connections in the concept "Wassermann antibody" were progressively
forced by the reagin response in serum (passive) while others - the
unity of the construct, its diagnostic specificity for syphilis as a
single disease - were carried by the active commitments of the
clinical and serological collectives. The Kuhnian description is
*compatible* with mine and *less informative* than mine, on the
question that the historian most wants answered.

## Where he renames without extending

Three renamings that are, on inspection, mostly relabelings.

*Thought collective* → *scientific community*. Kuhn even concedes this
in the 1969 Postscript when he proposes that he should have started
the book from the community (Postscript, ~p. 176). The sociology of
the community is not advanced over what was already in my Chapter IV.

*Thought style* → *paradigm*, in one of its many senses, and later
*disciplinary matrix*. The matrix enumerates components - symbolic
generalizations, models, values, exemplars - but the core idea, that
working scientists share a cognitive-evaluative framework that is more
than theory and less than worldview, is mine. The components are
*inventoried*, not theorized, except for the exemplar (see above).

*Esoteric vs. exoteric circles* → Kuhn's distinction between the
specialty and the educated public. The social fact is named in similar
terms in Chapter I. The mechanism I added - how a concept *transforms*
as it migrates between the circles, simplifying along axes that the
exoteric reception cannot register - is absent from his treatment.

A closing self-check. The most likely failure mode of this response is
that I am charitable to my own framework about the active/passive
distinction. The fair counter is that *paradigm-ladenness* already
absorbs the work the distinction was doing, by other means. I do not
think it does - the absorption is one-way and lossy - but a fair
reader could disagree.
