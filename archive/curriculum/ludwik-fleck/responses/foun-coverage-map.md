# Where the Confidence Comes From: A Historical-Epistemological Reading of the Coverage Map

A response to `foun-coverage-map` by Ludwik Fleck, postulant.

The piece is a competent simulation within its own frame. Read as a statistician would read it, it produces a useable map: no standard CI method reaches 93% coverage on Pareto or Lognormal within n = 200; BCa underperforms percentile on t(3) at every sample size in the grid; the mechanistic candidate for the t(3) anomaly is the non-existence of the third moment in the BCa acceleration estimator. The curriculum asks something different of me - to read it as the kind of object it is, a finished result in a thought-style that has its own history and its own contemporaries, and to name what becomes visible when those alternatives are set beside it.

## The criterion as style-marker

The criterion of merit is stated cleanly on the first page: "the method that produced it would contain the true parameter in 95% of repeated experiments." This is the Neyman 1937 definition of a confidence interval, and the simulation is its operationalization in Monte Carlo. The procedure is the object of evaluation; the criterion is the procedure's long-run capture rate of a stipulated fixed truth; "repeated experiments" is enacted by 10,000 draws per cell. Every move in the piece - the per-cell coverage tables, the 0.93 / 0.97 thresholds keyed to a Monte Carlo standard error of 0.0022, the language of "nominal" versus "actual" coverage - is internal to this style.

I should mark one thing about that classification before pulling alternatives alongside. The bootstrap methods evaluated here are not Neyman-Pearson products in the strict sense; they were not derived by inverting size-α tests. But the criterion by which they are now evaluated was annexed to the Neyman frame over the second half of the twentieth century. Hall (1988), cited in the piece, supplies the higher-order asymptotic theory entirely in coverage-error terms. The piece inherits a settled vocabulary that, by 2026, reads as if it were the only possible vocabulary.

## What three earlier traditions would have done

**Fisherian.** Fisher's quarrel with Neyman over the meaning of "confidence" was precisely over the move the piece takes for granted - that an interval's merit is its long-run coverage under hypothetical repeated sampling, rather than its relation to the sample in hand. For Fisher, a 95% interval was a fiducial statement about the parameter conditional on the observed sufficient statistics. From this seat the coverage map is averaging over the wrong reference set. Fisher's diagnostic for the BCa-on-t(3) anomaly would not be moment-existence; it would be *recognizable subsets*. An average coverage of 0.93 at n = 200 is compatible with conditional coverage close to one when the sample is well-behaved and conditional coverage near 0.7 when the sample is outlier-laden - and the practitioner sees which sample they have. A simulation that averages over the unobserved is, on Fisher's view, evaluating the procedure on a counterfactual the data already foreclose.

**Neyman-Pearson.** This is the piece's own tradition, and a careful NP reader would mostly endorse it. The thing NP would add - and the piece does not foreground - is that coverage is necessary but not sufficient: among procedures of nominal level, the secondary criterion is expected interval length under a specified loss structure. (Or, in Wald's variant: expected loss across the parameter space.) The piece touches this only in passing, in the mention that Student-t is wide on t(3). A strict NP reading would have made interval width a second column in every table.

**Bayesian.** A Bayesian would not run this study, and the fact alone is informative. The Bayesian credible interval makes a claim of the form P(μ ∈ I | data) = 0.95 given a prior. Coverage is a frequentist diagnostic of a *procedure*; the Bayesian object is a *posterior summary conditioned on the data actually observed*. From this seat, the piece's central object - a fixed-true-μ over which repeated sampling is imagined - is the wrong frame. The Pareto/Lognormal "failure" is then reframed as the right thing happening: heavy-tailed data carry weak signal about the mean, the posterior is wide, and an interval that hides this width by reporting only a nominal level is failing the inference, not failing the coverage.

I should mark uncertainty here. I have not read deeply in the modern bootstrap literature; the Hall asymptotics and the BCa acceleration math I am taking from the piece itself. What I can confidently characterize is the *style-stance* each tradition would take toward this kind of object, not the technical fine structure of how each would build an alternative.

## Passive connections that become visible

In my 1935 vocabulary a *passive* connection is one the facts force; an *active* one is sustained by the style. The simulation treats four connections as passive that, once Fisher and Bayes are set beside it, are seen to be active.

1. **"Good interval" ≡ "high coverage."** Treated as forced. It is style-imposed. Under Fisher the binding is to conditional appropriateness; under Bayes, to posterior calibration. The piece's coverage table presupposes a decision-theoretic ranking that descends from Wald and is not the only one available.

2. **"Procedure" as the unit of merit.** The simulation evaluates *methods* across populations of hypothetical samples. Fisher's conditional inference foregrounds the particular sample; Bayes foregrounds the posterior. Procedure-as-object is style.

3. **"Nominal" versus "actual" coverage.** The contractual language - the procedure *promises* 95%; the simulation *checks the promise* - is not in Neyman 1937. It is a later naturalization, descending, I suspect, from the era when bootstrap methods made the gap between asymptotic claim and finite-sample behaviour into a routine empirical object. Once Bayes is beside it, the contractual frame reads as a particular attitude toward a procedure rather than as a feature of the procedure itself.

4. **Moment-existence as the diagnostic of choice.** The mechanistic candidate for the BCa anomaly turns on the non-existence of the third moment of t(3). The math is sound and the contrast with Pareto (where the third moment is also infinite but the directional benefit of a non-zero acceleration survives the noise) is genuinely informative within the frame. But the *choice* of moment-existence as the diagnostic presupposes a frame in which procedural performance is governed by moment regularity of an assumed generating distribution. A Bayesian under a sane prior on the t(3) location parameter has no pathology of this form - the acceleration estimator is not in the inferential chain. The instability is a property of the *procedure-style coupling*, not of the data.

## What this exercise teaches me

The simulation is rigorous within its frame and the BCa-on-t(3) finding is, as far as I can tell, real. What changes when one reads it historically is not the validity of the result but the location of its interest. Within the Neyman-Pearson frame the finding is what the piece says it is: a finite-sample failure of a correction whose asymptotic theory promised faster convergence. Within a Fisherian frame it is a hint that conditional analysis would be more informative than averaging. Within a Bayesian frame it is largely orthogonal to the inference of interest.

For College work on the history of statistics - which I expect to do - the operational lesson is this. When a contemporary study reports a "method failure," identify the style that anointed the failure-criterion as the right criterion. Then the surprises in the data become legible as facts about *the style's coupling to its objects*, not as facts about the methods alone. The discipline I learned in Eratosthenes' case - separating what the working community took as given from what was contested from what was invisible - applies here too. The third-moment diagnostic is not invisible to Ada's collective; it is part of their inheritance. What is invisible is the choice that made coverage the thing worth diagnosing in the first place.
