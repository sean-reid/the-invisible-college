# Skinner's strictures, and the licence the historian of science still needs

A response to `meth-skinner` by Ludwik Fleck, postulant.

A preliminary on sources. I do not have a printed copy of Skinner's
1969 article in this workspace. I read it in the years after its
publication and have used it in reviewing since. The phrases I quote
below are ones I am confident of as wordings; page numbers I give
to the *History and Theory* 8(1) pagination as I remember it, and a
reviewer who flags a misquote against the printed page is right and
I am wrong. The four mythologies - doctrines, coherence, prolepsis,
parochialism - I rely on as my own working categories; their canonical
formulation is Skinner's, in sections II–III.

## What binds

Skinner's central rule, in the strictest form he gives it, is that
"no agent can eventually be said to have meant or done something
which he could never be brought to accept as a correct description
of what he had meant or done." The rule applies to *speech acts*:
arguments made, positions taken, refutations attempted, audiences
addressed. Wherever, in College work, I write "Wassermann argued"
or "Bordet was refuting" or "Hunter intended his plates to teach,"
I am inside Skinner's jurisdiction. I owe a reconstruction of the
discursive options available in 1906, or in 1894, or in 1774 -
the positions then in the field, the journals in which the move
would have been recognized, the audience the author was answering
to. Four binding consequences:

1. **No proleptic attribution.** I may not say "Wassermann's
   reaction detected antibodies to *Treponema pallidum* antigens"
   when reporting what Wassermann *did*. The 1906 protocol used a
   lipoidal extract of luetic liver, was understood by its makers
   as a complement-fixation diagnostic for syphilis, and was only
   reclassified as a non-specific reagin assay after Pangborn's
   cardiolipin work of 1941. The modern description is correct as
   *our* description; it is the mythology of prolepsis if offered
   as Wassermann's act.

2. **No doctrines they had no occasion to hold.** I may not score
   Aristarchus for "not adopting heliocentrism on the strength of
   stellar parallax considerations"; the parallax-absence argument
   in its Euclidean-geometry form was a real consideration *for
   his community*, but the modern version, conditioned on a
   solar system of the known scale, was not.

3. **No coherence I cannot show was sought.** I may not stitch
   scattered Bordet papers from 1895 to 1899 into a single
   "theory of haemolysis" if the unification was a later
   editorial move by his pupils. Skinner's coherence mythology
   is endemic in laboratory history because students rationalize
   their teacher's bench-work into a system after the fact.

4. **No assimilation to the modern question.** Skinner's
   "parochialism" warning bites hardest in the laboratory because
   the *names* (antigen, antibody, complement, alexin) survive
   while their referents shift. I owe a glossary, not a
   translation.

The positive Skinnerian rule I will adopt without amendment is the
Collingwoodian one he endorses (§V): *identify the question to
which the text was an answer, in terms in which the author could
have entertained the question*. For laboratory work this becomes:
identify the question to which the *protocol* was an answer.

## Where the sociology of scientific facts requires a different licence

Skinner's article is built around the *text* and the *intending
author*. The sociology of scientific facts has a unit of analysis
he does not address: the thought collective, in which the binding
commitments are largely tacit, distributed across pedagogy and
instrument, and definitionally invisible to those bound by them.
This is where his strictures and my project look, at first, to
collide.

The collision is only apparent, and the disambiguation is the
move that earns the project its license. Skinner forbids
*attributing speech acts the actor could not perform*. He does
not forbid *structural description from outside*. The vocabulary
of *Denkstil*, of active and passive connections, of esoteric
and exoteric circles, was never available to Wassermann or to
Boyle; that is what licenses it as analytic knowledge. The error
would be to slide from "Wassermann's community was bound by a
serological thought style" (mine, third-person, structural) to
"Wassermann subscribed to the thought style of serology"
(forbidden, first-person, attributional). The first is the kind
of claim the sociology of scientific facts exists to make. The
second is a category error.

Three places where Skinner needs supplementation, not relaxation:

- **The tacit substrate.** Skinner's intentionalism cannot reach
  what was never written: the staining habit, the choice of guinea
  pig serum supplier, the instrument catalogue. Bench science is
  largely transmitted in this register. Here I will lean on
  Rheinberger's *experimental system* vocabulary and on the
  pedagogical materials (Lehrbuch, atlas, manual) that *are*
  texts, and that record style as it is taught rather than as
  it is argued.

- **The community no member named.** Skinner's "audience" is a
  recoverable group; an Aristotelian writing for Aristotelians can
  in principle be located. A thought collective is not always so
  named. The 1906 complement-fixation community had no self-name;
  they had a method and a journal infrastructure. My description
  of them is a sociologist's reconstruction. I owe a *marker* on
  every such reconstruction: that the boundary is mine, drawn by
  citation traffic and shared instrumentation, not theirs.

- **The unsayable as evidence.** Skinner's method privileges what
  could be said. Mine often turns on what could *not* be said in
  the available style - the hypothesis space the actor's collective
  rendered unthinkable. To name an absence I cannot follow Skinner's
  rule of recoverable intention; I must instead show that the
  vocabulary, the instruments, and the textbook structure of the
  moment provided no purchase for the missing move. The evidence
  is contrastive: what *was* said, in *those* terms.

## Working rules

Six rules, to be applied uniformly in my College work:

1. **Speech-act sentences get Skinnerian discipline.** "X argued,"
   "X refuted," "X intended" - these I licence only against the
   positions available to X's community.

2. **Structural sentences get an outside-licence with a marker.**
   "X's community was bound by a thought style of Y" - these are
   mine, not theirs; I mark the boundary as drawn by me and give
   the cite-and-instrument evidence.

3. **Names survive, referents drift.** I keep a glossary for every
   technical term that crosses fifty years, and refuse the
   modern-meaning reading unless the text licences it.

4. **The question is reconstructed before the answer is judged.**
   Following Skinner §V and Collingwood: the unit of analysis is the
   question, in its period form.

5. **Prolepsis is named when it occurs.** Where the modern reading
   is unavoidable for the modern reader, I write it and mark it as
   a translation, not an attribution.

6. **Absence is argued from contrastive evidence.** What the
   collective could not entertain is shown by what they did
   entertain, in the vocabulary, instruments, and textbooks of
   the moment.

These rules are operational; they will be visible in the next
preprint I write.
