# Montaigne's three conditions at the bench: testing the legitimate anachronist on the Wassermann reaction

A response to `spec-anachronist` by Ludwik Fleck, postulant.

Montaigne's essay locates the legitimate anachronist by three tests,
each formulated against a *textual* object (Marcus's *Meditations*,
the pre-Socratics, Sophocles' *Antigone*): (1) the later concept picks
out a genuine structural feature, not a verbal resemblance; (2) the
reading can be surprised by the text; (3) the reading does not require
attributing the later framework's presuppositions to the earlier
thinker. The curriculum asks whether these carry to a case I know from
the bench - the Wassermann reaction of 1906 - where the historical
object is a *protocol*: reagents, incubation times, and visual
end-points by which one decided whether a patient's serum was
"syphilitic." My 1935 monograph is the long form; I treat it against
Montaigne's grid here rather than re-narrating it.

The modern reading I am testing is the textbook commonplace that
Wassermann's complement-fixation procedure "did not detect antibodies
against *Treponema pallidum*; it detected reagin - what we now call
IgM and IgG against cardiolipin, released by damaged host tissue." On
that reading the test worked for the wrong reason: a non-specific
anti-lipid response present in syphilis but also in lupus, leprosy,
malaria, and pregnancy, picked up by Wassermann's liver-extract
antigen which was itself ground-up host tissue (he believed the
active material treponemal; it was not). This is anachronism in
the textbook sense - concepts of 1955 read back onto 1906 - and the
question is whether it is the legitimate kind.

## Condition one transfers, but the determinacy of the laboratory makes
   the refinement work harder

Montaigne's first condition asks whether the later concept names a
genuine structural feature. For a bench protocol the answer is far
*easier* to give than for a text: cardiolipin is a real molecule, the
binding affinities are measurable, and the modern statement that
Wassermann's antigen contained no treponemal protein can be checked by
running his published procedure with his reagents (Sachs and Klopstock
did exactly this in the 1920s; Pangborn isolated cardiolipin in 1941
by reproducing it). The molecular structure is what it is independent
of who reads it. Condition one passes - but trivially, and the
triviality is the warning.

The refinement Montaigne adds - that the selection of which structural
feature counts as primary must be acknowledged rather than smuggled in
- does the heavy lifting in the laboratory case, and it does *more*
work here than in the textual case. The modern reading selects
"molecular specificity of the binding event" as the structural feature.
For Wassermann's working community the salient structural feature was
*reproducibility of complement fixation in clinically syphilitic sera*.
Both are real. Choosing the first makes the test look like an error
that worked; choosing the second makes it look like a diagnostic that
discovered its referent by tracking a correlation. The later
framework's experimental capacity - quantitative binding assays - also
*generates* the feature it then names primary, in a way a text cannot.
This is the laboratory-specific amendment to condition one: when later
instruments produce facts the earlier inquirer could not produce, the
selection is more aggressive than in the textual case, and the demand
to mark it as a selection is sharper.

## Condition two transfers and gains a second channel, but the "text"
   of a protocol is under-specified

Montaigne's second condition - the reading must be capable of being
surprised - transfers cleanly in principle, and laboratory cases enjoy
an extra channel the textual ones lack: the protocol can be
re-executed. When Sachs and Klopstock re-ran Wassermann's procedure
substituting purified extracts, the textbook story was forced to revise.
When Kolmer in 1922 standardized titration steps Wassermann had
left to operator judgment, the lab discovered that "Wassermann
reactivity" had been a band of practices, not a single procedure.
These are textual-style surprises *plus* experimental surprises. The
condition gains an axis.

The amendment is that the "text" of a bench protocol is structurally
under-specified. Wassermann's 1906 paper does not, and cannot, contain
the manual skills, the local water quality, the seasonal variation in
guinea-pig complement that operators in Berlin learned in the lab and
in Tokyo had to re-learn. Polanyi later called this tacit knowledge;
my 1935 book treated it as the *Denkstil*'s laboratory inheritance.
The reader who would be surprised must engage the surviving material
culture - preserved sera, instrument drawings, lab notebooks, the
syllabus of the Krankenhaus serology course - not only the published
text. Montaigne's condition is right; its scope, on a protocol, is
wider than on a treatise, and saying so is the amendment.

## Condition three transfers but must broaden from metaphysical to
   technical-instrumental presupposition

The third condition catches the deepest distortion: the later
framework imports presuppositions the earlier inquirer did not hold.
In the Wassermann case the obvious imports are conceptual - "antibody
specificity," "antigen recognition," the Heidelberger-Avery quantitative
immunochemistry of the 1930s - none of which had their modern shape in
1906. Ehrlich's *Ambozeptor* and *Komplement* belonged to the
side-chain theory's chemical lock-and-key, without the quantitative
binding theory the modern claim presupposes. Calling Wassermann's
test "non-specific" presumes a contrast class he did not possess.
Condition three does this work.

The laboratory amendment is to broaden the category from
*metaphysical* presupposition to *technical-instrumental* presupposition.
The modern reading presupposes not only a worldview but a measurement
capacity - to titrate against pure cardiolipin, to distinguish IgM
from IgG, to compute binding constants - that Wassermann lacked. This
is not exactly metaphysics. It is the *equipment* of a thought style.
Montaigne's condition will catch it once the category includes the
instrumental, and I think it must: in laboratory science the
metaphysical and the instrumental are inextricable in a way they are
not in textual interpretation.

## Where I doubt the conditions are jointly sufficient

A reading of the Wassermann reaction can pass all three conditions -
identify a real molecular feature, be open to experimental surprise,
not impute later worldview - and still strip from the case what made
it function as a *fact* in 1906: the disease "syphilis" the test
detected was, for Wassermann's community, partly constituted by the
test itself. Patient serum that fixed complement *was* the diagnostic
object; the spirochete and the molecule lay in a wider explanatory
field the test did not need to enter. The modern reading that says
"the test detected the wrong thing" presupposes that the referent is
fixed independently of the practice, when in 1906 the referent was
co-constituted with it.

I cannot tell whether this concern is a fourth condition or a
sharpening of the first's refinement. Provisionally I treat it as the
latter: the selection of structural feature must respect the
*boundaries of the thought-object* the earlier practitioner was
working on, not only the features within whatever boundaries the
later framework supplies. I owe Montaigne the working out of this in
a separate piece. For now I record it as a genuine remainder.

The honest verdict: all three conditions transfer, two need stated
amendments for the bench case (condition one's refinement bears more
weight; condition three must include the instrumental), and a
laboratory-specific concern about the boundaries of the diagnostic
object survives the grid and asks for further work.
