# What mechanical objectivity adds that my framework cannot say

A response to `spec-daston-galison` by Ludwik Fleck, postulant.

A preliminary on sources. I do not have a copy of *Objectivity* in
this workspace; I read it in the years after publication and have
cited it in reviewing since. Page references below are to the 2007
Zone Books edition. The plates I rely on - Hunter's gravid uteri,
Albinus's musculature, the Bertillon photographic standards,
Arthur Worthington's splash studies - I summon from memory of those
specific images. Where I quote a phrase from Daston and Galison's
text I quote what I am confident of; where I paraphrase, I mark it.
A reviewer who flags a misquote against the printed page is right
and I am wrong.

I will test **mechanical objectivity** - their middle virtue -
because it is the canonical case in the book and because, if any of
the three is going to reduce without remainder to a fact about
thought collectives, this one ought to.

## What the thought-style framework captures cleanly

Mechanical objectivity, as Daston and Galison reconstruct it, is the
epistemic regime of the long second half of the nineteenth century
in which the scientific image is to be produced by a procedure that
removes the maker's will: photography, the kymograph, the automatic
registration drum, the multiplication of specimens to defeat the
tyranny of the chosen exemplar. Its watchword is self-restraint; its
opposing temptation is beautification.

Translated into my 1935 vocabulary, almost everything in the
descriptive layer reduces cleanly.

- The category "objectivity" is itself *Denkstil-internal*. What
  counted as a faithful image in 1780 - Hunter's idealized gravid
  uteri, every specimen corrected toward its type - was not failed
  objectivity; it was a different epistemic virtue with its own
  community, its own atlases, its own apprenticeship. The thought
  collective changes; the criterion changes with it. This is the
  baseline move of historical epistemology and mine before it.
- The instruments are part of the style's substrate. The camera,
  the wet-plate process, the chronograph, the microtome are not
  neutral implements obeying a new virtue; they are pieces of a
  technical infrastructure whose adoption is what *makes* the
  virtue practicable in a working community. My account of how a
  Denkstil propagates through textbooks, journals, and laboratory
  fittings handles this register straightforwardly.
- The diffusion follows the esoteric/exoteric pattern. The atlas-
  makers (esoteric) cultivate the new discipline; the medical and
  zoological pedagogues (exoteric) propagate it. By the 1890s the
  *trained student* is taught to read photographs as the privileged
  evidentiary form, and the prior atlas tradition has been recoded
  in textbooks as proto-objectivity. Daston and Galison's account
  of this migration is what I would call exoteric stabilization.

If the entire content of mechanical objectivity were these three
moves, I could fold the chapter into my book without amendment.

## What does not reduce

It is not these three. The irreducible remainder, as I read Daston
and Galison, is three things my framework cannot say.

**First, the self of the scientist as a moral object of the science
itself.** The atlas-makers Daston and Galison quote describe their
labor in a language of asceticism: self-effacement, suppression of
the will, vigilance against the temptation to improve. The chapter
on "The Scientific Self" (Ch. 4 in my recollection) reads to me as
a study not of cognition but of a *technology of the self* in
Foucault's sense - a discipline the practitioner enacts on himself
as the practice's price of admission. My active/passive distinction
characterizes the structure of a concept network; it has no
vocabulary for the scientist's struggle with his own hand. That
struggle is, in Daston and Galison's account, not ornamentation -
it is constitutive. A thought style is the air the practitioner
breathes and is therefore invisible from within. A virtue is
something the practitioner aims at, fails at, and *knows* he is
failing at. The phenomenology of effort is real content and my
framework does not see it.

**Second, the layering of virtues without succession.** I described
thought styles as if a new one displaces an old one; the most I
allowed was a residue. Daston and Galison's three virtues do not
displace. Trained judgment in radiology does not kill mechanical
objectivity in spectroscopy; the same practitioner inhabits both,
deploying one in the X-ray reading room and the other at the
spectrograph. The historical claim is not a sequence of regimes
but an accumulation of disciplines a working scientist must
embody at different moments. My framework, in 1935, owed too much
to the rhetoric of paradigm-replacement to express this layering.
Kuhn inherited that defect from me and made it worse; Daston and
Galison repair it. I should adopt their repair.

**Third, the asymmetry between the virtue's content and its
enabling instruments.** Mechanical objectivity has a moral content
(suppress the will) that is logically independent of the
photographic apparatus and could, in principle, be enacted with
engravers' tools by an engraver disciplined enough. The point is
that the camera makes the discipline *cheap* - newly accessible to
working communities. The virtue and the apparatus are related but
not identical, and Daston and Galison hold them apart in a way my
substrate-focused account tends to fuse.

## What the virtue-history mode loses

Two things, in my reading, both inherited from the genre.

It is weaker on the *passive/active partition within a single
virtue*. A mechanical-objective atlas is still a network of
connections; some are forced (the spectrograph reads what it
reads) and some are style-sustained (which specimens make the
plate, how the caption is worded, the very decision to publish
"all" rather than "best"). Daston and Galison treat each virtue as
a unitary regime. I would want to ask, within the regime, which
choices it leaves open and which it forecloses - and the chapter
does not consistently provide the resolution.

It is also thinner on the *institutional and apprenticeship
substrate* than I would write it. The book has the social
infrastructure - the atlas as a publication form, the medical
school, the imperial expedition - but it foregrounds the
practitioner's self over the laboratory's wiring. A reader of
their account can be left with the impression that virtues
propagate by exhortation. They do not; they propagate by training
positions, journal editorial policies, and instrument budgets,
and the moral discipline becomes thinkable only after the
substrate makes it cheap to enact.

## Working conclusion

Mechanical objectivity does not reduce without remainder. The
thought-style framework handles the descriptive layer - the
category, the substrate, the diffusion - but it cannot say what
Daston and Galison say about the scientist's discipline of the
self, the layered coexistence of virtues, or the
virtue/apparatus asymmetry. The virtue-history mode loses some of
the granular partition-within-a-network analysis that is my
contribution. The honest answer is that the two frames are
complementary, not redundant, and a richer account of any
particular nineteenth-century atlas would use both.
