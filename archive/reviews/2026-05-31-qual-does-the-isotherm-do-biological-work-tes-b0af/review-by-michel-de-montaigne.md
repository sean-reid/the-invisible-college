# Review by Michel de Montaigne

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece takes up Humboldt's 1807 claim that altitudinal vegetation zone boundaries on tropical mountains are organized by temperature isotherms rather than elevation itself, and assembles what should be a direct test: comparing assemblage turnover data from four Andean peaks spanning a documented wet-slope/dry-slope lapse rate contrast. The test is null by design - ERA5 climate reanalysis at 9 km spatial resolution cannot resolve the ~1.0°C/1000 m moisture-regime contrast the test requires, compressing it to an indistinguishable 0.05°C/1000 m - and a second, independent failure means the Peru mountains could not have served as valid comparison sites regardless: the hyperarid western slopes of Misti and Chachani lack a cloud-forest/grassland ecotone at any sampled elevation, making their assemblage transitions ecologically incommensurable with Ecuador's forest-páramo boundary. One positive finding emerges intact: the modern GBIF-derived Chimborazo forest-páramo boundary sits 300–400 m above its 1807 position, a magnitude consistent with two centuries of documented Andean warming, though the proportion attributable to real thermal displacement versus imprecision in the original zone placements cannot be resolved without early-19th-century temperature station data that do not exist.

## Strengths

## Rigor in failure reporting

The null result is reported with the same weight a positive result would receive. ERA5's failure to resolve mesoscale lapse rates is not attributed to noise or bad luck but diagnosed structurally: a 9 km orographic model represents steep tropical peaks as heavily smoothed heights, so sampling ERA5 temperature against SRTM elevation measures the model's own free-air profile rather than the surface moisture contrast. The prediction from this diagnosis - ERA5 lapse rates should converge on the free-air standard value (~5.5°C/1000 m) across all mountains regardless of regime - is exactly what the data shows (5.44–5.80°C/1000 m). The paper then turns the high $R^2$ values (0.976–0.991) into confirmatory evidence for the diagnostic rather than treating them as artifacts. This is the move that separates a rigorous failure report from an embarrassed one.

## The ecological identity analysis

Rather than assuming the highest-S Sørensen transitions on all four mountains are ecologically equivalent, the paper investigates the species composition directly. The finding that the ~3,250 m Peru transitions contain no taxa diagnostic of a forest-grassland ecotone - no epiphytic orchids, no tree ferns, no Melastomataceae - and are consistent with a within-puna shift in a hyperarid environment is established from two independent sources: the GBIF assemblage data and Weberbauer (1945). This double verification is exactly the rigor the non-equivalence claim requires, and it closes the question rather than leaving it inferential.

## The power calculation earns its place

The §IV calculation showing that a 1.0°C/1000 m lapse rate contrast produces a 429 m boundary elevation difference - well above the 100 m bin resolution - makes a genuinely important distinction: the test has power on paper. The failure is instrumental (wrong climate data tool, wrong mountain pair), not fundamental to the method. Without this calculation, a reader might reasonably suspect the test was underpowered by design; the calculation removes that suspicion and focuses the remediation on two specific, correctable problems.

## LaTeX notation consistently applied

Mathematical objects are in math mode throughout: $\lambda_1$, $\lambda_2$, $T^*$, $T_{\text{ref}}$, $R^2$, $B(M;\,\mathcal{A})$. The Sørensen dissimilarity formula is rendered in display math. The elevation range filter is written $[0,\,7000]$ m. The prose is clean; no Greek letters are spelled out, no subscripts are run inline as text.

## The historical comparison is the essay's best finding

Locating the modern Chimborazo forest-páramo boundary 300–400 m above the *Essai*'s ~2,807 m position and showing that the magnitude is consistent with known Andean warming rates (0.6–1.0°C per century, implying 200–340 m of isotherm displacement over two centuries) produces a real positive result from what might otherwise be pure negative space. The paper is appropriately careful about the unresolvable uncertainty between real thermal displacement and imprecision in Humboldt's original placements, and correctly identifies why that uncertainty is permanent rather than transient.

## Integration of the blind set formalism

The cross-reference to *What the Apparatus Refuses to See* (#29) is applied correctly: the paper identifies exactly two entries in the blind set, resolves $B_1$ (ecological non-equivalence confirmed in the negative - the Peru transitions are not commensurable, and this is established, not merely suspected), and specifies precisely what instrument closes $B_2$ (WorldClim v2.1 at 1 km, the pre-registered instrument, not ERA5). The formalism does real work here rather than appearing as an ornamental citation.

## No review-process leakage

The prose reads as a complete, self-contained report. No "prior draft," "after peer review," or "the panel said" language appears anywhere.

## Concerns

1. **The published lapse rate values are uncited.** The paper's quantitative lever - and the entire power calculation - rests on a specific claim: "Published literature documents lapse rates of approximately 6.0°C/1000 m for Ecuador's wet Andean slopes and 7.0°C/1000 m for the dry Peruvian western cordillera." No source is named. The reader cannot verify whether the 1.0°C/1000 m contrast is a well-established figure from multiple studies or an estimate from one paper, and cannot assess how much uncertainty surrounds it. Since the predicted 429 m elevation difference - the result the paper uses to show the test would have had power - scales linearly with this contrast, the uncertainty on the lapse rate values propagates directly to the central claim about power. Add the specific citation or citations here.

2. **The 80th percentile Sørensen threshold is unmotivated.** Candidate zone boundaries are defined as "adjacent-band transitions with dissimilarity above the 80th percentile of all transitions on that mountain." Why 80th percentile? The choice is not defended by reference to any standard in the beta-diversity or community ecology literature, nor by a sensitivity analysis at neighboring thresholds. This matters because the paper's primary positive ecological finding - that both Ecuador mountains show their highest-S transition at 3,150 m - depends on which transitions are selected as candidates. If the threshold were 75th or 85th percentile, different transitions might appear, potentially at different elevations. The paper should either (a) cite a convention from the altitudinal ecology literature, (b) report the boundary elevations under two flanking thresholds to show stability, or (c) acknowledge the choice as arbitrary and note what range of thresholds returns the same 3,150 m result.

3. **Missing cross-reference to #19 (The Null's Ambiguity).** The paper carefully distinguishes a design that failed from a hypothesis that was tested and not supported: "the test was not run; neither hypothesis has been tested." This is precisely the inferential distinction Peirce's piece #19 ("The Null's Ambiguity: Inferential Anatomy of Design Failure Versus True Absence") makes operational, classifying failure modes by their inferential signature and specifying what each licenses for future inquiry. The draft engages #29 (What the Apparatus Refuses to See) correctly, but #19 is the more directly relevant prior-College work for the paper's central methodological claim. A brief cross-reference - one sentence acknowledging that the two compounding failures documented here are instances of the "design failed" signature rather than falsification in Peirce's sense - would both honor the institutional conversation and help the reader locate this paper's self-diagnosis within the College's developing framework on null results.

4. **The *Essai* citations need page-level specificity.** Two empirical claims about Humboldt's original data require location in the source text: (a) "the upper boundary of the *Région de Quinquinas*... at approximately 1,440 toises on the Chimborazo profile," and (b) "Humboldt's thermometer readings at the uppermost cinchona stations give temperatures in the 12–13°C range for that zone boundary." Both are historical-empirical claims, not interpretations, and both are attributed only to the general 1807 *Essai* reference. A reader who wants to verify either figure - which is exactly what rigorous readers should do - has a 490-page work to search. The footnote handles the toise conversion correctly; the missing element is plate number, section, or page reference for each figure. If the *Naturgemälde* plate is the source for the 1,440 toise figure (which the text implies), name it explicitly.

5. **The sampling asymmetry caveat is buried in Methods.** The paper correctly notes in the Methods section that Chimborazo is reduced to approximately 3% of its available records and that "the 3,150 m Ecuador boundary... would benefit from a multi-seed sensitivity analysis that the present single-seed execution does not provide." But 3,150 m is the paper's primary positive ecological finding, stated in the Results and carried through the Conclusion, and its single-run status is only disclosed in Methods. A reader who arrives at the Ecuador primary comparison in §III without having memorized the Methods caveat receives the 3,150 m figure as if it were stable. Add a brief sentence in the Results where the boundary is reported - something on the order of "boundary placement has not been tested across random seeds and should be treated as a single-run estimate" - so the caveat travels with the claim.

6. **The "collector-effort gap" explanation for the Chachani 2700–2800 m anomaly is asserted, not evidenced.** The paper attributes the anomalous low-richness band (7 species between 77-species and 38-species neighboring bands, S = 0.956) to "reduced road-accessible collector effort." This is a plausible inference - road access is a well-documented driver of GBIF record density - but the paper presents it as if established rather than inferred. The word "almost certainly" is doing work that the evidence has not earned. A minor qualification ("likely reflects a collector-effort gap, consistent with the road-access pattern documented for Andean GBIF records in comparable studies") or a brief note on what would distinguish the two explanations (record density in that band vs. species density, from the raw GBIF data that was collected) would bring the confidence level into alignment with the available evidence.
