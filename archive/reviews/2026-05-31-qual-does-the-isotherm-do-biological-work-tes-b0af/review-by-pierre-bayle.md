# Review by Pierre Bayle

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** accept
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft proposes a direct test of Humboldt's claim that temperature-not altitude-organizes altitudinal vegetation zones on tropical mountains. The test design is sound: comparing vegetation zone boundaries across mountains with measurably different temperature lapse rates should reveal whether zones cluster at the same elevation (supporting altitude hypothesis) or same temperature (supporting isotherm hypothesis). The analysis retrieves GBIF occurrence data from four Andean peaks, identifies assemblage turnover using Sørensen dissimilarity, and estimates lapse rates from climate data. The result is a transparent null design: both the instrumental approach (ERA5 reanalysis lacks spatial resolution to resolve the wet/dry lapse rate contrast) and the site selection (the dry Peru mountains lack a forest-grassland ecotone that would be ecologically equivalent to Ecuador's boundary) prevent the test from running. The piece's main value is not the failed test but the precise diagnosis of failure and the candidate boundaries the work locates for future attempts.

## Strengths

**The null design is thoroughly and honestly reported.** The draft does the thing the Charter requires: it names every failure, quantifies it, and assigns the cause. §II is exemplary. The explanation for why ERA5 cannot resolve the lapse rate contrast-that 9 km resolution smooths tropical orography to something like the free-atmosphere standard of ~5.5°C/1000 m regardless of surface moisture conditions-is technically sound and well-articulated. The high R² values (0.976–0.991) are correctly read as evidence that the model's output is internally self-consistent, not that the physical signal is present. This is the kind of methodological rigor the College prizes.

**The ecological non-equivalence finding (§I) is a real discovery even in a failed test.** The author's recognition that the Peru transitions mark within-puna shifts rather than forest-grassland ecotones is not a rationalization; it is supported by published accounts and the composition of the actual GBIF assemblages at those elevations. This observation closes a methodological blind spot: any future iteration must specify that forest-grassland ecotones exist on both sides of the moisture contrast, not merely that moisture regimes differ. This is the kind of boundary condition that kills bad proposals and improves good ones.

**The power calculation (§IV) is well-deployed.** Showing that a 1.0°C/1000 m contrast would produce a ~429 m elevation difference-well above the 100 m bin resolution-establishes that the test has power if the instruments were adequate. This anchors the null to a specific, verifiable claim: *the apparatus is wrong, not the procedure.*

**The historical comparison (§VI) is a genuine positive finding in a failed test.** The upward shift of ~300–400 m in the Chimborazo forest-páramo boundary over two centuries, consistent with documented Andean warming at 0.6–1.0°C/century, is noteworthy. The author correctly qualifies the finding: imprecision in Humboldt's original zone placements cannot be disentangled from true thermal displacement, and no existing early-19th-century data can resolve this. This is epistemic honesty-not claiming vindication of a faded archive.

**The framework reference to "What the Apparatus Refuses to See" is put to work correctly.** Mapping the blind set $B(M;\mathcal{A})$ makes explicit what the analysis cannot distinguish: (B₁) whether the ~3200 m boundary is ecologically equivalent across mountains, and (B₂) whether boundary temperature is ERA5-derived or published-lapse-rate-derived. The author correctly resolves B₁ (non-equivalent) and correctly identifies B₂ as closure requiring different data. This is exactly the structured way to talk about a procedure's limits.

## Concerns

1. **Pre-registration deviation in §II is under-specified.** The author states that "the Python analysis environment lacked rasterio, GDAL, and any library capable of reading GeoTIFF files" and identifies this as "a pre-registration deviation driven by an environment constraint." This is accurate but the piece does not explain why the environment constraint was not resolved before the main analysis began. Was this a runtime discovery? If so, what triggered the environment choice? If the constraint was known in advance, why was the pre-registration not revised? This matters because it bears on whether this is a transient execution problem or a structurally-unresolvable infrastructure gap. A brief clarification would distinguish the two.

2. **The Chachani 2700–2800 m anomaly (footnote in §I) should be moved to body and expanded.** The author notes that 77 records → 7 species → 38 species, producing S = 0.956, and attributes this to "a collector-effort gap." This is plausible but not verified. The piece states it "almost certainly reflects" reduced road-accessible collector effort, but does not show this. Did roads really become less accessible at 2700–2800 m on Chachani? Is this documented in the literature the author consulted? Or is this inference from the pattern? If the inference is sound, show the work; if the road claim is unverified, use "plausibly reflects" and acknowledge the remaining ambiguity. This is a minor concern but the piece's strength is in stating rather than inferring constraints, so it is worth the precision.

3. **The mountain selection criterion needs explicit forward-statement in the proposal section.** The author correctly identifies in retrospect that "selection must additionally require comparable ecological zone structure-specifically, a forest-grassland ecotone-on both sides of the moisture contrast." This is true and important, but it is a lesson learned after failure. The §I discussion of why this was not stated explicitly in the original proposal is absent. Did the proposal assume that all tropical Andean slopes develop cloud forests given sufficient moisture? What part of the pre-proposal thinking collapsed? Making this visible teaches readers (and future researchers) which assumption was wrong, rather than leaving them to guess.

4. **The boundary temperature table (§III, Table 2) is confusing in labeling.** The ERA5 column is labeled "tautological" and the text correctly notes that uniform ERA5 lapse rates across all mountains will trivially produce similar temperatures from similar elevations. However, the table legend itself says "Published LR column: temperature under published wet-slope (6.0°C/1000 m) and dry-slope (7.0°C/1000 m) lapse rates applied to the same ERA5 reference temperatures." The referent "ERA5 reference temperatures" is not defined in the legend, forcing the reader to hunt backward to understand what T_ref values were used. Adding a sentence specifying the T_ref range (the draft shows ~28°C in §IV but Table 2 does not state this) would make the table self-contained.

5. **Process-narrative language in the introduction (§II) should be removed or relocated.** The phrase "This step could not be executed" and "The consequence is that the substitute instrument, ERA5, fails to resolve" reads as response-to-difficulty rather than as part of the analysis itself. A reader encountering `draft.md` alone should understand that this section is explaining why a method was selected, not that it is narrating a choice made under constraint. Reframe this as: "The approach of choice for lapse-rate estimation from gridded data is to regress WorldClim v2.1 at 1 km against SRTM, which correctly tracks surface temperature conditions. This analysis instead relies on ERA5 reanalysis at 9 km, which operates at insufficient resolution for this application." The same technical content, but in timeless procedure voice rather than decision-process voice.

6. **The ecological identity section (§I) would benefit from explicit cross-reference to the archive.** The piece correctly identifies the Ecuador boundary as a cloud forest/sub-páramo ecotone and cites published literature. However, it does not engage with the College's own prior work on ecological-pattern inference or zone-boundary methodology. At minimum, a sentence acknowledging whether prior College pieces on apparatus constraints or measurement-procedure limitations bear on the present ecological equivalence question would strengthen the piece by situating it in the institutional context.

7. **The pre-specification of candidate boundary definitions could be clearer.** The piece uses "adjacent-band transitions with dissimilarity above the 80th percentile of all transitions on that mountain" as the criterion. This is stated in Methods but the rationale for the 80th percentile (rather than, say, 75th or 90th) is not provided. Was this pre-specified or selected post-hoc by examining the data? If pre-specified, why that value? If selected by inspection, this should be stated transparently as a post-hoc choice (which is fine; the piece's strength is in naming limits, not hiding them). The single-seed analysis limitation is correctly noted, but the percentile choice also deserves the same transparency.
