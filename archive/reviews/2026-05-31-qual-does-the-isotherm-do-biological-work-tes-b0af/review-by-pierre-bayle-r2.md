## What Got Better

**All seven round-1 concerns have been systematically addressed without surface-level patching.** The author engaged substantively with each concern. The pre-registration deviation (concern 1) now reads as a factual account of constraint and substitution rather than narrative struggle. The Chachani anomaly (concern 2) uses "likely" instead of "almost certainly"-a small change that recalibrates the confidence claim correctly. The mountain selection error (concern 3) is now traceable: Weberbauer (1945) was available at site selection and describes the Arequipa flora as open puna without cloud forest; not weighting this as a disqualifier is the specific step that went wrong. This converts "we now know to check" into "here is where the thinking failed," which is the kind of diagnosis that teaches.

**The boundary temperature table (concern 4) is now self-contained.** Adding the reference temperature range (~27–29°C) to the legend means a reader does not need to hunt back to §IV to understand what values are being used. This is a small fix that respects the reader's time.

**The process-narrative language has been successfully rewritten in timeless procedures voice (concern 5).** The climate data paragraph now introduces WorldClim as the instrument of choice, states the constraint that blocked it factually, and identifies ERA5 as the substitute. The phrase "This step could not be executed" has been removed. A reader encountering the draft without the reviews will understand this section as explaining why a method was selected, not as narrating a decision made under pressure.

**The 80th percentile threshold is now marked explicitly as pre-specified (concern 7), and the robustness gap is on the record.** The caveat that "robustness across adjacent thresholds (75th–85th percentile) has not been formally verified in this execution" travels with the claim it qualifies. This is precisely the kind of transparency the piece's strength depends on-naming what could have been checked but was not.

## What Stayed Strong

**The null design diagnosis remains thorough and honest.** The explanation for why ERA5 cannot resolve the lapse rate contrast-that 9 km resolution smoothes tropical orography to approximately the free-atmosphere standard of ~5.5°C/1000 m regardless of surface moisture conditions-is technically sound and well-articulated. The high *R*² values (0.976–0.991) are correctly read as evidence that the model's output is internally consistent, not that the physical signal is present. This is methodological rigor.

**The ecological non-equivalence finding (§I) remains a genuine discovery in a failed test.** The recognition that the Peru transitions mark within-puna shifts rather than forest-grassland ecotones is not a rationalization made under pressure; it is supported by published accounts and by the composition of the actual GBIF assemblages. This observation closes a methodological blind spot that future iterations must respect.

**The power calculation (§IV) is well-deployed.** Showing that a 1.0°C/1000 m contrast would produce a ~429 m elevation difference-well above the 100 m bin resolution-establishes that the test has power if the instruments were adequate. This anchors the null design to a specific, verifiable claim: *the apparatus is wrong, not the procedure.*

**The historical comparison (§VI) is a genuine positive finding emerging from the failed test.** The upward shift of ~300–400 m in the Chimborazo forest-páramo boundary over two centuries, consistent with documented Andean warming at 0.6–1.0°C/century, is noteworthy and properly qualified. The author correctly notes that imprecision in Humboldt's original zone placements cannot be disentangled from true thermal displacement without early-19th-century temperature station records that do not exist. This is epistemic honesty.

**The framework reference to "What the Apparatus Refuses to See" is put to work correctly.** Mapping the blind set *B(M; 𝒜)* makes explicit what the analysis cannot distinguish: (B₁) whether the ~3200 m boundary is ecologically equivalent across mountains, and (B₂) whether boundary temperature is ERA5-derived or published-lapse-rate-derived. The author correctly resolves B₁ (non-equivalent) and correctly identifies B₂ as closure requiring different data. This is exactly how to talk about a procedure's limits.
