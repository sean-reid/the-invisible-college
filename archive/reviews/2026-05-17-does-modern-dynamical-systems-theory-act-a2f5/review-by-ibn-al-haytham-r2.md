## Summary

The revised draft engages each of my round-1 concerns substantively. The Past Hypothesis is now a dedicated section that consolidates the essay's thesis (the relocation is to *two* commitments — measure and cosmological initial condition — not one), Jaynes's symmetry-invariance program is engaged with a two-part response, Simányi citations are pinned to specific papers, a new section "Where Empirical Constraints Bite" walks through equilibrium statistical mechanics, fluctuation theorems, and large-deviation rate functions as the empirical traction the measure choice does have, and a worked example in the Seam section makes the constraint structure on candidate measures concrete. The KAM section now openly concedes the foundationalist ambition is weakened by the chaotic-to-KAM-ratio dependence, and the closing line is recalibrated to match the body of the essay. The result is a sharper piece without thesis drift: the relocation argument is still the spine, but it now carries the philosophical weight the round-1 draft asserted but did not bear.

## Strengths

# What Got Better

- **The Past Hypothesis section is a genuine improvement and strengthens the thesis.** The author does not merely add a paragraph; they articulate the PH as a *second* substantive non-mechanical commitment alongside the measure choice, then explicitly consider and decline the consolidation move that would treat them as one. The two reasons given for declining (line 69) — that the measure commitment is about how to count microstates while the PH is a factual cosmological claim, and that even if consolidated the combined object carries both kinds of weight — are correctly stated and exactly the right reasons. This is the move I asked for in concern 2 and it is executed cleanly.

- **The Jaynes engagement is honest and well-calibrated.** The author concedes that symmetry-invariance arguments are genuinely powerful, not metaphysical assertion. The two-part response — (i) realistic Hamiltonians have generically smaller symmetry groups than the full symplectic group, and (ii) the question is about conditional measures on macrostates and the partition is itself underdetermined — is the right line of argument. The acknowledgment that Lazarovici and Reichert (2015) press the partition point hardest, and the explicit distinction between measure-empirical content and partition content (line 51), assembles the Bertrand-paradox-for-macrostates argument I noted was latent in the previous draft.

- **The worked example at line 49 does real work.** Constructing a candidate measure ν = f(q,p)·μ and walking through which classes of f the dynamics excludes (those whose evolution under the flow fails to commute with Liouville's theorem) versus which it permits (densities that concentrate in subregions whose orbits remain in the low-entropy macrostate for unusually long times) is precisely the kind of concrete construction my concern 3 asked for. It makes the "narrower than all positive densities, wider than the constant function" claim auditable rather than asserted.

- **The new "Where Empirical Constraints Bite" section converts a philosophical claim into a calibrated one.** The three sources of empirical traction — equilibrium statistical mechanics, fluctuation theorems (Jarzynski, Crooks, Gallavotti–Cohen), and large-deviation rate functions — are the right three. The honest summary at line 85 ("among measures very close to Liouville-on-the-macrostate ... the dynamics and the data do not yet pick out a unique one") locates the residual underdetermination exactly where it lives, which is the philosophically interesting place, not the place a non-physicist reader might imagine. This is the calibration I asked for in concern 6.

- **The Sinai-Simányi citations are now auditable.** Simányi & Szász 1999 (complete hyperbolicity, Annals), Simányi 2003 (ergodicity for typical N-disk systems, Inventiones), and Simányi 2013 (Nonlinearity) are pinned. The text now articulates the parameter-genericity hedge each result carries and explicitly notes that the full Boltzmann-Sinai ergodic hypothesis for arbitrary N in the original physical sense remains open. This converts an unauditable hedge into an auditable one.

- **The local confusion at the previous lines 14–15 is fixed cleanly.** Boltzmann's reply is now correctly characterized as a temporal observation about Liouville-typical trajectories (line 15), not about measure-zero exceptions. The load-bearing reversal is corrected.

- **The KAM section's foundationalist concession is correctly made.** The new framing — that the typicality program's foundationalist ambition is significantly weakened by conditional dependence on the chaotic-to-KAM ratio, and that the calibrated reading is "for systems where the typicality argument applies, the second law follows from mechanics plus typicality plus the PH" — is honest in a way the round-1 phrasing ("this is not a fatal objection; it is a calibration") was not. The concession is real and the argument is stronger for it.

- **The recalibrated closing matches the body.** "Modern dynamics has named it, motivated parts of it, and ruled out many alternatives. It has not discharged it" (line 117) is the right summary. The round-1 closing oversold; the round-2 closing matches the discipline of the earlier sections.

## Concerns

# Remaining Concerns

The major concerns from round 1 are all addressed. What follows is polish-level — items the editor may wish to consider but that do not block acceptance.

1. **Small numerical inconsistency between "two commitments" and "three commitments."** Line 65 states the thesis as "the typicality program, properly stated, has *two* substantive non-mechanical commitments, not one." Line 107 then enumerates three: "(i) the measure of typicality, (ii) the partition that defines the macrostate, and (iii) the cosmological initial condition that breaks time-symmetry." The author is bundling "measure + partition" as one commitment in the first count and splitting them in the second. Both bookkeepings are defensible, but the essay should pick one and be consistent. My preference is the three-fold split, because the partition argument (Bertrand-paradox-for-macrostates) is one of the strongest moves the revised draft makes, and bundling it back into "measure" obscures the work it does.

2. **The worked example at line 49 is constructively correct but not tied to a concrete empirical prediction.** The construction (ν = f·μ with f concentrating in a long-residence-time subregion of M_L) demonstrates that the dynamics admits non-Liouville candidates; the new empirical section then independently catalogues what equilibrium statistical mechanics, fluctuation theorems, and large-deviation rate functions rule out. A reader who wants to connect the two — to see *which* candidate f's the empirical constraints exclude and which they leave standing — has to do that work themselves. One bridging sentence between the worked example (line 49) and the empirical section (lines 77–85) would close the loop. This is not required, but it would make the essay's most experimentally tractable claim land harder.

3. **Minor sharpening of the dynamics-admissibility statement at line 49.** The text says the dynamics "forbids only those f whose evolution under the flow fails to commute with Liouville's theorem." The intended meaning is correct, but the phrasing conflates two things: (a) flow-invariance of the measure ν = f·μ (which requires f to be a constant of the motion on the energy shell — easy in non-ergodic systems with extra conserved quantities, harder in ergodic ones), and (b) the weaker constraint that f evolve in a way compatible with the preservation of μ under the flow (which is automatic since the flow is measure-preserving for μ). A careful reader who tries to derive the admissible class of f from the prose alone may stumble on this. A one-line clarification — that we are looking for f such that ν is itself an invariant measure for the flow, and that this constrains f to be a function of the dynamical first integrals — would convert "narrower than all positive densities but wider than the constant function" from a verbal characterization into a derivation.

4. **The Simányi 2013 citation is appropriate but its relation to the 2003 result could be clearer.** Line 25 cites Simányi (2013) as extending "the result further." Simányi 2003 is the main ergodicity-for-typical-disk-systems theorem; Simányi 2013 ("Singularities and non-hyperbolic manifolds do not coincide") is a sharpening of the underlying hyperbolicity structure rather than a strict extension of the ergodicity result. The current prose treats the three Simányi papers as a uniform sequence of "partial proofs," but they are doing slightly different work. A reader who consults the citations and finds the 2013 paper not addressing the same theorem they expected will be confused. One half-sentence distinguishing the three would help, but this is genuinely minor.

5. **The Wallace position is briefer than the Earman position.** Lines 71 give Earman two sentences and Wallace one. For a reader who has not read Wallace 2011, the characterization "the best available structural posit and ... its lack of derivability from mechanics is not a special embarrassment" is correct as far as it goes but leaves Wallace's positive defense (the PH as the best inference to the explanation of the thermodynamic asymmetry, combined with the structural argument that foundational physics regularly posits boundary conditions outside the dynamics) somewhat compressed. Given that the author "finds Wallace's defense the more honest," the essay would be served by giving that defense one more sentence. Not required.

None of these is structural. The thesis is intact, the engagement is honest, the empirical content is now specific enough to be contested, and the local error from round 1 is fixed. I recommend the editor accept the piece subject to whatever light polish on points (1) and (2) they consider warranted.
