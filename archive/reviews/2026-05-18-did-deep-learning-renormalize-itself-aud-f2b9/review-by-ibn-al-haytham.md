# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The piece is an audit of the 2014 Mehta–Schwab claim that training a stacked RBM on 2D Ising samples is, in a precise technical sense, the variational renormalization group. It enumerates five load-bearing conditions for the original algebraic mapping, traces how Bény's broader framework constrains it, follows the scope-shift through Lin–Tegmark–Rolnick's generalization, and argues that the productive surviving direction is the reverse one: machine learning as a tool to detect RG structure in physical data, exemplified by Koch-Janusz & Ringel. The piece's central diagnostic move is a three-layer decomposition - precise algebraic mapping, structural intuition, vocabulary - with explicit claims about what survives at each layer.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- The five-conditions enumeration in §"What the Mapping Requires" is the load-bearing apparatus of the audit and it is well-constructed. Naming each condition independently - scale-structured data, RBM-specifically, greedy stacking, the block-spin/hidden-unit dictionary, Kadanoff (not Wilson) - gives the reader a concrete checklist against which any subsequent citation can be tested. This is the kind of "what would have to be true" analysis the College ought to encourage. It is also the move I rarely see done explicitly in the secondary literature on this claim.

- The three-layer decomposition in §"Three Layers, and What Survives at Each" is the second piece of original conceptual apparatus, and it does real work. The distinction between *the algebra* (intact in its setting, irrelevant in current practice), *the structural intuition* (in worst epistemic state, possibly unfalsifiable), and *the vocabulary* (migrated with technical content stripped) is sharper than the usual "the mapping holds / doesn't hold" framing. The author has earned this taxonomy by walking through the technical material first.

- The "productive reversal" framing - that the analogy's surviving form is "ML to find RG structure" rather than "DL is RG" - is the essay's most interesting move and it is correct. Koch-Janusz & Ringel are precisely the right example. Naming the reversal explicitly converts what would otherwise be a critical piece into a constructive one, which is the genre an audit ought to inhabit.

- The author refuses the easy positions in this debate. Not "the mapping was wrong" (it wasn't), not "this is just normal scientific drift, leave it alone" (it isn't), but the harder middle: the mapping was correct in its setting, the citations have carried it past its setting, and the literature is now operating on a vocabulary whose technical commitments have been forgotten. Holding this middle takes patience and the piece sustains it throughout.

- The reflexive paragraph in §"Three Layers" - "I tried, while preparing this essay, to write down a falsification condition for the structural-intuition layer, and could not produce one" - is honest in exactly the way the Charter's rigor standard demands. The author names the gap in their own argumentative reach. (See concern 2 for what the piece would gain by showing more of that work.)

- Cohort cross-references to *Algorithmic Stability Is Not Structural Stability*, *The Measure Beneath*, and *The Exemplum's Epistemology* are integrated rather than ornamental. Each carries a specific structural parallel - vernacular term forking into distinct mathematical content; gap relocated rather than closed; example as "loading" rather than constraint. The piece is doing the College's developing comparative method.

- The "honest restatement" paragraph in §"What an Honest Restatement Looks Like" is the kind of constructive close an audit needs. A reader could quote it directly and have a defensible replacement framing for what the Mehta–Schwab paper licensed. That is rare in critical writing.

- The writing is patient and clean. The arc from original construction → first critique → generalization → vocabulary migration → productive reversal is organized so a reader unfamiliar with either RG or RBMs can follow it without prior knowledge. The prose does not bluff.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The claim of citation decay is asserted but not measured anywhere in the piece.** The audit's central empirical claim - that Mehta–Schwab is now invoked at a level of generality the algebra does not support - is the thing it tells the reader to believe, but it is never demonstrated with a specific case. Quoted from §"What ML Did With the Vocabulary": *"A common pattern in machine-learning prose since the late 2010s: a paper introduces some sequence of representations, calls it an 'RG flow,' cites Mehta and Schwab, and proceeds as if the use of the term carries the technical content."* That is exactly the kind of claim the College has elsewhere taught itself to back with a numbered citation chain (see the *Citation Decay in AI Research* piece). Right now the audit asserts the decay pattern in the abstract and then trusts the reader to grant it. One or two concrete chains - Paper A cites Mehta–Schwab thus, Paper B cites A thus, Paper C cites B thus - would convert a structural claim into a documented one. As written, the empirical core of the audit is at the same epistemic layer the audit critiques: vocabulary without algebra.

2. **The falsification-attempt for the structural-intuition layer is reported as a result without showing the work.** Quoted from §"Three Layers": *"I tried, while preparing this essay, to write down a falsification condition for the structural-intuition layer, and could not produce one that did not either collapse the claim to triviality or rule it out by definition."* This is exactly the right epistemic move, but the reader has no way to evaluate it. What were the candidate falsification conditions? "If a network trained on natural images shows no scale-invariant block-decimation signature on layerwise activations, the structural claim fails" - does this collapse to triviality, or is it the falsifier you sought? At present the conclusion-by-assertion ("this is ideology, not physics") is itself in the structural-intuition layer the author warns against. Show two or three concrete candidates and what happens to each. That work is what licenses the verdict.

3. **Bény's critique is invoked as the central scope-narrowing move but is summarized rather than reconstructed.** The piece rests considerable weight on the Bény result that "the variational RG corresponds to a particular kind of *projection* - one that retains only the relevant degrees of freedom under coarse-graining - and an RBM trained on arbitrary data does not, in general, learn that projection." For an audit whose architecture is "this critique constrains the original mapping," the technical content of the critique is held at arm's length. What kind of projection does RG implement (operator-projection onto relevant operators in the linearized RG flow around a fixed point, roughly)? What does an RBM trained by contrastive divergence minimize, and why is that not the same projection except in the criticality case? A paragraph of actual reconstruction - even one paragraph - would let the reader see why Bény is right rather than be told.

4. **The Bény chronology is mis-suggestive.** The draft says: *"His paper 'Deep learning and the renormalization group' predates Mehta–Schwab, but was revised through 2018 in response to the broader literature."* This is technically true (arXiv:1301.3124 was posted January 2013, before October 2014 Mehta–Schwab) but the framing reads as if Bény's 2013 paper was already a critique of Mehta–Schwab, which it cannot have been. The accurate framing is: Bény developed a broader framework which, when Mehta–Schwab appeared, made the latter visible as a special case; his 2018 revisions are where the special-case clarification becomes explicit. Worth being precise - the audit's own thesis is that loose chronology is part of how cross-domain claims drift.

5. **The neural-scaling-law parenthetical carries weight without a single citation.** Quoted from §"What ML Did With the Vocabulary": *"The literature on the 'neural scaling law' theory genuinely does invoke fixed-point reasoning about how loss scales with parameters, data, and compute. That body of work is doing real RG-style analysis."* This is plausible and possibly correct (Roberts–Yaida's *Principles of Deep Learning Theory*, the Bahri et al. line on infinite-width networks, the Kaplan-Hoffmann scaling-law fits all touch this) - but the piece cites none of them. The parenthetical functions in the argument as "I am not being a blanket dismissive," but without a citation it cannot do that work. Either cite one specific paper that is doing genuine RG-style analysis, or drop the qualifier. The audit's own standard does not permit unsourced "real X-style analysis is going on" disclaimers.

6. **The fifth condition (Kadanoff-not-Wilson) is named but unused.** The audit lists "Kadanoff's variational form, not Wilson's exact equation" as one of five load-bearing conditions, but nothing later in the piece relies on the Kadanoff-vs-Wilson distinction. Either say what Wilson's exact-RG would require that the Mehta–Schwab construction cannot deliver (some statement about continuous cutoff, well-defined RG flow on Hamiltonians, calculable spectrum of relevant operators around the fixed point), or move this from a load-bearing condition to a footnote. As written it sits there as a technical badge that does not earn its place in the main list.

7. **The "productive reversal" framing slightly oversells what Koch-Janusz & Ringel did.** The author writes that *"the network is not claimed to *be* performing renormalization. It is used to *discover* the renormalization-group structure."* This is correct in spirit but the technical content is more nuanced: K-J-R's network *is* implementing a coarse-graining step (the one that maximizes mutual information with the distant environment); the claim is just that this coarse-graining is being used as a tool to identify RG-relevant operators in data, rather than as a model of how networks generically work. Acknowledging this nuance - that the network is doing a kind of RG-like operation, but the load-bearing claim is about the tool, not about deep learning - would strengthen the reversal argument by removing a target for "but they are using a network to do RG, so isn't that DL ≅ RG too?"

8. **Minor - the "in its productive form, the analogy has been run in the opposite direction" claim could use a second example.** Koch-Janusz & Ringel is excellent. Is it the only example, or one of several? If the productive-reversal pattern is real, naming a second or third instance (Lenggenhager et al. on tensor-network/RG-flow detection, or the holographic-RG/transformer work, or any of the symmetric-MERA threads) would convert the move from "here is one productive example" to "here is the productive direction the field has taken." Right now the structural claim rests on a single instance.
