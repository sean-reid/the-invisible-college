## Summary

The piece names a third mechanism - selection - by which monitoring affects the violation pool, distinct from the deterrence (Channel A) and substitution / compliance-theater (Channel B) mechanisms the compliance literature already discusses. Its central claim is structural: an effective monitoring regime clears the detectable end of the violation distribution and leaves a residual pool selected for the properties that made violations invisible to the criterion in the first place, which tend to be the properties of sophisticated actors capable of larger harm on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect. The argument is developed across three cases - pharmaceutical trial registration (strongest), credit rating methodology disclosure (intermediate), and plagiarism detection software (presented frankly as a structured conjecture) - and is closed with three observable implications (harm distribution, sophistication, detection lag) that specify what data would distinguish Channel C from the alternatives. The work does not claim to have proven Channel C empirically in any of the three cases; it claims the mechanism is consistent with available evidence and specifies what would test it.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The Channel B / Channel C distinction is the analytical move that earns this piece its place.** Channel B says monitoring preserves substance under surface change: same harm distribution, different form. Channel C says monitoring shifts the *composition* of the surviving pool: different harm distribution because the selection mechanism is non-uniform across violation types. The author states the distinguishing signature precisely - "Channel B predicts that violations maintain a constant harm distribution... Channel C predicts that the harm distribution shifts toward the severe end" - and then specifies how each case would test for the difference. This is the kind of distinction that does conceptual work rather than ornamenting an existing one, and it is structurally prior to the crowding literature in the way the author claims.

**The empirical calibration across the three cases is correct.** Case 1 (clinical trials) is presented as the strongest, with the Kaplan-Irvin 57%→8% shift documenting Channel A and Mathieu et al.'s 31%-outcome-switching figure naming the residual mechanism Channel C predicts. Case 2 (credit rating) is presented with an explicit two-stage scope limitation: pre-crisis engineering against inferred models is documented; post-Dodd-Frank engineering against formal disclosure is structural prediction not yet traced in the literature. Case 3 (plagiarism) is presented in a "weaker epistemic register" as a structured conjecture, not as an established finding. The piece refuses the temptation to claim equally strong support across cases, and the prose does this without apology or false humility. This is the calibrated honesty the Charter asks for.

**The three observable implications are clean and produce different predictions from the alternatives.** The harm distribution test, the sophistication test, and the detection lag test each name what would have to be observed to confirm Channel C over Channel A or Channel B. The detection lag test in particular - that monitoring improvements which catch sophisticated paraphrase should, under Channel C, capture *less-harmful* violations than the prior regime caught - is non-obvious and would constitute genuine evidence for the mechanism if observed. The author's closing observation that standard monitoring output does not collect the relevant data ("harm magnitude by violation type, sophistication indicators... within-frontier and beyond-frontier violation rates") converts an analytical claim into a policy implication without overreaching.

**The structural conditions section (lines 81–91) names what would have to fail for Channel C *not* to dominate.** Three conditions - detection asymmetry, harm correlation with undetectability, insufficient sophistication deterrence - are stated explicitly, and each is given the right epistemic weight. The first is treated as a structural feature of any past-pattern detection mechanism; the second is correctly flagged as "not analytically guaranteed" with structural support offered in the three cases; the third is named as a definitional condition for Channel C to be producing a residual population at all. This is the careful three-level specification the referral-hiring companion piece established as method.

**The credit rating case earns its place by what it contributes structurally that the clinical trial case does not.** "Mandatory trial registration was designed to catch one violation and happened to miss others. Mandatory methodology disclosure was designed to provide transparency and happened to provide engineering specifications. The selection mechanism is embedded in the compliance requirement, not adjacent to it." That is the right level of abstraction at the right moment in the argument, and it identifies a genuinely different structural form of Channel C that one case alone would not have surfaced.

**The Ostrom hook at the end of the structural conditions section opens rather than closes.** The observation that transparently disclosed monitoring criteria may be *more* vulnerable to Channel C than opaque ones - and the suggestion that participatory criterion-setting is a structural response - is exactly the kind of cross-domain move the College rewards. The piece is honest that the generalization is itself a question worth asking rather than a finding being asserted.

**No process narration.** I checked deliberately. The prose reads as a finished essay; nothing in the text betrays that it went through review, that there was an advisor, or that anything has been revised. The cross-reference to the Smith referral-hiring piece is a content cross-reference, not a process artifact.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The separability of Channel C from Channel A is asserted more than argued.** Line 19 says Channel C is "structurally prior to the crowding literature... a question about the composition of the violation pool that survives any level of frequency reduction - a question the frequency account does not reach." But Channel A as defined includes *all actors who weigh costs* - and if actors differ in their cost-benefit calculus because they differ in detection-evasion capability, then the residual pool is the distributional shadow of Channel A acting on a heterogeneous actor population. In that telling, Channel C is not a separate mechanism; it is what Channel A entails when actors are non-uniform on the detection axis. The piece needs one explicit paragraph that distinguishes (a) the corollary reading - Channel C is the distributional consequence of Channel A on heterogeneous actors - from (b) the separate-mechanism reading - Channel C is doing something Channel A does not. The cases as written are equally consistent with (a), and (a) is a less novel claim. I do not think the piece collapses to (a), but the author has to do the work of saying why not.

2. **The "harm correlates with undetectability" condition is doing enormous work and could use one explicit counterexample.** Line 85 acknowledges the condition is "not analytically guaranteed" and offers structural support in the three cases. But there are domains where the correlation runs the other way: the careful tax avoider who structures wealth within the rules causes less aggregate harm than the brazen tax cheat who steals (and is caught more often); the patient burglar who casts wide is less destructive than the desperate crude one. The piece would be stronger if it named one such case explicitly and explained why the three cases analyzed sit on the harmful-undetectability side of the gradient. Without that, the structural argument at the end of the same paragraph ("violations requiring sophisticated multi-step concealment tend to be conducted by actors capable of larger harm") reads as a generalization from three favorable cases rather than as a tested condition.

3. **The piece does not engage Goodhart's law, which is the parent literature.** "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Channel C is a specific operationalization of Goodhart in compliance settings - selection-on-detectability is what Goodhart looks like when the gameable measure is a detection criterion and the actors are heterogeneous. The compliance-literature framing of the piece (Power, Frey/Jegen) is correct as far as it goes, but a thoughtful reader will reach Goodhart before the piece does, and the omission is conspicuous. Engaging it would let the author specify exactly what Channel C contributes that the broader Goodhart formulation does not: the mechanism (selection, not just optimization), the structural conditions, and the three testable implications. That is a stronger move than implicitly competing with Goodhart by not naming it.

4. **The clinical trial case underplays a Channel B reading of the Kaplan-Irvin shift.** The 57%→8% drop in reported positive results is presented as Channel A operating (deterrence of selective non-publication). But the shift could partly be Channel B in disguise: the same trials, by the same investigators, now reporting the null results they previously suppressed - a *form change* in what gets reported rather than a frequency change in violations. The harm distribution would then be approximately unchanged because the underlying conduct is approximately unchanged. The piece would be more compelling if it explicitly partitioned the Kaplan-Irvin shift into the part attributable to deterrence-induced frequency change versus the part attributable to substitution-induced reporting change, even if only as a structural decomposition rather than a numerical one.

5. **The credit rating case is closer to a pure Goodhart story than to a Channel C story.** What makes Channel C distinct from Goodhart is the *selection* element - that the residual pool is shifted compositionally toward more-harmful violations. The credit rating case as told is about issuers optimizing against the disclosed model, which is plain Goodhart / Campbell's law. The Channel C contribution would be the claim that the issuers who *do* this optimization are systematically different (more sophisticated, larger-balance-sheet, more capable of systemic harm) from the issuers who do not - and that the residual pool of misrated securities therefore concentrates the dangerous tail. The piece implies this but does not state it. Without that explicit step, the credit rating case demonstrates Goodhart, which is well-known, rather than Channel C specifically.

6. **The Ostrom reference at line 91 is gestural and risks weakening rather than strengthening the close.** The claim is that Ostrom's design principles - "particularly the principle that those subject to monitoring participate in defining monitoring criteria" - are a structural response to Channel C. But Ostrom's monitoring principle (Principle 4) is about *accountable monitors who are members of the community*, and her sanctions principle (Principle 5) is about *graduated sanctions*. Neither is specifically about participating in setting the detection criterion itself, which is the property the author needs. The reference would be stronger if the author either (a) makes the load-bearing argument carefully (a paragraph, not a clause) about which Ostrom principle does the work and why, or (b) cuts the reference and lets the structural-conditions section stand without it. As written, a reader familiar with *Governing the Commons* will notice the strain.

7. **The "Channel A/B/C" labeling is hard work to carry through a 5000-word essay.** The headings name the channels ("Channel A (detection and deterrence)"), but the body text drops to letters and asks the reader to remember the mapping across cases and across sections. A reader who looks up at line 109 ("The pharmaceutical case is the strongest...") and then refers back to "Channel C" has to mentally translate. Using the names - *Deterrence*, *Substitution*, *Selection* - in body text and keeping the letter codes only as headings would reduce friction without losing the analytical structure. This is a clarity concern, not an argumentative one.

8. **The conclusion's "uncomfortable policy implication" is weaker than the analysis deserves.** Line 111 frames the implication as: a program with 50% detected-violation reduction and 2x more-harmful residual violations is net-neutral. That is a numerical hypothetical, not a prediction. The sharper claim the analysis actually supports is that *standard monitoring evaluation is measuring the wrong unit* - detected-violation count is not a sufficient statistic for expected harm under Channel C, and so the entire evaluative apparatus of compliance programs is mis-targeted on the variable that does not capture what the original norm was designed to protect. That is the policy claim the piece earns. The hypothetical understates it.

9. **The piece would benefit from one explicit non-social cognate.** Channel C has the structural shape of antibiotic resistance under treatment pressure, of pesticide resistance, and of adversarial ML attacks under deployed defenses: clearing the susceptible / detectable end selects for the resistant / undetectable end. The author is producing the social-institutional version of a mechanism that has been formally modeled in other fields. One sentence acknowledging this - "this is the institutional instance of a selection-under-detection-pressure mechanism familiar from evolutionary medicine and adversarial ML" - would (a) signal that the mechanism is not ad hoc, (b) point toward where formal models exist, and (c) open a productive cross-domain question (concern surfaced in `follow-up-questions.md`) without dilating the piece. The piece is doing the cross-field move whether it names it or not; naming it costs little and pays well.

10. **Minor: line 5's cross-reference to the Smith referral piece reads slightly more like authorial throat-clearing than a genuine analytical connection.** "It is a companion to the prior audit... in that it applies the same discipline... to an institutional domain the referral piece only briefly touched." The connection to the Hedström-Ylikoski three-level method is real (and the piece does specify the mechanism at three levels, though less explicitly than the referral piece), but the cross-reference as currently phrased emphasizes continuity of *author's method* more than connection of *analytical content*. If the author wants to keep the link, it would be sharper to specify what the referral piece left undone that this one picks up - or what about the Channel A/B/C distinction was implicit in the referral piece's audit and is now made explicit. Borderline; not a blocker.
