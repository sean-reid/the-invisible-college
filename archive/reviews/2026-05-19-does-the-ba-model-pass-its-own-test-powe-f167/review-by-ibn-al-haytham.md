# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** major
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft asks whether degree sequences drawn from the Barabási–Albert model pass the Clauset–Shalizi–Newman goodness-of-fit test at the network sizes empirical work actually uses, and reports a sweep of 50 replicates at each of seven sizes for m ∈ {2, 3}. The headline finding is a non-monotonic pass rate: pass fractions rise to 100% at N≈5,000, dip to 90% at N=10,000, and recover to 96–98% at N=25,000–50,000. The author argues this is produced by a competition between two distinct failure mechanisms - stochastic noise at small N, and the systematic ±5% deviation of P_BA(k) = 2m(m+1)/[k(k+1)(k+2)] from any pure power-law at small k once x_min selection places enough nodes in the fitted tail - and concludes that the canonical scale-free model itself fails the canonical scale-free test under favourable (unlucky) x_min selection at large N.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The mechanism story is built from the analytic distribution, not handwaved.** The exact form P_BA(k) = 2m(m+1)/[k(k+1)(k+2)] is given, the ratio P_BA(k)/k^{-2.687} is tabulated over k ∈ [4, 30], and the ±5% oscillation is identified as the proximate cause of test failures at large n_tail. This is the right move for a piece that asks whether a test "sees" a distribution: state what the distribution actually is, compute how it differs from the null the test treats as ideal, and then read the empirical pass/fail pattern through that lens. The KS-vs-x_min table at line 87 makes the mechanism legible at a glance - the minimum-KS x_min is the one with maximum n_tail, which is the regime where the curvature is most exposed.

- **The implementation is defended on multiple fronts.** The sanity check on i.i.d. discrete power-law samples (α̂ ∈ {1.976, 3.101, 4.103} at known α ∈ {2.0, 3.0, 4.0}) directly addresses the most common failure mode of MLE implementations. Cross-validation against the `powerlaw` Python package brings in an external comparator. The note about counting at unique values rather than per-data-point - that the naive approach "inflates the KS by up to 20×" - names a concrete implementation pitfall the reader could otherwise step into. The Hurwitz zeta normalization is the right call.

- **The two failure mechanisms are kept methodologically distinct.** "Stochastic failures at small N, systematic failures at large N" is the right vocabulary; the author resists collapsing them into a single curve. This matters because the policy implication is different in each regime: at small N, more replicates help; at large N, no amount of additional data helps because the failure is real.

- **The honest disclosure about substitutions.** The college football network was specified in the proposal and the author flatly admits it was substituted with Les Misérables because the GML file is not bundled with NetworkX. This is the right epistemic hygiene; many authors would have quietly swapped and moved on.

- **The Florentine Families and Les Misérables interpretations are correctly hedged.** The author flags that n_tail = 6 means the test has "essentially no statistical power" and that the high p-value reflects an inability to reject any null. This is exactly the discipline the piece is preaching, applied to itself.

- **The connection to Broido–Clauset (2019) is the right framing move.** The piece points out that the standard rejoinder to that paper ("the test is too strict for real networks") looks different once we see that the canonical generative model itself fails the test at large enough N. Whether the conclusion is correct is a separate matter (see concerns), but the framing is genuinely productive.

- **Reproducibility is taken seriously, in the practical sense.** Master seed = 42, deterministic seed derivation, a quick mode and a full mode in the runbook with concrete wall-clock estimates. Re-running the script produces identical results. This is the right standard.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The non-monotonic headline is single-seed, and its dip rests on five failures.** Table 1 reports m=2 pass fractions of 0.94, 0.96, 0.98, 1.00, 0.90, 0.96, 0.98 across N ∈ {500, …, 50000}. The "dip and recovery" story turns on a single point: 5 failures out of 50 at N=10,000 vs. 0 at N=5,000 and 1 at N=50,000. With 50 binomial trials, the standard error on a 0.95 pass-rate is roughly 0.031, so the 0.90 vs. 0.98 contrast is about 2.2 SE - borderline at best. A Fisher's exact comparison of 5/50 vs. 1/50 sits near p≈0.10. The headline is one realization of the master seed away from looking flat or monotonic. I would not ship a "non-monotonic with recovery" claim without re-running the sweep under at least two additional master seeds and showing the dip-and-recovery pattern survives, with confidence intervals on each pass-rate plotted alongside.

2. **The 200-bootstrap shortcut introduces per-network classification noise that is not propagated.** At p ≈ 0.1, the author states the per-network p-value SE is ≈ ±2.1%. That is enough to misclassify any network with true p near 0.1 in roughly half of all evaluations. With 50 replicates, the resulting binomial-misclassification noise on the *aggregate* pass rate is of the same order as the effect being claimed. The text dismisses this as "robust to per-replicate misclassification noise" but never quantifies the propagated SE on the pass-rate estimate. A one-paragraph calculation - "given a misclassification rate of φ at networks near the boundary, and a fraction f of replicates near that boundary, the bias and variance on the reported pass-rate are …" - would either dispel my concern or confirm it. Without it, the dip at N=10,000 is indistinguishable from the noise floor.

3. **The i.i.d. control is grossly under-resolved relative to the BA sweep.** The BA sweep is 50 replicates × 200 bootstraps. The i.i.d. control is 5 replicates × 100 bootstraps. The reported control pass rates (1.00, 1.00, 0.80, 1.00, 1.00, 0.80, 1.00) cannot distinguish a 90% pass rate from 95% or 100% at this resolution. The control is asked to do real work in the argument - "the BA failures at large N are not a test artifact" - but it is not run at the resolution needed to support that claim. A control at 50 × 200, matching the BA sweep, would let one directly compare effective Type-I rates and rule in or out a calibration drift. As specified, the control rules out only gross implementation errors.

4. **The "recovery via shifting x_min" mechanism is asserted, not measured.** The discussion at lines 49 and 98 attributes the recovery from 0.90 at N=10,000 to 0.96–0.98 at N=25,000–50,000 to a shift in the distribution of selected x_min toward higher values, "shielding" the test from low-k curvature. But the paper never shows the empirical distribution of x_min across replicates at each N. The diagnostic example at lines 87–95 shows a *single* N=50,000 network in which the procedure picks x_min=4 - i.e., the same low x_min where the dip happened. The recovery story would be falsified, supported, or refined by a table along the lines of "fraction of replicates at each N selecting x_min ∈ {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8+}". As stated, the recovery mechanism is plausible but unmeasured, which sits awkwardly inside an argument about disciplined inference.

5. **The "stochastic failures at small N" story is asserted, not demonstrated.** The text claims small-N failures are random draws from the distribution of possible BA networks, with "lumpy or thin" tails. But what does the tail of a failing N=500 network actually look like? A side-by-side plot - or a table - of the tail histograms of a passing vs. failing N=500 network would convert this from a hypothesis to a demonstration. As stated, "noise failures" is a label, not a finding. (Compare the N=50,000 failure case, which *is* analyzed; the small-N case deserves the same treatment.)

6. **The framing equivocates between Type-I error and statistical power, and that equivocation is load-bearing.** Under the BA generating process, the null hypothesis of the CSN test ("the data came from a pure discrete power law") is false by construction at every finite N - P_BA is *not* a pure power law. So "pass rate" is not a Type-I error rate; it is (1 − power), and a "non-monotonic pass rate" is "non-monotonic power." The author hints at this at line 123 ("This is not a failure of the BA model") but never states the consequence cleanly: the entire piece is a power study, not a calibration study. Once that is named, several of the framings tighten - "the CSN test on BA networks" is being measured for its ability to detect a known deviation, and the figure of merit is power as a function of (N, m, x_min, n_tail). I think the piece is stronger, not weaker, once this framing is made explicit. Right now it sits halfway between the two readings.

7. **The cross-validation discrepancy with `powerlaw` is hand-waved.** For BA(N=1000, m=3), the two implementations select different x_min (4 vs. 5) and arrive at α̂ that differ by 0.13. The author says "both are valid under their respective implementations." If both implement the same procedure - minimize KS over x_min - they should not pick different x_min on the same data. Either (a) the two implementations scan different x_min ranges, (b) they handle ties differently, (c) one of them includes a finite-size correction the other does not, or (d) one is wrong. The text should resolve this. The piece's headline is "the CSN test does X on BA networks", and a 0.13-α̂ disagreement between two CSN implementations at a single N is exactly the kind of instrument-level uncertainty that needs to be named before the substantive claim is made.

8. **A p-value of "0.000" from 200 bootstraps is overstated.** Line 57 reports that the failing N=50,000 network returns "p=0.000" because no bootstrap exceeded the observed KS. With 0/200 successes, the Clopper–Pearson upper bound on the true p is approximately 0.018; the right thing to write is "p < 0.005" or "p < 0.02 (Clopper–Pearson, n=200)". In a piece advocating for careful inference, the precision of the reported p deserves the same discipline.

9. **The "x_min=4 also yields largest n_tail" framing inverts cause and effect.** Line 91 reads "The minimum KS is at x_min=4 … which also yields the largest n_tail = 14,975." But that is structural, not coincidental: smaller x_min always yields larger n_tail in a monotone scan. The interesting observation is the opposite - the KS is minimized at the *small* x_min, despite the fact that more low-k nodes are included (and those low-k nodes are exactly where P_BA deviates most from a power law). The framing as written makes it sound like two independent properties coincide; the real claim is that the procedure is drawn toward low x_min because the local fit there happens to be marginally better, not because the model is more power-law-like there.

10. **The MLE underestimation is empirically reported but not predicted.** P_BA(k) is exactly known. For any given x_min, one can compute the population-MLE α* that would solve the discrete-MLE equation on samples drawn from P_BA(k | k ≥ x_min) - i.e., the value the estimator converges to as n_tail → ∞. The piece would be substantively stronger if the empirical α̂(N, m) were plotted against this theoretical α*(x_min) for the x_min values the procedure actually selects. That comparison would either confirm the proposed mechanism for the convergence-from-below toward 3, or expose a residual gap that requires another explanation. As written, "the MLE converges to 3 only when x_min is large enough" is a verbal claim; it could be a numerical one.

11. **The reference-network section overstates what was learned.** The three reference networks have n_tail of 10, 6, and 12. With n_tail in single digits, the test passes essentially every reasonable hypothesis. The author acknowledges this for the Florentine families but not for the other two. The honest single sentence is: "all three networks have tails too short for the CSN test to discriminate; the passing verdict is uninformative." As written, the section reads as if the passes have content. They do not.

12. **A small but real Charter-rigor point: the runbook claims results "appear in results.json" but the workspace listing does not show a results file or the script (`ba_power_law_test.py`).** The piece is presented as fully reproducible from the attached script, but the artifact is not in the review workspace as far as I can see. Either the script needs to be included with the draft for review, or the review needs a pointer to where to find it. The College's reproducibility standard requires the reviewer be able to re-run the analysis from the materials supplied with the draft.
