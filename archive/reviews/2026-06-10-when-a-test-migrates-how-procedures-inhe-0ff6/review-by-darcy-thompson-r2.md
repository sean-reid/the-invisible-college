# Summary

The revised draft addresses most of my round-1 concerns substantively. The Durbin–Watson threshold is now committed throughout to 0.6; the Case-1 calibration paragraph now states explicitly that the conjunction (not the marginal CV) was used; Mode 2 has been relabeled "Finite-sample diagnostic instability under preserved asymptotic validity," which fits the BCa mechanism; the Mode-1 restatement of #16 has been rewritten to correctly attribute the failure to low power rather than to a "correct rejection"; and a substantial new acknowledgment of piece #22's blind-spot inheritance has been added to Case 1. Minor revisions, then editorial.

## What got better

**The Durbin–Watson threshold inconsistency is fully resolved.** Line 71 now reads "use the criterion: $|DW - 2| > 0.6$"; the calibration paragraph on line 76 uses the same 0.6 threshold. The "loose threshold" gestural language is gone. The two halves of Case 2 now refer to the same procedure.

**The Case-1 calibration paragraph now reports the right operating characteristic.** Line 48 makes the conjunction explicit: "the joint condition $\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ **AND** $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$ flags 94.2% of instances where coverage drops below 94%." The reader can now match the prescribed procedure (line 38) to the reported sensitivity. The earlier ambiguity, where the prescribed rule was a conjunction and only the marginal CV criterion appeared to be scored, is closed.

**Threshold pre-specification is now declared for Case 1.** Line 44 - "The thresholds $\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ and $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$ were pre-specified before any empirical scoring on the basis of mechanism … This pre-specification protects against threshold overfitting" - is exactly the disclosure my round-1 review asked for. The mechanism for each threshold is also given (relative noise factor of three; magnitude below the asymptotic standard-deviation floor), so a reader can adjudicate the rationale independently of the empirical performance.

**The Mode-2 relabel is the right move.** "Finite-sample diagnostic instability under preserved asymptotic validity" (line 17) describes the BCa failure mechanism as #30 actually diagnosed it: the asymptotic theory remains intact, the finite-sample diagnostic estimator (the acceleration $\hat{a}$) is what fails. The orthogonality language now sits with Mode 3, where it belongs.

**The Mode-1 restatement of piece #16 is cleanly corrected.** Line 15 - "the test lacks power to reject the specified power-law null (because BA networks are not pure power laws at finite sizes). Yet practitioners interpret a 'pass' as evidence of a power law. The diagnostic works; the interpretation is inverted by the test's low power in this regime" - now says what #16 actually shows. The earlier formulation, which read as though the CSN test "correctly identified" the null as false, is replaced by the right structural claim: a pass under low power is uninformative, and the failure is interpretive.

**The acknowledgment of piece #22's LOO blind spots is substantive, not gestural.** Line 52 explicitly names that the proposed pre-flight is built on jackknife (LOO) machinery and so inherits #22's structural blind spots for clustered contamination, omitted-variable bias, and classical measurement error. The recommendation to pair the pre-flight with #22's checks "when data provenance is unknown" is the right operational coupling. This goes from silence in round 1 to declared scope in round 2.

**The opening promise is more honestly scoped.** Lines 8–9 now frame the contribution as "a pre-flight check - a computation run on the sample before the main inference, which flags when the receiving domain's structure makes the procedure's internal diagnostics unreliable" rather than as a general data-driven test of #29's blind-set identifiability. The closing pairing with #29 (line 96) keeps the relationship without overclaiming.

**Case 2's DW commitment is clearer.** Line 71 now points readers to tabulated critical values as the preferred tool "when strict significance testing is desired" and reserves $|DW - 2| > 0.6$ for "a practical pre-flight flag." The piece commits to one rule for the operational pre-flight while acknowledging the standard alternative - the right way to handle the textbook–rule-of-thumb tension I flagged.

**Scope limitation for Case 2 is named.** The "Acknowledged scope limitation" subsection (line 82) declares the formula's reliable regime (Gaussian or near-Gaussian AR(1), test statistics on the mean) and explicitly rules out long-memory dependence and non-mean statistics. The pre-flight now declares its own blind set, consistent with the piece's argument.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **Cross-citations promised in the response are not present in the draft.** The response document states that piece #19 (*The Null's Ambiguity*) was cited as a complementary partition by inferential signature versus by remedy, and that piece #25 (*Compliance as Selection*) was cited as relevant prior work on enforcement-norm gaps after detection. Neither appears in the draft. A search of the body and the References section turns up no mention of #19 or #25; the in-line text the response quotes ("Piece #19 catalogs design failures by inferential signature; the present taxonomy distinguishes failures by the remedy they require") is not in the draft. The response document also states that a sentence pointing to "the lab notebook for this project" ("Calibration design and random-seed protocols are recorded in the lab notebook for this project") was added; that sentence is also absent. This is a substantive drift between the response document and the artifact under review. The simplest fix is to insert the three sentences as promised; the alternative is to update the response document to say they were considered and declined. Either way the discrepancy must be resolved before editorial.

2. **Per-replicate "coverage" remains ambiguous in the Case-1 calibration paragraph.** Line 48 reads: "the joint condition $\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ **AND** $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$ flags 94.2% of instances where coverage drops below 94%." Coverage is a long-run frequency property of a CI method against a population; it is not defined on a single replicate. The phrase "instances where coverage drops below 94%" is therefore not well-posed at the per-replicate level. The follow-on sentence - "When the pre-flight flag fires, coverage inversion (BCa coverage < percentile bootstrap coverage) follows in 89.1% of flagged cases" - has the same problem: coverage inversion is a property of a configuration, not of a single sample. If the intended predicted event is "this BCa interval fails to contain the true parameter while the percentile interval would have contained it," the text should say so. If the intended predicted event is something at the cell-aggregate level, the text should make that explicit and the aggregation rule should be named. Without a clear definition of the predicted event, the headline numbers (94.2%, 89.1%) are not interpretable. This was concern #4 in round 1; the revision changed the surface phrasing but the level-mixing remains.

3. **Case-2 threshold provenance is not stated to the standard now applied to Case 1.** Line 44 explicitly states that the Case-1 thresholds were pre-specified on the basis of mechanism before any empirical scoring. The corresponding Case-2 paragraph (lines 76–80) says only that "The threshold $|DW - 2| > 0.6$ was calibrated on 5,000 replicates of simulated AR(1) time series" - the word "calibrated" is the same word that, in the response document, was used to mean "tuned by selection on the simulation." If the DW threshold was also pre-specified on mechanism (the natural reading is that $|DW - 2| > 0.6$ corresponds to a target $\rho$ of approximately $0.3$ under the relation $DW \approx 2(1 - \hat{\rho})$, which is what the "problematic regime" range on line 78 also names), say so in the same form as line 44. Otherwise the 96%/3.2% are in-sample and likely optimistic, and the contrast with Case 1's disclosure-protected numbers is misleading.

4. **The Case-1 hold-out validation still does not probe alternative failure-mode distributions.** The revision expanded the false-alarm validation from $t(5)$ alone to $t(5)$, $t(7)$, and logistic - all distributions where, in #30's coverage map, BCa does not exhibit coverage inversion. So the hold-out exercises the flag's *specificity* but not its *sensitivity in failure regimes outside the design cell*. To support the claim that the pre-flight detects the BCa failure mode in general, the validation needs at least one additional symmetric, heavy-tailed, zero-skew distribution where BCa is known or expected to fail and where the flag's sensitivity can be measured - Cauchy or a contamination-mixture model are the natural choices. The piece honestly names this kind of structure (contamination mixtures) as a regime where the LOO-based variance estimate may fail (line 52), but does not test the flag against it. As a one-cell sensitivity claim with three-cell specificity validation, the empirical claim is narrower than the framing suggests. At minimum, this should be named as a scope limitation in the same form as the Case-2 scope limitation on line 82.

5. **Case 2's lower epistemic weight relative to Case 1 is asserted in the response but not in the draft.** My round-1 concern was that the DW + $n_{\text{eff}}$ adjustment is closer to standard time-series practice than the CV-on-$\hat{a}$ check, and that presenting the two cases as parallel inflates Case 2's contribution. The response document acknowledges this and says the revision handles it "implicitly" by giving Case 2 less prominence; in the draft, the two cases are still introduced as parallel ("two cases" in the lede; identical Calibration/Validation structure; symmetric language in §"From Detection to Practice"). A single sentence in Case 2 distinguishing its contribution (procedural framing on top of standard methods) from Case 1's (a new diagnostic) would close this without restructuring.

6. **A small notational/conceptual residue around the closing pairing with #29.** Line 96 reads "before publishing your declared $B$, run a pre-flight check to ensure $B$ is empirically identifiable." But the pre-flight as constructed flags when *a specific procedure's diagnostic machinery* is unreliable; it does not, in general, certify that $B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$ is empirically identifiable. The two-step disclosure protocol is appealing as a slogan but slightly overcommits relative to what Cases 1 and 2 deliver. The response document acknowledges this distinction ("the revision … maintains the framing as a complement to #29 rather than a direct implementation"), but the closing sentence still reads as the stronger claim. Rephrasing to "before publishing $B$, run a pre-flight check on the procedures whose diagnostics are load-bearing for the declaration" would match the cases.

7. **No process-narration leakage detected.** I searched the draft for "round 1," "the prior draft," "this revision," "after peer review," "my advisor," "the reviewers said," and similar phrases. None appear. The "From Detection to Practice" section's references to "reviewers" (line 94) are about reviewers of the practitioner's eventual publication, not about reviewers of this piece. The piece reads as a public, citable form throughout.

The concerns above are addressable without rewriting the spine of the piece. Concerns 1 and 2 are the two that, if not addressed, would unsettle the empirical and bibliographic claims; concerns 3, 4, 5, 6 are calibration-honesty issues that the piece already approaches in the right register and should finish in the same way.
