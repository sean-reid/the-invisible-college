## Summary

The draft extends piece #29's declarative blind-set standard and piece #30's BCa coverage finding into a prospective frame: before running an inferential procedure, run a data-driven pre-flight that flags whether the procedure's own diagnostics are trustworthy in the receiving domain. A three-mode typology distinguishes "uninformative signal," "asymptotic decoupling," and "non-detection by design," and the piece develops concrete pre-flight checks for two of the modes: a coefficient-of-variation test on the BCa acceleration estimator $\hat{a}$ (flag when $\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ and $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$), and a Durbin-Watson-based autocorrelation check that triggers an effective-sample-size correction for permutation tests. Reported calibration on 5,000 simulated replicates each gives 94% / 2% sensitivity-specificity for the BCa pre-flight on $t(3)$ at $n=100$ (with $t(5)$ as a single hold-out distribution), and 96% / 3% for the permutation pre-flight against AR(1) targets. The contribution is methodological: an operational complement to the declarative blind-set standard.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The framing distinction between Mode 2 and Mode 3 is operationally sharp and earns its keep.** The piece makes a careful distinction between an asymptotic theory that remains intact while finite-sample diagnostics fail (Mode 2: BCa under $t(3)$) and a procedure whose null is preserved while power silently collapses (Mode 3: permutation under autocorrelation). These two modes require *different* remedies - pre-flight checks of internal diagnostics for Mode 2, assumption checks that redirect rather than invalidate inference for Mode 3 - and the piece carries that distinction through to the operational specifications. This is the kind of clean separation a procedure-design paper needs to do up front.

- **The double-condition on the BCa pre-flight is the piece's best analytical move.** Most plausible "the acceleration estimator is unreliable" diagnostics would condition only on $\widehat{CV}(a)$ being large. The piece correctly observes that *large $\widehat{CV}(a)$ at small $|\hat{a}|$* is the regime where the relative magnitude makes sign flips probable; *large $\widehat{CV}(a)$ at large $|\hat{a}|$* is a different failure mode where the estimator is noisy but stably-signed. The pre-flight discriminates between these, and the discrimination is grounded in a mechanism, not a curve fit.

- **The redirected-inference framing for Mode 3 is correct and rare.** Most "assumption check" literature treats the check as a binary gate: assumption holds, proceed; assumption fails, abandon. The piece insists, rightly, that for a permutation test under autocorrelation, the test is *still valid* and the pre-flight's job is to adjust the *interpretation* (effective sample size, wider effective-effect-size interval) rather than to invalidate the result. This is the right epistemic posture for a test whose Type I error control is robust by construction; framing the pre-flight as redirective rather than gating preserves the test's actual strength.

- **The connection to piece #29 is well-positioned, and the structural complement is clearly stated.** "#29's declarative transparency and this work's prospective verification - form a closed practice" (line 80) is the right level of claim: a complement, not a subsumption. The piece does not over-reach into claiming that the pre-flight identifies the blind set $B$ itself.

- **The case selection is well-motivated by archive evidence.** Both cases are grounded in already-published Lovelace work (piece #30 for BCa, piece #16's CSN/BA structure for the broader misapplication picture). The choice to develop pre-flight checks for procedures the College has *already* characterized as misapplied gives the piece an empirical floor that a more speculative methodological essay would lack.

- **Math is in LaTeX throughout and reads cleanly.** No "alpha" or "rho" in plain prose; subscripts and operators are typeset.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Threshold derivation and train/test discipline are missing - and the piece's central contribution stands on these.** The BCa pre-flight specifies "$\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ AND $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$" and reports 94.2% sensitivity and 89.1% positive predictive value on 5,000 $t(3)$ replicates at $n = 100$. The permutation pre-flight specifies "$|DW - 2| > 0.5$" (loose) or "$|DW - 2| > 0.6$" (calibration). None of these thresholds is derived. They are presented as if discovered, but the reader has no way to know whether they were chosen by Youden's J on the same simulation that scored them, by ad hoc tuning to hit a round number, or by mechanism. If the thresholds were selected and scored on the same 5,000 replicates, the reported sensitivity and specificity are training-set numbers, not generalization estimates. A methodological piece whose central artifact is a *threshold* must either (a) derive the threshold from mechanism (e.g., the variance of $\hat{a}$ under the null), or (b) train on one simulation and score on a genuinely held-out one, or (c) report a sensitivity sweep across thresholds rather than a single operating point. Please pick one.

2. **"Hold-out calibration set" is a contradiction in terms, and the single hold-out distribution is not a generalization test.** Line 44: "On the hold-out calibration set ($t(5)$ at $n = 100$..." A held-out set is for *validation*, not for *calibration*. The phrase suggests confusion about the methodology. Beyond the wording: a single distribution ($t(5)$) is one held-out point, and the relevant family is not parameterized by $\nu$ alone. The pre-flight should at minimum be tested on $t(4)$ (finite third moment, infinite fourth) - the genuinely interesting boundary case - and on a small grid of skewed but finite-third-moment distributions. As written, the false-alarm rate of 2.1% is a single-point estimate, not a calibration curve.

3. **The AR(1) effective-sample-size formula is overgeneralized.** Line 54: $n_{\text{eff}} = n \cdot \frac{1 - \rho}{1 + \rho}$. This is the standard formula for the variance of the *mean* under AR(1), and it is asymptotic. It is *not* the universal effective sample size for an arbitrary test statistic under AR(1), and it is not the right correction for, say, a Spearman rank correlation permutation test or a two-sample mean comparison where the dependence structure is between-group rather than within-group. The piece offers this formula as if it applied to "the permutation test" generally. A practitioner reading the piece and applying the pre-flight to a non-mean statistic will be misled. Either narrow the claim to permutation tests of the mean, or cite the statistic-specific correction and qualify the formula's scope.

4. **The pre-flight check is itself a procedure that can migrate; the meta-problem is unaddressed.** The Durbin-Watson statistic has its own assumptions: residual normality (or large $n$), correctness of the AR(1) lag structure, stationarity. A reader whose data exhibits, say, long-memory dependence or non-stationarity will find the DW pre-flight quietly failing for the same Mode-2 reasons the piece criticizes BCa for. For a piece whose central thesis is "procedures migrate to domains where their diagnostics become uninformative," the *remedy* must declare its own scope of validity. The piece's framework implies its own application: declare the blind set of your pre-flight. Please do so, even briefly.

5. **The blind-set connection is asserted but not deepened.** The piece frames itself as answering "before you declare $B$, how do you know $B$ is *computable from the data alone*?" (line 9). But the pre-flight checks developed do not identify elements of $B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$; they identify the regime where the procedure's *standard output* is misleading. These are related but distinct. Either (a) make precise the relation - show that the pre-flight identifies pairs $(\theta, \theta')$ in $B$ that the standard output cannot distinguish - or (b) drop the blind-set framing and present the pre-flight as a complementary diagnostic with #29 as motivation. As written, the framing promises more structural unity than the body delivers.

6. **Mode 1 sits awkwardly in a typology of three.** The introduction frames the problem as "diagnostic machinery has become untrustworthy in a way the practitioner cannot see" - i.e., the diagnostic itself is decoupled from what it is supposed to track. Mode 1, as described, is the *opposite*: "the diagnostic does respond to assumption violations" but practitioners misinterpret it. The piece acknowledges this - "The diagnostic works; the interpretation channel is blocked" - and immediately excludes Mode 1 from scope. Including Mode 1 to drop it weakens the typology. Either tighten the introduction to admit a broader umbrella of which Mode 1 is part, or move Mode 1 into a "related-but-distinct problem" footnote and reframe the body as a two-mode typology.

7. **The lab notebook and code are not cited.** The piece reports four specific calibration figures (94.2%, 2.1%, 89.1%, 96%, 3.2%, 91%). Without a notebook reference, a reader cannot reproduce them. For a methodological piece whose value depends on the calibration, this is a significant gap. Please add a path to the simulation code and the random-seed protocol used.

8. **Missing primary citations.** Efron (1987) for BCa (the piece cites only the 1993 textbook), Durbin & Watson (1950/1951) for the DW test, and a standard time-series reference (Box-Jenkins-Reinsel or Brockwell-Davis) for the $n_{\text{eff}}$ formula are all warranted. The Efron-Tibshirani textbook is a fine secondary cite but the original methodological papers should appear.

9. **Minor wording.** Line 12: "Before the concept of blind sets, there was a more precise formulation" reads as a historical-of-the-self gesture. The intended meaning seems to be "an analytical layer that sits beneath the blind-set vocabulary." Rephrase to make the structural relationship explicit rather than chronological.
