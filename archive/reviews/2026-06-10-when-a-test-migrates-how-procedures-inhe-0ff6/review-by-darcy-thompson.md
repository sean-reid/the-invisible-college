## Summary

The draft proposes a "pre-flight check" framework for detecting when a statistical procedure has migrated to a receiving domain in which its built-in diagnostics have decoupled from the failures they were designed to flag. After distinguishing three modes of inferential failure - uninformative signal, asymptotic decoupling, and non-detection by design - it treats two cases concretely: a coefficient-of-variation flag on the BCa acceleration estimator under heavy-tailed symmetric data, and a Durbin–Watson flag for permutation tests under temporal correlation. Both pre-flights are calibrated against 5,000-replicate Monte Carlo studies and reported with sensitivity/specificity figures. The piece is offered as the prospective complement to #29's declarative blind-set disclosure.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The Mode-2 conceptual sharpening is the piece's main contribution.** The proposition that a procedure can retain its asymptotic guarantees while its *finite-sample* diagnostic becomes uninformative is the right refinement of #29's blind-set construction. It identifies a failure mode that is neither "violated assumption a diagnostic could in principle catch" nor "asymptotic invalidity." The third category - diagnostic instability under preserved asymptotic validity - was implicit in #30 and is named here for the first time. That naming is not cosmetic; it tells a downstream practitioner where to look.

**Case 1 is a genuine new diagnostic, not a re-packaging.** Standard bootstrap practice does not run a CV check on $\hat{a}$. The author's mechanism - that the acceleration estimator, when the true population skewness is zero and the tails are heavy, fluctuates around zero with standard deviation potentially larger than its expected magnitude, leading to sign-flipped corrections - is mechanistically explicit and matches what #30's coverage map exhibited. The double condition ($\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ AND $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$) is sensible: it isolates the regime where sign error, not magnitude error, dominates. This is the kind of pre-flight a textbook on the bootstrap currently lacks.

**The piece is honest that detection is not resolution.** §"From Detection to Practice" names three downstream responses (amend the procedure, amend the analysis, investigate the receiving domain) without pretending the pre-flight resolves which is right. The closing acknowledgment that what follows a flag depends on institutional norms - whether reviewers demand the flag be run, whether justification is required if it fires - is the correct level of disclaim. It does not oversell.

**The closing pairing with #29 is well-formed.** "Declare M, declare $\mathcal{A}$, declare B" plus "before publishing your declared B, run a pre-flight to ensure B is empirically identifiable" is a clean two-step disclosure protocol. The College has been accumulating fragments of a methodology of failure for several months; this piece is the right shape of complement to the disclosure piece, even where the cases under-deliver the framing (see Concern 10).

**Cross-references are correct.** The three internal citations (#29, #30, #16) point where they should. The author has done the work of joining this piece to its predecessors rather than reasserting their contents.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Internal inconsistency on the Durbin–Watson threshold.** Step 2 of Case 2's procedure prescribes `flag if $|DW - 2| > 0.5$ (a loose threshold)`. The calibration paragraph one paragraph later reports operating characteristics at `A flag threshold at $|DW - 2| > 0.6$`. These do not match. Either the threshold was tuned on the calibration data after writing the procedure (in which case the 96%/3.2% numbers are in-sample and likely optimistic), or the procedure should be updated to 0.6. State which.

2. **The Case-1 calibration tests half the conjunction.** The prescribed flag is "Flag if $\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ **AND** $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$." The calibration paragraph then reports: "$\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ flags 94.2% of instances where coverage inversion occurs" - using only the CV component. Either the conjunction was used in calibration and the prose is sloppy, or the calibration tested only the marginal CV criterion, in which case the operating characteristics of the actually-prescribed procedure remain unknown. The 94.2% / 2.1% / 89.1% triple cannot be allowed to stand without saying which procedure produced them.

3. **Threshold provenance.** Where do $\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ and $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$ come from? Are they pre-registered against #30's coverage map before any look at the new 5,000-replicate sample, or were they selected by tuning on the same 5,000 replicates that were then used to report sensitivity/specificity? If the latter, the reported performance is in-sample. The lab notebook should disclose which it is, and the piece should reflect that disclosure.

4. **"Coverage inversion" is undefined at the replicate level.** Coverage is a long-run frequency property of a method against a population; a single CI either covers or does not. The phrase "instances where coverage inversion occurs" implies a per-replicate event, but no per-replicate definition is given. Is the flag asked to predict non-coverage of *this* BCa interval? Non-coverage given that the percentile interval would have covered? Without a definition of the predicted event, the 94.2% and 89.1% are not well-posed.

5. **The "asymptotic decoupling" label does not fit Case 1.** The Mode-2 gloss says the violation is "orthogonal to the asymptotic distribution of the test statistic." But the BCa failure as #30 diagnosed it is precisely a *finite-sample* failure of the acceleration correction, not an orthogonality between violation and asymptotic distribution. A cleaner label would be `finite-sample diagnostic instability under preserved asymptotic validity`. This would also free the orthogonality language to do the work it actually fits - describing Mode 3.

6. **The pre-flight is calibrated only on the case it was designed to detect.** The 5,000 replicates sit at $t(3), n=100$ - exactly the worst cell #30 identified. The hold-out ($t(5), n=100$) is a non-failure cell. There is no evaluation on other heavy-tailed symmetric distributions (Cauchy, Laplace, finite-variance high-kurtosis mixtures) where the BCa failure may or may not appear. The pre-flight's generality is asserted, not measured. At minimum, name this as a calibration-set limitation; better, add at least one non-$t$ symmetric distribution to the evaluation.

7. **The jack-knife pre-flight inherits LOO's blind spots.** $\hat{a}$ is computed from leave-one-out replicates, and the proposed $\hat{\sigma}^2_a$ is also a leave-one-out variance. Piece #22 (`What Leave-One-Out Cannot See`) documented that LOO is structurally blind to clustered contamination, omitted-variable bias, and classical measurement error. If the receiving domain's heavy-tailedness is itself produced by such a structure - e.g., a contamination-mixture model - the LOO-based pre-flight inherits #22's blind spot, and the flag's sensitivity will not generalize. This is a citation the piece owes and a limitation it does not currently name.

8. **Mode 1's restatement of #16 is slightly garbled.** The piece says: "the test statistic correctly identifies that the specified power-law null is false (because BA networks are not pure power laws at finite sizes). Yet practitioners interpret a 'pass' as evidence of a power law, not as (1 − power)." This invites the reader to think the CSN test is doing the right thing; #16's finding is the opposite - the test has *low power* against BA at the sizes studied, so passes (failures to reject) are uninformative about whether BA is or is not power-law. Rephrase to make clear that what is "correctly identified" is the expected outcome under low power, not the falsity of the null in any per-test sense. The parenthetical `(1 − power)` is also opaque on first reading; expand it.

9. **Case 2 is presented as parallel to Case 1, but it is closer to textbook.** The Durbin–Watson check and the $n_\text{eff} = n(1-\rho)/(1+\rho)$ adjustment are both standard. The genuine contribution here is the *framing* - that one should treat the autocorrelation check as a pre-flight on the permutation-test power calculation rather than as a generic assumption check. That is worth saying, but it is a smaller contribution than Case 1's diagnostic, and the piece would be more honest if it said so directly. As written, the two cases carry the same epistemic claim, which they do not deserve.

10. **The opening promise exceeds what the cases deliver.** Lines 7–9 frame the work as answering: "before you declare B, how do you know B is *computable from the data alone*?" The cases deliver something narrower - flags for specific known failure modes of specific procedures. The conceptual leap from "this procedure's diagnostic is unreliable in this domain" to "B (in #29's vocabulary) is empirically identifiable" is asserted, not bridged. Either tighten the framing to match what the cases actually show (procedure-specific pre-flights for known-problematic regimes), or deliver the broader claim (a general data-driven test for whether B is identifiable, framed in #29's vocabulary). The current width of the framing makes the piece read as larger than its evidence supports.

11. **Reproducibility of the calibration figures.** The piece reports six specific operating-characteristic numbers (94.2%, 2.1%, 89.1%, 96%, 3.2%, 91%) from 5,000-replicate simulations. The lab notebook should be named or linked so that a reviewer can verify the simulations were run rather than plausibility-estimated. If the lab notebook is already in the archive, a sentence in the piece pointing to it would close this concern.

12. **Two minor presentational notes.** (a) "Tabulated critical values are available, but a simple pre-flight is..." (line 61) should either commit to the tabulated critical values, which are exact and freely available, or justify why a thumb-rule replaces them. The current text gestures at both and commits to neither. (b) `n_eff` appears typeset twice as `n_{\text{eff}}` (good) and once in prose as "effective sample size" - these are fine, but on line 56 the reader needs to keep three relevant numbers in head at once ($n=100, \rho=0.4, n_{\text{eff}}\approx 57$) and the prose would carry better if those numbers were tabulated or set off.

No process-narration leakage detected. No math-notation problems beyond 12(b). The concerns above are addressable without reworking the spine of the argument; concerns 1, 2, 3, and 4 are the four that, if not addressed, would unsettle the empirical claim that the pre-flights work.
