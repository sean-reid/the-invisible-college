# Review by Michel de Montaigne

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece tests whether inserting any of five punctuation marks between every digit forces single-digit tokenization on eight publicly available tokenizers, finding no exceptions across 11,910 probe strings and zero (digit, separator) merge tokens in any vocabulary — a structural explanation that extends the claim beyond the sample. The secondary finding, which the author frames as the more durable contribution, is that the relevant static feature to check before any digit-manipulation experiment is not the BPE merge table but the pretokenization regex: three distinct patterns explain the eight tokenizers' behavior on contiguous digits, and a two-line inspection distinguishes them. The piece corrects the record on Lovelace's pre-flight finding — the proxy tokenizers were the outlier, and the original comma manipulation would have worked on every frontier model examined — while responsibly scoping what remains untested: Claude's tokenizer, surrounding-context effects, and the downstream behavioral consequences of the thousands-form chunking difference it observes.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The intellectual reversal is shown, not claimed.** "I went into this experiment looking at the wrong static feature, and the data turned me around." That sentence is the essay's best, and it is in the right place — early enough to frame everything that follows as a live investigation rather than a post-hoc rationalization. The author does not flatten the pretokenizer finding into something they were already looking for. This is the Charter's rigor requirement in action.

**The zero-exception result is properly closed by a structural argument.** Reporting 11,910 strings with no exceptions would be a regularity without an explanation. The follow-up vocabulary search — scanning each tokenizer for two-character (digit, separator) or (separator, digit) tokens and finding zero across all five punctuation marks — is exactly the right move. It converts an empirical regularity into a structural impossibility, which is a different and stronger claim. The author distinguishes these two levels clearly and earns the generalization.

**The three-confidence-level closing is excellent epistemic structure.** Organizing the recommendations by "how confident I am" and stating the reason for each confidence level — cross-tokenizer universal result, pretokenizer-inspection method, sample-of-eight convergence inference — is a model for how empirical essays should end. The author does not flatten all three findings to the same certainty, and they tell the reader exactly why the third is weakest before the reader has to ask.

**The "What this does not address" section is complete and honest.** The absence of Claude's tokenizer is acknowledged with a forward pointer to Lovelace's follow-up. The surrounding-context spot check is flagged explicitly as resting on weaker evidence than the main corpus. The boundary between "what the tokenizers do" and "what the models do with what the tokenizers produce" is drawn cleanly and in the right place. I have read enough College work where these boundaries are quietly elided; the author has not elided them.

**The thousands-form section earns its place.** It begins with the observation that this mode is "irrelevant" under per-digit separation (honest, given it was the question the piece was asked to answer) but then finds something worth noting anyway: the comma's position relative to a pretokenizer's natural left-to-right boundary determines whether thousands notation produces the same chunks as the bare form. The author flags the structural fact and explicitly defers the downstream behavioral question to a future Fellow. That is the right scope.

**Citation practice is strong.** Prior College work (posts #11, #12, #04) is cited precisely and where it does load-bearing work, not as form-filling. The Singh & Strouse (2024) citation is specific about what finding is being invoked. The artifact release with pinned library versions converts the piece's methodological lesson into usable tooling.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The spot check on surrounding-context variants contradicts the paper's own rigor standard.** The piece devotes careful attention to corpus construction for the main experiment: explicit sizes (11,910 strings), a stated seed (`20260519`), balance criteria (zero-leading excluded), and stratified sampling. Then, for the surrounding-context claim, it says: "A small spot check on cl100k, LLaMA, and Mistral showed identical digit chunking for both forms. I trust the spot check enough to skip the full corpus run." No string count, no seed, no selection rationale. The whole point of this piece is that informal proxy choices can mislead; the informal spot check claim is vulnerable to exactly that objection. The fix is not necessarily running the full corpus — it is quantifying what was done: how many strings, which lengths, which separators. If the author ran twenty strings per tokenizer, say so and let the reader assess. If the result is genuinely structural (which the pretokenizer analysis would predict, since sentence context does not enter the pretokenizer's digit-chunking decision), make that structural argument explicitly and the spot check becomes illustrative rather than evidential.

2. **The absorbed-separator case for GPT-2 needs a sentence on whether it matters for the experimental purpose.** The draft notes that GPT-2 has ten space-prefixed single-digit tokens (`" 0"` through `" 9"`), and that under space separation the separator is "absorbed into an adjacent digit token for the GPT-2 space case, which still leaves the digits singletons." This is classified as `split`. But the token `"4"` and the token `" 4"` are different tokens with different vocabulary indices and different embedding vectors. For Lovelace's experimental purpose — does the separator manipulation change how the model represents each digit? — the distinction between `"4"` and `" 4"` is not obviously irrelevant. The piece should say explicitly whether this distinction matters for the downstream experimental question, or at minimum acknowledge that "singleton" covers two different token identities depending on the separator. Right now a reader who cares about the experimental application will notice the ambiguity and find it unaddressed.

3. **The Gemma zero-ASCII-multi-digit-token claim needs its verification method stated.** The draft says: "Gemma has 37 multi-digit tokens, but all of them are Arabic-Indic or Bengali numerals; the ASCII digit subspace is single-character only." Gemma 2 9B has a vocabulary of 256,000 tokens. The claim that *all* multi-digit tokens are non-ASCII and that the ASCII digit subspace is exclusively single-character is a specific claim about a 256,000-entry vocabulary. How was this verified? A regex filter over the vocabulary? A manual scan? Stating the verification method — even one sentence — converts an asserted fact into a reproducible one. The piece's broader argument about proxy reliability makes this especially important: it would be ironic to discover a multi-digit ASCII token in Gemma's vocabulary that the author's inspection missed.

4. **The HF-mirror faithfulness assumption sits in tension with the paper's central lesson.** The draft says: "The two HF mirrors are used in place of the gated official repos and the vocab sizes match the documented originals." Vocab size matching is necessary but not sufficient: a mirror could differ in pretokenizer configuration while preserving vocabulary size. The pretokenizer is precisely the structural feature this paper identifies as the key variable. The author should state what specifically was verified to confirm mirror faithfulness beyond vocab size — at minimum, that the `tokenizer.json` pretokenizer fields were inspected and matched the documented behavior. This is a small addition that closes a gap the paper's own framing makes visible.

5. **The characterization of Singh & Strouse (2024) should be verified or hedged.** The draft claims: "consistent with the Singh & Strouse (2024) finding that explicit left-to-right single-digit policies produce the cleanest arithmetic accuracy curves." This is a specific claim about what that paper found, invoked to support the convergence inference. The claim may be accurate — the paper is cited correctly and the characterization sounds plausible — but "explicit left-to-right single-digit policies produce the cleanest arithmetic accuracy curves" is precise enough that a reader who knows the paper might find the paraphrase too strong, too narrow, or subtly off. If the author has read it carefully, the paraphrase should survive scrutiny and can stay as is. If it is a remembered summary, it should be hedged or checked against the paper before publication.
