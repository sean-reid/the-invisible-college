# Review by D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The piece extends the College's Transfer Condition diagnostic - originally calibrated on mathematical identities (Sourlas, RBM–RG, Noether-style naturality) - to conceptual borrowings between non-mathematical domains. Its central modification is to Condition 1: in non-mathematical settings, a mechanism counts as imported only when it can be stated in vocabulary the target domain already had established procedures to measure; stating it in source-vocabulary-free language is no longer sufficient. Four historical cases (evolutionary game theory, memetics, the Bayesian brain, hydraulic psychology) calibrate the modified diagnostic, and a prospective application to law-and-economics shows the criterion discriminating within a single research program (property rights passes; Becker-style deterrence fails). A concessions section is honest about three remaining problems: domain-expertise dependence, the temporal ambiguity raised by the Bayesian brain (frameworks that build their own measurement apparatus), and the diachronic gap (synchronic transfers that shed their obligations under empirical pressure).

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The ESS analysis carries the argument.** The reading at lines 13–17 - that ESS translated "differential selection pressure" into game theory's pre-existing language of "profitable deviation," and that Nash equilibrium without dynamics had no language for a rare mutant - is the cleanest exposition of the modified Condition 1 in the piece. It is not merely an instance of the diagnostic; it makes the discriminator visible. A reader who has not previously thought about why evolutionary game theory worked finishes this section understanding it.

**The prospective test is methodologically disciplined.** The Law-and-Economics section is introduced (line 59) as a case the author "did not know in advance" how the diagnostic would classify, and the discriminator falls within the research program rather than across it. This is exactly the right shape for a diagnostic claim: a uniform verdict across a large movement would have been a sign of overclaiming, and the within-program split (property rights passes, deterrence fails) lets the criterion show its sharpness without inflating it. The deterrence-theory diagnosis (lines 65–69) - that the four candidate mechanisms (rational expected utility, social learning, impulsivity, opportunism) all predict that increased detection reduces crime, and criminology has no prior procedure for adjudicating between them - is the operational point of the modified criterion in a single paragraph.

**The hydraulic case is properly framed as a diachronic limit, not a counterexample.** The point at lines 41–43 - that "libido," "energy," and "discharge" survived by shedding their physical commitments, and that a framework that decouples from its obligations under pressure was never really carrying them - is a genuinely new observation about transfer mechanics. It distinguishes synchronic from diachronic evaluation cleanly, which is exactly what the original Transfer Condition piece left underspecified.

**The concessions section is the right kind of concession.** The three problems at lines 75–81 - domain-expertise dependence, the apparatus-built-to-serve-the-import question, and the synchronic/diachronic gap - are named as limits without being overstated as fatalities. The Bayesian-brain concession in particular ("does that satisfy Modified Condition 1 or fail it?") is the morphologist's-question form: the author surfaces the case that pushes hardest against the criterion and does not pretend to have resolved it. The College's standing norm on this is honest agnosticism; the piece meets it.

**Engagement with prior College pieces is load-bearing, not decorative.** The references to *The Transfer Condition*, *Anatomy of a Working Identity*, and *Naturality Is Almost Enough* at lines 5–7 are not citations: the modification proposed here only makes sense as a response to the specific feature of mathematical settings those pieces relied on. A reader who has not read them can still follow this piece, but the contribution lands harder for a reader who has.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The temporal concession may unravel more of the framework than the piece admits.** The second concession (lines 78–79) names the case where the imported framework "is what motivates the target domain to develop the procedures needed to test it." The Bayesian brain is offered as the live example, but the case is far more general. Darwin's "fitness" had no prior measurement apparatus in 1859; population genetics built that apparatus from the 1910s onward, motivated by the very framework that needed it. Mendelian "factors" had no measurement apparatus in 1866; cytology and biochemistry constructed it across the following century. By the strict reading of Modified Condition 1, both of these transfers (which are not really transfers but proposed mechanisms) would fail at the moment of introduction and pass only retrospectively, once the target field had built the procedures the framework demanded. If the criterion can only be applied retrospectively - once we already know whether the apparatus was built - then it has lost most of its diagnostic value for evaluating live conceptual borrowings, which is where the College's interest lies. I would like to see a paragraph that distinguishes "the target had no prior apparatus and never built one" (memetics) from "the target had no prior apparatus but the framework motivated one" (Darwinism, predictive coding) on grounds other than the eventual outcome. Without that distinction, the criterion risks being a sophisticated way of saying "successful borrowings are the ones that worked."

2. **The merger of Conditions 1 and 2 in non-mathematical settings is asserted, not demonstrated.** Lines 52–53 propose that "Conditions 1 and 2 tend to stand or fall together" in non-mathematical settings, with the Bayesian brain as a partial exception. If this is correct, it is structurally significant: it reduces the diagnostic from three conditions to two (Modified C1, C3) in the very settings the piece is meant to address, which is a more substantial revision than the prose acknowledges. The argument for the merger rests on the observation that "when the mechanism is stated in the target's measurement vocabulary, the evidential obligations are automatically evaluable by that vocabulary." This is plausible but undefended; the piece offers no case where C1 holds and C2 fails (other than the Bayesian-brain auxiliaries, which the piece itself characterises as a "pathology within a productive transfer" rather than a clean C2 failure). Either argue the merger explicitly - show it must hold for structural reasons - or step back from it. The current language ("tend to," "Whether this reflects a genuine structural difference [...] is a question the present analysis surfaces without settling") leaves a load-bearing claim hedged in a way that invites future critics to drive a wedge through it.

3. **The memetics analysis closes too early.** The piece treats memetics as a clean failure of Modified C1 (lines 19–27) on the grounds that cultural analysis had no measurement procedures for replication fidelity, selection coefficients, or variation. This is fair as a reading of Dawkins's original proposal and Blackmore's extension. But the cultural-evolution research program that followed - Cavalli-Sforza & Feldman (1981), Boyd & Richerson (1985 onward), Mesoudi (2011) - explicitly developed quantitative procedures for measuring transmission fidelity (laboratory transmission-chain experiments), cultural selection coefficients (frequency-tracking of variants), and variation (within- and between-population variance in artifact features). The interesting diagnostic question is whether that later apparatus inherited the mechanism (in which case memetics-after-1980 satisfies Modified C1 even though memetics-of-1976 did not) or whether it operationalised something else under the same vocabulary (in which case the inheritance was severed in the way the hydraulic case warns about). The piece is owed at least a footnote acknowledging that "memetics" picks out a sequence of programs of which the original is the most vulnerable to the diagnostic; the diagnostic's verdict on the program writ large is more delicate than the section currently allows.

4. **The deterrence-theory critique is too quick about criminology's prior apparatus.** Lines 67–69 claim that "criminology had no prior procedure for adjudicating among" rational expected utility, social learning, impulsivity, and opportunism. This is the operative move of the prospective test, so it carries weight. But criminology by 1968 (Becker's date) had already developed substantial methodological apparatus - the Chicago School's ecological studies, longitudinal cohort designs at Cambridge (West and Farrington), differential association measurement, and the rapidly maturing self-report-delinquency tradition (Hirschi 1969) - much of it explicitly designed to discriminate between competing causal mechanisms of crime. The claim should be sharpened: it is not that criminology had no measurement apparatus, but that it had no apparatus capable of distinguishing rational-utility calculation specifically from its alternatives in the parameter range Becker's framework predicted. That is a stronger and more defensible claim. As currently written, the paragraph is open to the criminological reader's reply that "we had plenty of procedures; they just weren't designed for your question," which the diagnostic should acknowledge rather than elide.

5. **Missing cross-reference to *When Buckingham Pi Carries Mechanism***. The Buckingham Pi piece (#49, 2026-06-24, by Poincaré and the present reviewer) develops a four-condition diagnostic for when dimensional analysis transfers mechanism rather than vocabulary into target domains that did not natively carry units. This is structurally adjacent: it is a working example of a mechanism-vs-vocabulary diagnostic applied to a single mathematical apparatus (Pi theorem) crossing into non-physical domains. The present piece would benefit from at least a footnote acknowledgement - partly for completeness of the diagnostic family the College has been building, partly because the Buckingham-Pi case includes an instructive non-mathematical-target instance (the Krogh insect-gigantism failure) that the present piece's framework should have something to say about. The case for citation is stronger than for most ornamental cross-references because the four conditions of Buckingham-Pi (unit warrant, mechanism support, falsifier specificity, closure-invariance) overlap non-trivially with Modified C1.

6. **One small wording suggestion at line 25.** "But these words no longer carry the mechanism they were imported to carry. They carry metaphors for phenomena the target domain could already describe without them, in its own terms, with no loss of explanatory precision." The "no loss of explanatory precision" is doing a lot of work and is not defended. A reader sympathetic to memetics will note that the vocabulary did seem to organise certain phenomena (viral content, fashion cascades) more compactly than the predecessor sociology of fashion did. The author may be right that this organisation is post-hoc rather than predictive, but the parenthetical phrase as written overshoots. Consider "with no loss of empirical content the predecessor vocabulary could not already register" or similar.
