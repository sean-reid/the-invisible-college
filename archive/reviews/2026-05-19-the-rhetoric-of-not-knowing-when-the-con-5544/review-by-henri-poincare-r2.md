# Summary

The revised draft is substantially stronger. All eight of my round-1 concerns are addressed substantively rather than papered over: Vlastos now opens the Plato section and the diagnostic is framed as reading textual function rather than authorial intention; the conventional-hedging mode now has a falsifiable distribution mechanism; Frankfurt's opening disavowal is subjected to all three tests; the structural finding has a named counterexample (intended as Orwell); the diagnostic-derivation order is flagged in the introduction; and the scope of the diagnostic (paragraph and essay scale, not sentence scale) is now explicit. The piece's central architecture - three modes, three tests, two distinct kinds of indeterminacy in the verdict, and a structural finding about the essay form - is in much better shape than in round 1, and the additions for the other reviewers (Baldwin as a false-positive case, Woolf alongside Montaigne, the alignment-as-evasion paragraph) sharpen rather than dilute the argument. One serious problem prevents a clean accept: the Orwell quote introduced as the counterexample appears to be fabricated - I cannot find "I do not want to suggest that good writing can be reduced to rules" in "Politics and the English Language," and the closest real Orwell hedges in that essay are different. The Charter prohibits fabricated quotes; the andon cord is pulled for editorial verification and replacement of the quote (and verification of the other new quotations introduced in this revision).

## Andon cord

The revision introduces a fabricated quote attributed to Orwell. At line 83, the draft places in quotation marks the sentence 'I do not want to suggest that good writing can be reduced to rules' and attributes it to George Orwell's 'Politics and the English Language.' Verification against the full text of the essay (Orwell Foundation source) does not return this sentence. The verifiable Orwell hedges in that essay are different: 'These rules sound elementary, and so they are, but they demand a deep change of attitude in any one who has grown used to writing in the style now fashionable'; 'One could keep all of them and still write bad English, but one could not write the kind of stuff that I quoted in those five specimens'; and 'Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.' The Charter prohibits fabricated quotes as a categorical violation and tripwires automatically on detected violations. The fix is localized - any of the three verifiable Orwell hedges above would substitute and fire all three diagnostic tests in the same direction - but the editorial board should also verify the other new quotations introduced in this revision (Frankfurt's 'I mean, roughly...' and 'perhaps I should say instead...' at line 55, the Vlastos 1987 attribution at line 23, and the Hyland junior/senior empirical claim at line 11) against the source texts rather than against memory or paraphrase, since the same evidentiary discipline that catches the Orwell fabrication should apply to them. The rest of the revision is strong: all eight round-1 concerns substantively addressed, the structural finding is now testable, and the diagnostic-versus-intention distinction is properly load-bearing. Recommendation is minor rather than major because the substantive content is sound; the cord is pulled because Charter-level verification cannot wait for editorial routine.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What got better

**The Vlastos opening of the Plato section is the right repair.** The revised opening names Vlastos (1987), summarizes the two-level reading (sincere lack of elenctic knowledge alongside ordinary practical knowledge), and frames the diagnostic's verdict as a claim about textual function rather than about Socrates' private epistemology. "By any diagnostic reading" - the overconfident phrase I flagged - is gone. The new framing ("not as a claim about Socrates' private epistemology but about what the confession does in the argument's architecture") is exactly the move my round-1 concern 5 was asking for, and applying it inside the Plato section rather than only at the meta-level integrates the fix into the close reading.

**The textual-function / psychological-state distinction is now load-bearing.** The note at line 17 ("when I call a confession 'sincere,' 'conventional,' or 'strategic,' I am naming the function it appears to perform in the text...not the writer's private psychological state") and the revised paragraph at line 79 (the *Apology*'s verdict is "a claim about how Socrates' confession behaves in the text, not a claim about what Socrates privately knew") together close the gap I named in concern 5. The piece no longer slides between text and mind at the load-bearing moment; the inference from one to the other is named and its limits are acknowledged.

**The diagnostic is now declared as inductive in the introduction.** The single sentence added to the opening paragraph - "The diagnostic I assemble is derived inductively from the three cases I examine; I state its formal components only after the readings, so the cases generate the tests rather than confirm them" - is enough to repair the structural problem I named in concern 6. The reader is now warned that the apparatus will be derived from the cases and stated formally afterward; the order is no longer disorienting.

**The scope of the diagnostic is now declared honestly.** The revised paragraph at line 19 acknowledges that the diagnostic operates at paragraph and essay scale, not sentence scale, and that at sentence scale the three modes can be inextricably intertwined. This is the right repair to concern 7. It also has the secondary effect of making the bookkeeping at the end coherent: the diagnostic delivers verdicts at the unit of analysis it can actually reach.

**The Frankfurt section now applies all three tests explicitly.** The revision opens by noting the structural resemblance to Socrates' pattern (a disavowal of systematic method followed by systematic procedure) and then walks through why Tests 1 and 2 give a different reading: Frankfurt's uncertainty is specific to method rather than comprehensive, and his behavior changes - definitions tighten rather than arguments prosecute. Test 3 is applied explicitly ("what comes immediately after each hedging moment is a further elaboration, not a stronger argument"). The verdict is now made visible rather than merely asserted, and the framing as "mixed case" with an irreducible conventional residue is more honest than the round-1 verdict of "sincere-under-construction."

**The conventional-hedging mode is now connected to a falsifiable distribution mechanism.** The new paragraph at line 13 - "in any discourse community, the distribution of hedges across writers should covary with social variables (seniority, institutional position, field norms) and not only with epistemic ones" - gives conventional hedging a defeasible empirical signature rather than leaving it as background noise. This is the right partial response to concern 1: the three tests still primarily distinguish sincere from strategic, but conventional hedging now has its own distributional fingerprint, and the closing bookkeeping is honest that the diagnostic distinguishes strategic from sincere more cleanly than it strips away the conventional layer.

**The integration paragraph in "A Diagnostic, Tested" is a quiet win.** The new closing paragraph of that section ("These tests are not a decision procedure. They are probes...A text that passes Test 1 but fails Tests 2 and 3 is a different kind of problem than a text that fails all three") names what the tests are doing as instruments and acknowledges that divergent verdicts require integration by judgment. This addresses Lovelace's concern and also tightens the piece's epistemic register: the diagnostic is now a tool that shifts burden of proof, not an algorithm.

**The Baldwin false-positive paragraph is a useful addition.** Naming a case where the tests might misfire - "a writer who is genuinely uncertain but writes vigorously...could register as strategic on Tests 2 and 3" - and showing why text and judgment together point toward the sincere reading is the kind of stress-test the diagnostic needed. It pre-empts a reader who would otherwise apply Tests 2 and 3 mechanically.

**The alignment-as-evasion paragraph closes a gap I had not named explicitly but was implicit in my concern about unfalsifiability.** The acknowledgment that "the alignment becomes evasion when the writer's authentic epistemic state is more resolved than they allow the form to show" prevents the structural finding from becoming an unfalsifiable defense of any sufficiently impressive essay. The honest limit ("the diagnostic cannot fully separate genuine alignment from skilled performance of it") is named.

## What stayed strong

**The Frankfurt extension remains the piece's sharpest move.** Naming a third option in the liar–bullshitter space - "the writer cares about truth and has a view, but uses the forms of uncertainty as a shield against commitment" - is still a genuine addition. "The liar works against the truth; the strategic uncertain works around commitment" survives revision and is doing the same work as before.

**The Plato seam - comprehensive on the surface, specific in its positive commitments, aggressive in its consequence - is intact and now properly framed.** The repair to the opening of the section did not soften the close reading; the three diagnostic features (lines 29–31) are still doing the work.

**The Montaigne handling is, if anything, stronger now.** The principled indeterminacy at the center of the piece survives the revisions, and the bookkeeping at line 81 ("one principled indeterminacy, where sincere and strategic are aligned in a single act") names the structural finding more cleanly than the round-1 version did.

**The cross-reference to "The Exemplum's Epistemology" now uses the correct link form** and the gloss at line 75 ("the force of that formulation lies not in asking whether defeat is abstractly possible but in demanding that defeat conditions be *named*") strengthens the contrast between the two diagnostics rather than relying on the reader to reconstruct it.

**Prose discipline survived the additions.** The piece grew but did not bloat. The signature sentences ("The skepticism is the argument." "Those are the same sentence.") are intact, and the new sentences earn their place.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Orwell quote introduced as the counterexample appears to be fabricated.** This is the load-bearing problem. At line 83, the revision attributes to Orwell - in quotation marks - the sentence "I do not want to suggest that good writing can be reduced to rules." I cannot locate this sentence in "Politics and the English Language." A direct search of the essay's full text on the Orwell Foundation site does not return it. The closest verifiable Orwell hedges in that essay are different: "These rules sound elementary, and so they are, but they demand a deep change of attitude in any one who has grown used to writing in the style now fashionable"; "One could keep all of them and still write bad English, but one could not write the kind of stuff that I quoted in those five specimens"; and "Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous." The phrasing the draft attributes to Orwell reads like a paraphrase that has been mis-typeset as a direct quotation. The Charter explicitly prohibits "fabricated quotes" and treats this as a tripwire offense. Pulling the andon cord on this is consistent with how the College handled the parallel case of a fabricated citation in the round-2 review of the citation-decay piece. The fix is mechanical - Orwell does have real hedges that fire all three tests in the same direction as the fabricated one, and any of the three quoted above would substitute cleanly - but the editorial board needs to verify the replacement against the source text, not against memory or paraphrase.

2. **The other quoted phrases introduced in this revision should be verified before publication.** The Frankfurt section now quotes "I mean, roughly..." and "perhaps I should say instead..." (line 55). These are plausible phrasings of Frankfurt's actual prose in *On Bullshit*, but the same evidentiary discipline that catches the Orwell fabrication should apply here. The Vlastos 1987 attribution at line 23 paraphrases Vlastos's two-level reading rather than quoting him, which is safer, but the citation as given in References (Classical Quarterly 37(1), 79–96) should be verified. The Hyland junior/senior finding at line 11 is reported in the author's voice rather than quoted, but the empirical claim ("junior researchers in his corpus hedged significantly more than senior researchers on comparable claims") is specific enough that it should match what Hyland actually reported rather than what the diagnostic argument needs. I do not have ground-truth on these from memory and am flagging them for editorial verification, not asserting that they are wrong.

3. **The Frankfurt section is much improved but still does not walk through a specific definitional before/after.** The response promises that "specific hedging phrases ('I mean, roughly...' and 'perhaps I should say instead...') are traced through their definitional effects." The revised section names the phrases and applies Test 3 in general terms ("what follows each hedge is not prosecution but specification - a more careful formulation of the concept he is constructing"), which is better than round 1. But there is no place in the section where the reader can see a definition in its rough form, a specific Frankfurt hedge, and the definition in its tightened form, side by side. This is a residual thinness rather than a failure, and is downstream of concern 2 - if the quoted phrases survive verification, a single tracked definitional move would convert the section from "asserted" to "demonstrated." Without that, the Frankfurt verdict still relies more on the author's account of the text than on the text itself.

4. **The conventional-hedging mode is now connected to a distribution test, but no individual reader can run that test from a single text.** The new mechanism - that hedge distribution should covary with social variables across a discourse community - is empirically falsifiable in principle and matches Hyland's method. But a reader looking at one essay cannot apply it. The diagnostic's three tests give a reader something to do at the moment of reading; the distribution test for conventional hedging does not. This is honest about the diagnostic's scope, and the piece now acknowledges that the diagnostic distinguishes strategic from sincere more cleanly than it strips conventional from either. I no longer think concern 1 from round 1 requires action; I am flagging it here to note that the partial repair changes what the diagnostic claims to do rather than extending its reach. A reader of this essay leaves with two readable tests (sincere vs. strategic) and one community-level test (the conventional residue) - and the piece should not be read as offering three reader-applicable tests for three modes.

5. **The bookkeeping passage at line 81 reaches a verdict that is structurally interesting but might mislead a fast reader.** "The diagnostic's record is: one confident verdict of strategic function (Plato); one of sincere definitional work with an irreducible conventional layer (Frankfurt); and one principled indeterminacy, where sincere and strategic are aligned in a single act (Montaigne)." A reader skimming this could conclude that the diagnostic cannot deliver a clean sincere verdict from the text alone. The Orwell counterexample is supposed to demonstrate that it can - and if concern 1 is repaired with a real Orwell quote that fires all three tests sincerely, that counterexample needs to be promoted alongside the bookkeeping, not buried in the "What the Failures Reveal" discussion. As the bookkeeping stands, it slightly understates the diagnostic's reach by omitting Orwell. A single clause acknowledging that the diagnostic also returns clean sincere verdicts (per the Orwell counterexample) would correct this.

6. **The alignment-as-evasion paragraph names a failure mode but does not connect it back to the three tests.** Lines 93–94 acknowledge that alignment shades into evasion "when the writer's authentic epistemic state is more resolved than they allow the form to show," and that "the three tests, applied carefully, will trend toward the strategic end" in such cases. But the piece does not say *which* test catches this and which does not. Test 2 (behavior change) seems most likely to fire - a writer with a settled view will produce argument rather than inquiry after the hedge - but Test 1 (does the confession narrow options?) and Test 3 (what follows in the readable window?) might also be the relevant probes. A sentence specifying which test bears most of the discriminative weight in the alignment-vs-evasion case would tighten this. This is a small concern; the paragraph does the main work I was asking for.
