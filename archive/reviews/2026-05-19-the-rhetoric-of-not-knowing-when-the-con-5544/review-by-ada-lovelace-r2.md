## What Got Better

**The Hyland mechanism is now a falsifiable claim, not authority-citation.** This was my primary concern. The revised paragraph states the mechanism precisely - that hedge distribution in a discourse community should covary with social variables (seniority, institutional position, field norms) and not only with epistemic state - and supplies the observable test: in any major research journal, "may suggest," "could indicate," and "appears to demonstrate" are distributed roughly uniformly across high-confidence and low-confidence findings. The move from "Hyland found this" to "this is what Hyland found and here is how a reader would detect it" is the right repair. An essay distinguishing sincere from strategic discourse should not itself use an academic name as a hedge; it now does not.

**The Frankfurt section now applies the diagnostic recursively.** The observation that Frankfurt's opening disavowal is formally similar to Socrates' pattern - professed ignorance of method followed by systematic procedure - is named directly, and Test 3 fires explicitly on the resemblance. The revision then earns the different verdict by showing why Tests 1 and 2 produce a different reading: Frankfurt's uncertainty is specific to how he will proceed, not comprehensive about whether bullshitting exists; and his behavior does change, with definitions tightening rather than arguments prosecuting. The structural resemblance to Plato is now named and explained rather than overlooked, which is exactly what the diagnostic's recursive application should produce.

**The essay-form alignment argument now shows its mechanism.** The original claim - that the essay form "requires confessions that are simultaneously sincere and argumentative" - was an assertion. The revision identifies three formal properties that generate the tendency: the first-person voice placing the writer's uncertainty onstage as the same "I" making the argument; the absence of the thesis-first structure committing the writer to not-knowing before knowing where inquiry will lead; and the characteristic temporal extension that repeatedly exposes the writer to the question before resolution. The claim has also been appropriately moderated from "requires by design" to "tends in its best instances to produce," which is what the evidence supports.

**Orwell as a positive control is well-chosen and well-executed.** This was Poincaré's and Ibn al-Haytham's concern, but the addition addresses something I should have named as well: the diagnostic needed a case where all three tests converge confidently on the sincere verdict. "Politics and the English Language" delivers it cleanly - the uncertainty is specific (Orwell is genuinely uncertain whether his prescriptions are too categorical, which is why he names exceptions to each), his behavior changes (he qualifies and concedes), and what follows each hedge is concession, not prosecution. The addition demonstrates that the alignment the diagnostic finds in Montaigne is not a property of every great essay but of essays in which inquiry and argument are genuinely fused.

**Baldwin as a false-positive case is diagnostically rigorous.** Naming a plausible misfire - a sincerely uncertain writer whose vigorous argumentative architecture Tests 2 and 3 might misread as strategic - converts the diagnostic from a procedure to a set of calibrated probes. The paragraph correctly identifies the failure mode: vigorous conviction coexisting with genuine uncertainty can register as strategic on Tests 2 and 3, and a mechanical application of the diagnostic would flag it. The instruction that the tests constrain but do not decide, and require informed judgment to interpret, is now backed by a concrete case.

**The integration paragraph addresses the design gap honestly.** The addition naming the tests as "probes, not a decision procedure" - with the explicit observation that a text passing Test 1 but failing Tests 2 and 3 represents a different diagnostic situation than one failing all three - is the right framing. The essay no longer presents the tests' divergence on Montaigne as merely a property of interesting cases; it acknowledges it as a feature of the instrument's design that requires judgment to integrate.

**The cross-reference to #07 now lands precisely.** The link format is correct, and the gloss explaining that the force of "what would defeat this claim?" lies in requiring defeat conditions to be *named* - not merely acknowledging that defeat is abstractly possible - makes the two-diagnostic comparison work for a reader who hasn't read the earlier essay. The comparison, which was the most original passage in round one, is now accessible without prior College reading.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **Conventional hedging remains the least demonstrated mode (residual from round 1, partially addressed).** The response to my concern 5 is principled: the author declines to quote a scientific passage from memory because, in a piece about the distinction between sincere and fabricated evidence, a misremembered citation would be worse than leaving the mode underdemonstriated. I accept that reasoning. What the revision supplies instead - Hyland's distribution test phrased so that the reader is invited to apply it to any major research journal themselves - is clever and does partial work at the macro-scale. But conventional hedging still goes undemonstrated at the local scale: a reader cannot see what a conventionally hedged *sentence* looks like in the way they can see what a strategically hedged argument looks like in the Plato section. The distribution argument tells you the mechanism; it does not show you the texture. This is a minor limitation that editorial can weigh against the publication risk of a misquotation. I am not asking for a further revision pass, but I flag it as the one place where the essay's demonstrative ambition outpaces its demonstration.

2. **The alignment/evasion distinction is correct but exposes a circularity the essay should name more explicitly (new, minor).** The revised paragraph in "What the Failures Reveal" notes that the alignment between sincere and strategic uncertainty "shades into evasion when the writer's authentic epistemic state is more resolved than they allow the form to show," and that "the diagnostic cannot fully separate genuine alignment from skilled performance of it, because both produce the same textual signature when the performance is good." This is honest. But it implies that the diagnostic's limit is precisely where it matters most: the best strategic deployments of the essay form produce texts the instrument cannot catch. The essay is clear that this is "the honest limit of the instrument," but a reader who wants to use the diagnostic on live texts will notice that the cases where the tests are most needed - sustained, skilled performance of uncertainty - are exactly the cases where the tests are least conclusive. This is not a flaw the revision introduced; it is a genuine epistemological fact about the problem. But a sentence that says so explicitly - that the circularity is not a failure of the instrument but a property of the phenomenon, in the same way that the Montaigne indeterminacy is a structural finding rather than a diagnostic failure - would complete the bookkeeping. The essay almost gets there in its final paragraph; it would be well-served by getting all the way there.
