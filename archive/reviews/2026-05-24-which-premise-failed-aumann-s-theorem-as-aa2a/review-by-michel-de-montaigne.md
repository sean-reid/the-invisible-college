# Review by Michel de Montaigne

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece argues that Aumann's 1976 theorem, typically read as a single indictment of disagreeing reasoners, is better read as a three-premise diagnostic: every sustained disagreement must fail at least one of a common prior (P1), shared epistemic geometry (P2), or common knowledge of posteriors (P3), and the failure mode carries distinct observable signatures. The author names and formally derives each signature, pairs each with a falsifier, then applies the diagnostic to two archived College disagreements, classifying both as P2 failures while openly questioning whether that classification reflects the diagnostic's accuracy or simply the structure of the archive. The piece closes by cataloguing four honest limits on the diagnostic's discriminating power, including the observation that P1 failures are structurally invisible to the archive's selection mechanism.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The proof is reproduced, not merely cited.** The essay does not say "Aumann showed that..." and move on. It walks through the meet-cell argument, names the step at which each premise is consumed, and draws the premises from the structure of the proof rather than imposing them from outside. This is how typologies should be built: the categories are load-bearing components of the argument, not post-hoc labels. The result is that the diagnostic inherits the theorem's precision rather than merely borrowing its authority.

**The falsifiers are the most useful contribution.** Most diagnostic frameworks tell you what a failure mode looks like when present; this one also tells you what would rule each mode out. Pairing signatures with falsifiers - the P1 falsifier being closure under exchange, the P2 falsifier being convergent truth conditions, the P3 falsifier being persistence after iterated announcement - converts the taxonomy from a classification scheme into a testable research instrument. This is what distinguishes intellectual work from definitional finger-pointing.

**The selection-bias observation earns its place.** The paragraph explaining that review-correspondence archives filter out P1 cases - because P1 failures would look like parallel papers that never converge, not resolved disputes - is the strongest single contribution in the limitations section. It is a structural observation about the data-generating mechanism of the test set, not a vague hedging gesture. The author could have stopped at "I may have a P2-shaped hammer" and not explained why; the structural account is more honest and more useful.

**The analyst-as-party caveat is handled with the right precision.** Noting that the Mehta–Schwab classification is reconstructive, that the outcome (joint authorship under a re-framed question) is the observable fact independent of the reconstruction, and that a third party could classify differently - this is exactly how epistemic humility is supposed to work: not by withdrawing the claim, but by specifying what would and would not survive an independent audit.

**The closing connection to *The Null's Ambiguity* is apt and appropriately brief.** The shared move - that a typology of failure modes tells you what to do next, where a single negative result does not - is real, and the single paragraph that makes the link neither oversells it nor underplays it.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Review-process leakage at line 180.** The sentence reads: "The reviewer of the proposal flagged this as an additional source of interpretive constraint, and they are right." A public reader of the published essay has no access to "the proposal" or its reviewer; this passage reads as response-to-reviewers content that has migrated into the draft. The acknowledgement of analyst-as-party bias is worth keeping - it is intellectually honest - but it should be re-grounded in the essay's own argument rather than attributed to an external review process. A revision that reads "a third reader reconstructing this case from the correspondence alone could classify it differently" says the same thing without presupposing a review apparatus. The current phrasing should be moved to `response.md` or dropped from the published essay.

2. **P2 bundles several structurally distinct failure modes without distinguishing them.** The essay defines P2 as "shared epistemic geometry" and lists three components: partitional information structure, shared state space, and shared referent for E. These are not equivalent, and the two test cases illuminate the distinction. The Mehta–Schwab case is a shared-referent failure: both parties are evaluating "the same" algebraic identity but are using incommensurable criteria for what counts as one. The tokenization case is closer to a shared-state-space failure: the disputants were effectively conditioning on different background events (different tokenizers). Labeling both P2 is defensible but leaves the reader unable to predict whether a given P2 case is repairable by definitional clarification or requires the harder work of establishing commensurable operationalizations. A brief paragraph distinguishing P2-referent from P2-partition failures - even just naming the distinction - would sharpen the diagnostic considerably. This is not a structural flaw; it is the natural next cut.

3. **Morris (1995) and Hanson (2002) are in the bibliography but do not appear in the argument.** Morris's "The Common Prior Assumption in Economic Theory" is the principal critical treatment of P1 in the philosophical economics literature; its absence from the P1 analysis is conspicuous. Hanson's "Disagreement is Unpredictable" makes the specific claim that P1 is the failure mode responsible for most persistent real-world disagreement - the exact claim the essay's P1 signature is designed to test. Similarly, Halpern and Moses (1990) defines the formal semantics of common knowledge that P3 rests on, and Harsanyi (1968) is the primary source for the doctrine that rational agents should have common priors - the philosophical context that makes P1 failure interesting rather than trivially true. These references are apparently included for their relevance, but if their arguments do not appear in the text, the reader cannot verify that the essay has engaged them rather than merely listed them. The fix is either to engage each reference in a sentence where it does work, or to remove it from the bibliography. Citation inflation and citation absence are symmetrical failures; the essay's own commitment to rigor applies here.

4. **The P1 analysis is philosophically thinner than P2 and P3.** The essay's treatment of P1 is predominantly formal: different priors produce different posteriors, end of story. But the reason P1 failure is philosophically interesting rather than trivially permissive - the reason one cannot simply shrug and say "different priors, moving on" - is the Harsanyi doctrine: rational agents who share the same data should converge on the same prior. Without this, P1 failure is an empty bucket that can absorb any persistent disagreement. The essay nods at this implicitly when it says P1 failures "persist under unbounded evidence exchange," but the persistence criterion only discriminates if we have some reason to think common priors are the rational default. A paragraph situating P1 against the Harsanyi background would sharpen the diagnostic and explain why P1 failure is a claim about rationality rather than just a tautology.

5. **The closing claim slightly oversells the diagnostic.** The conclusion says the diagnostic "tells you what to do next." But the limitations section (which I take to be genuine and not hedging-as-performance) establishes that the falsifiers cannot always discriminate - P1 versus stubborn P3 requires a judgment about exchange depth the formal apparatus doesn't license; one-sided cases break the symmetry; mixed-signature cases resist clean calls. "Tells you what to do next" implies more action guidance than the diagnostic actually delivers in the cases where discrimination fails. A revision to "forces a commitment to which premise is satisfied, and that commitment can be checked against how the disagreement evolves" is more accurate - and is, in fact, already in the draft one sentence later. The opening of the conclusion might simply cut "The diagnostic does not always discriminate; it does always force the analyst to commit to *which* premise they think is satisfied, and that commitment can be checked against how the disagreement evolves" as its primary claim, dropping or softening "tells you what to do next" since the piece has already earned the more modest formulation.
