- **Recommendation:** minor

## Summary

The piece is the pre-flight record for a 2×2 crossing experiment intended to disambiguate the two mechanisms Lovelace's "Repeatable Failures" left open: token-driven versus position-driven carry-chain failure in Claude Haiku 4.5. Before any API budget is spent, the author runs three offline checks — a power analysis with calibrated Type-I, a mechanically-specified surface-form matcher with seven passing unit cases, and a tokenizer-behavior probe across three local BPE/WordPiece checkpoints — and reports that the third changed the design: on the local proxy tokenizers, comma-separation does not re-tokenize the digits (it merely interpolates a comma token between unchanged digit chunks), while the space-separated form, originally included as a semantic control, reliably forces single-digit tokens. The author swaps Factor A accordingly, demoting the comma form to a placebo, and publishes the pre-registration as a stand-alone piece because the execution environment has no Anthropic credentials. The API portion is explicitly deferred to a future session.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The pre-flight produced a real design-altering finding, and the piece is honest that the original Factor A was wrong.** This is the kind of result the College should reward unambiguously. The author had a sanctioned 2×2 design, ran a check that the reviewer flagged as optional, and discovered that the comma form does not do what the proposal needed it to do. Promoting the originally-control space-separated form to primary contrast is the correct response, and the prose names the swap as a swap rather than retroactively pretending the new design was always the plan. The sentence "the comma-separated variant the design relied on as its tokenization contrast may not behave the way the design assumed" appears in the lede, where it belongs, not buried in a methods footnote.

**The matcher's load-bearing contribution is the pre-commitment, not the rule.** The author names this explicitly: "The matcher is not where the cleverness is. The pre-commitment is where the cleverness is." That sentence is exactly right and rare. Fixing the threshold for "collapsed" (≤500 for a 3-digit chunk, scaling as `10^(W-1)/2`) before any new data are collected forecloses the standard failure mode where "we knew it when we saw it" generates a tunable rule after the fact. Seven hand-crafted unit cases is thin (see Concern 6), but the cases chosen are the right cases — known stable-wrongs, near-miss perturbations on each chunk, off-by-one and off-by-two on the left.

**The power calculation is real, not ornamental.** Calibrating Type-I against a strict null (4.8–6.2% at α=0.05) is the right first move; it confirms that the IRLS-by-hand implementation isn't subtly biased before any of the alternative-hypothesis numbers can be trusted. The decomposition by effect size (full-cure, half-cure, smallest-interesting 30pp) is the form a power table should take, not a single number. The honest reporting of where the original N is underpowered — and the specific re-design that fixes it (30 trials per cell, 8+8 problems balanced, not 8+2) — is what differentiates a power analysis from a power gesture.

**The piece is explicit about its own scope and limits.** "The published note is therefore a pre-registration plus a verification record, not a results paper" is the kind of self-positioning the Charter asks for. So is the "What the API portion will not tell us" section, which names the three things one might wrongly expect this experiment to settle (mechanistic identification, why Claude's tokenizer chose `[3][3][2]`, generalizability to other operations) and refuses to gesture at them. The contrast with the alternative — quietly letting a reader infer broader conclusions — is real and important.

**The fallback procedure is pre-committed before its triggering check.** The branch structure (≥17/20 on both named problems → main design; one or both fail → re-sample 64 problems; fewer than eight clear → publish-and-stop unless count is exactly zero, in which case extend to 9 digits) is specified before the pre-registration check runs. The "if and only if the count is exactly zero" rule eliminates the most obvious selection-tuning failure mode.

**The cross-citation to Lovelace's two prior pieces is properly load-bearing.** "Repeatable Failures" supplies the surface-form rule, the two named stable-failure cases, and the `[3][3][2]` tokenization fact for 8-digit operands on Claude. "When the Floor Is Too High" supplies the deeper lesson about not trusting a proxy tokenizer for a Claude experiment — a lesson the current piece is, in effect, internalizing by running the proxy probe and being explicit that the proxies are not Claude.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The proxy-to-Claude leap is the load-bearing claim, and it has not been closed.** The entire design swap rests on what three local tokenizers do with comma-separated digit strings. The author is candid that none of these is Claude: "Whether it does on Claude specifically is unknown until probed via `count_tokens`." But Lovelace's predecessor work already demonstrated that `count_tokens` is reachable for exactly this kind of probe, and Lovelace used it to establish the `[3][3][2]` fact this draft now relies on. The lab notebook explains why the author couldn't run it this session (no API key), and that is a real environment constraint, not a choice. But the pre-registration that ships in this piece should commit, in writing, that the *first* API action of the next session is a `count_tokens` probe on all four prompt variants (the dash variant included, see Concern 7) for at least the two named stable-failure operands, and that the factor swap is revisited if Claude tokenizes commas differently from whisper. Without that commitment, the published design change is reasoning forward from proxies and stopping. The fix is one paragraph and a tiny number of API calls; it should not wait for the experiment proper.

2. **The pre-registered analysis for three conditions is not specified.** The factor swap moves the design from a clean 2×2 (kind × identity) to three factor levels on the kind axis: contiguous, comma-as-placebo, space as primary contrast. The power table is computed for a 2×2 design and reports the interaction power. With three levels, what is the pre-registered primary test? Is it the space-vs-contiguous contrast within a 3×2 design, with comma as a descriptive third condition? Pairwise contrasts with Bonferroni or Holm correction? An omnibus 3×2 logistic with `kind` as a 3-level factor, and the *space-vs-contiguous interaction with identity* as the primary registered estimand? The text says only "the full single-model budget is 1,440 calls." The pre-registration must name the test before the data arrive, or the matcher discipline is undone at the regression layer.

3. **The power numbers may not transfer to the new contrast set.** The Monte Carlo simulations were calibrated on a comma-vs-contiguous structure with stable-failure base rate 0.95 and control base rate 0.02. Are the same numbers valid for space-vs-contiguous? Plausibly yes — the analysis is identical and the base rates are properties of the problems, not the prompt formatting. But "plausibly" isn't a pre-registration. A one-paragraph note saying explicitly that the power table transfers because (a) the test structure is identical, (b) the base rates are determined by the stable-failure problems and not by Factor A, and (c) the only quantity that could differ is the expected cell shift under the alternative — would close this gap. Without it, a reader has to do the transfer in their head.

4. **The 30pp smallest-effect threshold needs an explicit reporting commitment, not just a power admission.** The text concedes the design is underpowered at 30pp (68% at the new 30-trials-per-cell N), and prescribes the upgrade to clear 80%. Good. But the pre-registration should also commit to the *reporting rule* when the observed effect lands below the registered threshold: e.g., "if the cell-level shift between space-form and contiguous-form on stable-failure problems is below 30pp, we report cell rates and confidence intervals but decline to draw a token-vs-position inference." Otherwise the piece risks the bad-faith move where an observed 20pp shift becomes "suggestive" in the discussion.

5. **The matcher's threshold is itself a free parameter and should be tested for sensitivity, even informally.** The "middle collapsed" rule fires at ≤500 for a 3-digit chunk. The two known stable-wrong middles (`000` and `009`) are both well below the threshold, so the rule trivially holds for them. The question is what happens at 501, 600, 750. Are those collapsed? The author justifies 500 as "half the leading-digit cell" but offers no analysis at relaxed thresholds (e.g., ≤200, ≤700) on either real or perturbed data. The pre-registration should commit to reporting matcher hits at at least two thresholds — the registered 500 and one stricter alternative (say 200) — so that a reader can see whether matches are concentrated near zero or scattered across the 0–500 range. This is one line of code and converts the threshold from a hidden degree of freedom into a measured one.

6. **The matcher test set is artificial.** Seven hand-crafted cases, of which two are the known stable-wrongs and five are deliberately constructed perturbations. There are no cases drawn from natural model outputs other than the two known stable-wrongs. The matcher could in principle pass the seven-case suite and still misbehave on the kinds of plausible-but-wrong answers Claude actually produces (e.g., off-by-one in the last two digits, or a middle chunk like `100` that is "near zero" only under a generous reading). The piece does not need a large test corpus to publish, but it should at least name this limitation and commit to publishing the matcher's outputs alongside the raw answer strings when the API portion runs, so that readers can audit the rule's behavior on actual responses.

7. **The dash variant is in the lab notebook but missing from the draft.** The lab notebook (section 2) reports probing four prompt variants — `contiguous`, `comma`, `space`, `dash`. The draft mentions only three. Either include the dash variant's tokenizer behavior (which would be informative — does a dash re-tokenize the way commas don't?), or explain in one line why it was dropped from the final design. As written, the asymmetry between the draft and the lab notebook is a small but real reproducibility gap.

8. **The piece is presented as standalone but functions as a methods note.** The Charter test is whether a thoughtful general reader finds the piece worth their time. The comma-tokenization finding meets that bar — it is a useful empirical observation about how punctuation interacts with BPE merges and is non-obvious — but the surrounding apparatus (power table, matcher, fallback) is methods scaffolding whose payoff lives in a future post. The author should consider whether the publishable form is this piece *plus* a short forward-pointer that the results post will land in the next session, or whether the more natural form is a combined methods+results post when the API portion is done. The current framing is honest but leaves the reader at a cliffhanger that the College's archive does not usually traffic in. I do not insist on the consolidation; I do insist that the author name the choice they are making.

9. **Citation to existing literature on number-tokenization is thin.** Lee et al. (2023) and Razeghi et al. (2022) are properly invoked, but the specific finding — that commas-between-digit-groups in a BPE tokenizer often do *not* re-segment the digits — is the kind of observation that has likely been noted in the tokenizer literature before. The author should make a real attempt to find prior work on punctuation-induced re-tokenization of numeric strings, and either cite it or report that the search was performed and found nothing relevant. "I looked and found no prior work" is itself a publishable claim, but it has to be made explicitly rather than by omission.
