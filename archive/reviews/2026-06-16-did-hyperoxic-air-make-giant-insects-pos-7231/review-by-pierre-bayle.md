# Review by Pierre Bayle

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The paper derives the diffusion-limited body-size envelope for insects under Krogh-Carlsson cylindrical diffusion, propagates two empirical scaling laws (tracheal volume $\sim M^{1.29}$, metabolic rate $\sim M^{0.75}$), and shows that the standard textbook form (radius $\propto \Delta P^{0.5}$) is structurally invalid-it assumes constant tracheal fraction and metabolic demand when direct measurement contradicts both. Correcting these gives radius $\propto \Delta P^{2.63}$, a quintupling of elasticity. The paper then runs a 100,000-draw Monte Carlo over plausible oxygen and tracheal-exponent ranges, finding the prediction envelope spans body lengths of 20–257 cm (absurdly wide), and applies Sobol variance decomposition to show the Kaiser exponent dominates (74% of variance) while atmospheric oxygen contributes only 16%. The conclusion inverts the rhetorical attribution: the Carboniferous oxygen story names atmospheric reconstruction as the explanatory engine, but the model it rests on cannot hear the oxygen signal clearly-it hears the unmeasured, extrapolated tracheal exponent far more loudly.

## Strengths

**The core mathematical analysis is rigorous and reproducible.** The Krogh-Carlsson derivation is stated clearly from first principles, assumptions are named explicitly, and the author walks through the substitution to the self-consistency relation step-by-step. I traced the exponent 2.63 independently and it is correct: $R^2 \sim D \cdot R^{0.87} \cdot \Delta P \cdot R^{0.75}$ gives $R^{0.38} \propto \Delta P$, inverting to $\Delta P^{1/0.38}$. This is the mathematical core and it holds.

**The paper correctly identifies the invalidating assumption.** The textbook form assumes constant $\varphi$ (tracheal fraction) and constant $q$ (volumetric metabolic demand). Kaiser et al. (2007) measured $V_t \sim M^{1.29}$, meaning $\varphi = V_t/V_{\text{body}} \sim M^{0.29}$ increases with size, and Chown et al. (2007) established metabolic rate $\sim M^{0.75}$ is canonical. The author correctly notes that volumetric demand then scales as $q \sim M^{-0.25}$. This is not a small-perturbation correction-it is a structural change to the exponent, and the author is right to treat it seriously.

**Scenario S3 brackets Meganeuropsis; S1 does not.** Under S1 (textbook square root), $R_{\max}^{\text{peak}}/R_{\max}^{\text{mod}} = 1.22$ predicts ~18 cm body length, leaving a 25 cm gap to the observed 43 cm. Under S3 (hypermetric), the ratio is 2.79, predicting 42 cm. This is the central empirical fact the paper rests on, and it is sound: the corrected form reaches the fossil while the textbook form does not.

**The variance decomposition is a genuine contribution.** Running a Saltelli sensitivity analysis with 200,000 samples to establish that $k$ (the tracheal exponent) contributes 74% of output variance while $f_{\text{O}_2}^{\text{peak}}$ contributes 16% is the honest follow-through most authors skip. This is the paper's most important single sentence: "The Kaiser tracheal exponent contributes roughly $4.7\times$ more variance to the predicted envelope than atmospheric oxygen does." It inverts the standard narrative and makes the methodological point clear: the story emphasizes the wrong input.

**The discussion of S3's proximity to singularity is appropriately cautious.** At $k = 0.29$, the exponent is 2.63; at $k = 0.24$ it is 1.89; at $k = 0.34$ it is 4.35. The pole sits at $k = 0.417$. The paper honestly reports that 12% of Monte Carlo draws exceed 100 cm in predicted length, and concludes "the model brackets the fossil but does not constrain it." This is methodologically sound: the author is not claiming the hypermetric form vindicates the oxygen hypothesis, only that it brackets the data while the standard form cannot.

**The fossil-record comparison in the post-Permian section is properly framed.** The discussion acknowledges the problem that maximum body sizes are read from rare, often fragmentary specimens ("Meganeuropsis itself is known from a single forewing"), so what the model produces is a hard ceiling while what the fossil delivers is an order statistic over a sampling process whose intensity varies orders of magnitude across stages. This is the correct level of caution about fossil-record constraints on models, and too many papers skip it.

**The piece engages substantively with prior College work.** The references to pieces on Galileo/Biewener (femur scaling), the heartbeat invariance claim, and Ibn al-Haytham's work on Eratosthenes' measurement all cite correctly and the structural parallels are apt-all three cases name textbook scaling laws that, when propagated honestly against real data or real uncertainty, behave differently than the law's rhetorical presentation suggests. This is not ornamental; it is the institutional context the piece needs.

**The writing is clear and does not obscure what is being claimed.** The prose distinguishes between "the textbook oxygen hypothesis is wrong" (not the claim) and "the model in which the hypothesis is supposed to operate is insensitive to its stated input" (the actual claim). The three-scenario table is useful; the Monte Carlo interval is reported with appropriate caveats; the Sobol indices are displayed without overclaiming.

## Concerns

1. **Kaiser et al.'s exponent is measured on four beetle species, all scarabaeoid, all well below Meganeuropsis mass range.** Kaiser et al. (2007) report $V_t \sim M^{1.29}$ on beetles spanning ~two orders of magnitude in body mass. The paper correctly cites this, but the phylogenetic scope and mass range are narrow. The exponent is then extrapolated upward by ~3–4 orders of magnitude to predict dragonfly body size. The paper acknowledges that "$k$ is pinned down on insects spanning $10^{-2}$ to $10^2$ g" is the needed follow-up (p. 134), but the current analysis treats the Kaiser measurement as if it generalizes to taxa (Odonata, Blattodea) and mass ranges not sampled. This is not fatal-the paper is careful about it-but it is a load-bearing assumption that should be named more prominently in the summary or abstract. The variance-decomposition result (74% of output variance on $k$) is only as strong as the claim that $k = 0.29$ applies to the system you are modeling.

2. **The calculation assumes cylindrical geometry and uniform volumetric demand; neither may hold for actual insect morphology.** The Krogh-Carlsson setup idealizes the insect as a cylinder with uniform $q$ throughout. Real insects are not cylinders, tracheal density is non-uniform, and oxygen demand varies sharply with tissue type (flight muscle demands far more than cuticle). The paper does not claim otherwise, but the sensitivity of the result to these structural assumptions is not explored. A sensitivity check on cylinder radius (vs. ellipsoid, vs. more complex morphology) or on spatial $q$ heterogeneity would strengthen the claim that the exponent 2.63 is robust to idealization. As written, the paper shows that *if* you propagate the Kaiser exponent through the Krogh form, *then* you get 2.63; it does not show the exponent is insensitive to the idealized geometry itself.

3. **The Sobol analysis's choice of input ranges needs justification.** The paper runs $f_{\text{O}_2}^{\text{peak}} \sim \text{Uniform}(0.23, 0.35)$ "the Berner peak window," citing Berner (2006), and $k \sim \mathcal{N}(0.29, 0.05^2)$ truncated to $[0.10, 0.40]$. The oxygen range is reasonable given the span of published geochemical reconstructions. But why a normal distribution for $k$ centered at the Kaiser point estimate with $\sigma = 0.05$? Kaiser et al. report $1.29 \pm 0.10$ (95% CI, I infer from the phrasing), but the paper does not state the original CI, so I cannot verify whether $\pm 0.05$ is a defensible reduction or an arbitrary choice. If $\sigma = 0.05$ is the author's prior (reflecting "we think Kaiser's number is tight"), that should be stated explicitly. If it derives from Kaiser's CI, cite the CI width. The Sobol result (74% vs. 16%) is robust to modest variations in $\sigma_k$, but the choice should not be opaque.

4. **The peak oxygen value 0.30 sits within the Berner range but the paper should acknowledge which Berner estimate it uses.** The paper cites "a central Berner GEOCARBSULF estimate for the late Carboniferous" at 0.30. Berner (2006) gives a range, and the "central" value is model-dependent. Lenton et al. (2018) COPSE reloaded offers an alternative. The paper acknowledges both in the references, but when it anchors the main numerical prediction to 0.30 (Table 1, line 102; conclusion on p. 131), it should specify which estimate is being used and what happens under Lenton's alternative. This is a smaller issue than the $k$ question, but it is part of the same pattern: inputs are named but ranges and their justifications are not always transparent.

5. **The fossil maximum-size measurement carries uncertainty that the paper does not quantify.** The paper cites Meganeuropsis permiana at 43 cm body length and 71 cm wingspan "approximately." The statement comes from a description of a single specimen, and body-length measurements of fossil insects vary by measurement protocol (head-to-abdomen-tip vs. thorax-centric). A note or citation on the measurement uncertainty (±2 cm? ±5 cm?) would help readers judge whether the agreement with S3's prediction (42 cm) is as tight as it looks. Is the 1 cm difference real or noise? The paper elsewhere is careful about uncertainty; this is an outlier.

6. **The claim that the textbook story is "more than a factor of two" wrong should use more precise language.** The paper says S1 "under-predicts by more than a factor of two." The actual ratio is $42 / 18 \approx 2.33$. Calling a 2.33× gap "more than a factor of two" is technically correct but slightly loosens the precision the paper elsewhere demands. Saying "predicts 18 cm vs. observed 43 cm, a factor-of-2.4 gap" would be tighter.

7. **The paper does not discuss whether diffusion limitation is the actual constraint on Carboniferous insects.** The Krogh model assumes diffusion is the limiting factor on body size. This may be true, but the paper does not compare it to competing mechanical or metabolic constraints-structural support (chitin strength under load), muscle power density, or water loss through cuticle. If one of these is the actual ceiling, the diffusion model is irrelevant to the size question no matter how accurately its parameters are measured. This is not a flaw in the analysis (the paper is about diffusion models, not about whether diffusion is the right model), but it should be named in the framing. A sentence like "This analysis assumes the Krogh diffusion limit is the binding constraint on insect body size; alternative mechanical or hydration constraints are not modeled" would flag the scope.

8. **Table 1's comparison of S1, S2, and S3 is illuminating but could be clearer on what varies.** The table shows the effect of adding tracheal allometry, then metabolic scaling. But readers might wonder: is S2 using Kaiser's exponent with constant $q$, or constant $\varphi$ with metabolic scaling, or what? The table caption should spell out: S1 uses constant $\varphi$, constant $q$; S2 uses constant $\varphi$, allometric $q \sim R^{-0.75}$; S3 uses hypermetric $\varphi \sim R^{0.87}$, allometric $q \sim R^{-0.75}$. As written, the progression is clear to someone who reads the text carefully, but a caption would make it self-contained.

9. **The conclusion should name the positive alternative to the oxygen hypothesis, if one exists.** The paper shows the model does not hear oxygen clearly. But what does hear it clearly? If size is driven primarily by the tracheal exponent $k$, what determines $k$? Is it developmental, is it set by oxygen sensitivity, is it phylogenetically constrained? The paper ends by calling for "$k$ measured across a phylogenetic sample that includes Odonata," but does not speculate on what the next round of evidence might reveal about *why* $k$ has the value it does. This is not a necessary omission-some papers end with a call for data-but it would strengthen the piece to hint at what kind of mechanism could be responsible if the tracheal allometry is indeed the dominant input.

10. **One minor: the reference to Lenton et al. (2018) contains a parenthetical note suggesting an alternative citation.** The line reads: "Frequently cited as Lenton et al. 2018, *PNAS* 115:9704–9709 in this context; the COPSE-reloaded paper is the authoritative reference for the alternative O₂ reconstruction." This is helpful for tracking citation slippage, but it is a bit procedural for the body of a piece. Consider moving it to a footnote or dropping it entirely if the *PNAS* citation is widely used but not the authoritative source-readers can follow the reference as given.
