- **Recommendation:** minor

## Summary

The piece proposes a structural diagnostic for institutional-mechanism accounts: at the action-formation level - the second of Hedström and Ylikoski's three steps - two analytically distinct mechanism families, information/calculation and identity/obligation, are routinely conflated, with policy consequences. Each mechanism is specified through three operative conditions, and their predictions diverge in failure mode, intervention design, and transplant feasibility. The diagnostic is applied retrospectively to three prior College audits - referral hiring, compliance monitoring, commons governance - and prospectively to occupational licensing, where it generates a testable prediction that quality degradation under deregulation should concentrate in practitioners outside the surviving professional community rather than spread uniformly across the deregulated cohort. The most actionable conclusion is an asymmetry of designability: information conditions can be installed at design time, while normative conditions require community pre-existence and cannot.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The action-formation focus is the right target.** Locating the ambiguity precisely at Hedström and Ylikoski's middle step - rather than at the situational step (where structures are usually well-specified) or the aggregation step (where assertion is endemic in a more familiar way) - is the move that gives the diagnostic its grip. It explains why the conflation is durable: it lives at the level where mechanism accounts most often run on intuition rather than specification.

**The two mechanisms get three operative conditions each.** This is not just naming; it is structure. The information mechanism's conditions - observability, belief-updating, cost-structure - name parameters with measurement traditions. The normative mechanism's conditions - participation in norm-setting, identity-as-membership, detection-independent emotional response - are also stated as conditions rather than waved at. The mechanism families become things one could in principle look for, not vocabularies one could merely deploy.

**The Reductive Challenge section directly engages the game-theoretic dismissal.** The piece grants that the reductive response - "normative compliance is just internalized information-mechanism with a longer socialization loop" - does not fail on its face, then derives four empirical commitments the reductive account would have to honor. This is exactly the right move: treating the alternative as a substantive empirical position with its own predictions rather than a definitional escape. Fehr–Gächter is the canonical discriminator and is correctly invoked.

**The diagnostic table is a real organizational achievement.** Cross-tabulating failure mode, transplant condition, policy lever, and observable discriminator across the three cases forces the reader to see that the mechanisms make differently shaped predictions in differently shaped settings. The same row structure applied to three different institutions is what gives the diagnostic its claim to generality.

**The licensing application is genuinely prospective.** Most cross-domain applications either argue from existing data or stop at "and this could be applied to X." This one identifies an institutional feature the standard literature ignores (the professional community embedded in licensing structure, distinct from the credential itself) and produces a specific empirical prediction with the data requirements for the test stated explicitly. The observation that the data have not been collected - rather than that the test has been run and failed - is the right institutional finding.

**The designability asymmetry is argued, not asserted.** The piece earns its strongest conclusion: that information conditions are activatable at install time and normative conditions are not. The argument - that observability infrastructure is a constructable artifact while community pre-existence is a historical fact - is right where it lands, and it inverts the surface impression that observability is "technically demanding" while community formation is "merely sociological."

**The prose is calibrated.** The piece does not overclaim. "Undischarged alternatives," "structurally distinct," "not yet established," "feasible test, data not yet collected" - these are honest quantifiers for what the diagnostic can and cannot do at this stage.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The reductive challenge is dispatched too quickly.** The section grants that the game-theoretic response "does not fail on its face," lists four divergent empirical commitments, then leans on the Fehr–Gächter result as the clean discriminator. But the reductive theorist has secondary moves the piece does not engage: that subjects bring lifetime priors from environments where one-shot anonymity is rare (so the heuristic mis-fires by trained habit), or that evolutionary baselines were calibrated for small-group repeat-interaction conditions and have not updated to the laboratory's one-shot anonymous frame. Either explanation reconciles costly punishment with reduction without invoking the normative mechanism. The piece doesn't have to settle the question - leaving alternatives "undischarged" is its own tradition - but the Fehr–Gächter discriminator currently reads as conclusive when it isn't. Naming these secondary lines, even in a sentence, would calibrate the claim.

2. **The asymmetry in operational tractability between the two mechanisms is invisible in the table.** The information mechanism's conditions reference observables with measurement traditions: detection rates, monitoring intensity, payoff structure. The normative mechanism's conditions reference constructs that resist operationalization: "identity structure," "community membership," "obligation, pride, shame." The diagnostic asks for symmetric tests but the conditions don't admit symmetric measurement. This shows through in the discriminator row: "Do violations cluster in the hard-to-detect region, or in the population *outside the normative community*?" - but how is "outside the normative community" identified without already taking the normative account seriously? Same problem in the commons row: "absent community preconditions" is a discriminator only if "community preconditions" has an independent operational definition. The piece should acknowledge that the two mechanisms are not measurement-symmetric and indicate what counts as evidence for the normative side.

3. **The Hechter participation requirement is taken as definitional but is theoretically contested.** The normative mechanism's first condition - "participation in a norm-setting process - not merely exposure to the norm's content, but voice in its formation" - is one theoretical commitment among several. Religious socialization, childhood internalization, and emotion-based internalization (Haidt's social intuitionism, work in moral foundations) produce strong normative compliance without anything resembling Hechter's participation condition. This matters because the licensing prospective application leans on "participation in standard-setting" as a key normative feature; if internalization happens through other routes, the deregulation prediction needs to be recast. The piece should either defend Hechter against the alternatives or relax the participation condition to a disjunction over internalization pathways.

4. **The licensing prediction has a confound the piece does not address.** The proposed test - quality degradation under deregulation concentrated in the unaffiliated population - cannot, as stated, distinguish "lost the normative mechanism" from "those who exit voluntary association are systematically lower-quality at baseline." Unaffiliated practitioners likely differ on dimensions correlated with quality (training history, financial marginality, risk tolerance) that predate the deregulation event. The piece should flag this and propose at least one design that handles selection: matched comparisons across multiple deregulation events, an instrument that shifts association cost without shifting practitioner type, or within-practitioner before/after tracking with affiliation status as the moderator. Without this, the test as written has an attractive prediction but cannot deliver the inference.

5. **The diagnostic creates institutional duty toward prior College work that the piece does not fully acknowledge.** The treatment of `Compliance as Selection` says the selection result and the normative alternative are "undischarged alternatives" and that the existing evidence base does not settle which is operative. That is a correction to the prior piece's framing, not merely a complement to it. The piece should either say so explicitly - and indicate what the prior piece would need to do to discharge the normative alternative - or be more careful that this is a complementary reading rather than a corrective one. Cross-referencing prior work is good practice; leaving the relationship vague when the diagnostic actually has implications for the prior piece's standing is not.

6. **The "Both invoked; neither specified" cell for commons governance underplays what the prior piece did.** `The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance` explicitly engaged the information/normative split in DP3 and noted divergent transplant predictions. The table cell as written reads as if the literature collectively had not addressed the split, but the prior College piece had - the new diagnostic makes that prior treatment more precise rather than discovering the split for the first time. A footnote or row qualification would protect the reader from missing this continuity.

7. **"More demanding precisely where it matters most" deserves a quantified illustration.** The designability asymmetry is the piece's most actionable conclusion. A reader would benefit from one worked example - even hypothetical - of the time-scale difference: install observability infrastructure in N months; build a normative community in N years. Without something concrete, "more demanding" reads as rhetorical rather than operational, and the rhetoric is doing the work the conclusion needs the structure to do.

8. **Minor: no process leakage; no LaTeX-eligible notation flagged.** The piece reads as a clean public artifact - no "this revision," "round 1," "the panel said," or similar process narration to excise. Symbols and equations are not present in a form that would benefit from math mode; "M^(4/3)" and Greek letters from related College pieces do not appear here.
