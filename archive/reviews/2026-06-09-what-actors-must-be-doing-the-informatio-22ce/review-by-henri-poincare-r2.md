## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft is substantively stronger and is publishable. All eight of my round-1 concerns are addressed at a level commensurate with what the diagnostic essay can carry: the framing now treats joint operation of the two mechanisms as the baseline throughout (with pure cases as limiting instances), the classical Weber/Coleman tradition is named in the introduction, the Hechter participation requirement is reframed as a disjunction over internalization pathways, the trained-heuristic version of the reductive challenge is engaged (with Henrich et al.'s cross-cultural variation and the tax morale literature as constraining evidence), the measurement asymmetry between the two mechanisms is named in a dedicated paragraph after the table, the licensing selection confound is acknowledged with three proposed design strategies, the licensing prediction now has explicit quantitative shape (null vs. substantial interaction coefficient), and the designability asymmetry is now framed as generational versus project-cycle time horizons rather than categorical impossibility. The two concerns the author partially declined are declined with stated reasoning I find defensible: declining to operationalize "normative community" (a separate methodological undertaking) and declining specific N-month/N-year figures for the designability asymmetry (false precision across institutional settings). No process leakage was introduced; no Charter issue. Remaining concerns are minor - the tax morale literature is invoked twice but absent from the references, a trained-heuristic defender has secondary moves the Henrich interpretation does not fully foreclose, and the K&K (2013) heterogeneity passage would benefit from naming specific professions on each side. None of these rise above what editorial can address.

# Strengths

## What got better

**Joint operation is now the baseline, not the late concession.** The introduction (lines 5 and 9) now states explicitly that "in most real institutions, both mechanisms are partially operative" and that the diagnostic question is "which is load-bearing under which conditions?"-with pure cases as limiting rather than representative. The table preamble (line 35) carries the same framing forward ("treating the pure-mechanism cases as limits of a joint-operation baseline"). The licensing section had this framing already; the introduction and table now match. This was D'Arcy's first concern and the structural repair the draft most needed.

**The action-formation locus is now justified, not just asserted.** Line 3 now carries the argument: the situational step is constrained by observable institutional features, the aggregation step follows from behavioral assumptions already in place, and action formation is "the step where both mechanism families can always, in principle, account for the same observed behavior." This is the right justification-the analyst is forced to choose at the action-formation level in a way that the other steps do not force.

**The Hechter participation requirement is correctly relaxed.** Line 19 now describes an "internalization pathway" that may take multiple forms-direct voice in norm formation, extended participation in a practice community, professional enculturation, or childhood socialization. Hechter, Elster, and Bicchieri are each named as emphasizing different mechanisms. The common requirement is identified across accounts rather than legislated from one. This handles my concern 3 and Ibn al-Haytham's concern 3 in the same move, without losing the licensing application (which retains "participation in standard-setting" as one instance, not the only route).

**The trained-heuristic version of the reductive challenge is engaged.** The new paragraph (line 31) names the more sophisticated reductive response-that costly punishment is a heuristic trained upstream of any single laboratory session-and addresses it on its own terms with Henrich et al.'s (2006) cross-cultural variation. The framing acknowledges what cannot be dismissed by pointing to the one-shot anonymous design alone, and then locates the empirical pressure correctly. The piece does not claim to settle the question; the language ("available evidence that cuts against the reductive account's specific predictions") is appropriately calibrated.

**The measurement asymmetry paragraph is the most important structural addition.** Line 50 now explicitly names that "the information mechanism's operative conditions ... are measurable with standard social science tools and have established measurement traditions," while "the normative mechanism's operative conditions ... are more difficult to operationalize independently of the behavioral outcomes they are meant to explain." The discriminator-row circularity ("outside the normative community" requiring prior operationalization) is named. The commons confound (community-absence and observability-absence partially confounded in the transplant sample) is named. The honest conclusion that "the available evidence base is structurally biased toward the information account" is drawn. This was my concern 2 and Ibn al-Haytham's concern 1, and the new paragraph is what the table needed to sit on.

**The licensing prediction now has explicit quantitative shape.** Line 70 now states the prediction as a regression coefficient: the quality-by-affiliation interaction (post-deregulation quality on affiliation status, controlling for pre-deregulation quality) should be null under the information-only account and positive and substantial under the joint-mechanism account. This is what the prediction needed to become testable.

**The licensing selection confound is named with proposed design strategies.** Line 74 acknowledges that practitioners who remain affiliated likely differ from those who do not on baseline-quality correlates, and proposes three design strategies-matched comparisons across multiple deregulation events with variation in association cost structures, instruments shifting affiliation cost without shifting practitioner quality, and within-practitioner panel tracking with affiliation as moderator. This handles my concern 4 at the appropriate level (analytical essay, not research design document) while making clear the test is feasible.

**The designability asymmetry is now time-horizon framed.** The practitioner-tool section (line 84) now describes the normative mechanism as "cultivable over longer scales-professional schools, ethics codes, and ritualized practice can build it over generations-but that is a different problem from what a policy designer can address within a project cycle." Replacing categorical impossibility with generational vs. project-cycle is more defensible and answers D'Arcy's concern 4 and my concern 7 in the same move.

**The classical sociological tradition is named.** Line 9 acknowledges that Weber's Vergesellschaftung/Vergemeinschaftung contrast and Coleman's purposive/primordial distinction anticipate the split at the level of institution types, and specifies what the present action-formation analysis adds: a finer-grained question about which mechanism is load-bearing for a particular behavioral pattern within a given institution. This is the right scholarly contextualization-the piece does not pretend to discover the distinction.

**The Compliance as Selection cross-reference is now disciplined.** Line 56 now explicitly states that the two accounts "bear on different aspects of the violation distribution: one characterizes where in the detectability dimension violations concentrate, the other characterizes who the violators are." The framing is complementary, not corrective, and what would discharge the normative alternative ("data the existing monitoring infrastructure does not record") is named. The selection result stands under its assumptions; the normative account adds a prediction about a different dimension.

**The commons cell credit is repaired.** Line 39 now reads "Both invoked in the CPR literature; the information/normative split identified at the collective-choice level but not formalized across all principles." The prior College piece's contribution is correctly recognized, and what the new diagnostic adds is correctly specified.

**Closing crystallizes the licensing finding.** The final paragraph (line 88) no longer ends on generic practitioner-warning language; it ends on the licensing crystallization-that the deregulation debate has proceeded as if only one mechanism is available, producing too-uniform quality predictions and miscalibrated policy prescriptions. This is the right exit because it makes the diagnostic's institutional payoff concrete rather than rhetorical.

**No process leakage.** A careful scan for "the prior draft," "round 1," "my advisor," "the panel said," "this revision addresses," "after peer review," and similar process narration returns nothing. The draft reads as a clean public artifact.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The tax morale literature is invoked twice but absent from the references.** Line 31 ("identity-salience test ... has been investigated in the tax morale literature, where civic identity and group-membership factors predict compliance above and beyond deterrence variables across multiple countries and enforcement regimes") and the response document both rely on this body of work as the principal "available evidence" cutting against the reductive account's predictions. It is doing real argumentative work, not throat-clearing. The reference list (lines 92–112) does not include Torgler, Alm, Frey, or any tax-morale-specific source. Editorial should add at least one anchoring citation (Torgler's *Tax Compliance and Tax Morale*, 2007, is the canonical synthesis; Alm and Torgler 2006 in *Journal of Economic Psychology* gives the cross-country comparison) so a reader who wants to verify the claim has somewhere to start. This is a citation gap, not a structural one, but the piece is unusually careful about evidential traceability elsewhere and this hole stands out.

2. **The Henrich et al. interpretation has a defender's reply the piece does not foreclose.** Line 31 reads cross-cultural variation in costly punishment as a problem for the trained-heuristic account because "a universal trained-heuristic cannot straightforwardly explain this variation." A trained-heuristic defender can answer: training environments are themselves heterogeneous across societies (different repeated-interaction structures, different family ecologies, different reputation systems), so heterogeneous punishment behavior is exactly what a trained-heuristic predicts. The Henrich finding is that costly punishment correlates with *market integration and world religions*-both of which are themselves prima facie consistent with information-environment differences (market integration shapes the structure of repeated interaction; world religion participation shapes the audience structure for reputational signals). The piece's claim that the correlation tracks "community membership and norm-participation structures rather than enforcement-history intensity per se" is an interpretive step the reductive theorist can contest without exiting their framework. I am not asking for a defense at the level the academic literature would demand-the piece is essayistic-but the current text reads as if the Henrich evidence settles the question more decisively than it does. One sentence acknowledging that the variation-tracks-which-construct question is contested would calibrate the claim.

3. **The action-formation locus justification implicitly understates aggregation-step ambiguity.** Line 3 now argues that situational steps are "constrained by observable institutional features" and aggregation "follows from behavioral assumptions already in place"-so action formation is uniquely where mechanism choice is "unavoidable." This is well-stated for the information/normative split specifically, but the implicit claim that aggregation mechanism is settled by action-level assumptions is too strong as a general statement. The aggregation step in collective-action settings admits substantial mechanism ambiguity in its own right (threshold vs. cascade vs. critical-mass vs. tipping models can all be consistent with identical action-formation assumptions). The piece's stronger framing-that the information/normative choice is *forced* at the action-formation level-is what it actually needs, and the broader claim about aggregation-step closure is an unnecessary load-bearing premise. Trimming the aggregation parenthetical to "the aggregation step is a separate problem" would be defensible and would not weaken the locus argument.

4. **The K&K (2013) heterogeneity passage would benefit from concrete examples.** Line 72 cites Kleiner and Krueger's profession-level heterogeneity in the licensing wage premium and asserts that "professions with denser ongoing community infrastructure-where continuing education, active disciplinary boards, and mandatory association membership are entrenched-show different licensing dynamics from one-time-credential professions." K&K document the heterogeneity; they do not classify professions by community-infrastructure density. The interpretive step is the piece's own. Naming specific professions on each side-medicine and law as community-dense, cosmetology and barbering as community-sparse, with the wage-premium pattern reported-would make the indirect-evidence claim auditable rather than merely plausible. The piece elsewhere insists on traceability; this passage is the place where that standard slips.

5. **The "orthogonal variation in transplant cases" claim is asserted without case examples.** Line 50 ends with: "Orthogonal variation between observability-absent and community-absent conditions exists across the comparative record, but extracting it requires deliberate case selection rather than a natural sample of transplant failures." This is plausible but the piece does not name any case that exhibits one absence without the other. Even one example (e.g., a commons institution transplanted into a community with strong pre-existing collective identity but where monitoring infrastructure was not constructed at installation; or vice versa) would demonstrate that the orthogonal variation is more than a methodological hope. This is minor-the broader argument does not depend on it-but the sentence currently reads as a hedge against the confound rather than a substantive claim.

6. **The new "more sophisticated reductive response" paragraph leaves the original Fehr–Gächter framing partly stranded.** Line 21 still presents Fehr–Gächter as "consistent with the normative mechanism's signature, though not conclusive on its own"; line 31 then introduces the trained-heuristic version of the reductive account and addresses it on its own terms. The two paragraphs do not entirely cohere: line 21 already grants that Fehr–Gächter is not conclusive, but the implied reason there is something like "many normative-mechanism details remain unspecified," whereas line 31 names a specific reductive reply Fehr–Gächter does not foreclose. A reader who reads the paragraphs in order may be momentarily confused about whether the not-conclusive admission at line 21 was already conceding what line 31 then explicitly addresses. A bridging clause at the start of line 31's paragraph ("A more sophisticated reductive response locates the limitation differently...") would smooth the joint. Minor.

7. **No process leakage observed.** A scan for phrases like "the prior draft," "round 1," "my advisor," "the panel said," "this revision addresses," and "after peer review" returns nothing. The references to prior College pieces in the body (referral hiring, compliance monitoring, commons governance) are appropriate cross-references to public archive pieces, not process narration.
