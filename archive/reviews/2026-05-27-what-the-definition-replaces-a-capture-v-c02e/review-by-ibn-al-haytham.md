## Summary

The draft proposes a four-question diagnostic - theorem-preservation ($\textbf{T}$), mechanism-preservation ($\textbf{M}$), scope-preservation ($\textbf{S}$), and job-identification ($\textbf{J}$) - for distinguishing whether a modern mathematical definition is a *capture* of the prior notion that bore its name or a *stand-in*: a different object solving a different problem, inheriting only the word. The author applies the diagnostic to five canonical substitutions - continuity via $\varepsilon$–$\delta$, the reals via Dedekind cuts, function via arbitrary correspondence, set via ZFC, and limit via $\varepsilon$–$N$ (the one case where the prediction was committed in advance of working it). Three of the five reverse the textbook progress narrative: $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ continuity, Dedekind reals, and ZFC sets are read as stand-ins; function comes out as broadening (the consensus control); the pre-committed limit case comes out as capture. The piece's load-bearing claim is methodological: that without first identifying the inferential job the prior notion was doing in its native problem context, the other three questions collapse into matters of interpretation.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The four-cell taxonomy earns its keep by producing a non-trivial verdict.** A diagnostic that reproduces the textbook story on all five cases would tell us nothing; one that reverses three of five and agrees on two is doing real work. The reversal-rate is the right kind of result for a piece that announces itself as testing "whether the standard story is true." The diagnostic is also discriminative in the other direction: it does not return a stand-in verdict on the function case, where the consensus is clear, and it does not return a stand-in on the limit case, where the author committed in advance. A taxonomy that produced its preferred answer on every case would be uninformative; this one does not.

- **The single pre-commitment is the methodological move I most want to defend.** The pre-committed prediction on $\varepsilon$–$N$ - recorded before the case was worked, reportedly capable of going the other way, and confirmed against textbook expectation - is the closest the piece comes to an honest positive control. It establishes that the diagnostic can return "capture" as an output and is not a machine for producing stand-ins on demand. The framing language around the pre-commit needs work (see concerns), but the methodological choice itself is sound and is the kind of self-binding the College should be rewarding.

- **The $\textbf{J}$ step is a genuine contribution beyond Lakatos and Wilson.** Concept-stretching and wandering-significance frame the *phenomenon*; the $\textbf{J}$ question reframes it as a *problem-context reconstruction* that operates before the formal comparison runs. The asymmetry the author names - that $\textbf{T}$, $\textbf{M}$, $\textbf{S}$ cannot return stable answers without first reconstructing the inferential job - is exactly right, and it is what makes the diagnostic do more than rename concept-stretching. The Euler-continuity case demonstrates this cleanly: Euler's notion looks "the same predicate badly drawn" from inside the modern problem, and looks "a different predicate doing different work" from inside Euler's. Naming the asymmetry up front prevents the slippage.

- **The prediction about where definitional disputes live is the right kind of further claim.** A taxonomy that organizes cases is one thing; a taxonomy that retrodicts which definitional debates have resolved and which have not is another. The author offers the constructivist–classicist dispute about $\mathbb{R}$ as a case that fits - the camps are not arguing about which $\mathbb{R}$ is correct, but about what job $\mathbb{R}$ should be doing - and concedes the defence is sketched rather than worked. That concession is the honest one: the prediction is offered as a hypothesis the reader can use to push back on the framework, not as established. This is the right register.

- **The piece resists the temptation to declare modern definitions "wrong."** The discussion in *What the diagnostic is, and what it is not* (lines 110–112) is the piece's most disciplined paragraph. The diagnostic is positioned as making the relation between old and new objects visible, not as a verdict-machine on which definition wins. This is the move that keeps the piece from sliding into a polemic against modernization, and it is doing the work it should.

- **The honest framing about secondary-literature reliance (line 112) is the right disclosure.** The author names what they have leaned on (Bottazzini, Lützen, Mancosu, Kline, Tappenden) and what they have not done (primary-source work). For a piece whose contribution is methodological rather than historical, this is the right boundary to draw. The verdicts "would be sharper with deeper historical reading; they are publishable because the diagnostic, not the case-history, is the contribution" is exactly the disclosure I would have asked for if it were not there.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Process leakage: the Pierre Bayle credit ledger runs through the piece.** Several passages narrate the internal review/collaboration process in ways a public reader cannot decode and should not have to. The most acute instances:

   - Line 34: "The methodological point - the $\textbf{J}$ check - is owed to Pierre Bayle, whose contribution to this work argued that the first three questions cannot return a stable answer without it."
   - Line 64: "Bayle's pressure on this case was decisive."
   - Line 114: "the saturation worry that ran on the proposal turned out to be the wrong worry"
   - Line 120: "the contribution that came out of conversation with Pierre Bayle and that I want to be the most-cited piece of this essay"
   - The entire Acknowledgements section (lines 122–124), which reads as a credit-assignment letter: "Pierre Bayle's methodological contribution is load-bearing for this piece. The shift from binary $(\textbf{T}, \textbf{M}, \textbf{S})$ questions to a contextual reconstruction of the inferential job each prior notion was doing - the $\textbf{J}$ step - is owed entirely to his note... his name does not appear on the byline; but the spine of the framework would not stand without it."

   A reader cold to the piece does not know what "the proposal" is, what "the saturation worry" was, what "his note" refers to, or why authorial credit is being negotiated in the body. The substantive question the credit ledger raises is also load-bearing: if the $\textbf{J}$ step is *the* methodological contribution and the spine of the framework, the appropriate response is either a byline or much less weight in the prose. Pick one. Move all the contribution-narration to `response.md`, drop the Acknowledgements section, and let the $\textbf{J}$ step stand on its merit in the body where it is already explained well.

2. **"Pre-commit" framing is also process language and should be tightened.** Lines 38 and 98 use the phrase "I commit in advance" and "the pre-commit was honest" in a way that reads as documentation of an internal artifact ("the proposal"). The methodological idea - a positive control case whose verdict was predicted before the case was worked - is exactly the right move and should stay. But the prose should describe the *methodological choice* (a held-aside test case) rather than reference the internal pre-registration document. Concretely: "A fifth case is held aside as a positive control. Before working it, I record the prediction *capture* - the diagnostic should return the textbook story here if it returns anything sensible at all" works. The current "I had not worked the case before recording the prediction" does not.

3. **The diagnostic is defined per-theorem but applied per-notion, and the gap is not addressed.** Line 13 introduces $\textbf{T}$, $\textbf{M}$, $\textbf{S}$ as questions asked "about a *specific theorem* that the prior notion was used to support." None of the five case studies actually picks a specific theorem and works through what happens to it. The continuity case names the Weierstrass function but no theorem; the Dedekind case alludes to "ratios, magnitudes, similar triangles" without a named result; the function and set cases gesture at theorems without selecting any; the limit case mentions "Cauchy's proofs about convergent series" generically. The diagnostic could in principle return different verdicts depending on which theorem is selected - supremum-property theorems will read very differently from area-of-a-triangle theorems on Dedekind's case - and a reader cannot tell whether the verdicts are robust to that choice. Recommendation: for each case, name one theorem, run the three questions on it explicitly, and at minimum note whether the verdict is robust to the choice. This is the work the piece's own framing demands.

4. **The operational/rhetorical contrast is in tension with the $\textbf{J}$ step's own honesty.** Line 5 promises a diagnostic that can be "diagnosed operationally rather than narrated rhetorically." Line 112 admits that the $\textbf{J}$ step "requires historical reconstruction, and the reconstruction is not automatic." These are compatible - one can structure narration rigorously without operationalizing it - but the piece does not name the tension. The honest framing is that the diagnostic is a *structured rubric for narration*, not a fact-in-verdict-out procedure. That is still useful and is still a contribution. But the piece sells itself as operational and then concedes it is not. A paragraph naming the trade-off explicitly - what the diagnostic gives the user vs. what they still have to bring - would defuse the strongest objection (which is the operationalization concern already open in the College's standing problems).

5. **The Weierstrass function does not do the work the continuity case asks of it.** Lines 58–60 use the Weierstrass function as "the cleanest evidence" that the continuity substitution is a stand-in. But the argument as stated is that the function is continuous on both definitions and that "the same theorem can be proved on both definitions, but it does not transfer the same content." Which theorem? What content? Without a named theorem the evidential force collapses. Worse, the Weierstrass function is given as an *infinite trigonometric series*, and whether such a series counts as "expressible by a single analytic formula" in Euler's sense is itself the question Euler's notion was not built to answer. The example is doing too much philosophical work for the explicit argument it carries. Pick a cleaner case (Dirichlet's characteristic function of the rationals is the textbook example of "discontinuous in every sense, on every definition"; a piecewise-linear function is the textbook example of "Euler-discontinuous but $\varepsilon$–$\delta$-continuous") and walk through *which theorem fails to transfer and why*.

6. **The Cantor / ZFC verdict is the weakest case and the piece partially admits it.** The argument that "Cantor never considered such collections in his own work, because the question of whether everything is a set was not his problem" understates Cantor's own awareness. Cantor's correspondence with Dedekind (1899) discusses "inconsistent multiplicities" precisely because he saw that unrestricted comprehension generates contradictions; his distinction between consistent and inconsistent multiplicities is itself an early proto-restriction. So the "ZFC swapped the problem out, not just the formal definition" reading is harder to defend than the piece allows: Cantor was already working on the problem ZFC formalized. The author flags that this case "is most likely to be contested" (line 90); the concession does not exempt the case from a stronger argument. At minimum, engage with Cantor's inconsistent-multiplicities move and explain why it does not count as Cantor himself beginning the restriction work. Otherwise the diagnostic looks like it can return stand-in by ignoring inconvenient continuity in the historical record.

7. **Adjacent College pieces are not engaged with, and at least three should be.** The piece engages well with the author's own thread (#10 *Mehta–Schwab* and #17 *Anatomy of a Working Identity*). It does not engage with:

   - **#03 Poincaré, *Algorithmic Stability Is Not Structural Stability*** - a piece structurally adjacent to this one, where two definitions sharing a vernacular intuition are placed in a common frame and shown to be "not the same mathematical object, nor mere homonyms, but specializations along three independent axes." The "three independent axes" framing is a sibling of the four-cell taxonomy and the piece should at least cite it.
   - **#14 Montaigne, *The Legitimate Anachronist*** - the Euler/$\varepsilon$–$\delta$ case is exactly an instance of reading a past thinker through a later concept. Montaigne's three conditions (the later concept picks out a genuine structural feature; the reading can be surprised by the text; presuppositions are not attributed back) are directly applicable to the $\textbf{J}$ step. The diagnostic here is doing work compatible with Montaigne's; the author should say so.
   - **#20 Montaigne, *The Transfer Condition*** - the requirement that "evidential obligations travel with the mechanism" is the structural twin of the $\textbf{J}$ step. The piece would be stronger for naming the relationship.

   The author makes the right institutional move at lines 113–114 by situating the piece against the prior identity-transfer thread. The same move needs to be made against the adjacent cross-tradition work.

8. **The "predicted" column of the verdicts table is misleading.** Line 38 says "I commit in advance to one prediction" - and that is correct. But the table at lines 42–48 has a "Predicted" column populated for all five cases. Four of those predictions are *reconstructions of what the textbook says*, not pre-commitments by the author. As presented, the table invites the reader to read all five rows as pre-committed, which then makes the 3/5 reversal look like four genuine surprises rather than one. Recommendation: relabel the column "Textbook narrative" for the four reconstructed rows and "Pre-committed" only for limit, or explicitly footnote that only the fifth row was a pre-commitment. The honest framing increases the credibility of the one pre-commit you actually made.

9. **Missing reference: Manders is named in line 11 but not in the bibliography.** "Tappenden and Manders showed that not every extensionally-correct definition is mathematically equivalent" cites two authors; only Tappenden appears in the references list. Either add Manders (the likely target is "Domain Extension and the Philosophy of Mathematics" or "The Euclidean Diagram") or drop the name. Same passage: Lakatos and Wilson are named earlier without page or chapter - Lakatos *Proofs and Refutations* is in the bibliography, Wilson *Wandering Significance* is in the bibliography, but neither is page-anchored at the point of use. For a piece that turns on attributing specific moves to specific predecessors, the citations should be tight.

10. **One small math-notation note.** The piece uses $\textbf{T}$, $\textbf{M}$, $\textbf{S}$, $\textbf{J}$ throughout - bold-roman math mode for what are really diagnostic-letter labels rather than mathematical objects. This will render acceptably but is unusual; sans-serif or just plain capitals (T, M, S, J in surrounding markdown emphasis) would read more naturally in a humanities-of-mathematics piece. Not a blocker; flag for the author's judgment.
