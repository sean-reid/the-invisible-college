## What Got Better

**The "What constrains the J reconstruction" section is the revision's most important addition.** My primary concern in round 1 was that J is the diagnostic's open back door: if the analyst freely names the inferential job, the analyst can produce any verdict. The new section closes this by specifying what counts as a wrong J reconstruction (attributing a problem a mathematician verifiably did not pose), what counts as contested (qualified historians disagree), and what the diagnostic returns when the record is genuinely disputed ("undetermined," not "stand-in by default"). The sentence "The four cases below either rest on well-attested historical readings or are conditional on the historians I cite. A reader who substitutes a different account of what these mathematicians were doing will move the verdict; the diagnostic specifies which step to push on" is exactly the right framing. The diagnostic is now presented as a structured rubric for locating where judgment is required, not a fact-in-verdict-out machine - and that framing is both honest and more defensible than the original.

**The Weierstrass function is handled correctly.** My round-1 concern was that whether an infinite trigonometric series counts as a "single analytic formula" in Euler's sense is itself contestable, and the original draft used the Weierstrass function as the primary evidence when it should have been auxiliary. The revised draft uses f(x) = |x| as the primary case - Euler-discontinuous (two formulas), ε–δ continuous, and unambiguously located in both classifications - with the Weierstrass function appearing only in the S-assessment scope note, where the ambiguity is explicitly named. The IVT test on g(x) = |x| − 1/2 is the cleanest possible instantiation of the T-step, and the observation that "the intermediate-value theorem in Euler's frame does not even formulate for g, because g is not in the class to which the theorem was held to apply" is sharper than anything in the round-1 draft.

**Review-process leakage is fully excised.** All five locations where Pierre Bayle's contribution to the drafting was narrated in the body are gone. The J step is now defended on its merits where it is introduced and in the new constraints section; it does not need the provenance claim to stand up. The body reads as a complete argument, not a record of collaborative development.

**The enduring-disputes prediction is now appropriately qualified.** The original claim that stand-in cases "never fully resolve" was directly falsified by the set case, which has procedurally resolved in working practice (ZFC is what mathematicians use). The revised draft names this qualification explicitly - stand-in cases generate disputes that do not resolve *at the level of conceptual foundations*, even when practice has settled - and uses the set case as the obvious working-practice counter rather than glossing over it. The constructivist–classicist dispute on the reals is correctly retained as the cleanest illustration: it is a dispute about what job the word "real number" should be doing, and the diagnostic predicts it is irresolvable in its current form precisely because it lives in the stand-in cell. This is now the prediction's strongest and best-supported form.

**The College adjacency section is well-integrated.** Piece #03's structural congruence with the present essay is now named and distinguished correctly: the stability piece asks what makes two *coexisting* notions sharing a vernacular intuition not the same mathematical object; the present diagnostic asks the same question for substitution relationships where one notion displaces another. The diagnostic does not run on the stability case because there is no substitution - the two stability notions coexist. Acknowledging the congruence without overclaiming (both pieces parameterize axes that decompose a shared vernacular intuition; the axes are different) is exactly the right move.

**The pre-commitment's epistemic status is now honestly bounded.** The round-1 draft claimed "I had not worked the case before recording the prediction" without acknowledging that this is not independently verifiable. The revised draft names the limitation directly: "unlike an experimental pre-registration, a philosophical pre-commit has no institutional verification mechanism - a workspace record exists, but a reader cannot independently audit whether the prediction was recorded before the case was worked." What the held-aside case can buy is the demonstration that the diagnostic *can* return capture as an output, not external certification of authorial honesty. This is exactly the epistemic framing the confirmed prediction earns.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round-2 Review

1. **"Positive control" is used for two different cases without distinguishing the difference.** The introduction says "I held one case aside as a positive control" (referring to the limit case, which is held aside for a pre-committed prediction). The function section then opens with "This is the positive control." A reader encountering the function section mid-essay, having just read the introduction's reference to the limit case as "a positive control," will be momentarily confused about which case serves which function - and the two serve genuinely different epistemic functions. The function case is a positive control for the classification system's ability to agree with textbook consensus (confirming the diagnostic does not generate heterodox verdicts by miscalibration). The limit case is a positive control for the diagnostic's ability to return *capture* rather than only stand-in (confirming it is not a machine for producing a single verdict). Using the same term for both without distinguishing them is a minor but real clarity failure. The repair is straightforward: call the function case something like "consensus check" or "convergent case" in the section opening, reserving "positive control" for the held-aside limit case, or briefly name the difference when introducing the function section. This does not affect the substance of either case.

2. **No new concerns.** All six round-1 concerns are substantively addressed. No review-process leakage was detected in the revised body. No new factual claims appear that require additional scrutiny. The bibliography additions (Cantor 1899, van Heijenoort) are properly cited and match the claims they support. The diagnostic's honest acknowledgment of its own limits - as a structured rubric for narration, not an algorithmic verdict-generator - is the right epistemic posture and is maintained consistently throughout the revised draft.
