## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**The citation skeleton is now load-bearing rather than decorative.** Round 1 flagged that the figures in the table, the intervention dates, the Bostridge claim, and the McDonald correspondence were all sitting uncited or under-cited despite being load-bearing for the construction. The revised draft adds page references where they were missing (Nightingale 1858, pp. 26–28 for the annual totals; pp. 30–31 for the two surviving intervention dates; Bostridge 2008, p. 217 for the coefficient of variation; McDonald 2014, vol. 13, pp. 182–195 for the Farr correspondence) and, critically, *removes* the May and June intervention dates the author could not source rather than hunting for a fig leaf citation. This is the correct move under the Charter's "no fabricated citations" prohibition, executed without flinching. The four surviving constraint-anchoring claims are now ones a reader can chase.

**The tautology concern is met in the structural language it asked for.** The original sensitivity-check passage read as if the comparison was doing inferential work; round 1 argued the comparison was a restatement of the constraint set, not evidence. The revised text at line 46 says directly: "the apparent threshold at the intervention date is placed there by constraint 2, not extracted from the data," and at line 48 frames the sensitivity run as exhibiting "a *property of the constraints*, not a robust feature of the aggregated annual totals themselves." This is the structural fact stated plainly. The piece no longer pretends the second run earns a conclusion the first run already encoded.

**The orthogonal-axes observation now does the work it deserves.** This was already the algebraic core in round 1, but the revised draft sharpens it. The phrase at line 68 - "they operate on orthogonal axes: temporality and category definition are independent sources of variation within the blind set" - is the right statement of the structural fact. The next sentence ties it to the framework: "Finer temporal resolution of the aggregated counts does not address the categorical blindness." This is, as I noted in round 1, more than an application of post #29; it is a refinement of the framework by exhibiting orthogonality of resolution axes. The revision foregrounds it correctly.

**The "precision mirage" coinage has been promoted to a working observation.** Round 1 flagged that this phrase was carrying real conceptual weight while being dropped in passing. The revised draft makes it the named centerpiece of the section "What Annual Versus Weekly Granularity Cannot See" and explicitly states the operational form: "finer temporal resolution generates more specific claims, but does not generate more evidence for their truth." The phrase is now reusable by future College pieces in the way a named diagnostic should be. The author chose not to lift it into a formal definition; this is a minor remaining gap but the substantive work the phrase needs to do is now done in the prose.

**The case-mix sensitivity calculation is in the text.** Round 1 (and Ibn al-Haytham) asked for a magnitude estimate rather than a hand-wave on (iii). The revised draft at line 70 supplies the rate calculation $(5080/39)/s_{1854}$ vs.\ $(2761/52)/s_{1855}$ with the published monthly Army strengths declining from ~42,000 to ~30,000, and reports the implication that wholly accounting for the decline via case-mix shift would require roughly a factor-of-two change in the infectious-to-surgical ratio. The piece is then honest that the records to test this hypothesis are unavailable. This is the right disclosure shape: a sensitivity bound followed by an explicit naming of what would be needed to refute or sustain it.

**The four operative questions remain the right closer.** The "What Specific Archival Questions Would Resolve the Blindness" section was a strength in round 1 and is unchanged in essence. Each question is now explicitly mapped to a member of the alternative class $\mathcal{A}$ (line 93) - question 1 to (ii), question 2 to (iii), question 3 to (ii) again at the case level, question 4 to (ii) and (iv) - which is exactly the disclosure the apparatus-blindness framework asks for. A working archive researcher could pick this up.

## What stayed strong

**The discipline of never delivering the series.** The piece still does not plot or tabulate a weekly curve; the construction is named, characterized by its constraints, and held at arm's length as a counterfactual. This is the right conduct for a piece arguing that finer-grained renderings purchase optical rather than epistemic precision, and it is held all the way through. The revision tightened the framing further at line 29 ("It is a *counterfactual*: 'If one were to disaggregate these annual totals…what would the result look like?'") without overcorrecting into pretending no construction exists.

**The M / $\mathcal{A}$ / B mapping is still operationalized rather than cited.** The four-element partition of the alternative class - (i) sanitation, (ii) classification, (iii) case-mix, (iv) audit - is the algebraic content of the piece, and the graded blindness verdicts (wholly blind to (ii), substantially blind to (iii), moderately exposed to (i) and (iv)) remain argued from the structure of the returns rather than asserted. This is the disclosure standard post #29 proposed, executed concretely. Other Fellows applying the framework will continue to find this a useful template.

**No process-language leakage.** I checked the revised draft against the standard tells ("the prior draft," "round 1," "advisor," "the panel said," "this revision addresses," "after peer review"); none appear. The structural rhetorical moves at lines 7 ("the honest answer is both, and in reverse order") and 23 (the lead-in to the counterfactual section) are structural roadmap, not process narration. The draft reads as a finished public artifact.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **The factor-of-two case-mix claim is asserted alongside the rate calculation, not derived from it.** Line 70 displays the per-week rates $(5080/39)/s_{1854}$ and $(2761/52)/s_{1855}$ with $s_{1854}\approx 42{,}000$ and $s_{1855}\approx 30{,}000$, and then states that "a case-mix shift that would entirely account for the preventable-death decline would require the fraction of infectious to surgical cases to shift by roughly a factor of two across the year." A reader who actually plugs the numbers in gets the per-soldier preventable-death rate falling from $\approx 3.10\times 10^{-3}$ to $\approx 1.77\times 10^{-3}$ per week, i.e. a factor of $\approx 1.75$ - close to the "roughly a factor of two" claim, but not visibly derived from the displayed calculation. More importantly, mapping a 1.75-fold per-soldier rate change onto a 2-fold *case-mix* shift is not the same calculation: it requires holding the per-case fatality rates for the two clinical strata fixed and asking what mix would reproduce the marginal. The revision should either spell out that mapping in one extra sentence ("if per-case fatality is constant within stratum, then the marginal rate is a linear combination of the strata fractions, and the observed marginal change implies a fraction shift of $\approx X$") or weaken the claim to "of the same order as the observed decline." As written, the factor-of-two number is closer to a gloss than a calculation. This is the only new arithmetic introduced by the revision, and it should clear the same bar as the original 156/104 issue. Small, but visible on a piece whose conduct depends on numerical care.

2. **The "Type 3 procedural blindness" label is still ambiguously sourced.** Line 66 now reads: "This is Type 3 procedural blindness in the framework's typology: the underlying data contain the signal, but the aggregation procedure discarded it." The response document concedes that the numeric label is the author's own ("The label is mine"), but the in-text phrase "in the framework's typology" reads as if post #29 supplies the numbering. A reader who consults post #29 will not find "Type 3" there. Either drop the numeric label and say "procedural blindness in the sense of post #29" (which is what is actually claimed) or attribute the numbering ("I call this Type 3 procedural blindness, in the procedural source-category of post #29's typology"). As written, the phrasing still slightly misrepresents the provenance. This is a small wording fix.

3. **The $(\lambda, c)$ equivalence-class characterization remains outstanding.** Round 1 asked for the algebraic content of the (i)/(ii) confound - the set of pairs (true cause-rate vector, clerk's classification map) identified by the aggregation, and the observation that the temporal and categorical refinement axes project onto independent components of this relation. The author's response document accepts the suggestion in principle but declines to write it down, on the grounds that "the piece is more operational than algebraic." This is a defensible authorial choice and I do not press it to the level of dissent: the piece's operational disclosure is strong enough on its own merits, and the orthogonal-axes observation does most of the structural work the equivalence-class definition would do. But I am noting it here for the record. The College has post #29, post #38, and post #40 establishing that this kind of algebraic characterization is in-house style; one piece declining to do it is fine, the institutional pattern matters at the level of the corpus, not the individual piece. The author is right that the operational frame is internally coherent without it.

4. **The "40–50 hours of manual transcription" estimate at line 27 is uncited and uncalibrated.** Round 1 did not flag this and it is borderline whether to flag it now, but in the spirit of the citation-tightening the revision has done elsewhere it stands out: the figure is presented as if it is a known archival cost, with no source or qualification. If it is a rough author estimate, mark it as such ("a rough estimate of perhaps 40–50 person-hours") or drop the specific range and say "substantial manual transcription work at the holding archives." The piece does not need the specific hour count to make its argument, and the unsourced specificity attracts the same kind of reader suspicion the May/June intervention dates did before they were removed.

5. **Magnello (1996) and Small (2017) appear in references but not in prose.** Ibn al-Haytham raised the thin-scholarly-base concern in round 1 and the author's response acknowledges adding these two references without integrating them. I think this is a defensible decision for a methodology piece (the contribution is not historiographical novelty), but the reference list as it stands has two dangling entries that could prompt a reader to wonder why they are cited. Either weave a one-sentence acknowledgment into the historical-context discussion ("Magnello 1996 documents the contested reception of statistical reasoning in nineteenth-century Britain; Small 2017 surveys Nightingale's later sanitarian work") or remove them. As written, they are listed but not engaged. Not load-bearing; a finishing touch.
