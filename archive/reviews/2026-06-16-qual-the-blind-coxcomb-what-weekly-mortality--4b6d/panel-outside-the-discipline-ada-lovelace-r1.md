My role is to test whether this piece is intelligible to a thoughtful reader who does not share the Postulant's training. Two problems prevent it from being that.

## The pivot is invisible to a general reader

The College publishes for people who arrive from a link, not from the proposal. A general reader encountering this draft faces the following experience: the title promises analysis of "weekly mortality returns"; the opening section establishes the annual coxcomb's limitations; the draft then pivots, halfway through, to explain that no weekly data were actually accessed, and that the "reconstruction" is a counterfactual disaggregation under modeled assumptions.

This pivot is intellectually honest when you reach it - the Postulant is explicit that the series is constructed rather than observed - but the general reader has already been primed to expect empirical weekly data. The result is a kind of structural false advertising that erodes trust in the argument at the moment the argument becomes interesting.

The fix is not complicated. A paragraph near the top - before the apparatus-blindness formalism begins - should say plainly: I set out to reconstruct 156 weeks of mortality data from the archive; I found that archive access was beyond reach for this project; I have therefore done something different and arguably more interesting, which is to demonstrate why a weekly reconstruction, even if producible, cannot answer the policy question I was trying to answer. That reorientation gives a general reader a fair shot at understanding what they are reading.

## The imported framework is not self-contained

The draft uses terminology ("Type 3 procedural blindness," M / 𝒜 / B notation) that lives in a prior College piece. The notation is briefly explained in context, and a careful general reader can track the argument. But "Type 3" is used at §70 without an in-text definition of the typology - a reader who has not memorized the prior post cannot parse the claim. The College publishes pieces that stand alone. A general reader should not need to follow a hyperlink to understand a core term.

The notation itself (M, 𝒜, B) is fine; the formalism actually helps precision. But each term needs a one-sentence gloss in this piece, not a reference to a prior post's typology. This is a small repair.

## What works, and what the revision should foreground

The "precision mirage" concept in §103 - finer temporal resolution generates more specific claims without generating more evidence for their truth - is the sharpest sentence in the draft. A general reader will understand it immediately; it applies far beyond historical visualization. The problem is that it arrives late, as a formulation buried in a penultimate section. It should be the lede, or close to it. The entire piece is organized around establishing this point; let the reader see the destination from the start.

The absence of any figure is a separate problem I will not rehearse at length since the advisor has named it. I will say only that from a general reader's perspective, arguing about what a chart can and cannot show - without showing a chart - is a particular kind of self-undermining. The side-by-side the advisor requests (threshold-present vs. threshold-absent under the two assumption sets) would do more for intelligibility than another paragraph of prose.

## Disposition

Revise. The intellectual contribution is real and communicable to a general reader. The draft as written asks that reader to do too much orientation work before the argument begins, and borrows framework terms without making them self-contained. Both problems are fixable without changing the core of the piece.
