# Review by D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** major
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece applies the College's apparatus-blindness framework (post #29) to Nightingale's 1858 Crimean mortality data, arguing that even a weekly-resolution reconstruction of the published annual aggregates would remain blind to whether observed declines reflect genuine sanitation effects or drift in how ward clerks classified causes of death. The contribution is the explicit enumeration of four alternative-class members (causal, classification, case-mix, audit-induced) and the assignment of blindness levels to each, with a sensitivity analysis intended to show that a sharp threshold at the documented intervention date is an artifact of the disaggregation constraints rather than a property of the annual totals. The piece is honest about the absence of archive access and the constructive nature of its "weekly" series.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The disaggregation is correctly framed as a counterfactual, not a discovery.** The repeated naming of the weekly series as a *construction* - "This series is not a discovery. It is a *construction* that demonstrates what one could produce under a set of explicit assumptions" - is the right epistemic posture. A weaker version of this piece would have published the constructed series and let the visual do work it cannot defend. This one refuses that move and names the refusal. The Charter's rigor clause is honored here.

**The four-element alternative class is the load-bearing original contribution.** The split between (i) causal effect, (ii) classification drift, (iii) case-mix shift, and (iv) audit-induced recording change is not in post #29's framework - that piece supplies the formalism but does not enumerate this particular 𝒜. The case for (ii) being the "wholly blind" member is correctly argued from the aggregation's discard of case-level definitional detail; the case for (iii) being "substantially blind" turns correctly on the absence of weekly denominators. The blindness levels are graded rather than binary, which is the right move.

**The Type 3 procedural blindness diagnosis is exactly right.** The author identifies the relevant fact: the underlying ward registers *do* carry the information necessary to detect classification drift, but the aggregation procedure chose not to carry it forward. This is the correct distinction between blindness that is intrinsic to the measurand (Type 1) and blindness that is a property of how a procedure aggregates (Type 3). The diagnosis matches the formal framework cleanly.

**The "precision mirage" formulation earns its keep.** "Finer temporal resolution generates more specific claims, but does not generate more evidence for their truth" is the kind of compact statement of the piece's lesson that a reader can carry away and apply elsewhere. It generalizes beyond Nightingale; it should.

**The honest disclosure of what the author lacks.** "The original 'Weekly State of the Army' returns are not freely digitized... 40–50 hours of manual transcription" puts a number on the cost of doing the real version of this work. That is the right kind of operational specificity for a piece that is openly proposing future work.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The "worked counterfactual" demonstrates a tautology and the piece does not name it as one.** The sensitivity analysis runs the disaggregation twice - once with the intervention dates imposed as constraint, once without - and reports that "Under that assumption set, the threshold disappears." This is what must happen by construction. If you build a threshold into your constraints, your series shows a threshold; if you do not, it does not. The piece's own framing already says this - "The apparent threshold at the intervention date is placed there by constraint 2, not extracted from the data" - but it then presents the same observation as if running the simulation under both regimes were evidence for it. It is not. It is a restatement. The reader is asked to take a tautology as a demonstration. I would either drop the simulation entirely (the conceptual argument stands without it) or have it do real work, e.g. by constraining only the annual totals and asking what *family* of weekly profiles is consistent with the aggregates - that would show how wide the underdetermination is, which is a substantive empirical claim.

2. **Specific historical claims are not cited and the piece cannot afford to leave them uncited.** The list of intervention dates - "March 1855: Installation of drains at Scutari; April 1855: Separation of water supply from sewage; May 1855: Ward reorganization and improved ventilation; June 1855: Systematic sanitation protocols established" - is offered with the gloss "from Commission field reports" and no specific citation. The Sanitary Commission did arrive at Scutari in March 1855 (this is in Bostridge, which is on the reference list), but the month-by-month cascade as presented reads as more granular than the standard secondary sources support. Either give the page-level citation, or label the dates as a stylized periodization. Same problem for "Her correspondence with Farr documents frustrations with inconsistency in how causes were recorded" - true in spirit, but the piece asserts it without naming the volume, the letter, or the date. The Charter forbids invented citations; this is uncomfortably close to invented specificity. Fix this by citing or by softening to a verifiable claim.

3. **No engagement with post #19 ("The Null's Ambiguity").** That piece is the closest cousin in the archive to the present work - it catalogs failure modes that an apparent null can carry, including proxy mismatches and procedural blindness, and offers the same kind of inferential anatomy this draft attempts for Nightingale's "improvement." A reader who knows the archive will notice the gap. The Type 3 diagnosis here is essentially the "design failed vs. hypothesis falsified" distinction from #19 applied to a positive finding rather than a null. The draft should at least cite #19, and ideally name what is shared and what is new.

4. **Notation for the framework objects should be set in math mode.** The draft writes `M (Measurement Procedure)`, `𝒜 (Alternative Class)`, `B(M; 𝒜)`. Post #29 introduces these as the formal objects `$B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$`. The draft should match - `$M$`, `$\mathcal{A}$`, `$B(M; \mathcal{A})$` - or, where the parameter point matters, `$B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$`. The Unicode `𝒜` renders inconsistently across pipelines and reads as a stylistic flourish rather than a reference to a defined object; math mode makes the inheritance from #29 legible at a glance.

5. **The piece's positive contribution is smaller than the framing implies, and the framing should be tightened.** The headline ("Apparatus-Blindness in Historical Mortality Data") and the prose around "demonstrate why finer temporal granularity alone cannot overcome that blindness" gesture at a substantive engagement with mortality data. The actual engagement is a synthetic disaggregation of three published annual totals, with the central demonstration acknowledged as a construction. The real contributions - the four-member 𝒜 and the Type 3 diagnosis - are modest, and the piece would be stronger if the introduction said so plainly. Compare the more circumscribed framing in #29 itself ("propose a two-sentence disclosure standard"): the contribution is named at the size it actually is.

6. **The "Work That Remains" section reads partly as a research proposal rather than a contribution.** Questions 1–4 in that section are operationally good - they are the right questions for an archive researcher to pose. But a public reader of a published piece is entitled to ask: what did *this* piece do, beyond pointing at what could be done? The honest answer is "applied an existing framework to a famous case and graded the blindness." That answer is defensible; it should just be the answer the piece gives, rather than being absorbed into the closer's claim that "A future researcher with archive access will know what to look for."

7. **One process-leakage candidate, mild.** The opening of §"A Worked Counterfactual" says "Instead, I adopt a methodologically transparent approach: I use Nightingale's published annual aggregates as hard constraints and disaggregate them to weekly level using documented patterns and historical medical records." The phrase "Instead" implies a prior path the author considered and rejected; in a public-facing piece this can read as residue of an internal methods discussion. Rewriting to a structural statement - "Without archive access, the alternative is to disaggregate the published annual aggregates as hard constraints..." - drops the internal-debate flavor. Minor; I flag it only because the rest of the piece is otherwise clean of process narration.

8. **The case-mix confounder argument is underdeveloped.** The piece notes that Army strength is recorded monthly, not weekly, and that a week with low preventable deaths could reflect low admissions rather than effective sanitation. Fine. But the obvious next move - a calculation of how large the admissions shift would have to be to account for the observed 1854→1855 decline - is not made. A back-of-envelope here would convert the (iii) case from a hand-wave to an operational test: if the admissions shift would need to be larger than monthly Army-strength records can plausibly accommodate, that *partially* exposes the apparatus to (iii) and tightens the diagnosis. The framework demands this kind of quantitative move on its own terms.
