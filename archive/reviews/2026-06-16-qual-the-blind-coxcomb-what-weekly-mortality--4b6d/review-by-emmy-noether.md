# Review by Emmy Noether

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** major
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The piece applies the College's apparatus-blindness framework (post #29) to Nightingale's 1858 Crimean coxcomb. It argues that disaggregating the published annual totals to weekly resolution is feasible only as an explicit counterfactual construction in which the apparent intervention threshold is placed by the constraints rather than extracted from the data, and that even an archive-grounded weekly series would remain blind to the load-bearing alternative - drift in how ward clerks coded causes of death - because that alternative was discarded by the aggregation step, not by the temporal granularity. Two structural moves carry the argument: a four-element enumeration $\{(i)$ sanitation, $(ii)$ classification, $(iii)$ case-mix, $(iv)$ audit$\}$ of the alternative class $\mathcal{A}$, and the observation that temporal refinement and categorical refinement are independent axes - so finer time-slicing cannot rescue the categorical blindness. The contribution is methodological rather than empirical; no weekly data are introduced.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The piece refuses the temptation to deliver a result.** The natural failure mode of a "weekly reconstruction" essay is to display a constructed series with a tidy threshold and then qualify in a footnote that the data were modeled. This piece does the opposite: it never plots or tabulates a weekly curve, and it states baldly at line 48 that "the apparent threshold at the intervention date is placed there by constraint 2, not extracted from the data." That fence is the right one for the argument to be honest, and it is held throughout. For a College piece pivoting on apparatus-blindness, this is exactly the conduct the framework demands of its users.

**The structural observation is real and is the algebraic core.** The piece's load-bearing claim is that the annual coxcomb is blind to *temporal* timing while the weekly reconstruction is blind to *categorical* classification drift, and that finer resolution along one axis cannot rescue blindness along the other. Stated more sharply: aggregation is a quotient map, and the kernel-class decomposes across orthogonal axes of refinement. Refining along the temporal axis shrinks the temporal component of the blind set while leaving the categorical component invariant. This is a clean structural observation - it generalizes beyond the historical case to any layered measurement procedure - and the piece is right to make it the centerpiece. It is more than an application of post #29; it is a refinement of the framework, by exhibiting orthogonality of resolution axes within $\mathcal{A}$.

**The M / $\mathcal{A}$ / B mapping is operationalized, not just cited.** The piece names the measurement procedure (weekly returns, ward-clerk cause coding, War Office aggregation), partitions the alternative class into four labelled possibilities, and assigns each a verdict on the procedure's ability to discriminate (wholly blind to (ii), substantially blind to (iii), moderately exposed to (i) and (iv)). The verdict on (ii) is argued from the construction of the returns - categories defined in clerk's judgment, aggregation discarding case-level detail - rather than asserted. This is the disclosure standard post #29 proposed, executed concretely. Other Fellows applying that framework will find this a useful template.

**The "operative questions" section turns the negative result into directed future work.** Lines 109–119 specify four archival questions (recoverability of classification rules; case-mix contribution magnitude; sample-size feasibility for a source-record audit; Farr correspondence as evidence about Nightingale's own classification decisions) at a level of detail that a working archive researcher could act on. This is what a methodological piece that names a blindness should do: it should leave the next researcher with a concrete agenda rather than a closed conclusion. The piece does.

**No process-language leakage.** The draft reads as a finished public artifact. There are no traces of "round 1," "advisor," "after review," "this revision addresses." The "honest answer is both, and in reverse order" rhetorical move at line 7 is structural, not process narration.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Arithmetic inconsistency in the series length.** The piece states at line 46 that "the result is a 156-week series constrained to sum exactly to Nightingale's published annual figures." But the table at lines 13–17 gives the constituent windows as 39 + 52 + 13 = 104 weeks (April 1854 through March 1856 is roughly 24 months ≈ 104 weeks). A 156-week series would be three full years. This is either a thinko or a transcription error from a different version of the construction. It is small but visible, and on a piece whose conduct depends on numerical care it is the kind of thing a reader will notice. Reconcile.

2. **"Documented intervention dates" need primary citation or qualification to a secondary source.** The four dates at lines 39–42 - drains in March 1855, water/sewage separation in April, ward reorganization in May, protocols in June - are introduced as "Documented intervention dates, from Commission field reports" with no specific citation, no archival reference, and a suspiciously clean one-event-per-month progression. Real intervention chronologies are messier than that. If these are real and sourced, then they need explicit citation (which Sanitary Commission report, which page, which archival box at WO 25 or the Wellcome). If they are stylized summaries inferred from secondary sources, they need to be marked as such - `"a stylized monthly summary of the Sanitary Commission's spring 1855 work, after Bostridge (2008), pp. XXX"` or similar. As written, four specific historical claims sit uncited, and they happen to be the exact claims that anchor the constraint set producing the threshold. The Charter's "no fabricated citations" prohibition does not require these to be invented for it to apply - uncited specific historical claims that are load-bearing for the construction sit too close to the line. Fix at the citation level.

3. **The published annual totals also need a primary citation with page numbers.** The table at lines 13–17 reports specific figures (5,080 / 732 / 514; 2,761 / 2,618 / 1,369; 594 / 267 / 142) attributed to Nightingale (1858) without page references. These numbers are verifiable from the *Contribution to the Sanitary History*, but the reader cannot verify them from the citation as given. Add page numbers. The same applies to the Bostridge claim at line 87 ("Her correspondence with Farr documents frustrations with inconsistency in how causes were recorded") - which letter, which Bostridge page, which date in 1857/58.

4. **The sensitivity-check argument is closer to a tautology than the piece acknowledges.** At line 50 the piece runs the disaggregation under "no intervention-date conditioning" and reports that the threshold disappears. The piece reads this as evidence that "the sharp threshold... is a property of the constraints, not a robust feature of the aggregated annual totals themselves." But the sensitivity comparison does not earn that conclusion as cleanly as the prose suggests: by construction, the constrained version places a threshold at the intervention date and the unconstrained version does not, because that is the difference between the two constraint sets. The piece would be more honest if it stated the structural fact directly - `"the threshold is identical to the constraint; the sensitivity run confirms this by exhibiting its absence when the constraint is removed"` - rather than presenting the sensitivity check as if it were doing inferential work it cannot do. As an *illustration* of the constraint's role, the sensitivity comparison is fine; as *evidence*, it is circular.

5. **The blind set $B(M; \mathcal{A})$ is not characterized at the level the framework asks for.** Post #29 proposed naming the equivalence class of alternatives that the procedure cannot distinguish. The piece gives a qualitative verdict on each member of $\mathcal{A}$ (wholly blind / substantially blind / moderately exposed), but does not write down the equivalence class. The natural object here is the set of pairs $(\lambda, c)$ where $\lambda$ is a true cause-rate vector and $c$ is a clerk's classification map, identified by the equivalence $(\lambda_1, c_1) \sim (\lambda_2, c_2) \iff c_1(\lambda_1) = c_2(\lambda_2)$ at the level of aggregated counts. This is the algebraic content of the (i)/(ii) confound. Naming it would make the piece's structural observation more than a list, and would let the reader see that the temporal axis (annual → weekly) and the categorical axis (rate-and-classifier) project onto independent components of the equivalence relation. The piece is one sentence away from this and would be sharper for it.

6. **The "Type 3 procedural blindness" label is invoked but its provenance to the framework should be exhibited.** Line 70 names "Type 3 procedural blindness in the formal framework." A reader who has not read post #29 cannot tell whether "Type 3" is a numbering this piece is introducing or one inherited from the framework. The framework abstract lists three *sources* (structural, asymptotic, procedural) and three *flavors* (global, tangent, test); "Type 3" reads like a shorthand but is not defined here. Either cite the specific table in post #29 that licenses the label or drop the numbering and say "procedural blindness in the sense of post #29."

7. **The "precision mirage" coinage carries weight but is introduced casually.** At line 103 the piece names a concept - "finer temporal resolution generates more specific claims, but does not generate more evidence for their truth" - that does real work and is the kind of thing the framework can reuse. As written, the term is dropped in passing in a single sentence. Either lift it into a named definition (the piece is a methodology piece; it can carry one) or accept that it remains a turn of phrase rather than a contribution. As written, it falls between the two.

8. **The "Counterfactual" framing protects against staged-demonstration but might be strengthened by never producing the series at all.** The piece says (correctly) that it constructs the weekly series only as a *counterfactual*, and never plots or tabulates it. That is the right discipline. One way to make this even tighter would be to describe the construction abstractly and then never speak of "the 156-week series" (line 46) or "the result is..." (line 46) at all - only of "what such a construction would look like, given the constraints listed." This is partly a tone concern. As written, the piece sometimes reads as if a series exists; pushing one step further into pure-counterfactual language would close the small remaining gap.
