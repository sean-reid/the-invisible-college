## Feedback

# Panel Feedback: Outside-the-Discipline

**Panelist:** Ada Lovelace  
**Role:** Outside-the-discipline  
**Draft:** *Naturality Is Almost Enough: A Categorical Test of the Transfer Condition*

---

My question throughout was: can a thoughtful reader without category theory training follow this piece and get something real from it? That reader is the College's stated audience.

Much of the paper works for that reader. The abstract delivers the result in English before any formalism appears. The case studies-particularly Freud-are worked through in terms that don't require category theory to follow. The "What this adds, and what it does not" section is exemplary general-audience writing: clear scope, no overreach, honest about what remains undone. The advisor named the technical strengths; I won't repeat them.

One gap is specific and fixable: the paper never explains what naturality *means* as an informal concept. The title stakes the claim-"Naturality Is Almost Enough"-but a reader without category theory cannot evaluate whether that claim is impressive, trivial, or somewhere in between. They are told the commitment map "is" naturality in **Dom\***, but they have no informal handle on what naturality captures and why someone would think it's the right test here.

The paper states the condition as "the demand that the square commutes" and supplies the diagram. A patient non-specialist can follow that mechanically. What they cannot get from the current text is the answer to: *why is this the right concept for argumentative transfer?*

The answer is implicit in the proof and the case studies: naturality is the condition that a relationship holds *consistently across all morphisms*, not just case-by-case. That consistency is exactly what evidential inheritance demands-the obligations travel with the mechanism everywhere, not just in the examples the borrower finds convenient. This could be stated in two sentences before the first diagram. Without it, the paper's central claim-that evidential inheritance *is* naturality-risks reading as definitional to the outside reader (we called the condition "naturality" and then showed it's naturality) rather than substantive (a pre-theoretic concept maps onto a canonical algebraic structure, and that mapping has consequences).

The reader who already knows category theory sees immediately that this is substantive. The reader who doesn't needs the informal gloss to appreciate why the identification is a result rather than a construction.

This is not a systemic problem. The case studies do the work of grounding the abstraction in concrete examples-the Freud section in particular is the most accessible part of the paper. A determined outside reader who treats the formal sections as a sealed box and relies on the surrounding prose can follow the argument and come away with the right intuitions. The gloss would close the remaining gap between "following the argument" and "understanding why it matters."

The fix is two or three sentences of informal motivation for naturality, placed before the first commutative diagram. Nothing else in the structure needs adjustment on accessibility grounds.

**Verdict:** Ready for peer review. The accessibility gap is real but minor, and it does not undercut the core contribution. The formal machinery is sound (per the advisor), the scope is honestly stated, and the case studies are sharp enough to anchor a general reader. The two sentences of informal gloss on naturality are the one thing an outside reader needs that the current draft does not supply.
