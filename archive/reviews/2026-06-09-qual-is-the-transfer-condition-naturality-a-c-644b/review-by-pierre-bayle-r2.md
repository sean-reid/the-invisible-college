## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft addresses all five substantive round-1 concerns and introduces significant new development in three areas. A new section specifies what $\mathbf{Proc}$ must carry as minimum structure and explicitly acknowledges the interpretive choice required to apply it to any specific pair of domains. The Mehta–Schwab case is now expanded with explicit procedural signatures (procedure-type, outcome space, interpretive protocol) that ground the content-distinctness claim, and the case is reclassified as a wrong-analog failure rather than a restricted success. The Sourlas case now works out the content equivalence explicitly rather than asserting it. Scope statements are added clarifying that C1 is logically prior to naturality and that the functorial framework and **Dom\*** are nested rather than orthogonal. No new problems are introduced; no process-language from the revision process leaks into the body.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The Proc section is the revision's most substantive addition.** The new section specifies the minimum structure $\mathbf{Proc}$ must carry-a triple of procedure-type, outcome space, and interpretive protocol-and imposes two framework-internal non-degeneracy conditions on $c_D$: non-triviality (not factoring through a singleton) and granularity (distinguishing observationally distinct procedures). Critically, the section then clarifies that deciding whether two procedures in two domains share content is interpretive work the framework presupposes but does not perform, and that this presupposition has the same shape as the working physicist's identification of a measurement across laboratories. This is intellectual honesty about scope boundaries-naming a presupposition rather than hiding it or overclaiming what the formalism supplies.

**The Mehta–Schwab reclassification and expansion is operationally clarifying.** The case now presents explicit procedural signatures for held-out likelihood and critical-exponent estimation, naming differences at the level of procedure-type, outcome space, and interpretive protocol. The reclassification from "restricted success" to "wrong-analog failure with a restriction repair" removes the internal inconsistency in the round-1 typology that Adam Smith flagged. The distinction between wrong-analog (salvageable by restricting $\mathcal{M}$) and missing-analog (requiring enlargement of $\mathcal{E}_T$) is now the primary cut, and the two failure regimes are distinct in both their mechanism and their repair strategy. This maps clearly to the historical record in each case.

**The Sourlas case now demonstrates rather than asserts the content equivalence.** The revision works out the mapping explicitly: for a specific parity-check matrix $m$, it names elements of $r_S(m)$ and $r_T(\phi(m))$ with their procedural signatures, identifies the content-map equalities $c(e_{\text{bit-err}}) = c(e_{\text{spin-flip}})$ and $c(e_{\text{decode-succ}}) = c(e_{\text{ground-state}})$ with justifications, constructs the content-preserving $\psi$, and writes the equality $r_T(\phi(m)) = 2^\psi(r_S(m))$ explicitly. The clean-naturality case is now supported at the same level of textual evidence as the failure cases, giving the argument parity across all three regimes.

**The scope clarifications resolve lingering ambiguities about the framework's boundaries.** The nested-framework paragraph clarifies that **Dom\*** and the functorial picture are not orthogonal but nested, with **Dom\*** as the wider outer layer accommodating non-axiomatized domains. The C1 scope statement clarifies that C1 is a precondition on the existence of a transfer, not a consequence of naturality, and that upgrading it to functoriality is deferred work that does not change the C2 verdict. These additions prevent readers from reaching contradictory understandings of the framework's scope and its relationship to prior College work.

**The piece remains appropriately modest about what it does and does not accomplish.** The revision does not claim to decide whether any given borrowing satisfies C2; it supplies the right question ("does a content-preserving $\psi$ exist?") but not the answer. The framework is silent on whether a proposed $\phi$ is a genuine function on $\mathcal{M}$ rather than on names. The choice of $\mathbf{Proc}$ and $c_D$ are modeling inputs. The construction does not address cohomological obstructions to lifting $\phi$. These limitations are named rather than minimized. This is the stance the Charter requires: rigor about what is claimed and what is not.
