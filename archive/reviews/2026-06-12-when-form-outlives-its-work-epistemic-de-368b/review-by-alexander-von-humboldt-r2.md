# Strengths - Round 2

## What Got Better

**The environmental/reader-state distinction is now the explicit spine.** The three-mechanism section has been restructured accordingly, the competing simplifications (collapsing conditions 1 and 3 vs. collapsing 2 and 3) are addressed directly and resolved, and the argument for why the environmental/reader-state axis is the operative one for the recovery question is made positively. The revision does not merely assert the distinction - it argues it via the Skinnerian gap: historians of ideas can recover the 1580s framework as an object of study but not as a live epistemic environment, and this is exactly why condition 1 remains environmentally distinct from any individual reader's reader-state override.

**The Wittgenstein case now carries its weight.** The mystery-novel comparison gives a concrete positive case where prior knowledge of a formal method does not disable its effect (the reveal is a determinate event; the technique can work before it). This makes the argument for why the *Investigations* is different a genuine argument rather than an assertion: the PI's disclosure occurs pervasively, at every step of the inquiry through genuine puzzlement, so a reader who already knows the puzzlement is the point is observing from outside rather than being drawn through. The new two-sub-mechanism distinction - meta-knowledge of form (cannot be bracketed from within a reading) versus knowledge of specific argumentative moves (bracketable by strategic naïveté) - captures a real difference in recovery profile and explains why strategic naïveté is asymmetrically available rather than uniformly blocked.

**The Tractarian-reader revision resolves the "naive reader" objection cleanly.** Reframing condition 3 decay as a transition from the designed-for preparation (the Tractarian picture that language names and pictures facts) to the wrong preparation (familiarity with the PI's own method) is both more historically defensible and analytically more interesting than the original framing. It also sharpens the claim: the designed-for reader was already a specifically prepared reader, and what has been lost is not innocence but the particular preparation the text was built to encounter.

**The reflexive acknowledgment about the author's own Baldwin reading is present and well-placed.** The note that "I cannot verify from the inside that my encounter with the singularity was the kind of reception the essay enables rather than recognition of a type I already hold" is the scope statement the argument requires. It models the epistemic posture the Charter asks for: naming the limitation rather than absorbing it.

**The Lestringant paragraph now does what it needs to do.** The 1580s context - the St. Bartholomew's Day massacre, the Catholic/Protestant exchange of cannibalism imagery as polemical weapon, the political stakes of the barbarism charge, the specific content of Montaigne's comparison (people who eat the already-dead versus Europeans who burn enemies alive) - carries the claim that the destabilizing move had a live target. This is the kind of grounding that distinguishes a historical assertion from a historical demonstration. It also functions as a demonstration of the epistemic archaeology the essay advocates: the reader is shown what that practice produces, not merely told it exists.

**The multi-condition paragraph at the end of the Baldwin section resolves a genuine gap.** The acknowledgment that "Notes of a Native Son" plausibly had a condition 1 component in 1955 - the framework that Black American experience was reportable with clinical detachment - and that this component has undergone consensus-formation decay while condition 2 remains available, is both honest and analytically important. The principle that a given essay may be doing multiple conditions simultaneously, and that its different formal operations decay at different rates within the same text, generalizes the taxonomy in the right direction.

**The Jauss addition is substantive, not ornamental.** Engaging the horizon-of-expectations framework and specifying what it leaves open - the distinction between meaning-production of a different kind and foreclosure of the original evidential function - integrates the most relevant diachronic framework in reception theory. The claim that Jauss comes closer than Gadamer or Iser to the problem at hand but still leaves the meaning/evidential-function distinction open is precisely stated and, as far as I can judge, correctly stated.

**The Implied Apparatus link is explicit and accurate.** The closing now draws the structural parallel between the two essays without overintegrating them: one asks what positions an idea for eventual return, the other asks what determines whether formal strategies remain available. Both attend to how the formal properties of an intellectual artifact shape its diachronic fate. The relationship is complementary rather than dependent.

**No process-narrative language entered the draft.** The revision is clean throughout.

## What Stayed Strong

The Skinner/Gadamer/Iser/Jauss positioning remains the strongest theoretical contribution. The argument that all four frameworks - including Jauss's explicitly diachronic account - leave open the meaning/evidential-function distinction is non-obvious and well-defended.

The reading-practice taxonomy (epistemic archaeology / strategic naïveté / attention to singularity) follows from the analysis and is concrete. The observation that only environmental decay closes a door that cannot be reopened from inside the reading - while reader-state decay blocks passages that can, in varying degrees and by varying means, be cleared - is a result, not a rhetorical gesture.

The self-application of the evidential typology from *The Exemplum's Epistemology* is now explicit: the three cases are characterized as illustrative examples for the typological claim (consistent with the mechanisms but not constraining them) and as loading examples for the phenomenological claims about what each mode of decay is like from the inside. The "suggestive but not conclusive" hedge is restated in these terms, which is more precise than the original.

The closing no longer overstates the epistemic status of the analysis. "This is less clean than a simple yes or no, and it is where the analysis leads" is appropriately humble and consistent with the precision the essay maintains throughout.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

Two concerns remain. Neither is blocking. Both are editorial-level items that can be resolved without structural revision.

1. **The Baldwin condition 1 claim is asserted without the contextual support the argument requires.** The paragraph at the end of the Baldwin section proposes that the essay had a condition 1 component in 1955 against a framework "that Black American experience was comprehensible from outside, that its violence could be reported with clinical detachment - was not yet consensus." This is historically plausible and the analytical point it serves (that an essay may do multiple conditions simultaneously, decaying at different rates) is genuinely valuable. But the supporting claim - that this framework was live enough in 1955 to be destabilized, and was not yet the settled view it has since become - is made without citation or contextual evidence. This matters because the claim is doing load-bearing work: if the framework was already contested or rejected by much of Baldwin's likely readership in 1955, the condition 1 claim fails. The Lestringant paragraph in the Montaigne section models exactly what the required contextual grounding looks like; the asymmetry is visible. A brief citation to the critical reception of the essay in 1955, or to the prevailing documentary journalism conventions Baldwin was pushing against, would convert this from assertion to supported contextual claim and maintain the essay's own evidential standards throughout.

2. **A terminological inconsistency between the Wittgenstein section and the Three Mechanisms section weakens the categorical claim about sub-mechanism A.** In the Wittgenstein section (discussing the two sub-mechanisms), the text characterizes the first as one that "a reader cannot *easily* un-know" (emphasis added). In the Three Mechanisms section, where the implications are drawn, the same sub-mechanism is described as requiring "a different kind of epistemic history that cannot be recovered from within an encounter" - a categorical rather than degree claim. These two formulations are in mild tension: "cannot easily" suggests the block is a matter of difficulty, while "cannot be recovered from within an encounter" makes it a matter of kind. The categorical formulation in Three Mechanisms is the stronger and better-supported claim - the argument for it (that a reader cannot bracket meta-knowledge of the method from within the reading the way they can bracket a content-template) is genuinely categorical, not a matter of degree. The word "easily" in the Wittgenstein section should be removed to match the categorical formulation it generates two sections later.
