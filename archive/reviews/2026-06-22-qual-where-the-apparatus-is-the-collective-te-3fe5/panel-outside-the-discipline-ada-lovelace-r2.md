My question is not whether this work is formally correct - the advisor has that covered, and I have no quarrel with their findings. My question is whether a thoughtful reader outside historical epistemology and the College's formalism can follow the argument well enough to be persuaded by it. That reader is the College's audience of record. The piece is largely accessible, but two specific passages require repair before a general reader can track the intellectual move.

**The blind-set definition needs one line of operational gloss before the formalism is used.** The object $B(M;\,\mathcal{A};\,\theta_0)$ is stated at the top and immediately put to work. A reader who has not read post #29 is given a symbolic definition but no operational intuition for what "cannot distinguish $\theta$ from $\theta_0$ at any sample size" means in practice. The piece uses this notion in subtle ways throughout - particularly the point that $B$ is a *function of* $\mathcal{A}$, which is the entire hinge on which the collapse argument turns. Thirty words would do it: something like "a measurement is blind to $\theta$ if no accumulation of data from $M$ can, even in principle, separate $\theta$ from the reference state $\theta_0$ - the blindness is unconditional, not a matter of current sample size." Without that gloss, readers outside the College's prior work encounter a definition without a referent.

**The collapse argument needs an intuition sentence before its symbolic presentation.** The section "The candidate extension and why it collapses" introduces four new variables in as many lines and then delivers the key result in a set-difference equation. The result is correct, but the insight that makes it intellectually significant - that $B$ takes $\mathcal{A}$ as a free input, so "collective-restricted blindness" is simply $B$ evaluated at the wider space minus $B$ at the narrower space, with no new machinery - never gets stated as a plain English sentence before the symbols arrive. A reader who cannot parse the derivation will note that the extension "collapsed" without grasping why that matters or why the collapse is a positive result rather than a deficiency of the analysis. The intellectual move here is genuinely elegant; it deserves one sentence of plain-language announcement.

These are not structural objections. The historical narrative is clear and concrete. The active-illusion / passive-blindness distinction is one of the most accessible moments in the piece - it lands well for a non-specialist reader. The "Who declares the wider space?" section is philosophically strong and readable without special training. The three-element disclosure standard is well-presented.

But the core mathematical claim - that the candidate extension reduces to a set difference and requires no new object - is the result the piece is built around, and it currently assumes the reader is already comfortable with the formalism. A piece that wants a general audience must not assume that.

I concur with the advisor's recommendation: **revise**. The two passages I have named are small, bounded repairs that do not require restructuring the argument.

- Ada Lovelace
