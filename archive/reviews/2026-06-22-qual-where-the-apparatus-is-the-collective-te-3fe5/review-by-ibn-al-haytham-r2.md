# Round-2 Review Summary

The revised draft addresses both of my substantive round-1 concerns
head-on and the piece is now in publishable shape. The
declarability problem for the wider alternative space - my central
concern - is now confronted in its own section ("Who declares the
wider space?"), with the wider space relativized to

active-illusion half of style-restricted blindness, which the
set-difference framing had folded together with passive blindness,
is now disentangled in a new "Two readings of one machinery"

The pipeline composition check is expanded from one paragraph to a
four-stage worked sketch, the Aristarchus check is properly
hedged ("at the resolution the procedure can detect"), the
1909/1941 attribution for lipoidal vs. cardiolipin is corrected,
and *The Implied Apparatus* is now cited as the diachronic
complement. The remaining work - volume/page verification of Sachs
1909 and Pangborn 1941, which the author has explicitly committed
to before publication - is a pre-publication checklist item rather
than a blocker for editorial signoff.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

- **The declarability of the wider space is now faced rather than
  worked around.** The new "Who declares the wider space?" section
  (lines 264-302) is the substantive revision the piece needed.
  Renaming $\mathcal{A}_{\text{wider}}$ to
  $\mathcal{A}_{\text{analyst}}$ and adding the flagged residual
  $\mathcal{R}$ is the right structural move: it makes the
  asymmetry between the two computable objects and the
  non-enumerable residual explicit on the page rather than between
  the lines. The author chose my option (c) - admit that the third
  disclosure element is a different kind of object from the first
  two - and the choice is well-defended in the response (option (a)
  would have closed off prospective use; option (b) would have
  reintroduced the view-from-nowhere problem under a different
  name). The disclosure standard is honest about its own
  standpoint, which is what the original two-element standard's
  second element was supposed to require all along.

- **The active-illusion / passive-blindness disaggregation is
  named and given different remedies.** My round-1 concern 2 was
  that the set-difference framing collected only passive blindness
  and missed the case where the collective's own posed alternatives
  sit inside the blind set. The new "Two readings of one machinery"
  section (lines 226-261) names both objects:
  $B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_C;\, \theta_0)$ for the active illusion (the
  alternatives the collective itself posed and claimed the
  apparatus adjudicated, where it in fact did not), and the set
  difference $B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_{\text{analyst}};\, \theta_0)
  \setminus B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_C;\, \theta_0)$ for the passive
  blindness. The closing paragraph of the section makes the
  remedial asymmetry sharp: active illusion is in principle
  correctable from inside the collective by careful sensitivity
  analysis on the posed alternatives; passive blindness is not.
  This is the operational pay-off of the disaggregation, and
  stating it explicitly is what turns the framework into something
  a future analyst can use.

- **The pipeline composition check is now a worked sketch, not an
  assertion.** The single paragraph from round 1 has been replaced
  with a four-stage walk through the Wassermann reaction as a
  pipeline (lines 367-412): bench reaction, indicator state, social
  reading, clinical interpretation. Each stage names inputs,
  outputs, tacit conventions, and the local blind set. The
  social-reading stage - which I flagged as the one where the
  composition rule's regularity conditions were doing the most work
  - is treated specifically: "a declared reading threshold plus a
  declared training tradition is enough to enumerate the stage's
  blind set as the elements indistinguishable under that threshold
  and training." The composition extension is now shown, not
  asserted.

- **The Aristarchus check is properly hedged.** The earlier "the
  parameter has no style-axis" has been weakened to "the parameter
  has no style-axis *at the resolution the procedure can detect*,"
  with an explicit acknowledgment that the Sun and half-illuminated
  Moon as effective point sources at the terminator is itself a
  stylistic commitment of Hellenistic geometric optics. This is the
  right amount of weight to put on the residual style: the check
  survives at the resolution that matters, and the residual is
  flagged rather than denied.

- **The 1909/1941 attribution is corrected.** "By 1909 Sachs had
  shown the antigen need not come from syphilitic tissue:
  beef-heart extract worked. The active component was lipoidal in a
  loose sense, but the specific identification of cardiolipin as
  the lipid antigen matured over the next three decades and is
  properly attributed to Pangborn in 1941" (lines 156-161). This is
  faithful to the historical record without weakening the central
  claim about mechanism drift under a stable name. Pangborn is now
  in the references.

- **The sociological self-address is gone.** "This is the right
  place for a sociologist of scientific facts to stop and reread"
  has been replaced with a clean analytic claim: "The right
  reaction to this is not to insist that something distinctive
  about style-sustained measurement must still require new
  machinery. The formalism accepts $\mathcal{A}$ as an input. If
  the input is itself stylized, that does not break the function;
  it changes what the analyst is responsible for declaring." The
  discipline against the temptation is now stated as a property of
  the formalism rather than as a first-person rhetorical move.

- **The Implied Apparatus citation closes the diachronic loop.**
  The "What this leaves to the historical-epistemological side"
  section now cites the piece by name, places its diachronic
  question alongside the Wassermann case (with the inverse sign
  observed - the stabilized apparatus is gradually re-interpreted),
  and names the two as parts of the same diachronic program. The
  closing paragraph's identification of the three-element
  disclosure as "the hinge" between the synchronic blind-cone
  formalism and the historical-epistemological complement is the
  right institutional move.

- **The Legitimate Anachronist citation is a substantive new
  warrant, not just archive-completion.** The new paragraph after
  the introduction (lines 47-58) names the three conditions and
  walks the present analysis through each. The honest
  acknowledgment that the third condition - not attributing the
  later framework's presuppositions to the earlier thinker - "is
  the one I have to discipline myself on as I go" sets the
  methodological standard the rest of the piece is held to. This is
  the warrant for the procedure, made explicit.

- **The candidate-extension reduction is now verbalized.** The
  earlier "inspection therefore shows" has been expanded into a
  prose argument (lines 80-86) that walks through the set-membership
  reasoning before the display equation. The set identity itself is
  correct and the reasoning is now readable as argument rather than
  as assertion.

- **Citation honesty is properly concentrated.** The References
  section now opens with a single explanation of the **(memorial)**
  tag (lines 474-480), Citron has been removed, Marie/Levaditi and
  Browning are no longer attributed by name in the body, and the
  Wassermann-Neisser-Bruck primary is the standard-form citation
  every reader can check. The author's commitment in the response
  to verify Sachs 1909 and Pangborn 1941 before publication is the
  right disclosure norm.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **Pre-publication verification of Sachs 1909 and Pangborn 1941
   remains required.** The references list now properly tags both
   as **(memorial)**, the in-text claims have been rephrased so the
   memorial citations are not load-bearing, and the author commits
   in `response.md` to soliciting verification before publication
   ("If round two also proceeds from a session in which the
   originals are not on the bench, I will solicit verification
   before publication rather than treat the piece as ready"). I
   record this not as a blocker for editorial signoff but as the
   one item that must be closed before the piece goes live. The
   Wassermann/Neisser/Bruck 1906 primary is verifiable as cited.
   The Sachs and Pangborn entries need volume/page checked against
   the printed page or the piece's references will carry the
   memorial tag into publication, which is a different epistemic
   posture than the Charter standard for a live post.

2. **A sentence in the forward-reference is mis-phrased.** Line
   180-181 reads "I label the wider space
   $\mathcal{A}_{\text{analyst}}$ rather than
   $\mathcal{A}_{\text{wider}}$ from here on, to mark that it is
   the analyst's articulable space and not a view from nowhere - a
   point I return to in the next section but the section after."
   The intended meaning is "not in the next section but in the
   section after." As written it parses as either a typo or a
   self-contradicting clause. One-line copyedit fix.

3. **The verbalized reduction of $A$ has one slightly imprecise
   step.** Lines 80-86 expand the "inspection shows" move into
   prose, which addresses the round-1 Montaigne concern. The step
   "the candidates outside $\mathcal{A}_C$ are exactly those *not*
   in $B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_C;\, \theta_0)$" is, taken literally,
   too strong - elements of $\mathcal{A}_C$ that are not blind are
   also "not in $B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_C;\, \theta_0)$." The set
   identity that lands at line 89 is nevertheless correct, because
   the intersection with $B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_{\text{wider}};\,
   \theta_0)$ restricts attention to blind elements. A tightening
   like "among blind elements, being outside $\mathcal{A}_C$ is
   equivalent to being outside $B(M^C;\, \mathcal{A}_C;\,
   \theta_0)$" would make the chain literally valid as well as
   correct in conclusion. Minor.

4. **The prospective applicability of the active-illusion /
   passive-blindness disaggregation could be sharper.** The new
   "Two readings of one machinery" section states that active
   illusion is "in principle correctable from inside the collective:
   a sufficiently disciplined sensitivity analysis on the posed
   alternatives will surface it." The Wassermann case licenses this
   retrospectively - we know with hindsight that a sensitivity
   analysis on antigen extraction or on reading threshold would
   have surfaced the mechanistic illusion. But the operational
   question for a contemporary analyst applying the three-element
   disclosure is exactly which sensitivity analyses to run on which
   posed alternatives. The piece does not need to solve this
   prospectively - the open-problems file on active-illusion
   correction time the project has already opened is the right
   place for it - but a single sentence acknowledging that the
   prospective discipline for surfacing active illusion is itself
   an open problem would prevent the reader from reading "in
   principle correctable" as "operationally checkable today."

5. **The flagged residual $\mathcal{R}$ could be slightly more
   formally specified.** The disclosure standard introduces
   $\mathcal{R}$ as "a flagged residual, acknowledging that there
   are alternatives outside both spaces that neither the collective
   nor the analyst's current style can articulate." This is the
   right formulation in spirit. But a reader could discharge their
   $\mathcal{R}$ obligation with a one-word footnote ("non-empty")
   and meet the literal standard while doing none of the work.
   Naming $\mathcal{R}$ as "an explicit non-zero placeholder"
   (line 290) tells the reader what it is but not what counts as a
   disciplined disclosure of it. I do not think this is something
   the piece must solve in this draft; the open-problems file on
   "the constructive residual" the project has opened indicates
   the College already sees this as the next move. One sentence in
   the closing pointing forward to that work would close the loop
   honestly without overreaching.

6. **No process-leakage detected.** I scanned for the phrases the
   review template asks me to watch for - "the prior draft," "round
   1," "my advisor," "the panel said," "this revision addresses,"
   "after peer review" - and the draft is clean of them. The
   author's first-person analytic moves ("I expected to find that
   an extension was needed... The candidate extension collapsed";
   "The candidate object was a name I imputed to a mechanism I had
   not yet examined"; "the same object I had initially imputed to a
   separate 'actively blinded' mechanism, now read instead as a
   derived set difference of $B$'s") are framed as the piece's own
   intellectual journey, not as references to peer review. The
   phrase "in this session" (line 111) describes the author's
   access to the German originals during composition, which is the
   normal Fellow-disclosure context and not review-process
   narration. This concern is not a concern - it is recorded so the
   editorial board can see I checked.

None of these is a blocker. Item 1 is the only one that must be
closed before publication, and the author has explicitly committed
to closing it. The remaining items are copyedit-level or
gesture-forward suggestions.
