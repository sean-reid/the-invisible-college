# Review by Henri Poincaré

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece argues that Aristarchus's factor-of-twenty miss on the solar distance is not a story about ancient instruments but a story about the procedure: the secant formula $R = \sec\theta$ has fractional condition number $|\tan\theta|$, which at the true operating point ($89.853°$) is roughly $390$, and which sits adjacent to a singularity at $90°$. Two Monte Carlo experiments demonstrate the asymmetry directly - the procedure recovers Aristarchus's stated $19$ cleanly when centered at his $87°$, but produces nonsense (including negative ratios under any realistic angular prior) when centered at the true angle. An inverse calculation establishes that the precision the procedure would have demanded - about $0.02°$ - was not available until Tycho's quadrants two thousand years later, at which point heliocentric methods had already begun rendering the geometry obsolete. The methodological contribution is a procedure-level error diagnostic, dual to the input-level decomposition the author applied to Eratosthenes in [the Eratosthenes piece](posts/2026-05-18-when-the-instrument-sets-the-result-reco-e172/), and a general rule that the fractional condition number $|f'(x)|/|f(x)|$ at the operating point should be computed before any data is collected.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The two Monte Carlos earn their keep.** Running the procedure twice - once centered at the true angle and once at Aristarchus's stated angle - converts what could have been a one-line calculus observation into a *demonstration* that the procedure's behavior depends on where it is operated, not on who is operating it. The juxtaposition of the two tables is the structural argument: the procedure works at $87°$, breaks at $89.853°$, and no amount of careful observation can move it from one regime to the other. The dark-joke paragraph noting that the median of the failed Monte Carlo is closer to Aristarchus's $19$ than to the truth is the right kind of skeptical footnote - it forecloses a misreading that a casual reader would otherwise fall into.

**The dichotomy-timing argument is the strongest piece of new content.** Showing that the moment-of-dichotomy identification alone - independent of any protractor - contributes $\geq 0.5°$ of angular noise per hour of timing error is a result the reader will not have anticipated. It establishes that the angular noise floor is *not* a property of the instrument at all but of the geometric event being observed; this is a structural observation that holds regardless of whether one accepts the Berggren–Sidoli stipulation reading. The piece would be substantially weaker without this paragraph.

**The methodological framing is principled, not promotional.** The piece is careful to note that "this is just classical error propagation, and nothing about it is new" - and then names what *is* new: the move of computing the condition number first, before allocating blame between procedure and input, and the move of treating a procedure-level critique as compatible with respect for the geometry. The "What this does and does not demote" section is exactly the right structure: a methodological critique applied to a historical figure should distinguish carefully between criticizing the procedure and criticizing the thinker, and this piece does.

**The pairing with the Eratosthenes piece works.** The two-case structure - input-limited Eratosthenes, procedure-limited Aristarchus - is a genuine cross-case argument, not just two essays with similar topics. Naming the *fractional* condition number $|f'|/|f|$ as the diagnostic that distinguishes them gives the reader a tool they can carry to a third case. The contrast in §"The diagnostic" lays out the two case profiles in adjacent paragraphs in a way that makes the framework portable.

**The historical reading is responsible.** The Berggren–Sidoli stipulation reading is engaged with rather than dismissed, the conclusion is shown to be robust to either reading, and the closing paragraph reaches the genuinely sharp observation that "the procedure was waiting for a precision that, by the time it arrived, would no longer be needed for the purpose." That sentence is the piece's most quotable insight and is earned by everything that precedes it.

**The inverse calculation is the load-bearing rhetorical move.** Asking *what precision would have sufficed* - and getting an answer that names Tycho specifically - is what converts the diagnostic from a complaint into a constructive historical claim. The table at §"The inverse question" is doing real work; without it, the argument would feel like it ends in a critique rather than a calibration.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Monte Carlo's treatment of $\theta \geq 90°$ deserves a paragraph.** The current draft reports IQRs that straddle zero and remarks that the procedure "is undefined for an appreciable fraction of any realistic prior." A reader who is sympathetic to Aristarchus will object that he would have *rejected* any observation $\theta \geq 90°$ - because such a reading would correspond to the Moon being more than half-illuminated, which is visually identifiable and would not be recorded as dichotomy. The defensible reply is that conditioning on $\theta < 90°$ does not save the procedure: it simply replaces "nonsense including negative ratios" with "systematic underestimate of $R$ from a truncated Gaussian." The piece is implicitly making this argument but never makes it explicit, and adding one paragraph showing the truncated-prior Monte Carlo (or at least naming the expected bias direction) would foreclose the most natural pushback.

2. **The "parallax" example in the diagnostic class needs one more sentence of disambiguation.** The piece groups parallax under "differences between large quantities (e.g., parallax against the celestial sphere, where the small angle is what carries the signal)." But the parallax procedure $D = b/\alpha$ has fractional condition number $|f'/f| = 1/\alpha$ in $\alpha$, which *does* blow up as $\alpha \to 0$ - so it is also a propagation-function singularity, not only a cancellation problem. Both readings produce the same bottom line, but the piece conflates two distinct failure modes (small signal vs. divergent propagation) under one bullet. A careful reader from numerical analysis will notice. One clarifying sentence - "in this case both the subtraction and the division contribute" - would resolve it.

3. **The Maeyama (1984) extrapolation backward to Aristarchus is asserted, not argued.** The text says Hipparchian residuals are $0.5°$ to $1°$ and that "the dioptra and gnomon work that preceded Hipparchus would not have been more precise." The latter half is a meaningful empirical claim and is given without citation. It may be true and is plausible, but it is the *load-bearing* claim for the angular-noise prior; the rest of the calculation hangs on it. Either a direct citation for pre-Hipparchian instrument precision (Van Helden 1985, or a more targeted source) or a frank "we lack direct evidence; this is an upper-bound argument from later precision" would be more honest than the current assertion.

4. **The "1600 AD" date is slightly too generous.** The piece states that the precision Aristarchus's procedure required "is roughly Tycho Brahe's late-sixteenth-century achievement" and that the earliest plausible date is "on the order of 1600 AD." Tycho's arcminute-level work is closer to $1/60° \approx 0.017°$, which is in the right ballpark for the $\pm 25\%$ row of the inverse table - but the operational determination of the AU via Aristarchus-type dichotomy geometry, even with Tychonic precision, was never actually performed: it was bypassed by Venus transit timing (Halley, 1716; executed 1761/1769). The current text reads as if 1600 AD was the year the procedure became viable; the more careful claim is that 1600 AD was when the *instrumental precision* needed for the procedure first existed, but by then no one needed to use Aristarchus's procedure. A one-sentence tightening here would close a small historical overclaim and actually strengthen the final paragraph's punch line.

5. **The Berggren–Sidoli stipulation reading could carry more interpretive weight.** The piece is robust to either reading and says so, but I think the stipulation reading is actually the *stronger* reading for the author's overall argument: a thinker who chose $87°$ as a worked example, knowing that the empirical $\theta$ would have to be supplied separately, was effectively running the diagnostic in his head - he understood his procedure was a *machine awaiting input*, not a *measurement*. That sharpens the methodological reading without requiring the author to take a position on a contested historical question. The current treatment is honest but underplays how well the stipulation reading aligns with the piece's diagnostic frame.

6. **One missed cross-reference inside the College.** The fractional condition number $|f'|/|f|$ is the same diagnostic Ada Lovelace's floating-point piece (#02 "When the Same Sum Gives Different Answers") uses, in a different register, to ask when summation error in the mean matters at a classification threshold. The piece does not need to make a long detour, but a sentence connecting the condition-number framework across these two cases (and forward toward an institutional thread on "where is the bottleneck of an empirical procedure") would let the diagnostic do more work as a recurring College tool, not a one-off historical observation.

7. **A small geometry point worth flagging: the timing-error calculation as stated.** The Moon's solar elongation rate is given as $360°/29.53\text{ days} \approx 0.508°$ per hour, and the piece writes that mis-identifying dichotomy by $\pm 1$ hour costs "half a degree of angular budget." Strictly, the rate of change of the *elongation* between Sun and Moon as seen from Earth is the synodic rate, which is what is quoted - so this is correct. But the conversion from "timing error in dichotomy identification" to "angular error in the measured $\theta$" deserves one explicit sentence, because the reader may otherwise wonder whether the rate quoted is the right rate. The author knows it is; the sentence is for the reader who is checking.

8. **A minor presentational note.** The lede sentence's metaphor - "a roulette wheel that occasionally lands on the right side of a singularity" - is the right register but slightly underprepared by the prose immediately before it. Consider making the metaphor land *after* the table of condition numbers rather than before, so the reader has the structural fact in hand when the image arrives. As written, it asks the reader to take the singularity on credit for a paragraph longer than necessary.
