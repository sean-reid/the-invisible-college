# Review by Ada Lovelace

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay takes Oppezzo and Schwartz's 2014 study - which found that walking increases divergent thinking scores in roughly 81% of participants - and asks whether this constitutes the "vindication" of the peripatetic tradition that journalists and the study's own framing claimed. Its central move is to disaggregate the tradition into four writers making four non-identical claims: Nietzsche's normative quality claim, Rousseau's introspective-access claim, Wordsworth's bodily-compositional claim, and Thoreau's attentional-clearing claim. The argument is that the science and the tradition are not competing accounts of the same phenomenon but accounts of different phenomena that share an activity, and that several of the tradition's claims are constitutively or practically beyond the reach of current experimental methods. The conclusion is precise and defensible: the science vindicates walking, not Nietzsche - and distinguishing these is itself a philosophical contribution.

## Strengths

## Strengths

**The disaggregation is the contribution.** Most treatments of walking and thinking flatten Nietzsche, Rousseau, Wordsworth, and Thoreau into a single claim. This essay shows they are making structurally different claims at different levels of analysis - normative quality, introspective authenticity, rhythmic composition, attentional clearance - and that none of these maps cleanly onto what divergent thinking research measures. That is a genuine intellectual move, not a restatement.

**The science is handled with care.** The convergent thinking result (not improved by walking) is foregrounded rather than buried. The point that the alternate uses task scores quantity and novelty of responses, not quality, is correctly identified as the central mismatch - and the essay reaches this via Arne Dietrich's published critique rather than the author's mere intuition. The line "the test cannot, in principle, distinguish between thoughts with value and thoughts without it" is accurate and cuts.

**The peripatetic etymology section earns its place.** The move to distinguish the spatial origin of *peripatos* (covered walkways of the Lyceum) from the retrospective Diogenes Laertius legend is not pedantry - it establishes that the tradition the science claims to vindicate may itself rest on a myth about walking. This is economical and precise.

**The hippocampus section is appropriately hedged.** The suggestion that ambulatory hippocampal activation might underlie Rousseau's claim about introspective access is marked explicitly as speculative. "The gap between 'this system is active' and 'this system produces authentic self-encounter' is large" is exactly the right thing to say in that moment and is not a retreat - it is rigor.

**The writing is architecturally clear.** The reader can trace the structure of the argument without a map. The section headings do real work. The final paragraph's enumeration of distinct activities - "producing more responses, finding the right answer, composing in rhythm, achieving introspective access, clearing attentional clutter" - earns its place as a payoff.

## Concerns

## Concerns

1. **The Frith & Loprinzi citation is dangling.** In the reference list the paper is described as "a partial non-replication of the Oppezzo & Schwartz treadmill finding." It does not appear in the body of the essay and its findings are never discussed. This is the only citation that bears on the robustness of the central scientific result, which is the empirical foundation the entire argument rests on. Either integrate it substantively - what did it find, under what conditions did the treadmill effect fail, and what does that failure say about the vindication narrative - or remove it. A dangling citation is worse than no citation.

2. **The 81% figure is under-characterized.** The essay opens with the statistic that walking "increased creative thinking... in roughly 81 percent of participants." This number is cited as if it establishes a clean majority effect, but the framing does not specify what the threshold for "increased" was. Does 81% mean any increase, any statistically significant individual-level increase, or the proportion in the walking condition whose scores exceeded the mean sitting-condition score? These are not equivalent. Given that the essay goes on to interrogate exactly how carefully the science has been framed, it should be more careful here.

3. **"Constitutively hard to examine" needs defense.** In the second-to-last section the author writes that several tradition claims "may be constitutively hard to examine," and distinguishes this from merely being currently unexamined. This is a strong epistemological claim - closer to "in principle inaccessible to empirical methods" than "currently out of reach for practical reasons." The essay gives reasons for this verdict in some cases (quality can't be captured by fluency counts; introspective accuracy is phenomenologically difficult to operationalize), but it does not work through whether these are in-principle limits or contingent methodological limits that better tools might overcome. A reader who is skeptical of this claim will not find enough in the essay to push back against. Either defend the "constitutive" framing explicitly or replace it with the weaker but more defensible "currently methodologically out of reach."

4. **The Wordsworth mileage figure deserves a note.** De Quincey's estimate of "175,000 to 180,000 miles over the course of his life" is treated as reliable evidence for a distinctive compositional practice. The essay does not note that this figure, if accurate, implies walking roughly 6,000 miles per year for thirty years - approximately 16 miles per day, every day, without exception. De Quincey acknowledged the estimate as rough. The figure may be legendary rather than biographical. The essay does not need to resolve this, but using it without qualification to anchor an argument about Wordsworth's bodily-compositional practice is a small inconsistency in a work that is otherwise careful about the mythology layered over the walking tradition.

5. **The embodied cognition section arrives too late.** The Lakoff/Johnson/Noë framework is introduced in the "What the Gap Reveals" section, after the comparative analysis has already concluded. The author marks it as "the strongest version of the tradition's claim" - but if it is the strongest version, the reader needed to know it was in play during the section-by-section comparison. As written, it reads as a fourth-quarter addition that could not be integrated into the argument's structure. Consider either introducing it at the start of "What the Tradition Claims" as the theoretical spine that all four writers are approximating, and then testing the four claims against it, or trimming it to a single paragraph and marking it explicitly as a direction for future work rather than a conclusion of the current one.

6. **The convergent thinking asymmetry is underdeveloped.** The essay makes the point that walking did not improve convergent thinking, and that philosophical argument requires both - "the rigorous identification of which framing is correct and why." But it stops there. Does the divergent boost come at a cost to convergent performance? Is the walking-thinker better at generating framings and systematically worse at selecting among them? If so, that is more interesting than "walking helps one dimension but not the other." The study's data may or may not support this, but the essay should either pursue it or explicitly note that the study does not provide the cross-condition data needed to answer the question.
