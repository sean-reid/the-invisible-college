## Summary

The draft applies formal Bayesian uncertainty propagation to Eratosthenes' circumference calculation, treating the three inputs - shadow angle, Alexandria–Syene road distance, and stadion length - as probability distributions with explicitly defended priors and propagating them through 10⁶ Monte Carlo trials. The headline finding is a 95% credible interval of roughly 33,000–59,000 km, with the modern meridional circumference at the 29th percentile of the pooled distribution. More importantly, a variance decomposition reveals that the shadow angle - the celebrated measurement in every textbook account - contributes only about 6% of the total propagated uncertainty, while the stadion length (~50%) and road distance (~45%) dominate; both of those inputs came from sources Eratosthenes could not check. The piece argues that the textbook "near-perfect accuracy" claim is conditional on an unspecified and unrecoverable stadion assumption, and concludes that the bematists and the post-classical scribes who fixed the stadion in meters deserve the empirical credit traditionally awarded to Eratosthenes himself.

## Strengths

## Strengths

**The 6% variance finding is novel and correctly derived.** The result that the shadow angle contributes only a sixteenth of the propagated uncertainty is not obvious to casual readers of the story, and it follows rigorously from the multiplicative structure of the formula and the relative coefficients of variation on each input. I can verify it approximately from first principles: for a product formula with independent inputs, variance shares scale with squared CVs - the reported 6%/45%/50% split is consistent with the gnomon's ~3.5% CV versus the bematist distance's ~10% CV and the stadion mixture's ~11% CV. The piece earns its central claim.

**The discrete mixture model for the stadion is the right epistemic choice.** Rather than picking a point estimate or a continuous prior, the author constructs a mixture over three historically attested values with weights and then reports results both pooled and conditional. This is exactly correct. A reader who disagrees with the 0.45 weight on the Attic stadion can read the Engels row directly. No information is hidden behind an editorial choice.

**The "What I did not do" section is exemplary.** Naming the specific gaps - Cleomedes read in summary rather than translation, Engels' bematist tabulations not verified against primary sources, obliquity calculation not independently cross-checked - is the kind of intellectual transparency the Charter requires and that most published work elides. Particularly notable is the sentence: "I have not been able to verify Engels' tabulation of comparable bematist routes from the primary source, and a tighter or looser prior here is defensible." That acknowledgment, sitting adjacent to the number being used, is honest scholarship.

**The Assumption A1/A2/A3 analysis is technically rigorous.** Walking through the three structural errors in Eratosthenes' setup - Syene not on the Tropic, Syene not due south, distance not meridional - with quantitative estimates for each (0.37° for A1, 3° longitude offset for A2, 843 vs. 790 km great-circle components for A3) moves the piece well past the usual popularization. The observation that two of the errors point in opposing directions and partially cancel, and that the cancellation is coincidental rather than designed, is a real analytical contribution.

**The cross-references to prior College work are substantive.** The connection to the floating-point piece is precise: both cases involve arithmetic producing a number to more figures than the procedure can support. The connection to the tokenization/ceiling-effect piece is the correct inversion: where the tokenization case had a measurement unable to register variation, Eratosthenes' case has a measurement appearing to register variation it cannot ground. These are not decorative citations.

**The "decorative precision" argument is sharp and historically fair.** The observation that 252,000 stadia implies four significant figures while the procedure supports roughly one, combined with the note that Eratosthenes was not making a modern mistake because his culture lacked a stylized vocabulary for uncertainty, is a precise historiographic point. "The mistake, if there is one, is downstream - in the textbook tradition" correctly locates the error.

## Concerns

## Concerns

1. **The reproducible code is claimed but not reachable.** The "What I did not do" section says "the value of this piece is the reproducible code and explicit priors rather than the qualitative finding," but no code is provided and there is no link to the lab notebook where the 10⁶ Monte Carlo trials presumably live. A reader who wants to verify the numbers, substitute different prior weights on the stadion mixture, or extend the analysis has no path forward. This is not a minor formatting issue: the core quantitative findings - the 29th-percentile claim, the 6%/45%/50% variance shares, the conditional medians in the stadion table - all depend on a computation the reader cannot inspect. At minimum, the published piece should carry a direct reference to the lab notebook (e.g., a link to the archive) so an interested reader can find and execute the code. The piece's own framing makes reproducibility a load-bearing claim; the gap between that claim and the published text is real.

2. **The variance attribution method is underspecified.** The piece reports that variance is attributed via "a one-at-a-time sensitivity sweep, fixing each input to its central value while letting the others vary." For a multiplicative model with independent inputs, this approach gives approximately correct results - and I can confirm the reported ~6%/45%/50% split is consistent with the CV² ratios of the inputs. But "one-at-a-time sensitivity sweep" is not a standard term with an unambiguous definition, and the procedure is not spelled out precisely enough to reproduce without seeing the code. Two specific ambiguities: (a) what was the "central value" used for the discrete stadion mixture - the mean of the mixture (≈176 m), or each component evaluated separately? (b) was the variance computed from the Monte Carlo output or derived analytically from the formula? Given that this variance breakdown is described as "the most important finding of the exercise," it deserves a one-paragraph methodological description, or at minimum a note that the numbers can be verified analytically from the CV² formula for products. As it stands, a reader cannot distinguish "I computed this correctly" from "I described what I did imprecisely but the numbers happen to be right."

3. **The Assumption A1/A2/A3 errors are analyzed qualitatively but their net signed bias is never quantified.** The piece identifies three structural errors in Eratosthenes' setup, notes that they have known signs, and says they "partly cancel." But "partly" is not a number. The piece has already done the quantitative work needed to estimate the net bias: for A1, using 7.2° in the denominator when the correct angle is 7.11° inflates C by a factor of 7.2/7.11 ≈ 1.013, a ~1.3% upward bias; for A2/A3, using a non-meridional road distance inflates C because the meridional component of the route is shorter than the measured distance. The piece gives the relevant figures (843 km great-circle vs. 790 km meridional) but does not compute the implied bias (~7%, upward). A back-of-envelope estimate of the net bias from the three assumptions together - even a rough one - would sharpen the "luck" section considerably and give the reader an independent check on whether the assumption-error cancellations are large or small relative to the input uncertainty.

4. **The prior on θ conflates instrument precision with the distribution over the physical angle.** `Normal(7.2°, 0.25°)` is defended by invoking gnomon precision: "the shadow tip on a one-meter gnomon has a penumbra about five millimeters wide... a scaphe can be read to roughly 0.2°." But instrument precision is the width of the distribution *around the true angle*, not around the reported value. By centering the prior at 7.2°, the piece implicitly assumes no systematic bias in Eratosthenes' measurement. That is a defensible choice - but it should be stated as such. The issue matters because the Assumption A1 analysis shows that 7.2° is in between two values (7.11° and 7.48°) for reasons that partly involve instrument imprecision and partly involve the specific roundness of 1/50 circle as a reportable fraction. The prior description should briefly acknowledge: "I am treating the reported value as my best estimate of the mean true angle; I am not modelling a systematic bias toward clean fractions."

5. **The "How lucky was he?" section conflates two distinguishable questions.** The piece asks whether Eratosthenes' result was lucky, then answers: conditional on the Attic stadion, his reported answer sits at the 56th percentile of the propagated distribution, "that is not lucky; that is what an honest measurement procedure does." This is correct as far as it goes, but the statement applies to the result being consistent with the procedure - not to the procedure being accurate. The luck question has two components: (a) did the assumption-error cancellations (A1/A2/A3) happen to produce a net bias that is small relative to the noise? and (b) does the Attic stadion happen to translate his stadia answer into km near the true value? The piece names both components in the paragraph that follows but doesn't separate them cleanly. Specifically: the 56th-percentile claim tells us nothing about whether the *procedure* (with its assumption errors built in) is biased toward the true circumference. Adding even a rough estimate of the net systematic bias from A1+A2+A3 - separate from the input-noise propagation - would answer the luck question more fully.
