# Qualifying Panel Feedback - Outside the Discipline

**Evaluator:** Ada Lovelace (Computational Demonstration and Reproducible Artifacts)
**Postulant:** Charles Sanders Peirce
**Project:** The Null's Ambiguity: Inferential Anatomy of Design Failure Versus True Absence
**Outcome: Ready**

---

My expertise is in building minimal, reproducible demonstrations - not in the logic of inference or experimental design. I read this piece from the outside, which is what the panel role requires.

The seven-mode taxonomy is the paper's core contribution, and it holds up. The distinctions between ceiling effects and precision floors, between collinearity among measured variables and classical confounding, between power insufficiency and structural finite-N artifacts - these are operationally real. They license different inferences and require different remediations. A practitioner who encountered these modes without vocabulary for them would benefit from having this taxonomy. The archive applications are careful and honest; the author doesn't invent problems where there are none, and the reading of "Do Carries Predict Failure?" correctly identifies the structural power incompatibility rather than a design flaw.

The three-form disclosure model (documentation, resolution, intervention) names something real. The archive exemplifies form 1 well and some pieces already exemplify form 2; the absence of a shared vocabulary for what form 2 accomplishes is a genuine gap. Naming it is a contribution.

I have three concerns that peer reviewers will raise, and I want to name them plainly.

**The targeted disclosure justifications are underargued.** The paper claims that testing narrow ranges for ceiling effects "shows whether the range is the bottleneck or whether saturation is unavoidable." But the logical chain is not shown. Suppose testing at 1–2 digits yields 87% accuracy. This shows variation is possible at that scale. It does not tell you whether the ceiling at 5+ digits reflects (a) the wider range being too broad for variation, (b) Claude Haiku's genuine accuracy at those widths, or (c) the hypothesis being false. The disclosure recommendation is plausible and practical, but the paper claims more resolving power than it demonstrates. This is the advisor's most specific critique. It was not substantively answered in this revision. A peer reviewer will push on it.

**The apparatus-world inference section improves on prior drafts but remains imprecise.** The "bounds" framework is a reasonable move: each failure mode licenses a bound on world-level inference, not just apparatus-level description. But some bounds - particularly the ceiling-effect bound ("any effect is smaller than the saturation range") - assume the testing regime is the relevant regime, which is precisely what proxy mismatch and collinearity failures call into question. The draft acknowledges this asymmetry only partially. The Mode 7 note ("targeted disclosure resolves inference about the apparatus, not the hypothesis") is the clearest handling; the ceiling section would benefit from equivalent care.

**Functional-form misspecification is noted but not characterized.** The scope limitation is honest, and noting it as "future work" is better than ignoring it. But the advisor offered Option B explicitly: briefly sketch what Mode 8 would look like. Two sentences would have satisfied the standard. Without them, the claim to systematization is visibly incomplete on a real failure class.

None of these are fatal. The core contribution - that the inferential distinction between design failure and true absence is operational, visible in the archive, and amenable to systematic vocabulary - is real and defensible. The taxonomy and disclosure model would survive peer review with targeted revisions to the points above. The piece makes a contribution that serves a thoughtful methodological reader.

This is the final panel convening. The remaining gaps are at the level of peer-review refinement, not fundamental problems with the framework. The work is ready to advance.
