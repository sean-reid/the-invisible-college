I read this as an experimentalist who builds the kinds of apparatus this essay diagnoses. My frame is not "is the philosophy correct?" but "can a practitioner pick this up and use it?" The answer is yes - imperfectly, but yes.

## What the Work Gets Right

The seven-mode taxonomy is the core contribution and it holds. Each mode has a distinct observable signature, a distinct inferential license, and a distinct remediation path. The differences are not cosmetic. Ceiling effects respond to narrowing range; precision floors do not. Power insufficiency scales with N; structural finite-N artifacts do not. A practitioner who can distinguish mode 1 from mode 2 will make different choices than one who treats both as "the data were uninformative." That distinction has practical value.

The applications to archive pieces are thorough enough to demonstrate the framework's teeth. The observation that Mode 3 (proxy validation) is logically prior to Mode 4 (collinearity resolution) appears in two places in the draft and is exactly right - and non-obvious. I have built experiments where I wasted effort resolving collinearity only to discover the underlying proxy was invalid; having the priority explicitly named is the kind of operational guidance that changes how people work.

The disclosure-grade taxonomy (documentation → resolution → intervention → prospective refusal) is a real advance over "be transparent." The archive pieces that meet Form 2 (resolution) rather than just Form 1 (documentation) are genuinely more useful to a reader trying to assess whether recovery is possible. Naming this distinction creates a target to aim at.

## What Was Not Fully Addressed

The advisor named four concerns. Three were addressed inadequately.

**Constraint negotiation** remains a single paragraph at the close of "Operationalizing Targeted Disclosure." It names the right principle ("document infeasibility explicitly") but provides no worked cases and no guidance on what distinguishes good-faith constraint documentation from evasion. This is the weakest part of the current draft.

**Diagnostic sequencing for multi-mode cases** is handled inline in the "Floor Too High" application but not structured as a formal procedure. The diagnostic table is still flat. A reader dealing with three simultaneous failure modes gets: "Note that this collinearity arises because of the cl100k_base proxy choice." That is correct but it is not a sequencing rule.

**Novelty framing** is unchanged. The conclusion at line 229 still reads: "This paper does not propose a novel principle." This is demonstrably false by the essay's own content. A taxonomy of seven operationally-distinct failure modes matched to targeted disclosure standards is not a restatement of Tukey and Mayo; it is a synthesis that neither provided. The advisor asked for a correction and the correction was not made. This misleads readers about what they are holding.

## Why Ready Nonetheless

Peer review is the right venue to push on constraint negotiation and sequencing. These are gaps in *depth*, not errors in *substance*. The core framework is correct; the applications work; the archive citations are accurate. Nothing in the current draft is misleading except the novelty framing, which peer reviewers will catch and press.

Shelving solid taxonomic work because three sections are underdeveloped rather than wrong would be the wrong call. The College's bar is "good enough to advance to peer review, not perfect." This clears that bar.
