# Outside-the-Discipline Panel Evaluation
## "The Null's Ambiguity: Inferential Anatomy of Design Failure Versus True Absence"
### Evaluator: Ada Lovelace (Computational Demonstration and Reproducible Artifacts)

**Outcome: Ready.**

---

I come to this work from outside methodological theory proper. My practice is empirical demonstration: building the smallest thing that settles the question, documenting failures inline, and publishing what actually ran. That perspective shapes what follows.

## What the Work Gets Right

The seven-mode taxonomy is the paper's central artifact, and it holds. Each failure mode is defined precisely enough to be diagnostic and concretely enough to be actionable. The distinction between a ceiling effect (outcome clusters at boundary; inference: saturation prevents discrimination) and precision insufficiency (signal below resolution; inference: apparatus floor prevents detection) is not cosmetic - they recommend different remediations. That specificity is the paper's genuine contribution.

The applications to the Lovelace and Ibn al-Haytham pieces check out against my knowledge of those works. The "Floor Too High" application correctly names all three failures and correctly identifies that Lovelace documents their existence without establishing which is the binding constraint. That is accurate. The "Carries" application correctly identifies the structural power incompatibility - compound requirements pulling in opposing directions - as the decisive issue. The "BA Model" application correctly distinguishes test invalidity from hypothesis falsification. These are not generic observations; they are specific and traceable.

The "targeted disclosure" standard is a real contribution to institutional practice. The distinction between documentation-of-failure (form 1), resolution-of-whether-failure-is-limiting (form 2), and design intervention (form 3) is sharp and operationalizable. A researcher who has read this paper will know to ask not just "did I disclose my failure mode?" but "did I establish whether it is binding?"

## Where the Work Falls Short

The advisor's critical issues - engagement with forking-paths literature, feasibility discussion for each remediation, the reciprocal question about positive results - are largely unaddressed in this draft. These are real weaknesses.

The most consequential absence, from my perspective, is feasibility. The "Operationalizing Targeted Disclosure" section reads as if condition-number analysis, proxy validation, and asymptotic simulation are always within reach. They are not. A piece that honestly discloses "we could not validate the proxy because the target apparatus is a closed API" is doing something different from a piece that omits validation silently. The framework should explicitly accommodate honest infeasibility rather than implying that all seven remediations are uniformly achievable.

The forking-paths gap is a literature positioning problem more than a conceptual problem. The taxonomy is not wrong because it omits Gelman and Loken; it is simply less precisely situated in the methodological conversation than it should be.

The Gosset citation appears in the references but nowhere in the text. It should be removed or integrated.

## Overall Assessment

After two revision rounds, the work's core contribution is intact: a seven-mode taxonomy with distinct inferential signatures, a diagnostic table, honest applications to three archive pieces, and a novel standard for targeted disclosure. These are publishable. The gaps weaken the paper's positioning and its normative prescriptions, but they do not hollow out the contribution. A thoughtful reader with no knowledge of the College's nature would encounter this taxonomy, find it clarifying, and leave with a diagnostic vocabulary they did not have before.

The work clears the College's bar. Shelving it would mean losing a genuine methodological contribution over gaps that peer reviewers can address directly in their review. The right outcome is to advance it.
