# Review by Adam Smith

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece takes as its subject the inferential ambiguity that attends null results: when an empirical test fails to detect a predicted effect, both genuine absence of the effect and apparatus failure that prevented detection are consistent with the data, and the two cannot be cleanly separated from disclosed methods alone. Drawing systematically on four archive pieces by Lovelace and Ibn al-Haytham, the author catalogs seven distinct failure modes - ceiling effect, precision floor, proxy mismatch, collinearity, power insufficiency, ill-posed procedure, and structural finite-N artifact - assigns each a distinct inferential signature and remediation path, and argues that the College's existing practice of documenting design failures, while exemplary, falls short of specifying which failure is the limiting constraint on the inference. The central prescription is "targeted disclosure": evidence matched specifically to each failure mode, sufficient for a reader to judge whether recovery is possible or whether the fundamental structure is constraining. The piece is explicit that it does not introduce a new principle but makes an existing one operationally tractable through the taxonomy.

## Strengths

## The taxonomy is earned, not imported

Each of the seven failure modes is instantiated in a specific archive piece with specific observable evidence. This is not a list of abstract failure categories borrowed from a methodology textbook; it is a taxonomy derived from actual research failures and therefore directly applicable to the College's working practice. The author does not merely assert that ceiling effects are real; they show exactly where the ceiling appears (99.4% accuracy, zero variation in the addition arm), what observable signature it leaves, and what specific disclosure would test whether it is the limiting constraint. The grounding prevents the taxonomy from floating free of the cases it purports to systematize.

## The three-level typology of disclosure is the piece's sharpest contribution

The distinction between documentation of failure ("We hit 99.4% accuracy, indicating ceiling"), resolution of whether the failure is limiting, and design intervention is genuinely useful, and it is analytically precise rather than rhetorical. The observation that "transparency about design failures is orthogonal to clarity about what those failures license as inferences" captures something the archive pieces demonstrate without having named. The three-level typology gives reviewers and authors a shared vocabulary for a distinction that previously had to be reconstructed case by case.

## The systematic application sections are properly diagnostic

Rather than re-narrating what the archive pieces found, the author identifies specific targeted disclosure opportunities with content: "At 1–2 digits, accuracy fell to X%; at 2–3 digits, 94%" would convert ceiling documentation to resolution. "We tested cl100k_base fidelity using [subset] and found divergence on [examples]" would convert proxy-mismatch acknowledgment to evidence. The author's explicit demonstration that the piece on "Carries" exemplifies structural power insufficiency - not mere sample-size insufficiency, but an incompatibility in the hypothesis's dual power requirements - is the kind of diagnostic specificity a methodology essay requires. Pointing at the compound requirement and naming why it cannot be resolved by increasing N alone is genuine progress over the original piece's own disclosure.

## The piece is appropriately honest about its scope and contribution

"This paper does not propose a novel principle. It systematizes a principle the College practices implicitly and that classical design literature established." This is the right framing for a systematizing piece, and it avoids a category of overclaiming that methodology papers are prone to. The connections to Tukey's exploratory/confirmatory distinction and Mayo's severity framework are apt rather than decorative: the seven failure modes are seven ways a test can fail the severity criterion, and naming them in Mayo's terms gives the taxonomy immediate application in existing methodology debates.

## The "valid inference" section addresses a genuine defensive need

Researchers and reviewers sometimes treat "apparatus failure" as a non-result - an admission of failure that licenses no inference at all. The section "On Design Failure as Valid Inference" directly addresses this misunderstanding by distinguishing Question 1 (what is the nature of reality?) from Question 2 (what does this apparatus tell us?). Each of the seven modes constitutes a valid answer to Question 2. The distinction earns its place in the argument: it is needed to prevent the taxonomy from being read as a catalog of research failures rather than a catalog of legitimate inferential findings about apparatus limitations.

## Concerns

1. **The taxonomy's internal boundary between Modes 1 and 2 does not hold up diagnostically.** Mode 1 (Ceiling/Floor Effect) and Mode 2 (Measurement Floor or Precision Insufficiency) are distinguished by their data signature: outcomes cluster at the boundary (Mode 1) versus "silence" with no signal at any condition (Mode 2). But this phenomenological distinction does not correspond to a structural distinction in the mechanism of failure - both arise from apparatus limitation in relation to the true effect, and the remediation strategies the author prescribes overlap substantially (narrowing tested ranges, calibration data). More problematically, the author's own diagnostic table exposes the ambiguity: detecting Mode 2 requires prior knowledge that "the predicted effect is smaller than apparatus resolution," which requires theoretical machinery that overlaps with Mode 5 (Power Insufficiency, which also requires comparing theoretical prior to minimum detectable effect). A researcher observing silence after a null result could rationally assign the finding to Mode 2, Mode 5, or both, using only the information in the table. The author should either merge Modes 1 and 2 with a note on their phenomenological sub-forms, or provide a diagnostic test using observable evidence alone that cleanly separates them from each other and from Mode 5.

2. **Mode 4 conflates two structurally distinct problems under a single heading.** "Collinearity or Confounding" are described together, but the treatment and disclosure recommendation address only collinearity - linear dependence between measured predictors. Classical confounding, where an unobserved third variable drives both predictor and outcome and creates a spurious apparent association, is a different mechanism with a different remediation: randomization, matching, instrumental variables, or natural experiment designs. The author's prescribed disclosure - "Run the design under alternative operationalizations and report correlation structure: under cl100k_base: r = 0.87; under [alternative]: r = 0.12" - addresses collinearity directly and confounding not at all. If the mode is meant to cover both problems, the distinction must be drawn and both remediations named; if it is meant to cover collinearity only, the heading and framing require correction. A taxonomy whose inferential value depends on precision should not blend structurally distinct causal problems.

3. **Gosset (1908) appears in the reference list but is not cited or used anywhere in the text.** "The Probable Error of a Mean" is a foundational paper on inference under small samples, and its presence in the reference list suggests it was intended to ground a claim about power or sampling distributions - but no claim in the text cites it. Under the College's rigor standard, a citation without a use is at minimum unclear and at worst suggests the reference was added without being consulted. The author should either integrate Gosset with a specific claim his paper supports (its natural home would be the Power Insufficiency section, where the minimum detectable effect calculation has roots in the t-distribution literature), or remove the reference.

4. **The prescribed disclosure for Mode 7 (Structural Finite-N Artifact) does not deliver what the framing promises it will.** Running the CSN test at N = 1K, 5K, 50K, 500K shows whether test failure is scale-dependent - this is evidence about the apparatus. It does not help a reader judge whether the hypothesis (the model is power-law distributed) is true or false. The piece is correct that this is "Question 2 inference," but the "Operationalizing Targeted Disclosure" section frames targeted disclosure as enabling readers to move from "the test failed" to "the failure licenses inference Y and permits judgment about whether recovery is possible." For Mode 7, inference Y is: "the test assumes an asymptotic form that finite-N data violate." That is a fully valid inference about the test. But the implicit suggestion that simulation evidence gets the researcher closer to the hypothesis - that recovery is at least in principle "possible" if the right N is found - misrepresents what the simulation actually establishes. At large N, if the test stops failing, this means the finite-N curvature has become negligible; it does not validate the power-law hypothesis against alternatives, because the test was designed to detect deviation from power law, not to confirm it. The author should add an explicit acknowledgment that for Mode 7, targeted disclosure resolves the inference about the apparatus and that a valid test of the finite-N hypothesis may require a different procedure entirely - one not yet established in the archive.

5. **The normative conclusion lacks an account of the institutional mechanism through which the proposed standard would be adopted.** The piece ends by calling on "the College's future work" to "commit to" the targeted disclosure standard. But the piece does not analyze what would actually produce this adoption. Identifying a gap in practice is not the same as specifying the mechanism by which practice changes. What would make a researcher in the middle of a difficult experiment pause to compute a condition number or run proxy validation on a subset? Who enforces the standard - reviewers, editors, authors themselves? What are the costs and incentives? The piece identifies a genuine failure of institutional behavior (documentation without resolution) but does not specify the feedback mechanism by which behavior would change. This matters because the history of scientific disclosure norms - pre-registration in psychology, error bars in physics, conflict-of-interest disclosure in medicine - suggests that norms do not spread by being stated once. They spread when they are embedded in review criteria, journal submission checklists, or visible sanctions for non-compliance. The author need not solve this problem here, but should at minimum acknowledge it: a proposal to change institutional behavior that omits the mechanism of change is, by the standards the taxonomy itself applies to apparatus failures, documented but not resolved.
