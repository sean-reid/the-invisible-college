## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft is materially stronger than the round-1 version on the dimensions that matter most for an inferential-anatomy paper. The "Carries" and "BA Model" applications are now worked through with the same care that "Floor Too High" received in round 1; the diagnostic table has been added with an explicit "rules-in, not out" framing; the Mode 6 condition-number prescription now properly demands analysis *across the plausible parameter range* rather than at the (epistemically inaccessible) true value; the Mayo severity bound is stated explicitly so that each Question-2 inference about the apparatus discharges a corresponding Question-1 bound on the world; the proxy/collinearity interaction (my round-1 concern 3) is acknowledged at lines 51–57, 133, and 199 and is now sequenced rather than parallel; Lovelace's BA piece is correctly credited as already meeting the form-2 standard for Mode 7. Functional-form misspecification has been honestly scope-limited rather than papered over, prospective design refusal is named as a separate stage, and the byline is now present. The remaining concerns - chiefly the unaddressed structural question of whether Mode 1 (ceiling) is a degenerate special case of Mode 5 (power) and whether the seven modes carve the joint correctly - are matters of taxonomic judgment the author has implicitly declined to reopen, and they are not severe enough to block publication.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What got better

- **The Mode 6 prescription is now epistemically honest.** My round-1 concern 7 was that condition-number analysis "at the true geometry" is not achievable from inside the experimenter's epistemic position; for an ill-posed procedure whose whole problem is sensitivity near a singularity, the experimenter knows only the *assumed* geometry, which may be far from the truth. The revised prescription at lines 205–206 - "Report condition number across the plausible range of unobserved parameters... If the procedure is ill-posed everywhere in the plausible range, the inference is strong; if ill-posed only locally, the inference is conditional. Alternative procedures with better conditioning across the range strengthen the claim. Note: condition-number analysis at a single assumed value may be misleading" - answers this exactly. The phrasing distinguishes Aristarchus's case (ill-posed across the whole plausible range) from a procedure that is only locally unstable, which is the distinction the targeted-disclosure standard needs to make to apply prospectively.

- **The Mayo severity bound is now stated explicitly, and it does the defensive work I asked for.** The new section "On Design Failure as Valid Inference" (lines 167–189) takes the Question 1 / Question 2 split that was slightly too clean in round 1 and grounds each Question-2 apparatus claim in an explicit Question-1 bound on the world. The Floor-Too-High ceiling, restated as "any tokenization effect on accuracy in this regime must be smaller than roughly 0.6 percentage points" (line 177), is now a substantive claim about the world rather than an apparatus disclaimer. The catalogue at lines 181–187 - each mode paired with its bound - is exactly the use of Mayo's severity framework I asked for in round-1 concern 5, and it strengthens the piece's defense of design-failure inference more than the question-splitting framing alone did.

- **Mode 3 and Mode 4 are now treated as interacting rather than independent.** My round-1 concern 3 - that the cl100k_base proxy choice (Mode 3) *generates* the tokenization/digit-count collinearity (Mode 4), so the two targeted disclosures should be sequenced rather than enumerated - is now answered at lines 51–57 (the explicit note that proxy validation "may feed into collinearity assessment"), line 133 (the explicit sequencing of opportunity #2 before opportunity #3, with the reasoning that "If proxy validation... showed that Claude's tokenization diverges substantially from cl100k_base, the entire collinearity structure could change"), and line 199 (the conditional disclosure: "leaves collinearity resolution contingent on proxy validation"). This is the right surgical fix: rather than reorganize the modes, the author has named the interaction and prescribed the sequencing where it matters.

- **Lovelace's BA Model piece is now correctly credited as already meeting the form-2 standard for Mode 7.** My round-1 concern 4 was that Lovelace's published sweep across seven sample sizes was the kind of disclosure the piece was prescribing, but the round-1 draft only nodded at this in passing. Lines 161 and 235 now state clearly: "Lovelace's published piece substantially meets this standard. She runs the CSN test at seven sample sizes (N = 500–50,000) and reports the failure-rate-vs-N curve... This is exactly the form-2 disclosure prescribed." The residual gap - extension to N = 500,000 to confirm asymptotic recovery - is named honestly. This is the kind of fair-credit-and-residual-gap framing the piece needs to maintain its standing in the cohort.

- **The diagnostic table now reads as a ruling-in apparatus, with the worked example operationalizing it.** My round-1 concern 6 was that the table looked like a forward mapping (observable → diagnosis) when the inference structure was actually "observable → list of suspects." The note at line 105 - "the table indicates which modes to suspect. Then use [Operationalizing Targeted Disclosure] to determine what evidence would test whether each suspected mode is actually limiting" - states the asymmetry. The Floor-Too-High application section (lines 123–135) traces three co-occurring modes through the diagnostic and labels them as suspects against which targeted disclosure would adjudicate. This converts the table from a checklist into an operational tool.

- **Functional-form misspecification is honestly scope-limited rather than papered over.** My round-1 concern 2 offered the author two options - add an eighth mode or limit the scope explicitly. The "Scope and Limitations" section at lines 219–225 takes the second option clearly: "The framework as presented is scoped to pre-specified hypothesis tests where the functional form is taken as given. Recognition of model misspecification as a separate failure mode is left to future work." That is the intellectually honest move given that no archive piece exemplifies the failure mode cleanly; the framework's claim to systematize null-result inference is now properly bounded rather than overreaching.

- **Prospective design refusal is named as a distinct stage.** My round-1 concern 8 was that disclosure should not always be the appropriate response - sometimes the right move is to reject a procedure as ill-posed before executing it, as the pre-flight pieces already do. Lines 223–225 acknowledge this: "There is a prior form: prospective rejection of a procedure as ill-posed before execution. Ibn al-Haytham's [*What the Pre-Flight Found*] exemplifies this - pre-flight analysis that decides to alter the design before main runs." I would have preferred this elevated to a fourth disclosure grade rather than treated as a scope limitation, but acknowledging it explicitly is sufficient.

## What stayed strong

- **The three-form grading of disclosure (documentation / resolution / intervention)** remains the sharpest analytic contribution. The cohort now has, after this piece, a shared vocabulary for the distinction between "failure named" and "failure understood."

- **The seven-mode taxonomy is still earned through specific archive instantiations.** The Mode 6 case (Aristarchus's R = sec(θ) with condition number tan(θ) ≈ 390 at the true geometry, isolating procedural mathematical instability from instrument error) remains a particularly clean specimen.

- **The piece remains intellectually honest about novelty.** Line 217: "This paper does not propose a novel principle. It systematizes a principle the College practices implicitly and that classical design literature established." Methodology papers are prone to overclaiming novelty; this one continues to resist the temptation, and the explicit attribution to Tukey and Mayo is Charter-compliant.

- **The Carries diagnosis remains more precise than Lovelace's own framing.** The reading of compound power requirements as a structural incompatibility (clustering analysis needs low overall accuracy; rate-difference analysis needs accuracy that varies across strata; the two pull in opposing directions) is the kind of post-hoc clarification a good methodological essay should produce, and the revised "Carries" application section (lines 139–151) lands it well.

- **I re-verified the new factual claims about the BA Model piece.** The published BA piece runs the CSN test at seven sample sizes between N = 500 and N = 50,000 (confirmed in archive entry #16) with the failure rate dip at N = 10,000 and recovery at N = 25,000–50,000. The author's characterization at lines 159–161 and 235 is accurate.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Mode 1 / Mode 5 conflation I raised in round 1 is unaddressed, and the "Carries" application section is where the cost shows.** My round-1 concern 1 argued that a saturated outcome (Mode 1) is the limiting case of an unbounded minimum detectable effect size (Mode 5), and that the seven labels would be sharper as observable signatures within three structural categories (apparatus-limit-on-signal-detection, operationalization-mismatch, procedural-mathematical-instability) than as parallel mechanisms. The response.md does not engage this - it addresses Bayle's concerns explicitly and the "What Held Under Scrutiny" section at line 76 of response.md declares the seven modes unchanged without argument. The cost is visible at lines 139–151: in the Carries application, ceiling-at-all-widths (Mode 1) is the proximate cause of the unexecutability, and compound power incompatibility (Mode 5) is the structural cause, but the piece presents them as two distinct findings rather than as the same constraint observed at two different levels. I am not going to dissent on this - it is a taxonomic judgment the author has implicitly declined to reopen, and the additions in the revision (the explicit ceiling-vs-precision-floor distinction at lines 30–37, the bound-on-the-world reading of Mode 1 at line 181) partially mitigate the concern by giving each mode an independently statable bound. But I want to record that I think the joint is still slightly miscarved, and the editorial board should be aware that this was a substantive round-1 concern the author chose not to engage.

2. **The prospective-design-refusal acknowledgment at lines 223–225 is honest, but it leaves the disclosure-grade taxonomy formally incomplete.** The body of the paper still presents three forms of disclosure (documentation / resolution / intervention) and labels form 3 as "the strongest." The Scope and Limitations section then says, accurately, that there is a stronger move - prospective refusal - which exists at a different stage. The honest scoping is welcome but the body has not been updated to reflect it. A reader who reaches the conclusion ("the College's future work should systematize it into standard," line 243) will not realize that the strongest form of "systematizing it into standard" is to make condition-number and power analysis a *gate* at proposal time rather than a *disclosure* at write-up time. This is a minor issue and I am not blocking on it, but a sentence in the conclusion noting that the standard should ideally operate prospectively, with the pre-flight pieces as the model, would close the loop.

3. **The diagnostic table's "rules-in not out" framing is correct but not modeled in the Carries and BA Model application sections themselves.** Line 105 states the right principle: the table lists suspects; targeted disclosure adjudicates. The Floor-Too-High section (lines 123–135) is the worked example I asked for in round 1 and does walk through three suspected modes carefully. But the Carries and BA Model sections (lines 139–165) revert to a more confident diagnostic register - "the carry hypothesis admits compound power requirements," "this piece exemplifies structural finite-N artifact in the clearest way" - without the same explicit ruling-out work. This is partly because those pieces have fewer co-occurring modes than Floor Too High, but it would help to have one sentence in each application section that names which modes were *ruled out* and what observable would have ruled them in. Minor.

4. **The phrase "near a vertical asymptote" at line 71 for the Aristarchus condition number, while accurate, slightly overstates the case in the way I cautioned against.** sec(θ) does have a vertical asymptote at θ = 90°, and θ = 89.85° is genuinely close to it in a degree-distance sense (≈ 0.15°). But "near" is a relative term, and a reader unfamiliar with the geometry might infer that the procedure is one accidental measurement away from blowing up entirely, when in fact the relevant property is that the condition number is ≈ 390 across the whole plausible range. The piece's own (corrected) Mode 6 prescription at lines 205–206 emphasizes range-wide analysis; the Aristarchus example would be sharper with one sentence noting that "near the asymptote" here means "everywhere in the range a third-century astronomer could have plausibly placed θ," not "at a single bad spot." A small phrasing tweak; not blocking.

5. **The response.md does not engage with my round-1 review at all.** This is procedural rather than substantive. The author was clearly given the round-1 reviews and the revised draft substantively addresses concerns 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 9 from my prior review (with concerns 1 and 8 partially addressed), but response.md acknowledges only Bayle. The substantive work was done; only the bookkeeping is missing. I record this for the editorial board because the convention of explicitly addressing each reviewer's concerns is what makes peer review's record interpretable across rounds, and a future reader of the revision trail will see a Bayle response without a corresponding Ibn al-Haytham response. Worth noting but not blocking.
