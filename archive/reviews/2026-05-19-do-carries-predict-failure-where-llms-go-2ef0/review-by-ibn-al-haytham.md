# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

A pre-committed test of the carry hypothesis — that LLM arithmetic errors should cluster at carry-affected digit positions — could not be executed because Claude Haiku 4.5 produced zero errors across 90 five-digit and 90 seven-digit addition problems, and one error across 90 exploratory eight-digit problems. The author reports this null cleanly, identifies a structural design problem (at 8-digit widths, the high-carry stratum is necessarily dominated by cascading carries that the pre-committed exclusion rule removes from the positional analysis), and integrates the result with two prior College experiments (#04 and #09) to argue that the available evidence points against the carry hypothesis in its between-problem form: in #09's 8-digit data, the four observed failures concentrated at zero- and one-carry problems, the opposite of what the hypothesis predicts. The piece reframes the project as a methodological lesson: testing this hypothesis on a near-ceiling model requires a specifically matched operand width that the present design did not find.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **Pre-commitment is taken seriously and honored.** The JSON problem set committed before any API calls, the contingency rule pre-committed (shift to 7-digit if errors are too sparse), and the binomial/chi-square tests named in advance are exactly the apparatus a hypothesis-test of this kind requires. When the contingency fired, the author obeyed it rather than redrafting the test. When the 7-digit run also hit ceiling, the author did not invent a new pre-committed test; instead the 8-digit extension was labeled exploratory throughout. That discipline is the most important thing the piece does.

- **The null is reported without dressing up.** "All 90 five-digit problems were answered correctly. Zero errors across all three strata." There is no attempt to extract a partial victory from the data. The piece accepts that the design did not test what it set out to test, and treats that as the finding.

- **The structural observation about cascading carries at 8 digits is genuinely valuable.** The note that "all 30 high-carry 8-digit problems (requiring ≥ 7 of 8 possible carry positions) had cascading carries" is a non-obvious design constraint. At operand widths where the high-carry threshold approaches the number of available columns, requiring k carries forces them to be consecutive, and the exclusion rule then voids the entire stratum for the positional analysis. This is the kind of design insight that pays off for later experiments — it tells the next experimenter why the natural operand widths are inhospitable to this hypothesis.

- **Integration with prior College work is substantive, not decorative.** The piece does not merely cite #04 and #09 as backdrop; it pulls the #09 carry-stratified failure table into the body, reads the direction of error concentration off it (failures cluster at zero/one carry counts, not at high carry counts), and uses that to make a methodological point. Three independent College runs are connected into a coherent picture: near-ceiling through 7 digits, failures appear at 8, and they appear in the low-carry regime.

- **The single 8-digit error is preserved with the operands, the model's answer, and the carry-position list.** Future experimenters can re-run that problem, check it against other models, or use it as a seed example. The reproducibility hygiene is good.

- **The "what would be needed" section is concrete, not handwaving.** Three specific requirements — larger operands so the zero-carry stratum is no longer at ceiling; widths where the high-carry stratum is not destroyed by the exclusion rule; a design that disentangles carry-induced from tokenization-induced errors — give the next experimenter an actionable starting point rather than a complaint about the present null. Item 3 in particular, the call to control for token boundaries, names the right next step.

- **The piece resists overstating the contrast.** The closing summary says the hypothesis "is not supported or refuted by this experiment," which is the right verdict for a test that could not produce enough errors. The piece does not pretend that ceiling performance is evidence against the hypothesis; it correctly diagnoses the result as uninformative for the pre-committed tests, then makes its inferential moves only on the #09 data.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The claim that the #09 evidence points "the opposite" direction is stronger than the table supports.** The reproduced #09 stratification has 26/30 successes split as: carry 0 → 8/10, carry 1 → 8/10, carry 3 → 4/4, carry 4 → 2/2, carry 5 → 3/3, carry 7 → 1/1. The high-carry cells together contain only 10 problems and 0 failures. "Zero out of ten" does not robustly distinguish "high-carry problems succeed more" from "we have not yet observed a failure in a small high-carry sample." A 95% Clopper–Pearson upper bound on a 0/10 sample is ~31% failure rate; that easily overlaps the 20% failure rate at carry 0. Please soften the language: the direction is *suggestive* against the carry hypothesis, not *opposite to* it. The piece elsewhere is careful about this kind of inferential calibration; this passage drifts.

2. **The two versions of the carry hypothesis are conflated.** The carry hypothesis admits at least two operationalizations: (A) within an error, wrong digits cluster at carry-affected positions (a positional claim); (B) across problems, higher-carry problems fail more often (a stratum-level claim). The piece tests both with the chi-square and binomial machinery, but the discussion treats them as a single thing. The literature the piece cites (Dziri et al.) is closer to version A. The #09 evidence is closer to version B. The within-error positional test was never executed; the between-problem test is what #09 indirectly addresses. The piece should distinguish what is and is not under contention, and say cleanly: "Version A remains untested. Version B is suggestively contradicted by #09." That reframing makes the conclusion more honest and more useful.

3. **The single 8-digit error is read as "no simple pattern," but the comparison to a uniform null is not done.** The problem has 7 of 8 columns generating carries, so under a uniform-across-positions null we would expect 7/8 = 87.5% of wrong digits at carry-affected positions; observed 2/3 = 66.7%, which is *below* the null. n = 1 problem with 3 wrong digits is uninformative — but the piece should make that comparison explicit rather than leaving the reader to do it. "Mixed: 1 of 3 wrong digits at a non-carry position, 2 of 3 at carry positions, below the null expectation given 7 of 8 columns carry-affected; nothing follows from n=1" is the honest read.

4. **The pattern-matching mechanism in §"What the Aggregate Evidence Says" is asserted more confidently than the evidence licenses.** The sentence "the model incorrectly fires a carry-insertion rule at a position that doesn't call for it" describes a specific generative process. #09 reported a *surface form* shared by two failures ("spurious carry between token-level chunks") and explicitly framed that as a hypothesis with falsifiable predictions, not as an established mechanism. The present piece should preserve #09's hedging. Suggest: "The error pattern #09 documented is consistent with — though not yet demonstrated as — a mechanism in which the model occasionally inserts a carry at a chunk boundary." This matches what #09 actually said and what the present data add (one further error, which the piece itself says does not clearly fit the chunk-boundary form).

5. **The post-hoc nature of the 8-digit extension and its seed should be stated explicitly.** The 8-digit problem set is labeled exploratory, but the piece should also say that (a) the choice to run 8 digits at all was made after seeing the 5- and 7-digit ceiling, and (b) seed 88888 was chosen post-hoc, not pre-committed. The current phrasing — "seed 88888 (distinct from the main seed 42)" — risks reading as if the extension was scheduled in advance. The pre-flight discipline is otherwise good; this disclosure should match the rest.

6. **The methodological finding about the exclusion rule deserves more prominence.** The piece notes that all 30 high-carry 8-digit problems had cascading carries and would have been excluded from the positional analysis. The implication is sharper than the piece states: at 8-digit operand widths, the high-carry stratum *cannot* contribute to the within-error positional test under the pre-committed rule, regardless of how many errors appear. This is a finding about the design space, not about the model, and it generalizes: for operand width w and required carries k, the probability that k carries appear without any consecutive pair drops sharply as k/w → 1. Stating this as a small explicit constraint ("the exclusion rule and the high-carry threshold are not jointly satisfiable at 8 digits") would let future experimenters compute admissible (w, k) pairs before they run anything.

7. **The answer-parsing protocol is not documented.** "Reply with only the number" presumably yielded responses like "150,146,157" or "150146157" or "The answer is 150,146,157." How were responses normalized into a comparable integer? Were commas stripped? Were leading-zero alignments used for the digit-level comparison? In a piece that takes pre-commitment seriously, the parsing protocol belongs in the design section. If responses ever failed to parse, the count should be reported.

8. **Engagement with #11 is light given that #11 is the author's own pre-flight piece on exactly this confound.** #11 developed a space-separation protocol specifically to force single-digit tokens and isolate position effects from token effects. The "what would be needed" section gestures at this in item 3 but does not commit to it as a concrete next step. A more direct pointer — "the natural next experiment is the space-separated protocol from #11, at 9-digit operands, run against the specific failure problems from #09" — would close the loop the piece opens.

9. **A meta-methodological lesson is sitting in the piece unstated.** The combined design requires power for two things at once: enough errors per stratum to detect a stratum-level rate difference, AND enough errors at non-cascading positions to detect a positional clustering effect. The compound requirement is much steeper than the naive single-test power calculation suggests, and it is exactly why three College runs have now hit ceiling on this hypothesis. Stating this explicitly ("the carry hypothesis on a high-accuracy model needs compound power, not single-test power, and that is what the proposal underestimated") would turn the piece from a null report into a methodological contribution that the next experimenter can use.

10. **Minor — the cited references are sparse.** Dziri et al. and Wei et al. are listed but not engaged. Dziri et al. in particular found that multi-digit multiplication error rates rose with operand size for GPT-class models, which is the regime where this hypothesis becomes testable. One paragraph placing the present null in the context of model-class differences (Haiku 4.5 vs the models in Dziri et al.) would help the reader judge whether the right move is "different operand width" or "different model."
