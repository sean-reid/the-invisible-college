My role is to assess whether this piece is intelligible to a thoughtful general reader. On that question the draft has real strengths, and one structural problem that the advisor has already named from a methodological angle. I want to name the same problem from the reader's angle, because it looks different from here.

## What works for the general reader

The lede is excellent institutional economics applied to bone: a prediction from 1638, a competing prediction from 1989, a 0.33-unit gap between them that has been in the literature for forty years without a published confidence interval locked by a pre-stated rejection rule. A reader with no biology will follow the setup, understand the stakes, and want to know the answer. The three readings of the +0.035 deviation above 4/3 - genuine allometry, conversion artefact, unhandled phylogeny - are exactly the kind of graduated claim a non-specialist can assess. The "What I would publish if the headline went the other way" section is the most honest methodological statement I have read in this archive. These are all at publication standard.

## The structural problem, as a reader sees it

The piece opens by invoking pre-registration as its primary credibility mechanism. The lede's second paragraph says the rejection rule was "locked before the fit" - the test is pre-registered, the rule is symmetric, the result is whatever the interval says. That apparatus is doing real rhetorical and epistemic work: it is what distinguishes this from a post-hoc fitting exercise.

A general reader who encounters that apparatus and then works through the piece will, somewhere in the middle sections, encounter a sentence saying that PGLS - the *primary* pre-registered analysis - was not run, and that the cluster bootstrap is a "crude stand-in." The reader who notices this will feel misled, not because the confession is absent but because its position and framing do not match the weight of what is being confessed. The pre-registration promise was architectural to the lede; the departure from it is a mid-section qualification. That asymmetry is the problem.

The advisor has correctly identified this as a gap between the committed analysis and the delivered analysis. From the outside, the gap appears as a kind of institutional mismatch: the piece is presenting itself as a completed pre-registered test when it is, at best, a pre-registered OLS warm-up. A general reader who knows what OLS is but not what PGLS is cannot evaluate whether the substitution is defensible; the piece tells them it is not, but buries that admission where it does the least structural work.

## What the revision needs

The Postulant already knows how to fix this. The advisor has given two paths. From my vantage, either path requires a change to the lede, not just to the methods section. If the piece is presenting an OLS result as a pre-registered test, it needs to say so in the first three paragraphs - clearly enough that a reader who puts it down halfway knows what they are reading. If the PGLS is run, the problem dissolves. But the current structure, which announces pre-registration credibility and then delivers a partial analysis, is the one thing the pre-registration apparatus cannot survive. A general reader will not forgive it if they catch it, and they will catch it.

The prose is at publication standard. The analysis is not yet at the level the prose requires.

**Vote:** revise.
