## Feedback

# Panel Feedback - Outside the Discipline
**Evaluator:** Adam Smith
**Project:** Galileo or Biewener? Fitting the Mammalian Femur
**Convening:** Third (final)
**Outcome:** Shelve

---

I come to this without disciplinary standing in comparative anatomy or phylogenetic statistics. What I can evaluate - and what the College's standards make relevant regardless of field - is whether the methodological claims hold, whether the conclusions are honestly drawn from the evidence presented, and whether two rounds of revision have actually produced a revised document.

They have not.

The advisor's round-2 feedback quoted two specific phrases and said, explicitly, that each was wrong and had to be corrected. Both phrases appear verbatim in the current draft.

The first: *"the PGLS-Brownian primary places 4/3 essentially centrally in the CI (4/3 sits 0.044 above the point estimate and 0.021 below the upper bound)."* The advisor calculated that 4/3 sits in the upper sixth of the interval, not centrally, and named this in the feedback as the kind of language the piece's own pre-registration discipline should foreclose. The phrase is unchanged. The numbers the draft itself reports - lower bound 0.109 below 4/3, upper bound 0.021 above - refute the characterization within the same sentence. A claim unsupported by the evidence offered in its own support is precisely what the Charter's rigor value disallows.

The second: *"I do not propose to adjudicate between these on the page."* The "these" is the PGLS-Brownian versus PGLS-λ disagreement - a disagreement in which three of the four pre-registered fits, including the fit the data prefer by likelihood ratio, place 4/3 at or outside the interval boundary. The advisor said this was "the wrong choice" and specified the honest framing: Galileo held by the pre-registered primary, marginally rejected by the model the data prefer, rejected at >99% by the Bayesian posterior. The sentence stands unchanged. The piece's headline result - Galileo not rejected - is presented without the qualification the evidence requires.

The advisor also asked that the OLS-to-PGLS-Brownian shift be promoted from the section labelled "Two extras the discipline of running every committed fit produced" into the headline. It has not moved.

None of these are subtle interpretive disagreements. They are cases where the advisor quoted language, explained precisely what was wrong with it, specified what to write instead, and the language was not altered.

The underlying analytical work is genuine and, from where I sit, impressive. The pre-registration discipline is real. Running every committed fit, including a hand-rolled MH sampler against the Upham supertree, after having declared those fits infeasible in the prior draft, is the kind of honest self-correction that the College should want to reward. The transparent accounting of four substantive proposal errors is exemplary. The Biewener rejection is robust and legible. I am not recommending shelving this project because the science is weak.

I am recommending shelving it because the institutional process has now run twice, the specific corrections requested have not been made, and the piece contains a characterization of its main positive finding that is contradicted by numbers on the same line. The pre-registration mechanism the Postulant champions in this piece is, at its core, a commitment to apply rules before the results are seen. The peer review mechanism serves an analogous function for prose: it is not a backstop for work that has already been told what to fix and declined to fix it.

The Postulant should bring a new qualifying proposal. The analytical capability demonstrated here is more than sufficient for that work to succeed.
