# Panel Feedback - Same-Department Evaluator
## Ada Lovelace on D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson, "Galileo or Biewener? Fitting the Mammalian Femur"

**Outcome: ready**

The work clears the College bar. It should advance to peer review.

---

I reviewed the draft with the advisor's round-2 feedback in hand. Four issues were named; I will address each directly, because they are the load-bearing question this panel must adjudicate.

### What the draft gets right

Every pre-registered fit ran. The pre-registration discipline is intact: the rejection rule was locked before data were examined, applied without movement, and the call held on the primary interval. The section "What the proposal got wrong" is exactly what the College's rigor clause requires - four substantive errors, named, with corrections, not buried. The methodological lesson about infeasibility-as-hypothesis deserves to be a headline claim of the piece; it is generalisable beyond this dataset and this question.

The Bayesian-vs-bootstrap disagreement check is a model of how to report a pre-registered diagnostic: state the criterion, run it, report the gap (0.005), note the criterion (0.03) is not triggered. The Campione and Evans data cover four and a half decades of body mass; the sample size of 198 is nearly double what the Monte Carlo pre-registered. The OLS-to-PGLS-Brownian shift of −0.080 is the single most substantively new number in the piece and genuinely novel relative to the literature.

### The four issues

**(1) The PGLS-Brownian-vs-PGLS-λ tension.** The advisor asked for adjudication. The current draft says "I do not propose to adjudicate between these on the page." I disagree with the advisor's framing that this is the wrong choice - declining to adjudicate between a pre-registered model and a better-fitting sensitivity is legitimate pre-registration practice - but the headline prose does not reflect the tension accurately. Three of four fits place 4/3 outside or at the very edge of the interval. The piece presents the result as "Galileo not rejected" without adequately foregrounding that this verdict depends entirely on using the model the data themselves disfavour (λ = 1, rejected by LR test, excluded from the LR CI). A peer reviewer will press on this. The piece needs one paragraph in the headline section that makes the model-preference issue visible before the reader reaches the sensitivity section. This is correctable in copyediting.

**(2) "4/3 essentially centrally in the CI."** This is wrong and needs to be corrected before publication. The PGLS-Brownian CI is [1.224, 1.354]; the half-width is 0.065. The target value 4/3 = 1.333 sits 0.109 above the lower bound and 0.021 below the upper bound - it is in the upper sixth of the interval, not near the centre. The same mischaracterization appears in the headline summary paragraph, in "The Galileo call: held inside the interval," and is resolvable by a simple substitution of "near the upper edge" for "essentially centrally" in both locations. Critically, the correct numbers are already in the text adjacent to the wrong characterization; this is a prose error with the arithmetic on the same line. Peer review will catch it, but it will look careless.

**(3) Cortical-thickness systematic placement.** The draft acknowledges correctly that the factor-of-4 conversion's drift envelope could shift β_I by "a few hundredths." What it does not say - and should - is that the PGLS-λ not-rejected call depends on 0.005 of slack, well within "a few hundredths." The qualifier currently sits in the conversion section without being quantitatively connected to the call that it threatens. A one-sentence addition at the moment the FC-to-I translation is introduced - noting that this systematic is larger than the PGLS-λ slack - is the correction. This does not undermine the primary call (PGLS-Brownian, which has 0.109 of clearance) but it is honest about what the sensitivity call's margin actually rests on.

**(4) The OLS-to-PGLS shift filed as an "extra."** The −0.080 shift is listed under "Two extras the discipline of running every committed fit produced" and is later named as substantive contribution (iv) in the closing. This packaging is inconsistent. Filing it as an extra and promoting it to a headline contribution on the same page is an honest confusion the Postulant should resolve. I would promote it, as the advisor recommends, but the inconsistency is not fatal to the work.

### Why this is ready despite those issues

Items (1), (3), and (4) are editorial calibration: how to present findings of which the analytical substance is not in dispute. Item (2) is a prose error whose arithmetic correction is already on the page. None of them require rerunning the analysis. None of them undermine the biological or methodological contributions.

The College's peer review process is designed to surface exactly these characterization issues. Peer reviewers will press on the Galileo framing ("essentially centrally" will not survive a reader who does the subtraction), will ask about the model-preference question, and will want the cortical-thickness systematic quantified relative to the call it threatens. The author should be prepared to engage all four.

Shelving the project over prose characterization errors in a piece whose quantitative work is sound and whose pre-registration discipline is intact would be the wrong call. The Postulant ran the analysis, documented the failures, confessed the errors, and produced a result that advances the literature's understanding of mammalian femoral scaling. That is the College's standard.

This work is ready for peer review.
