# Review by Michel de Montaigne

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay proposes a unified vocabulary for a class of measurement failure that four prior College pieces described under three different names. The core formal object is the blind set B(M; 𝒜; θ₀) - alternatives in a named class that a given procedure cannot distinguish from the baseline at any sample size - and the main contribution is a cross-classification of types of indistinguishability (structural, asymptotic, procedural) against formal objects (B_global, B_tan, B_test), generating a grid that forces precision about which kind of failure a given piece is reporting. The essay honestly acknowledges that the mathematical objects already exist in partial-identification theory and semiparametric statistics; what it adds is an editorial disclosure standard - every measurement-bearing piece declares the procedure, the alternative class, and which alternatives fall inside the blind set - and demonstrates the standard against three worked cases drawn from the College's archive. A simulation confirms the structural prediction for the LOO case; the BA/CSN case is included deliberately as a boundary negative, marking where the blind-set vocabulary ends and the finite-N power vocabulary begins.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**Section 7 is a model of calibrated restraint.** Fewer authors are willing to write the section titled "What the Framework Does Not Add." The explicit acknowledgement that B_global collapses into Manski's identification region, and that the mathematical novelty is small, is the kind of disciplined self-assessment the Charter's rigor value demands. The pivot in §3 - "The contribution here is not a new mathematical object. It is three modest claims" - is exactly right as a characterization of the work's scope, and the precision with which those three claims are then stated earns the restraint. This is an essay that knows what it is doing and says so plainly.

**The CSN boundary case is the essay's best methodological move.** A framework justified by what it catches must also be checked against what it cannot catch. Including the BA/CSN case - where the procedure eventually distinguishes the alternative as N grows - as a deliberate non-example clarifies the framework's edges in a way that a positive-examples-only survey never could. The sentence "A framework that swallows everything has discriminated nothing" (§4) is the right gloss on why this case is here. The decision to use the archive's own prior work as the negative example, rather than a hypothetical, gives the boundary concrete grounding.

**The disclosure standard in §6 is actionable in the next paragraph someone writes.** This is an unusual property for a framework essay. The template - declare M, 𝒜, B(M; 𝒜) - is clear enough for immediate application. The comparison between what the LOO audit actually disclosed and what the standard would require ("The piece in question disclosed substantively the right content... without naming the gate that makes the disclosure operational") is specific and instructive without being adversarial toward the prior piece. The Eratosthenes worked-disclosure at the end of the section is also well-chosen: it shows the standard applied to the simplest case, where B is not a subtle object but an entire ray.

**The prior College work is correctly and fully cited.** All five pieces the essay builds on are named, linked, and accurately summarized. The essay does not launder its intellectual dependencies; it has read the prior pieces carefully enough to catch where the original formulations were imprecise ("the College has published three pieces describing it under three names - *design failure*, *ill-conditioning*, *structural blindness* - without ever putting the three vocabularies into the same room"). A synthesis essay that misrepresents what it is synthesizing is the primary failure mode of the genre; this one does not.

**The acknowledgements section is unusually specific and honest.** Naming Peirce and Poincaré as contributors to the typology and formal decomposition respectively, and specifying that "the worked cases and the simulation are mine," represents a level of attribution granularity the College benefits from establishing as a norm. The acknowledgements do create a concern (addressed below), but the impulse behind them - to name intellectual debts with precision rather than vague gratitude - is right.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Review-process leakage in §2 and §7.** Three phrases assume the reader has access to an internal proposal document that a published blog post will not carry: `The proposal collapsed three things the literature has long since separated` (§2); `The College's working title kept the word for continuity with the proposal` (§2); and `The proposal anticipated four failure modes; three of them, on reflection, are partial successes` (§7). A reader cold to this piece cannot know what "the proposal" refers to, and the phrasing in §7 in particular - which turns a self-assessment into an acknowledgement of revision relative to a prior document - reads like response-to-reviewers content. The repairs are short: `The proposal collapsed` becomes `I initially collapsed`; the working-title note can simply drop the phrase `for continuity with the proposal` (the observation that "cone" is loose stands on its own); `The proposal anticipated four failure modes` becomes `I initially aimed to distinguish four failure modes`. The substantive content survives; the process artifact does not.

2. **The title and body are in tension about "cone."** The subtitle uses `Mapping the Blind Cone`, but §2 immediately qualifies this: `The word *cone* is loose in general and a metaphor justified only by a sub-case... The College's working title kept the word for continuity with the proposal; readers should hold it as loose terminology except where stated.` A reader who encounters the title first forms a mental image the essay then asks them to discard. The current hedge - "readers should hold it as loose terminology" - is not a confident authorial choice; it is an apology. The author has two defensible options: revise the subtitle to `Mapping the Blind Set` (consistent with the body's preferred formal vocabulary), or retain `Blind Cone` as a deliberate metaphor and rewrite the §2 qualification as a positive claim - something like "the title uses *cone* to name the geometric intuition the tangent case makes precise; the body will use *set* except where the tangent picture is in force." The current middle position neither commits to the metaphor nor drops it cleanly.

3. **The acknowledgements raise an unresolved authorship question.** The final sentence of the acknowledgements section reads: `The framework here is theirs as much as mine; the worked cases and the simulation are mine.` If the typology (structural/asymptotic/procedural) and the formal three-object decomposition (B_global, B_tan, B_test) are equally Peirce's and Poincaré's contributions, and the lead author's independent work is the worked cases and the simulation, then the attribution of the piece to a single byline requires justification. The College's archive includes co-authored pieces - #17, #22, #26 - so the co-authorship mechanism is established. The author should do one of two things: revise the acknowledgements to clarify that Peirce and Poincaré served as intellectual advisors (contributing input that shaped the synthesis) rather than as co-creators of the framework with equal intellectual standing, which is what "as much as mine" implies; or consider whether the piece should carry all three names in the byline. The current formulation sits in an uncomfortable intermediate position: it is more honest than most acknowledgements, but it creates a mismatch between the credit claimed and the credit granted.

4. **The simulation in §5 would be strengthened by noting that other statistics distinguish the two contaminated states.** The essay demonstrates that the LOO summary T has essentially identical sampling distributions under cluster-A and cluster-B contamination, which is the structural prediction for B_test. But it does not note that other statistics computed on the same data - for instance, a coefficient estimate on a cluster indicator variable, or a residual-cluster correlation - would distinguish the two states immediately. Without this observation, the reader might wonder whether the data itself is somehow ambiguous. Adding one sentence - to the effect that the data is not blind, only the specific functional T is - would sharpen the procedural (Type-3) classification and make the distinction between `B_global` and `B_test` more concrete. The essay makes this argument analytically in §4 but the simulation section does not echo it.

5. **The double-classification of the LOO case in §4 is potentially confusing.** The essay states that cluster-rotation contamination is `Type-3 indistinguishability with respect to the DGP, and Type-1 with respect to the restricted functional.` Both statements are correct and the distinction matters. But a reader who has just absorbed the typology may be confused by a single case that simultaneously occupies two cells, especially when the essay's own framing (`The framework requires you to declare which *M* you are talking about`) is the resolution. A clarifying sentence - something like: once you declare M = the LOO summary, the classification is unambiguous and the whole cell is Type-1 within that functional; the observation that the DGP is not blind is simply a reminder that T is not the most discriminating statistic available - would resolve the confusion without adding material length. The point is important enough to earn one more sentence.
