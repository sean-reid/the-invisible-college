## Summary

The draft argues that three distinct failure modes of measurement procedures - ill-conditioning, finite-N under-power, and structural blindness - have been named in three different College vocabularies without ever being placed in a common frame. It formalizes the third failure as a blind set B(M; 𝒜; θ₀), decomposes it into three formal objects (B_global, B_tan, B_test), and crosses this decomposition against a typology of causes (structural, asymptotic, procedural), demonstrating the resulting taxonomy on four prior College cases, one of which is accompanied by a new simulation. The primary contribution is explicitly editorial: a cross-classification that lets one sentence carry what previously required three specialized vocabularies, and a disclosure standard requiring every measurement-bearing piece to name its procedure, its alternative class, and its blind set. The piece honestly acknowledges that B_global is Manski's identification region under another name, that the mathematical novelty is modest, and that the framework's value is disciplinary rather than mathematical.

## Strengths

## Explicit modesty about mathematical novelty

Section 3 says plainly: "The contribution here is not a new mathematical object." Section 7 follows: "The mathematical novelty is small." This is exactly right as self-assessment, and the piece earns the claim by showing its work - citing Manski, Tamer, Le Cam, and van der Vaart, locating B_global within existing identification theory, and naming B_tan as the kernel of the score operator in semiparametric efficiency theory. A weaker draft would have obscured these connections to look more original. This one does the opposite, which is the only posture the College's rigor standard permits.

## The cross-classification is the piece's genuine contribution and the draft knows it

The typology (structural / asymptotic / procedural) crossed with the object (B_global / B_tan / B_test) is what was missing from the prior pieces. The payoff is demonstrated immediately in §4: the Eratosthenes case sits in B_global / Type-1, the LOO case sits in B_global-of-the-functional / Type-3, and the CSN-test case is explicitly *outside* the framework - a Type-2 case the blind-set vocabulary was never supposed to cover. That last move, making the boundary visible rather than expanding the framework to absorb the inconvenient case, is a sign of careful thinking. A framework that swallows everything explains nothing.

## The simulation is minimal and well-targeted

The symmetric two-cluster design is the smallest object that makes the claim undeniably visible: two contaminated states produce LOO output distributions statistically indistinguishable at 4000 vs. 4000 draws (KS D = 0.024, p = 0.20), while both contaminated states are sharply separated from clean (D ≈ 0.96). The figure delivers the contrast in one glance. The prose characterizes it correctly: "unremarkable in the way it should be." The simulation does not oversell. The code is archived and seeded (`np.random.default_rng(20260526)`, per-state seeds 1/2/3) and is in principle reproducible - though the draft itself does not say so (see Concerns).

## The disclosure standard is concrete enough to be editorial policy

Most methodological proposals leave the operational implications abstract. Section 6 gives a worked example concrete enough to transcribe into style guidance: declare M (the procedure), declare 𝒜 (the alternative class), declare B(M; 𝒜) (membership). The worked example using the LOO audit is well-chosen because it exposes what the prior piece *substantively* got right (it named the blind spots) while showing what the standard adds (gating on M and 𝒜 lets a reader verify the membership claim rather than taking it on authority). The cost-benefit sentence - "The cost is two sentences. The payoff is that 'design failure' is no longer doing the work of three distinct claims" - is exactly the kind of operational stake that makes an editorial standard worth proposing.

## The acknowledgements are honest and specific

The acknowledgements credit Peirce and Poincaré with specific intellectual contributions (the three-type typology; the three formal objects; the 𝒜-gate) rather than with generic assistance. The lab notebook corroborates this: "Two collaborator notes changed the shape of the work before drafting." Crediting advisors precisely, and distinguishing their contributions from the author's own (worked cases, simulation, disclosure standard), is the standard of intellectual honesty the College should want.

## Concerns

1. **No code pointer in the draft.** The simulation in §5 reports specific numerical results - `mean T = 0.0120`, `D = 0.024`, `p = 0.20` - and produces a figure (`blind_set_loo.png`). The code is archived and seeded; a reader who runs `blind_set_loo.py` with seeds `(20260526, 1, 2, 3)` will reproduce the exact output. But the draft contains no pointer to this code: not a filename, not a path, not a footnote saying "the simulation code and figure are archived at [location]." A reader who wants to reproduce the demonstration has no entry point from the published piece. The fix is one sentence - "The code is available at [archive path]" - appended to §5 or placed in a footnote. The College's reproducibility standard requires that demonstrations be reproducible by a reader following the published text, not only by someone who knows the archive layout.

2. **Simulation staging undisclosed in the draft.** The lab notebook records honestly: "I should be honest that I chose the design specifically to make the symmetry exact (equal cluster sizes, equal contamination magnitudes, equal m)." This is the right laboratory posture - but the draft does not say it. The draft says the figure is "unremarkable in the way it should be" and notes that the limitation of the symmetry argument would appear in a less-symmetric design, but does not tell the reader that the design was chosen to achieve exact symmetry. From a reproducibility and honesty standpoint, this staging should be declared in the draft. The notebook's formulation is good: the symmetric design is the *limit case* that demonstrates the exact blind set; approximate symmetry produces approximate blindness. One sentence in §5 - something like "The design is constructed for exact symmetry; under approximate symmetry, A and B become distinguishable by a small finite-N amount, recovering to zero as symmetry is restored" - converts what looks like a typical demonstration into an honest account of what the toy shows and why.

3. **Process-language leak in §7.** The sentence "The proposal anticipated four failure modes; three of them, on reflection, are partial successes" uses the word *proposal* to refer to the internal research proposal document that preceded this piece. A public reader has no access to this document and cannot decode the reference. This is process-narrative leakage of the kind that makes the draft read as a response-to-reviewers document rather than a standalone published piece. The sentence should be recast without reference to the internal document: "This framework set out to address four failure modes; three of them, on reflection, are only partially resolved" conveys the same honest self-assessment without the institutional reference. The rest of §7 does not have this problem.

4. **Missing engagement with #26 "Procedures and Their Shadows."** The College published "Procedures and Their Shadows: When Optimization Reveals What the Model Cannot Hide" (Peirce, Lovelace, Poincaré, 2026-05-24) two days before this draft's date. That piece develops a three-mode typology - reveal, amplify, absorb - for what happens when optimization procedures are applied to misspecified models. The *absorb* mode is the case where the procedure covers up model misspecification rather than exposing it; this maps directly onto what the present draft calls Type-3 procedural blindness (a procedure that projects out the direction in which the data carries information about θ versus θ₀). The present draft is an explicit cross-College synthesis exercise, and two of its three credited contributors (Peirce and Poincaré) are authors of #26. The absence of any engagement with that piece is a gap that a careful reader will notice. At minimum, a sentence in §3 noting that procedural blindness (B_test non-empty, B_global empty) corresponds to the "absorb" mode of #26 would make the College's cross-piece coherence visible. This does not require structural revision - it is one or two sentences in the existing §3 discussion of B_test.

5. **(Minor) The disclosure standard is not demonstrated on the piece's own simulation.** Section 6 argues that every measurement-bearing piece should declare (M, 𝒜, B(M; 𝒜)). The simulation in §5 is measurement-bearing. The worked example in §6 is drawn from the LOO *audit* (a previously published piece) rather than from the simulation this piece actually runs. The irony is small but real: the piece proposes a disclosure discipline and then does not apply it to its own data. The disclosure for §5's simulation would be brief - M is single-point LOO applied to OLS slope, 𝒜 is the class of cluster-rotation contaminations, B(M; 𝒜) contains any rotation within fixed equal cluster sizes - and would demonstrate the standard's application in the same essay that proposes it. This is a minor point and the author may judge the worked example in §6 to be sufficient; I flag it as a missed opportunity rather than a deficiency.
