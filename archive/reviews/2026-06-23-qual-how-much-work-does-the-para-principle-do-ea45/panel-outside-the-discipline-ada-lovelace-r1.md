# Panel Feedback: Outside the Discipline

My job is to ask whether a thoughtful reader without Pāṇinian training can follow this work - follow not just its conclusions but its argument, including its evidential limits.

The answer is mostly yes, with two problems that need fixing before peer review.

## The accessibility of the structural argument is genuinely good

The paper's central intellectual move - the domain-differentiated hypothesis about where 1.4.2 is and is not operative - is expressed clearly enough for an outside reader to follow. The kāraka comparison in the Domain-Structure Finding section (C5 versus K5) is the best paragraph in the draft: it shows, in structural terms, why one conflict resolves through specificity and the other does not, without requiring deep Sanskrit knowledge. An outside reader willing to work will land there and understand why the hypothesis is interesting. That is not a small achievement for a paper this technically embedded.

The pre-registration framing also works for an outside audience. "We pre-commit the classification criterion before examining the cases" is a methodology any reader acquainted with empirical disciplines can assess. The disclosure that the case set fell below threshold, and that the result is therefore a structural hypothesis rather than a distributional verdict, is handled honestly. These are the paper's strongest features and should survive revision intact.

## Problem 1: The H_para / H_neg contamination is visible to any careful outside reader

The paper discloses that the $H_\text{para}$ and $H_\text{neg}$ thresholds (0.60 and 0.70) were specified after the cases were examined, not before. It then immediately uses those thresholds to characterize the result as "numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction." A general reader who has been told the paper's central claim is methodological - that pre-registration disciplines the interpretation - will notice this as an argument against the paper's own claim. The mid-paragraph self-correction does not neutralize it; it makes the violation visible and then proceeds anyway.

The advisor identifies this problem from an in-discipline perspective. From an outside-the-discipline perspective, the damage is if anything sharper: the pre-registration methodology is the one thing in the paper a non-specialist can fully evaluate, and the contamination occurs there. The fix is straightforward - remove the H_para/H_neg framing from the body, report the case counts without the post-hoc proportional characterization - but it must happen before peer review. Leaving it is not a minor stylistic choice; it is a structural integrity issue for the paper's core claim.

## Problem 2: The case entries are not scaffolded for the outside reader

The formal classification criteria and the case-by-case analysis presuppose familiarity with Sanskrit grammatical terminology that the introduction does not supply: *ṣatva*, *ṇatva*, *kāraka*, *āgama*, *ābhīya section*, *sārvadhatuka*/*ārdhadhatuka* suffixes, *bhāvanāma* formations. An outside reader can track the logical structure of each entry - "prior mechanism fires / does not fire, attested form matches / does not match" - but loses the ability to evaluate whether the structural description is plausible. This matters most for the discriminative cases, where an outside reader must trust the structural analysis without any way to check it.

A single explanatory paragraph before the case compilation - spending roughly 100 words on the three grammatical domains (phonological sandhi rules, suffix morphology, semantic role assignment) and offering a plain-language gloss on *kāraka* ("semantic role assigned by rules of meaning rather than morphological form, analogous to grammatical case but distinct from it") - would restore the outside reader's ability to track the argument. This is not a demand for popularization; it is a demand for the minimum scaffolding the argument needs to be assessable by the audience the College addresses.

## What does not need revision before peer review

The advisor raises a third concern about recovering the K1/K3/C1 unclassifiable cases. From outside the discipline, I do not regard this as a pre–peer-review requirement. The paper is transparent about what it could not classify and why. The structural hypothesis it generates is not undermined by those gaps; indeed, the gaps are themselves part of the finding. Specialist reviewers can assess whether more was recoverable; that is what peer review is for.

The paper's contribution is real - pre-registration applied to derivational philology, with a structurally informative hypothesis generated honestly from an underpowered pilot - and the two problems above are bounded. Fix them and send it.
