C5 carries the diagnostic load and the analysis is now proportionate to that load.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **C7's structural argument carries weight comparable to C5's but is not walked through to the same standard.** The C5 walkthrough at line 104 now does what I asked for: it lays out the *apāya* condition as a proper extension of the karma condition, names the explicit textual ground for the subset relation, and notes the absence of contrary readings in the surveyed literature. C7 carries one-third of the discriminative load (alongside K5 and C4b) and its structural argument depends on the claim that the two semantic conditioning conditions are *not* in a proper-subset relation. Line 96 states this in one sentence: "the two rules' semantic domains are not in a proper-subset relation, so *apavāda* also does not resolve the conflict." The specific semantic features that establish non-nestedness are not named, and the source for the non-subset claim is given as "Patañjali, documented by Cardona, explicitly accepts 1.4.2 as the operative mechanism" - which is acceptance of the *verdict*, not the *structural argument* for non-nestedness. If Cardona's documentation supports the structural argument (the specific verbal-class features and why they are independent), a sentence drawing that out would proportion C7's treatment to its diagnostic role. If it does not, the limitation is real - the diagnostic load is then resting on a case whose non-nestedness rests on Cardona's authority rather than on a structural argument the reader can evaluate. Either disclosure resolves this; the current draft does neither. Minor.

2. **The "foreseeable threshold" disclosure at line 23 is honest but invites a question the draft does not answer.** The paragraph states: "Additionally, the threshold was foreseeable as likely to fire: Kiparsky's class-level characterizations whose competing rules are unnamed route to $P_\text{unclass}$ under condition (d) regardless of domain-subset analysis. The structural fallback was a genuine pre-committed contingency; the case-density limitation was nonetheless foreseeable from the outset." This is unusually candid - it admits that the case-density gap was visible at the protocol stage. The reader's natural next question is: given that visibility, why was the fifteen-case threshold not adjusted downward to a level the citation corpus could meet, or why was the corpus not pre-expanded to primary *Mahābhāṣya* access at the proposal stage? The current framing answers neither. The honest answer may be that the College's resource constraints at the proposal stage did not permit primary-source access, and lowering the threshold would have weakened the test's power against any threshold worth issuing - in which case the right move was exactly what was done (commit to the structural fallback as a real alternative output). A sentence at line 23 making that reasoning explicit would close the loop. Minor.

3. **The "Numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction" framing at line 128 sits in slight tension with the verdict-not-issued claim.** The pre-committed protocol says the distributional verdict cannot be issued at $N = 8 < 15$. The Results section then says "the observed proportion of discriminative cases ($3/8 = 0.375$) is numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction but cannot be distinguished statistically from any proportion in the $0.20$–$0.60$ range." The "numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction" half is a soft directional reading that, combined with the selection-bias argument from line 21 (which strengthens the directional reading further toward $H_\text{neg}$), risks reading as a partial verdict the protocol said it would not issue. The line 19 disclosure already names that the post-hoc thresholds shape this characterization. The remaining tension is in the Results section itself, not in the disclosure. One possible fix: in Results, state only the structural finding (eight classifiable, three discriminative, five inert, the case-by-domain organization to follow) and leave the directional reading of $3/8$ to the structural account section that derives its weight from selection-bias analysis rather than from the post-hoc thresholds. As is, the draft both withholds and softly issues the directional verdict. Minor; on the boundary of "would be cleaner" rather than "is wrong."

4. **No review-process leakage detected.** I scanned the draft for "round 1," "prior draft," "my advisor," "the panel said," "this revision addresses," "after peer review," and the related family of process-narrative phrases. None appear. The methodological self-disclosures at lines 19, 21, 23, 132, 138, and 142 are protocol-level disclosures that belong in a public methods section, not process narration about the review. This concern is included for completeness, not as a problem.
