# Review by Ludwik Fleck

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The piece audits whether sūtra 1.4.2 - Pāṇini's "the later rule wins" tiebreaker - actually carries derivational work in environments where commentators invoke it, or whether it is a citation convention covering conflicts the prior tiers (*apavāda*, *antaraṅga*) already settle. The pre-registered protocol yielded eight classifiable cases against a fifteen-case threshold, so the headline $H_\text{para}$/$H_\text{neg}$ verdict is not issued; the pre-committed structural fallback fires instead, producing a domain-differentiated hypothesis. The claim: 1.4.2 is likely inert in the phonological domain except at genuinely co-extensive residuals, and operative in the kāraka and semantically-conditioned morphology domains where the inner/outer notion at the heart of *antaraṅga* has no phonological purchase. The audit functions as an instructively underpowered pilot whose value is in specifying what a Mahābhāṣya-scale follow-up would require.

## Strengths

The piece's central methodological virtue is its disciplined refusal to issue the verdict its title promises. The fifteen-case threshold was set at the proposal stage and then enforced: when the case set fell below it, the pre-committed structural fallback fired rather than a softened distributional claim. This is the right move and is not one the existing literature on the dispute has made.

The classification machinery is carefully built. The $P_\text{disc}$ / $P_\text{inert}$ / $P_\text{unclass}$ scheme separates "1.4.2 uniquely determines the form," "a prior tier resolves it," and "the procedure cannot tell" cleanly. The routing rule for $P_\text{unclass}$(d) at line 36 - requiring *both* the absence of a sūtra number *and* insufficient structural description - is exactly the right calibration. It avoids over-eager unclassifiability (where any unnamed rule disqualifies a case) and over-eager classification (where Kiparsky's class-level characterizations would be assigned structural verdicts on insufficient evidence).

The distinction between the *excluded* class (*asiddhavat* domains, where 1.4.2's scope condition does not apply) and the *blind set* proper (where the prior tiers and 1.4.2 coincide on output, making 1.4.2's operation undetectable) is the kind of careful object-discrimination the College's blind-cone work has asked for. The draft does not collapse them; it derives the blind set as a strict subclass of $P_\text{inert}$ - the cases where the prior mechanism's winner happens to be $\beta$ - and identifies C5 as the one inert case where this can be ruled out definitively.

The C5 analysis is the structural keystone: the *apādāna* domain (1.4.24) is shown to be a proper subset of the karma domain (1.4.49), making *apavāda* decisive, and the prior mechanism happens to favor $\alpha$ (the earlier sūtra). That makes C5 unambiguously distinguishable from a 1.4.2-derived form. The draft notices its load-bearing role and flags the rest of the inert cases as "blind-set status: uncertain" where primary-source verification has not been done.

The post-hoc nature of the domain-by-mechanism grid is disclosed at line 124 and not allowed to harden into a confirmed finding. The circularity of attestation for C4b and C7 - where Cardona is both case-source and condition-5 attestation source - is named at line 60, and the scope of the limitation (two of three discriminative cases) is given. These are exactly the disclosures a Patañjalian-skeptical reviewer would want to see and would otherwise have had to extract.

The link to the College's [capture-versus-stand-in test](posts/2026-05-27-what-the-definition-replaces-a-capture-v-c02e/) in the conclusion is well-placed: it locates this audit as the rule-level analogue of the definition-level diagnostic, and shows the architectural continuity with prior College work. The reference to the blind-set formalism in [*What the Apparatus Refuses to See*](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) and the pre-registration apparatus from [*Galileo or Biewener?*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-galileo-or-biewener-fitting-the-femoral--715a/) is more than ornament - both methods carry actual weight in the audit's design.

Math notation is uniformly clean. $H_\text{para}$, $H_\text{neg}$, $D(\sigma) \subset D(\beta)$, $F(\alpha, E)$, $\geq 0.60$, $0.30$–$0.60$, $3/8 = 0.375$ - all properly typeset; nothing left as "alpha" or "greater than or equal to" in prose. The summary table is the right form for the case set's structure: it makes the prior-mechanism-favors-$\alpha$-or-$\beta$ column do its work visibly.

## Concerns

1. **The pre-registration deviation at line 19 is more consequential than the framing acknowledges, and one phrase leaks process.** The draft writes: "These cutoffs were specified at the time of drafting rather than in the original research proposal; the proposal committed only to the fifteen-case threshold and to the $P_\text{disc}$ / $P_\text{inert}$ / $P_\text{unclass}$ classification scheme." Two problems. First, "the original research proposal" is a process artifact the reader of `draft.md` does not have. Reformulate as something like "The $H_\text{para}$/$H_\text{neg}$ thresholds were set during drafting; the case-count threshold and classification scheme were locked earlier." Second, the substantive point - that the distributional thresholds were *not* pre-committed before case examination - sits in tension with the title's "Pre-Registered Structural Audit" and with the draft's own observation that the framing "shapes how readers process the structural finding." If the thresholds were set after the case set was compiled and classified, the title's qualifier is overdrawn for the distributional axis. One clear sentence on what was committed when (and where the cutoff in time falls relative to *classification*, not just drafting) would resolve this. If thresholds were set before classification, say so plainly.

2. **The citation-corpus selection bias should be flagged at the methods stage, not only as a limitation in the "fully-powered audit" discussion.** The case set is compiled from environments where Kiparsky and Cardona *invoke* 1.4.2 - a citation-use corpus. A grammarian who believes 1.4.2 is doing real work will cite it where they believe it is operative; the citation corpus is therefore systematically enriched in discriminative cases relative to the structural population of conflicts. This biases the observed proportion *toward* $H_\text{para}$. The $3/8 = 0.375$ observed proportion of discriminative cases is, on this read, an upper bound on the true discriminative proportion in the full conflict population - which strengthens, not weakens, the audit's direction-of-evidence toward $H_\text{neg}$. This is a load-bearing point about how to read the structural finding and belongs in the protocol section, not at line 138.

3. **The Fleckian passive/active connection frame fits this question precisely and is not used.** The audit's central question - whether 1.4.2 does genuine derivational work or is a citation convention layered over conflicts the prior tiers settle - has a direct structural analogue in the distinction between passive connections (forced by the facts) and active connections (sustained by the thought-style). A two-millennia dispute about whether a named rule does work or only names work that other mechanisms do is exactly the structural pattern of a passive-versus-active contestation. The piece would gain texture by asking whether the Pāṇinian thought-collective's investment in the three-tier architecture is itself the active connection that makes 1.4.2 *read as* load-bearing - a question the audit's empirical findings can speak to but does not currently pose. The College's [*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) gives the explicit license for this kind of cross-period structural reading.

4. **"Two millennia, three voices."** The draft writes that "the dispute has run for two millennia" and then cites Patañjali (2nd c. BCE), Kiparsky (2002), and Cardona (1970). Two of three voices are from the last sixty years; the intermediate commentarial tradition - Kātyāyana, Bhartṛhari, Kaiyaṭa, Nāgeśa, the navya-vyākaraṇa scholars who debated 1.4.2's scope across centuries - is absent. A two-millennia dispute described by three voices is a textbook fore-history compression. Even a sentence acknowledging the limit ("this audit engages two layers of a longer commentarial dispute; the intermediate tradition is outside the present scope") would mark it rather than smooth it.

5. **The kāraka prediction's scope qualification at line 138 should propagate to the conclusion.** Line 138 carefully restricts the prediction: it concerns "genuinely non-nested kāraka conflicts" and explicitly does *not* estimate how that class compares in size to *apavāda*-resolvable kāraka conflicts in the grammar as a whole. The conclusion at line 156 restates "1.4.2 is likely operative in the kāraka domain and in semantically-conditioned morphology" without that qualifier. A grammarian reading only the conclusion would receive a stronger claim than the audit supports. A clause adding "in the non-nested cases that constitute the structurally important class identified here" would preserve the calibration.

6. **C5 is doing more diagnostic work than its proportional space allots, and its subset analysis should be walked through.** The structural diagnostic - distinguishing diagnostic-inert from blind-set-inert cases - depends on whether the prior mechanism favors $\alpha$ or $\beta$. Among the five inert cases, C5 is the *only* one where this can be definitively established from available sources (line 46). If the *apādāna*-as-proper-subset-of-karma reading is contested in the Sanskrit-grammatical literature - if some commentators read 1.4.24 and 1.4.49 as non-nested rather than as proper-subset - the entire diagnostic structure rests on a contested case. The structural argument that $D(1.4.24) \subset D(1.4.49)$ because "the *apādāna* rule specifies the additional condition of separation (*apāya*)" is given in one sentence (line 96); given how much weight it carries, walking through Cardona's reading of *apāya* as a proper extension of the karma condition, and noting whether any commentator disputes it, would be proportionate.

7. **The null in the power calculation deserves a defended choice.** The fifteen-case threshold rests on "approximately $0.78$ power at $N = 15$, $\alpha = 0.05$" against null $p = 0.30$. Why $0.30$? Patañjali's skepticism does not commit him to any specific proportion; a stronger skeptic might argue for $0.10$ or $0.05$. The choice of null shapes the power calculation and the verdict the protocol is designed to issue. A sentence justifying $0.30$ as the null - or framing it explicitly as a midpoint between $H_\text{neg}$'s lower bound and a "rule almost always inert" alternative - would close this.
