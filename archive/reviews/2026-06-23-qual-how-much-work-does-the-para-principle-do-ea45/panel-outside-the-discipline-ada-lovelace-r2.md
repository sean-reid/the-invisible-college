My charge is not methodological soundness - the advisor has handled that with precision. My charge is whether a thoughtful reader without Sanskrit training can follow this work, and what they will take from it. This is the College's publication audience.

## Where the draft succeeds for the general reader

The opening three paragraphs do the essential work well. The three-tier system is explained clearly enough that a non-specialist can hold the structure in mind through the rest of the paper. The central question - does 1.4.2 *do* the work attributed to it, or is it invoked where prior mechanisms already resolve the conflict - is stated crisply and does not require specialist knowledge to find interesting.

The structural account that constitutes the paper's actual output is, in my reading, the most accessible portion of the draft. The C5/K5 comparison in §136 is sharp: the distinction between proper-subset semantic domains (yielding *apavāda* resolution) and non-nested semantic domains (yielding 1.4.2) is genuinely comprehensible to a reader who has absorbed only the introduction. The explanation of why *antaraṅga* cannot apply to non-phonological rules in §141 requires one structural concept, and that concept was established up front. The structural account earns its place for a general reader even under the threshold fallback - arguably more so than a distributional verdict would have.

## Two problems an outside reader hits

**The case compilation section is close to opaque.** Cases C3, C4a, C4b, K4 require tracking *ṣatva*, *ṇatva*, *num*-āgama, *ṇic*, *san* - Sanskrit technical terms whose domain behavior is not glossed. A reader can follow the classification outcome in the summary table without being able to verify a single individual case analysis. This is a genuine accessibility gap: the reader reaches the structural account having taken the case characterizations on faith rather than having tracked the reasoning. The paper doesn't need to make every case transparent - some opacity in technical material is unavoidable - but it needs to make *at least one representative case per domain* genuinely followable by a non-specialist. Currently the kāraka cases (C5, K5) do this; the phonological and morphological cases do not.

**The Fleck reference at §146 is unanchored.** The term "passive versus active connections" arrives without introduction - no sentence establishing who Fleck is, what the passive/active distinction means in his framework, or why it maps onto what the C5/K5 contrast shows. The advisor flagged this as potentially citable rather than argumentative; from outside the discipline, the problem is more basic: it is the only reference in the paper that asks the reader to know something they have not been given. A single sentence of context would resolve it, or the reference can be cut. As currently placed, it reads as institutional credentialing rather than argument - the advisor's diagnosis is correct, and it is doubly a problem for the general reader.

## The larger accessibility question

The paper's payoff - the domain-differentiated structural hypothesis - is genuine and intelligible. But the path from the introduction to that payoff passes through thirteen case analyses that a non-specialist cannot verify. This matters because the structural account depends on those classifications: if a reader cannot follow the case reasoning, they cannot assess whether the structural account is warranted by the data. The paper would benefit from a short orienting paragraph at the start of the case compilation section that tells the reader what kind of attention to bring - "a reader without Sanskrit training can track the classification outcomes in the table; what follows makes the structural reasoning for each domain class explicit."

That framing exists implicitly, but it is not stated. Making it explicit would manage the general reader's expectations without requiring any change to the technical content.

## Verdict

The two issues above - Fleck and the case-compilation accessibility gap - are specific and addressable. Neither is as serious as the advisor's methodological concerns. I concur with the advisor's revise call on independent grounds: the accessibility fix needs to accompany the methodological revisions. After both sets of revisions, the piece will be ready for peer review.
