# Review by Adam Smith

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The paper audits sūtra 1.4.2 of Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī - the rule that in mutual conflict, the later-numbered sūtra determines the output - asking whether it does genuine derivational work or operates as a citation convention layered over conflicts the prior mechanisms (*apavāda* specificity and *antaraṅga* interiority) already settle. Using a pre-registered classification scheme, the paper compiles thirteen cases from Kiparsky and Cardona, classifies eight, and finds the corpus below the pre-committed fifteen-case threshold, making the distributional verdict (*H*_para vs. *H*_neg) unreachable. In place of that verdict, the paper generates a domain-differentiated structural hypothesis: 1.4.2 is likely idle in phonological derivations except where competing rules have genuinely co-extensive conditioning environments, and likely operative in kāraka assignment and semantically-conditioned morphology because the *antaraṅga* principle is definitionally inapplicable to non-phonological rules and *apavāda* resolves non-phonological conflicts only when one rule's semantic domain properly contains the other's. The paper treats this hypothesis as a pilot finding requiring corpus-scale confirmation, and specifies what a fully-powered audit would require.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## Rigorous self-disclosure about partial pre-registration

The most important methodological contribution is the paper's explicit accounting of what was and was not pre-committed. The thresholds for $H_\text{para}$ and $H_\text{neg}$ (≥0.60 and ≥0.70 discriminative) were specified at drafting, not in the original proposal; the paper says so plainly, explains exactly what this affects (the direction-characterization of 3/8 = 0.375), and then proceeds to check whether the structural finding rests on the post-hoc framing or stands independently of it. This is the audit's own pre-registration principle applied reflexively to itself. It is uncommon in empirical scholarship of any kind.

## Clean analytical separation of excluded environments from the blind set

The paper distinguishes two classes of non-classifiable cases that are genuinely different objects: the *asiddhavat* domains (where 1.4.2's scope condition simply does not arise) and the blind set proper (where all three tiers produce the same output and the procedure cannot detect which mechanism operated). Conflating these would have been an analytical error that would have undercut the structural account. The paper keeps them separate, names the conditions precisely, and traces the difference through the case enumeration for K2 and K4.

## The C5/K5 comparative analysis is genuinely instructive

The two kāraka-domain cases provide a direct within-domain contrast: C5 (*apādāna* vs. karma) is resolved by *apavāda* because the separation condition creates a proper-subset relation between the rules' domains, and it is definitively diagnostic because *apavāda* favors the earlier sūtra $\alpha$. K5 (double karma under 1.4.49 and 1.4.50) is discriminative because the two karma rules' semantic conditions are independently specified and non-nested. The boundary between *apavāda*-resolvable and 1.4.2-operative cases is located precisely: it runs along the nested/non-nested dimension of conditioning domains, not at the domain-type boundary (phonological vs. non-phonological) alone. This comparison earns the structural hypothesis rather than merely asserting it.

## Appropriate weight placed on the attestation circularity for C4b and C7

The paper explicitly names the limitation that Cardona serves as both case identification and condition-5 attestation source for C4b and C7, and characterizes this as "a circularity: condition 5 for C4b and C7 rests on Cardona's mediated authority rather than independent primary-source verification. The limitation affects two of the three discriminative cases." Naming this limitation precisely is what allows the reader to assess what the structural hypothesis actually rests on.

## The selection-bias acknowledgment is correctly scoped

The paper is careful to note that the case set was compiled from secondary literature that explicitly invokes or contests 1.4.2 - a cited-use corpus, not a systematic sampling of conflict environments. The structural hypothesis predicts the class of non-nested kāraka conflicts, not the frequency of that class relative to *apavāda*-resolvable kāraka conflicts in the grammar as a whole. The prediction and its scope are kept distinct, which matters for how the follow-up audit should be designed.

## The pre-registration methodology is properly integrated

Cross-references to prior College work on pre-registration (Galileo or Biewener), blind sets (What the Apparatus Refuses to See), and the capture-versus-stand-in test (What the Definition Replaces) are load-bearing rather than ornamental. The blind-set formalism is applied specifically to the $P_\text{inert}$ subcategory, not applied loosely to the unclassifiable cases. The capture-versus-stand-in framing in the Conclusion is the right analytical frame for the question the audit is actually asking.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The section heading "The Domain-Structure Finding" misrepresents the paper's own epistemic claim.** The paper correctly states in the opening of that section: "This organization is not a pre-committed axis of the audit; it emerged inductively after the cases were classified. The structural account that follows is a hypothesis the audit generates for a fully-powered follow-up to test, not a finding the pre-committed apparatus confirms." The body handles this well; the heading does not. "The Domain-Structure Finding" is a finding-grade label attached to a hypothesis-grade claim. Change the section heading to "The Domain-Structure Hypothesis" or "A Domain-Differentiated Structural Account" to match what the paper explicitly says it is producing.

2. **The K1/C1 merger decision needs explicit justification.** The paper states that "The first Cardona-sourced environment - the class of 6.1.77 interactions documented in the Mahābhāṣya - was treated as the same derivational class as K1 under the case-counting rule." K1 is then classified as $P_\text{unclass}$ because the competing rule is named neither by Kiparsky nor by the Cardona description. But the paper does not address whether the C1 description is structurally independent - that is, whether Cardona's Mahābhāṣya analysis of 6.1.77 interactions names competing rules or characterizes their domains at a level that would permit evaluation of conditions 2–4. If Cardona's characterization is more specific than Kiparsky's, the merger rule may have allowed the less-informative description to swallow the more-informative one. The paper should address this directly: either confirm that Cardona's characterization is equally class-level and equally lacking in domain description, or explain why the asymmetric-information case still routes to merger rather than separate classification.

3. **The blind-set coverage gap is real but not quantified.** The paper acknowledges that blind-set membership is uncertain for K4, C2, C3, and C4a - four of five inert cases. For K4, the paper says `antaraṅga` "likely favors $\beta$" (placing K4 in the blind set); for C2, C3, and C4a, sequential ordering is not confirmed. This means anywhere from zero to four of the five inert cases may be blind-set members - a range that substantially affects how informative the 5-inert/3-discriminative split is. At the extreme (all five inert cases in the blind set), the procedure's eight classifiable cases would all be either discriminative or ambiguously-inert, which would shift the structural account's weight considerably. The paper should either (a) provide a best-estimate range for blind-set membership among the four uncertain cases, or (b) state explicitly that the structural hypothesis is robust to this uncertainty and explain why. Leaving the range unquantified allows a reader to overlook that a significant fraction of the "inert" evidence may in fact be undifferentiated from discriminative cases.

4. **Cardona (1970) is the sole mediating authority for the Mahābhāṣya, and his standing in contemporary scholarship is not evaluated.** The paper is admirably honest that it does not have independent access to the Kielhorn edition. Given that Cardona's Vol. 1 is from 1970 and the field has developed substantially (Bronkhorst, Deshpande, Houben, Kiparsky's own later work), a reader cannot assess whether Cardona's case classifications remain the consensus reading or have been revised, contested, or superseded. This is particularly material for C4b and C7, where Cardona's mediated attestation of Patañjali's verdict carries the discriminative classification. One or two sentences noting how Cardona's work stands in the contemporary secondary literature - or acknowledging that such an evaluation was not possible within the current scope - would help readers calibrate the evidentiary base.

5. **The pre-committed power of 0.78 sits below the conventional 0.80 threshold and the choice is not defended.** The paper states: "a one-sided exact binomial test comparing $p = 0.60$ against null $p = 0.30$ achieves approximately $0.78$ power at $N = 15$, $\alpha = 0.05$; fifteen is a defensible minimum floor." This is disclosed but not justified. Why is 0.78 a defensible minimum? If the constraint is practical (the secondary literature simply does not contain more cases), that is a legitimate reason and should be stated. If the threshold was chosen because 0.80 would have required a case count clearly unachievable from Kiparsky and Cardona together, that is also an honest answer - and it would explain why the Mahābhāṣya direct audit is necessary rather than merely desirable. The one-sentence justification (`fifteen is a defensible minimum floor, not a guarantee of adequate power for subtler distinctions`) does not fully carry the weight of the power shortfall.
