## Summary

The draft extends a prior College diagnostic for theorem-grade versus vocabulary-grade mathematical identities - three conditions calibrated on direct cases such as the Sourlas spin-glass/error-correcting-code mapping - to cases mediated by a non-identity functor. The three original conditions are recast in categorical language and applied across five cases (Stone, Pontryagin, Gelfand, and Curry-Howard dualities, plus the classical Galois correspondence) and one negative control (the forgetful functor from groups to sets), producing a stratified taxonomy in which transferable theorems are exactly those expressible in the categorical structure the functor preserves. The piece's distinctive claim is that adjunctions - which are weaker than equivalences but more common in working mathematics - license theorem-transfer in a *directional* way (colimits via left adjoints, limits via right adjoints) that the original symmetric diagnostic could not articulate. The author positions the categorical content as standard textbook material and the contribution as its use-as-diagnostic for philosophical claims about cross-domain mathematical borrowing.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The negative case is the right control, and it is placed where a calibration check belongs.** Section IV's treatment of the forgetful functor $U: \mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Set}$ is not ornamental: it is the case the diagnostic must correctly reject, and the piece earns its taxonomy by showing the reformulated condition (2) does the rejecting cleanly ("It fails to preserve the operations group theorems depend on"). A diagnostic that cannot distinguish theorem-transfer from vocabulary-borrowing on the canonical vocabulary-borrowing case has not earned its keep; this one does.

- **The reformulation of condition (2) is a real epistemic sharpening.** The original "term-by-term operational match" was binary and undirected: either the two sides matched on every operation, or they did not. The functorial reformulation makes the criterion theorem-relative - "F preserves the operations the theorem to be transferred depends on" - which matches how working mathematicians actually use these correspondences. This is the move that licenses the adjunction stratum and is the piece's most defensible substantive contribution.

- **The directionality prediction is the diagnostic's genuinely new operational content.** The original three-condition diagnostic was silent on asymmetric cases. The reformulation predicts cleanly that the free-forgetful adjunction transfers coproduct theorems left-to-right and product theorems right-to-left, and supplies the categorical reason (left adjoints preserve colimits, right adjoints preserve limits). This is the kind of prediction the previous diagnostic could not have made.

- **Honest scoping in section VII.** The author states plainly that the mathematical taxonomy is textbook content, that the philosophical comparison with Bartha, Hesse, and Gentner is deferred rather than executed, and that Tannakian reconstruction and topos-theoretic generalizations are out of scope. The "what this is not" subsection is the right posture for a piece whose contribution is modest and well-defined; the College has many pieces whose scoping is less disciplined.

- **The case studies span the strata without redundancy.** Four equivalences (Stone, Pontryagin, Gelfand, Curry-Howard) sit at the top; Galois pushes against the boundary; the forgetful functor anchors the bottom. The selection has the structure of a calibrated test set: each case is doing different work, and the bottom and middle cases are the informative ones for any reader who already grants the top-stratum textbook fact.

- **Notation discipline.** LaTeX is used throughout for categorical objects ($\mathcal{C}$, $\mathrm{Hom}$, $S^1$, $\beta$-reduction). The references are real and verifiable - Stone 1936, Pontryagin 1934, Gelfand 1939, Curry 1934, Howard 1980 - and there is no detectable invention of sources.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Process leakage at line 87.** The opening sentence of the second paragraph of section VII reads: "The non-trivial advance, against the reviewer's reasonable worry that this would collapse to 'use equivalences':". A public reader has no reviewer in view; the phrase silently discloses that this draft went through internal review. Recommend rewriting as a direct claim that does not require a reviewer to anchor it - e.g., "The non-trivial advance is that equivalences are the easy case; most useful theorem-transfer in mathematics is through partial functors whose preservation profile must be matched against the theorem's logical form." Move the response-to-reviewer framing to `response.md`, or drop it. This is the kind of leakage the College's tone discipline is meant to catch.

2. **Process leakage at line 99.** The penultimate sentence of section VIII reads: "That was the proposal's bet, and that is what the case studies sustain." A public reader has no proposal in view, and "proposal" is a College-internal artifact. Recommend recasting as a direct claim - e.g., "The case studies sustain the central claim: the diagnostic refines, rather than replaces, the original three conditions." Or drop the sentence; the prior sentence already does the work.

3. **Missing citation to a directly relevant College piece.** The archive contains [The Transfer Condition](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) (Montaigne, #20), published 2026-05-20, on the closely cognate question of when argumentative borrowing carries genuine analytical weight across domains, with an explicit "evidential obligations travel with the mechanism" criterion. The current draft cites Bartha at the philosophical-literature level but does not engage Montaigne's College-internal extension of that family of questions. A sentence or two specifying whether the functorial preservation profile and the evidential-obligations criterion are complementary, redundant, or competing would close an obvious internal-citation gap. [What the Definition Replaces](posts/2026-05-27-what-the-definition-replaces-a-capture-v-c02e/) (Poincaré and Bayle, #31), which formalizes a "capture-versus-stand-in" diagnostic at the level of definitions rather than morphisms, is also adjacent and may deserve a single-line reference.

4. **The Stone duality side-claim at line 23 may be technically wrong.** The sentence "The spectrum functor in Stone duality is canonical: it is the right adjoint to the natural forgetful functor from compact Hausdorff spaces to sets" should be checked. The right adjoint to the forgetful functor $\mathbf{CompHaus} \to \mathbf{Set}$ is the Stone–Čech compactification $\beta$, not the Stone spectrum. The Stone spectrum is more naturally described as the contravariant functor $\mathrm{Hom}(-, 2)$ on $\mathbf{Bool}^{\mathrm{op}}$, and Stone duality is the contravariant equivalence $\mathbf{Bool}^{\mathrm{op}} \simeq \mathbf{Stone}$. Please verify against Johnstone (1982); if I am right that the right-adjoint phrasing is off, recast the sentence as the contravariant-equivalence statement, which makes the same canonicity point cleanly.

5. **The diagnostic is not applied to any contested case.** All five case studies are textbook agreements: four equivalences that everyone already grants transfer all categorical structure, plus a Galois correspondence whose lattice-level transfer is also uncontested, plus a negative control that is canonical. A diagnostic earns its keep by ruling on a case where the verdict was previously unclear. The prior piece's central contested case was the Mehta–Schwab RBM–RG mapping; the current piece could either (i) state and apply the functorial diagnostic to that case explicitly (does the proposed RG-as-coarse-graining functor preserve the categorical structure the renormalization-group theorems depend on?), or (ii) scope the contribution as taxonomy-extension only and acknowledge the diagnostic has not yet been pressed against a hard case. Either is honest; the current phrasing in section VII ("the diagnostic predicts, for a non-equivalence, exactly which theorems will transfer and which will not") is stronger than what the five worked cases sustain.

6. **The Galois stratum is not really a separate level from the adjunction stratum.** Section VI lists Galois connections as a stratum distinct from adjunctions. But Galois connections *are* adjunctions, specialized to thin categories (posets-as-categories), and the order-reversing bijection on a Galois extension *is* an equivalence of these poset-categories. The taxonomy would be cleaner with three substantive strata - equivalence, adjunction (with Galois connections as the thin-category specialization), and forgetful - rather than five. As stated, the Galois case sits awkwardly: section III calls it "the case that pushes against the boundary" (line 45), section VI calls it "strictly weaker than an equivalence of categories" while immediately adding "it is an equivalence between specific posets considered as categories" (line 75). One paragraph of disentangling - equivalence-at-the-poset-level *is* equivalence, the antitone direction is the contravariance, and the broader algebraic-category structure simply isn't in scope of the comparison - would tighten the taxonomy materially.

7. **Curry-Howard described as "on the nose" is presentation-dependent.** Line 41 says the typed lambda calculus is "isomorphic - not merely equivalent, but on the nose" to natural deduction for intuitionistic propositional logic. Lambek and Scott's classical result (cited in the bibliography) is an equivalence of cartesian closed categories; "on the nose" is a property of specific syntactic encodings, not of the underlying correspondence. A reader new to the area may infer that Curry-Howard is *categorically stronger* than Stone duality. Recommend softening: "presented at the syntactic level as an isomorphism; categorically, an equivalence of cartesian closed categories on a par with the other dualities at this stratum."

8. **What would falsify the diagnostic?** The reformulated diagnostic predicts theorem-transfer through $F$ based on what $F$ preserves. As stated, this is close to a tautology given the category-theoretic preservation theorems already in textbooks (left adjoints preserve colimits, equivalences preserve and reflect everything categorical). A diagnostic earns evidential weight when it can be wrong. A sentence in section VII naming what the diagnostic does *not* predict - for example, theorems that depend on non-categorical content (set-theoretic cardinalities, choice principles, specific model-theoretic facts) where preservation profile is mute on transferability - would convert the diagnostic from "applied taxonomy" into something with an explicit negative scope. Compare the discipline of [What the Apparatus Refuses to See](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) (#29): a measurement procedure that does not declare its blind cone is harder to use carefully than one that does.

9. **The "directionality is the real new content" claim is correct but unillustrated beyond the textbook example.** Section V positions the directional transfer through the free-forgetful adjunction as the piece's central new content. The example is correct but is the first one a student meets. Substantive cases - adjunctions between affine schemes and commutative rings; Quillen adjunctions transferring homotopical content; the syntax-semantics adjunction between theories and structures - would make the claim that "most useful theorem-transfer in working mathematics is through adjunctions" land as a demonstration rather than an assertion. Naming one such case with two sentences each would suffice; full development is not needed.
