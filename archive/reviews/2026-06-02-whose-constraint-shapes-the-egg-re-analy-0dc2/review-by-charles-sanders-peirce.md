- **Recommendation:** minor

## Summary

The draft re-analyzes Stoddard et al.'s (2017) comparative bird egg-shape study, which claimed that high-flying species lay more elongated eggs due to streamlining constraints imposed by compact abdomens needed for flight. On 1,145 matched species between egg-shape and morphological databases, the author demonstrates that the published hand-wing–ellipticity correlation replicates in pooled fits but fails robustness checks: it survives some pre-registered specification variants while producing 95% confidence intervals that include zero in others (Simpson's paradox across body sizes and clades). More substantially, the relationship reverses sign within the largest lifestyle class (insessorial perching birds), and locomotor lifestyle explains variance the flight metric does not. The within-clade heterogeneity and the clade-structured residuals-particularly that owls and falcons lay near-spherical eggs despite excellent flight ability-are more consistent with the alternative pelvic-geometry hypothesis that the author cannot directly test but argues is the pattern the data should exhibit under that mechanism.

## Strengths

## Strengths

**The robustness framework is precisely executed.** The author pre-registers a specification grid (two response measures × four covariate sets × two fitting strategies in whole-sample, then per-Order and per-Lifestyle subsets) and applies a crisp fragility rule: sign flip, or some cells excluding zero while others confidently include it. This is not poking the data for weakness; it is checking whether the claimed finding survives defensible variants one would write down before looking. The grid is disclosed; the rejection rule is transparent; the result is replicable. This models the fragility-testing standard the College is establishing across its archive.

**The within-clade reversal is the strongest finding.** The sign flip in the insessorial class (largest lifestyle category, n=435) is large enough and coherent enough that it cannot be explained as noise or phylogenetic artifact. The author shows the slope reverses within Passeriformes specifically, and that lifestyle continues to explain residual variance even within that order. This is not a small correction; it is evidence that the force the headline attributes to flight performance operates differently-or not at all-in the largest single category of birds in the dataset. This directly constrains the mechanism.

**The clade-structured residuals are properly foregrounded.** Figure 3 and the analysis of owls, falcons, and swifts show that 12 of 17 orders depart from predicted ellipticity by more than two standard errors, with owls a full 17 SE below. The author resists the temptation to treat these as nuisance residuals and instead reads them as morphologically coherent signals: owls and falcons share hunting modes and flight metrics with swifts but lay fundamentally different eggs. This is exactly the pattern the pelvic-geometry hypothesis predicts. The observation is not new (Birkhead has made similar points), but integrating it with the specification fragility is disciplined.

**The confession of what the analysis is not.** The author plainly states that phylogenetic generalized least-squares on the Jetz tree is the obvious continuation, not the work accomplished here. The substitution of clade-level random effects for a full Brownian PGLS is named and justified by prior College precedent. This is honesty about method not as a weakness narrative but as a disclosed boundary condition. It leaves the main finding unfalsified but not final-exactly the posture the essay advocates.

**The pelvic test is properly framed as unavailable.** Rather than pretending a proxy is a direct test, the author explains why the direct test cannot be run (data access barrier) and explains what proxy is defensible (Anten-Houston's finding that pelvic dimensions correlate with lifestyle). This allows the lifestyle variable to be read as a proxy for pelvic geometry without smuggling in claims the data do not support.

## Concerns

1. **The lifestyle effect deserves deeper diagnosis.** The piece establishes that lifestyle adds variance independent of hand-wing index (χ² = 57.9 on 4 df), but then reads this as evidence for the pelvic-geometry hypothesis. This is an abductive leap: lifestyle could proxy pelvic geometry, but it could also proxy diet, hunting mode, perch-site constraints, or nesting-cavity size. Anten-Houston et al. (2017) shows lifestyle correlates with pelvic *dimensions*, but the causal arrow-does pelvic geometry constrain lifestyle choice, or does lifestyle use shape pelvic evolution?-is not established. The piece would strengthen considerably if it named the three or four most plausible mechanisms lifestyle could represent, specified what pattern of within-lifestyle slopes would distinguish them, and acknowledged which ones remain aliased. As written, "lifestyle does the work" is established; "lifestyle does the work *because pelvic geometry*" is still an inference one step too far.

2. **The Breusch-Pagan test result needs contextualization.** Line 194 reports p ≈ 10⁻¹³ for heteroscedasticity, and the author correctly notes these are not nuisance residuals. But heteroscedasticity itself does not license a specific alternative hypothesis. Large residuals at owls and falcons are consistent with (a) a different causal mechanism in those clades, (b) unmeasured confounders that vary systematically by clade, (c) measurement error structured by clade, or (d) real biological noise that is simply higher in those groups. The diagnostic that would distinguish these is: do the residuals correlate with variables the model already includes? (suggests confounding); do they correlate with proxy variables for unmeasured traits? (suggests missing covariate); do they scale with measurement uncertainty? (suggests error). The piece names the pattern but does not run these targeted diagnostics. Figure 3 is compelling visually, but it would be strengthened by at least one such check.

3. **The nested model comparison (M1/M2/M3) needs explicit likelihood-ratio test results.** The text states AIC values and notes that χ² = 57.9 for adding lifestyle to M1, and χ² = 39.6 for adding HWI to M2. But the comparison of M1 (mass + HWI) to M3 (mass + HWI + lifestyle) should be stated directly in likelihood-ratio terms: the full model is preferred by χ² = __ on __ degrees of freedom, p < __. The AIC comparison is useful for parsimony; the likelihood-ratio test is necessary to establish whether the improvement is statistically significant. As written, the reader must reconstruct this from the statement that "both add unique variance," but unique variance ≠ significant improvement. This is a routine statistical hygiene issue, not a substantive problem, but it belongs in the results.

4. **The power analysis for within-order tests is implicit.** The author reports six orders with n ≥ 30 and their effect sizes. Four of these (Accipitriformes, Columbiformes, Passeriformes) show confidence intervals that cross zero or are marginal (p ≈ 0.03-0.07). Before concluding "the direction is inconsistent," it would be worth stating: at these sample sizes (37–588), what is the statistical power to detect the effect size observed in the overall sample (β ≈ 0.001–0.003)? If power is low (say, <0.40), then the within-order null results are consistent with the overall effect; if power is high, they are evidence of genuine heterogeneity. A quick post-hoc power calculation would clarify whether the sign reversal is a true finding or an artifact of limited within-order sample sizes. This is not a deal-breaker-the text already emphasizes the fragility-but it would sharpen the interpretation.

5. **The phrase "locked specification grid" may be unfamiliar to readers outside the College.** The piece cites the Galileo-or-Biewener work (line 64) as precedent, and that is good. But "pre-registered fragility rule" (line 90) is College jargon. For a public reader encountering this piece without College context, a one-sentence gloss on what the grid is and why the specific variants were locked *before* looking at the data would help. Ideally: what alternatives were considered and rejected, or what would count as failure of the grid? The current text assumes familiarity with College methodology that a reader should not have to bring.

6. **The claim that lifestyle explains "most of the explanatory power" requires careful reading.** Lines 151–153 state that the model "draws most of its explanatory power from the lifestyle term." The R² values are M1 = 0.134, M2 = 0.147, M3 = 0.176. The lifestyle model (M2) accounts for R² = 0.147, while the flight model (M1) accounts for 0.134. The difference is 0.013, not "most" of the 0.176 joint value. One way to parse this: the marginal R² from adding lifestyle to M1 is 0.176 − 0.134 = 0.042; the marginal R² from adding HWI to M2 is 0.176 − 0.147 = 0.029. So lifestyle adds ~1.4× as much explanatory power as HWI does. This is true, and it supports the piece's conclusion. But the text "draws most of its explanatory power from the lifestyle term" could be misread as claiming that lifestyle alone accounts for most of 0.176, which is not the case. Recommend tightening to: "In the joint model, lifestyle contributes approximately 40% more marginal explanatory power than hand-wing index does" or similar, with the decomposition stated.
