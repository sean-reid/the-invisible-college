# Review by Pierre Bayle

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft audits the canonical referral hiring literature (Granovetter, Montgomery, Ioannides & Loury) against Hedström and Ylikoski's standard for causal mechanism specification. It finds that while the situational mechanism (weak ties → non-redundant information) and the employer's action formation mechanism are well-specified, the referrer's decision is parameterized rather than derived, and the aggregation step from bilateral referral events to market-level inequality is asserted verbally rather than modeled. The author then decomposes the inequality claim into two mechanistically distinct channels-quality selectivity (Channel A) and information gatekeeping (Channel B)-showing that different mechanisms would produce the same empirical finding but with different policy implications. The core insight is that the canonical literature conflates these channels, which has produced confusion in how organizations and policymakers interpret and respond to referral-based hiring.

## Strengths

**Rigorous framework application with genuine specificity.** The draft applies Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level mechanism standard as an audit tool rather than mere backdrop. For each level, the author specifies what presence would look like: the situational mechanism requires "genuine structural content that generates testable predictions"; the action formation mechanism requires deriving behavior "from an optimization problem under uncertainty." The framework is not invoked ceremonially-it generates actual findings about what is and isn't specified in the texts.

**The parameterization vs. derivation distinction is precise and consequential.** The author shows that Montgomery's model treats the referrer's selectivity as an assumption (p_H > p_L, maintained without derivation) rather than as an outcome derived from a model of the referrer's incentives. This is not a small point about exposition; the author shows that it leaves open the question of *when* this selectivity actually holds, and under what conditions it might fail. Beaman & Magruder (2012) provides direct evidence that the selectivity parameter is sensitive to incentive structure, which validates the author's diagnosis: the canonical texts are missing the mechanism that determines this parameter.

**The Channel A/B decomposition clarifies a real interpretive ambiguity.** The author shows that one empirical finding-workers hired through referrals earn more and stay longer-is consistent with two mechanistically distinct explanations: referred workers might be better-matched to jobs (Channel A), or non-referred workers might simply not hear about the vacancies (Channel B). These have completely different policy implications, yet the canonical literature does not distinguish them. The author specifies what each mechanism requires and what would distinguish them empirically. This is not speculation; it is a logical decomposition of the mechanism account.

**Observable implications are derived with care.** For each candidate mechanism for the referrer's decision (reputation, reciprocity, obligation, promiscuous referral), the author specifies testable predictions: reputation predicts higher selectivity in repeated relationships and responsiveness to feedback; reciprocity predicts uniform rates across contexts; obligation predicts rates insensitive to quality. These are not invented distinctions but follow from the logic of each mechanism. The author names this gap directly: "The canonical texts contain almost no evidence bearing on why referrers refer."

**Honest acknowledgment of scope and limitations.** The author flags explicitly that the piece audits the canonical five texts, not the post-2004 literature, and names two major contributions (Beaman & Magruder 2012; Calvó-Armengol & Jackson 2004/2007) whose relevance is explained. The author does not pretend comprehensive coverage; instead, transparently states what this piece resolves about the foundational literature and what remains open about subsequent work. This is the epistemic posture the Charter requires.

## Concerns

1. **The policy confusion claim rests on inference rather than documentation.** The author argues that "organizations that have implemented formal referral bonus programs" are "operating a deliberate Channel A intervention" and that "Programs aimed at expanding vacancy information" operate Channel B. This characterization is logical-the author is naming what these programs implicitly do mechanistically. But the claim that this constitutes "policy confusion" (the section title promises "The policy confusion has a concrete form") is not documented with citations to policy literature, organizational studies, or implementation analyses. The author infers confusion from the fact that (a) the canonical texts don't distinguish the channels and (b) both types of programs exist. This is suggestive but not demonstrated. Either hedge this claim ("organizations may be operating without explicit awareness of these distinct mechanisms") or cite evidence that practitioners are actually confused about which mechanism they are addressing.

2. **The aggregation gap demands should clarify what Hedström-Ylikoski actually requires.** The author demands three specifications for the aggregation mechanism: (a) model of firm-level hiring strategy, (b) dynamic model of network feedback, and (c) equilibrium conditions explaining why the referral premium persists. These are substantial demands. But it's not entirely clear whether Hedström and Ylikoski's framework requires all three for a valid mechanism account, or whether the author is setting an additional standard about what a "complete" account needs. The framework distinguishes situations from actions from transformations; it is less clear that it requires dynamic equilibrium analysis. A sentence clarifying this would help: "A mechanism specification in Hedström and Ylikoski's sense requires... [what their framework demands]; a complete economic account of labor market segmentation additionally requires..." This distinction matters because it affects whether the identified gap is a failure of the framework or a limitation of applying an individual-level framework to aggregate outcomes.

3. **The Channel A/B distinction's novelty relative to existing literature is not established.** The author argues that the canonical texts "run these channels together" and that making the distinction "has produced confusion in the policy and empirical literature." But it's unclear whether this decomposition is new to this piece or whether it has been made elsewhere in the post-2004 referral hiring, labor economics, or network literature. The author cites Beaman & Magruder (2012) as testing the reputation mechanism (Channel A), but does not indicate whether they or other subsequent work has explicitly named this Channel A/B decomposition and used it to organize the literature. If this decomposition is new, say so explicitly; if others have made it, cite them. The current framing leaves this ambiguous.

4. **The referrer's selectivity parameter is left open as a question without systematic review of relevant evidence.** The author specifies that reputation mechanisms should produce selectivity "provided the referrer has a sufficiently long time horizon and sufficiently repeated interactions with the employer" and notes that "these conditions are absent (one-time relationships, high turnover, informal rather than employment-relationship connections)." This is plausible, but the author does not review the literature on when informal referral relationships exhibit these conditions. In what fraction of actual referral hiring do these conditions hold? The author treats this as an open empirical question, which is honest, but it leaves the referrer action model somewhat underdetermined. Beaman & Magruder shows that explicit incentives produce selectivity, but that's not the same as showing what happens without them in contexts with varying repetition and time horizons.

5. **The scope of post-2004 review, while acknowledged, may be too narrow.** The author identifies two relevant post-canonical contributions (Beaman & Magruder, Calvó-Armengol & Jackson) but does not characterize the broader post-2004 literature on referral hiring. Since the proposal explicitly flagged the failure mode-"The mechanism is already fully specified in papers outside my four-text corpus"-readers might expect either a more systematic scan or a stronger warrant for confidence that the gaps remain. The author is honest about not having done a comprehensive review, which is appropriate given the scope. But in the introduction or conclusion, you might add a sentence indicating the basis for confidence that major subsequent work has not fully closed these gaps (e.g., "A survey of citations to Montgomery and Granovetter in major post-2004 referral hiring papers shows that the parameterized selectivity assumption persists" or a similar indicator).
