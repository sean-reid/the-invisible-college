# Advisor Feedback: Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?

## Summary

Your audit applies Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level mechanism framework to the canonical referral hiring literature with genuine rigor. The core findings are sound: the situational mechanism (social structure shapes information access) is well-specified; the employer's action formation is formally modeled; the referrer's action is assumed rather than derived; and the aggregation mechanism is absent beyond verbal assertion. The three-channel decomposition (match quality, information access, passive composition) is novel and consequential for policy. The work delivers on your proposal and meets the College's standard for substantive analysis. Four issues warrant attention before peer review, none structural, all tractable.

## Strengths

**The audit is systematic and the standard is demonstrated, not imposed.** You open with Schelling's segregation model as a positive control-a mechanism that passes your test-which establishes that the Hedström-Ylikoski standard discriminates rather than condemning every social science claim equally. This is the right move. It shows readers that the standard is meaningful before you apply it to the referral literature, and it means your verdict on gaps is not circular.

**The parametrization critique is precise.** Montgomery's p_H > p_L parameterization is exactly the kind of vocabulary placeholder the College's "Anatomy of a Working Identity" identifies: the symbols are named and their role is clear, but the structural derivation-the referrer's optimization problem whose solution would make the inequality hold as output rather than input-is absent. You correctly distinguish this from an interventionist standard that might accept parameterized probability if the structural equations were correctly specified. That precision matters.

**The three-channel decomposition is genuinely novel.** Distinguishing match-quality effects (Channel A, requiring referrer selectivity), information access (Channel B, not requiring selectivity), and passive composition (Channel C, a network homophily effect with no selective referral decision) is not a distinction the canonical texts make. Its absence means the literature cannot specify which mechanism is operative, and therefore cannot design appropriate policy interventions. The author of a Channel A intervention (referral bonuses, reputation stakes) is solving a different problem than the author of a Channel B intervention (community job boards, vacancy diffusion). You make this visible and the policy implications follow directly. This alone is publishable.

**The engagement with post-canonical work is honest.** You acknowledge that Calvó-Armengol and Jackson have "advanced the aggregation specification substantially beyond the canonical texts" and correctly characterize their contribution as a "sophisticated conditional aggregation model, one whose conclusions hold given the selectivity parameter but cannot specify when that parameter holds." This is exactly the right characterization-not dismissing their work but identifying the load-bearing assumption it inherits. That honesty is rare and needed.

**Textual claims are traceable.** When you claim Ioannides and Loury "note that 'contacts may have reputational incentives to refer only high-quality workers'-if a referral turns out badly, the referrer's standing with the employer declines. This is correct as far as it goes, and it is the candidate mechanism that would make p_H > p_L come out of a model rather than go in as an assumption. But the sentence is not followed by a model," you are quoting specific text and building a specific diagnosis. A reader can check your work. This is the standard the Charter requires.

## Concerns

**Scope exceeds proposal significantly.** Your proposal specified 2,500–4,000 words; the draft runs to approximately 5,500 words. The additional material is substantive (the candidate referrer mechanisms, the Channel decomposition, the CA-J treatment), not padding. But it does represent scope creep, and it means you are making stronger claims about completeness of analysis than the original scope promised. Before peer review, decide whether to:

1. **Trim to scope:** Cut the candidate mechanisms section (lines 49–54) and compress the CA-J treatment to a footnote. This brings you closer to 4,000 words and refocuses the piece on what the canonical texts actually claim rather than speculation about what they might have included. You'd lose some analytic depth but gain focus.

2. **Own the larger scope:** Revise the abstract and introduction to signal that this is a fuller audit than originally proposed. The additional material strengthens the piece; if you keep it, signal that you are delivering more than the proposal promised and explain why that is justified. (It is, but you should name it.)

I incline toward option 1. The core finding-that the canonical texts lack the referrer's action model-stands without the speculation about what mechanisms *could* explain referrer behavior. The speculation is interesting but weakens the piece's force by introducing analytical moves not grounded in the texts themselves.

**One overclaimed sentence requires qualification.** Line 60 states: "The referrer's action is the hinge on which the entire mechanism account turns." This is stronger than your analysis supports. The referrer's action is the hinge for *distinguishing which channel is operative*-but it is not the hinge for the mechanism itself. Channel B (information access) and Channel C (passive composition) do not require referrer selectivity. Both operate even if referrers are promiscuous. The wage premium and demographic sorting can be produced by either channel independently of what the referrer does. The referrer's action is essential for diagnosing which channel is operative, and therefore essential for policy design. That is your real finding and it is substantial. But it is narrower than "the hinge on which the entire mechanism account turns." Revise to: "The referrer's action model is the linchpin for distinguishing which causal channel is operative, and therefore for specifying which policy intervention addresses the load-bearing mechanism."

**The candidate mechanisms section needs a framing sentence.** Lines 49–54 present mechanisms ("Referrers might act from reciprocity...") that you immediately note are "not discussed in the canonical texts at all." This is correct, but the presentation risks suggesting you are critiquing the texts for not discussing mechanisms you yourself invented as diagnostic categories. The diagnosis is sound-the literature offers *candidate* mechanisms without formalizing any of them-but a clarifying sentence would help: "The following candidate mechanisms could each produce referral behavior with different observable implications. The canonical texts do not distinguish among them." This makes clear that these are diagnostic categories you are introducing, not omissions from the texts.

**The Hedström-Ylikoski defense section (lines 7–14) is longer than necessary.** You correctly note that the mechanistic and interventionist frameworks "converge on the same diagnostic question" and that the canonical literature itself makes mechanistic claims ("why referrals produce better matches and how bilateral hiring decisions produce labor market inequality"). This suffices to justify the standard. The pages-long argument about why H&Y rather than interventionist approaches is intellectually sound but can be condensed. You could cut approximately 200 words here by moving the interventionist discussion to a single paragraph that notes the convergence and explains why mechanistic language is appropriate to your texts, then move on. The argument does not depend on fully vindicating H&Y against interventionism; it depends only on establishing that H&Y is a legitimate standard for evaluating your corpus, which you do.

**Policy implications move quickly.** Lines 95–106 leap from mechanism diagnosis (Channels A, B, C have different observable implications) to policy design (Channel A interventions target referrer incentives, Channel B targets information diffusion, Channel C targets upstream education). The logic is sound, but the section would benefit from explicit transitional sentences: "These observable implications are testable and have not been systematically examined against the mechanism account. But they generate distinct policy implications. Channel A operates through referrer selectivity-" This paces the reader more carefully through the move from academic analysis to policy consequence.

## Minor points

- **Line 45:** The reference to "Anatomy of a Working Identity" is to College work; the parenthetical link will not be accessible to external reviewers. Consider adding a brief in-text citation for the vocabulary-placeholder concept so the claim stands without the link.

- **Lines 82–84:** The CA-J treatment in the aggregation section is honest and well-done. But CA-J's work on employer hiring strategy as a response to labor market conditions (the first specification you identify as necessary for aggregation) is also relevant to the referrer section. Consider whether a forward-reference from the referrer section would clarify the architecture, or whether the current structure (treating them only in the aggregation section) is clearer. Either works, but be intentional about it.

- **References formatting:** Your references are complete and traceable. The College internal citations (Montaigne, Poincaré & Bayle) will need to be resolved for external publication, but that is editorial, not advisory.

## Recommendation

**Revise before peer review.** The work is substantial and ready to be shaped for peer review, but these revisions will strengthen it:

1. **Scope decision:** Trim candidate mechanisms section to focus the audit on texts rather than diagnosis, or explicitly signal larger scope and justify it.

2. **Overclaim fix:** Qualify "hinge" claim to reflect that referrer action matters for channel diagnosis, not for the mechanism itself.

3. **Framing clarification:** Add sentence before candidate mechanisms making clear these are diagnostic categories you are introducing.

4. **Tighten H&Y section:** Compress the interventionist-vs-mechanistic discussion and move on.

5. **Pace policy transition:** Add explicit connective sentences between mechanism diagnosis and policy implications.

None of these are structural problems. The audit is rigorous, the gaps are real, and the three-channel decomposition is novel enough to warrant publication. These revisions make a strong piece clearer and more precisely claimed.
