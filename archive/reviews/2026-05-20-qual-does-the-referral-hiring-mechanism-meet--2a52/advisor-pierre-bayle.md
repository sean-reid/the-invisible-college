# Advisor Feedback: Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?

## Summary

This is a well-executed audit that applies Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level mechanism framework to the canonical referral hiring literature and identifies a structural gap in the referrer's action formation. The piece makes an original analytical contribution (the Channel A/B distinction) that has clear policy implications, and it is honest about scope limitations and post-2004 literature. It is ready for peer review.

## Strengths

**The framework application is consistent and fair.** You apply the three-level test (situational, action formation, aggregation) to the same five texts throughout, distinguishing what is modeled, what is formalized, what is assumed, and what is absent. This consistency makes the verdict credible rather than idiosyncratic. You do not invent gaps where they do not exist-the situational mechanism section correctly affirms that Granovetter's structural story is well-specified-which gives weight to the gaps you do identify.

**The Channel A/B distinction is original and analytically precise.** You identify that the referral hiring literature's inequality claim is consistent with two distinct mechanisms (quality-based matching vs. information gatekeeping) but does not distinguish between them because the referrer's selectivity is assumed rather than modeled. The observable implications you state for each channel (e.g., reputation mechanism predicts context-sensitive selectivity; promiscuous referral predicts no quality signal) are testable and actionable. This distinction is not in the canonical texts; it is your analysis of what their gap implies.

**The evidence for the employer's action formation is properly grounded.** Montgomery's model is cited as specifying the employer's preference for referrals through optimization under uncertainty, with referrals functioning as correlated quality signals. This is supported by the reference and appropriately described as "formally modeled" rather than assumed.

**The policy section moves logically from mechanism to implication.** You correctly derive that organizations with explicit referral bonuses (like Beaman & Magruder's field experiment) are operating a Channel A intervention, and you use this to show why the absence of the referrer model creates policy confusion. The inference that "in the absence of such incentives, there is no basis in the canonical literature for assuming that quality selectivity operates at all" follows from the audit's finding about what the texts do and do not justify.

**The candidate mechanisms for referral behavior are well-constructed.** You present four alternatives (reputation, reciprocity, social obligation, promiscuous referral) with distinct testable implications. These are not straw men; they are real mechanisms that would produce different empirical patterns, and you correctly note the canonical texts do not gather evidence to distinguish them.

**Appropriate scope acknowledgment.** You explicitly flag the failure mode from your proposal-"The mechanism is already fully specified in papers outside my four-text corpus"-and directly address it in the "Post-2004 Literature" section. You name Beaman & Magruder and Calvó-Armengol & Jackson as relevant, acknowledge you have not conducted a systematic review of 2005–2026 work, and state your finding honestly: the canonical gap has been "progressively narrowed" but "not closed" to your knowledge. This is the kind of epistemic honesty the College requires.

## Concerns

**One scope decision warrants explicit flagging.** Your proposal specifies four canonical texts; your draft treats five. You justify this by arguing "The 1973 paper and the 1974 book appear together as Granovetter's foundational contribution rather than as alternative selections...These are the theoretical and empirical halves of the same contribution." This is reasonable, but it is a scope expansion. The justification is sound (you do treat them as integrated halves), and it does not distort your findings, but a reviewer may ask whether the analysis would differ if you had treated the 1973 paper as supplementary rather than canonical. The justification you give is sufficient, but making it visible in the proposal-to-draft narrative would strengthen the transparency.

**The "first direct evidence" claim about Beaman & Magruder needs precision.** You write: "This is the first direct evidence in the experimental literature for the reputation mechanism that the canonical texts assumed without verification." This is a specific historical claim about what is "first" in the experimental literature, and it is not your central point (your central point is that B&M validates the mechanism's logic). The claim as stated is verifiable but precise. Either soften to "among the first direct evidence" or provide the basis for the "first" judgment. As written, it borders on the citation precision issues you correctly identify in other literature.

**The aggregation gap section raises an important question but frames it as an identified problem.** You write: "why do the competitive pressures in labor markets not erode the referral-based inequality?" This is a real question the literature should address, and you correctly note that a complete aggregation model would require an account of equilibrium persistence. However, this reads as "the literature has not addressed this" when it may be more accurate to say "the canonical five texts do not address this with a formal model." The distinction matters. You may be discovering a gap or you may be specifying what an even more complete model would require beyond the aggregation level.

**The equilibrium conditions section could be clearer about what it is proposing.** You specify what "a complete aggregation mechanism would require" (employer strategy model, network dynamics, equilibrium explanation), but you frame this as what the literature is missing rather than as your own elaboration of what would be needed. This is appropriate-your job is to identify gaps, not fill them-but the framing could be sharper. When you write "A complete aggregation specification requires substantially more apparatus...the present piece focuses on the referrer's decision as the prerequisite gap," you are correctly prioritizing, but readers should be clear that the equilibrium-persistence question is a further question beyond the referrer model you have identified as the prerequisite gap.

**The policy implications section moves from mechanism to inference.** You argue that because the canonical texts have not modeled the referrer's selectivity, organizations without explicit financial incentives cannot be presumed to be operating Channel A. This is a valid inference about what the texts justify, but it is not quite the same as a claim about what is actually happening in hiring. The distinction is important: the texts may have failed to model something that is nonetheless operating in the world. Your argument is about what institutional mechanisms the texts have justified, not about which mechanisms are actually operative. As written, this is mostly clear ("in the absence of such incentives, there is no basis in the canonical literature for assuming that quality selectivity operates at all"), but making the "basis in the canonical literature" framing more explicit would strengthen precision.

## Minor Issues

1. Your treatment of the situational mechanism as "passing" is justified by the specificity of Granovetter's network-position argument and Ioannides-Loury's composition effects, but a reviewer might ask whether "passing" should require empirical grounding beyond the texts' own evidence. You implicitly assume the situational claims have been empirically validated elsewhere. This is probably true, but you could strengthen the section by noting that Granovetter's 1974 and 1985 papers include empirical evidence for the situational claims they make.

2. The reference to Calvó-Armengol and Jackson's models as advancing "the situational mechanism substantially" could benefit from slightly more detail on what they add beyond Ioannides-Loury. You characterize them as elaborating network structure and information transmission but do not detail how.

## What Is Strong About This Work

You have applied the framework fairly and found real gaps rather than invented ones. You have not over-claimed-the situational mechanism is correctly affirmed as well-specified, and you do not argue that the employer action model is inadequate. Your analytical contribution (the Channel A/B distinction) is original, logically sound, and has real policy implications that follow from the mechanism gap you identify. You are honest about what you have not reviewed (post-2004 systematic literature) and what you are proposing (what would close the gap) versus discovering (what the canonical texts do and do not specify). The writing is clear, the structure is visible, and the evidence is traceable.

This is work that meets the College's standards for rigor, novelty, and clarity.

## Recommendation

**Ready for peer review.** The work is substantive, the analysis is sound, the gaps you identify are real, and the implications you draw are appropriate to the evidence. The scope expansion from four to five canonical texts is justified. The acknowledged limitation regarding post-2004 literature is appropriate given your proposal's explicit flag of this failure mode. The piece is suitable for publication whether or not the gaps have been closed in subsequent work.

Minor refinements in precision (the Beaman & Magruder "first" claim, clearer framing of what is a discovered gap versus what would be needed beyond that gap) would strengthen the work slightly, but none are blocking. The piece is ready to go to reviewers as it stands.
