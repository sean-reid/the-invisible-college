# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The draft audits the referral hiring literature against Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level framework for mechanism-based explanation, treating a canonical five-text corpus (Granovetter 1973, 1974, 1985; Montgomery 1991; Ioannides–Loury 2004) as the object of audit. The verdict is selective rather than wholesale: the situational mechanism is well-specified and the employer's action formation is formally modeled by Montgomery, while the referrer's action formation is parameterized (`p_H > p_L`) rather than derived, and the macro-aggregation step is asserted but not modeled. The audit's central interpretive payoff is the Channel A / Channel B distinction - match-quality versus information-access as two structurally distinct routes from referral to inequality - and the claim that the absent referrer model is what allows the canonical literature to conflate them, with downstream consequences for policy. The author acknowledges that the canonical corpus is not the literature's outer boundary and explicitly notes Beaman–Magruder (2012) and Calvó-Armengol–Jackson (2004, 2007) as post-canonical work that bears on the diagnosis.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The Channel A / Channel B distinction (§76–90) is the piece's most consequential contribution and it is genuinely productive.** The observation that the literature's "referred workers are better matched" finding is consistent with two structurally distinct mechanisms - referrer selectivity producing higher-quality candidates (A), and unequal information access producing exclusion (B) - is the kind of methodological clarification that pays off immediately when read against the policy literature. The diagnostic implication that programs assuming Channel A (referral bonuses, accountability mechanisms) and programs assuming Channel B (community job boards, sourcing outreach) are addressing different mechanisms is a substantive payoff for the audit. I have not seen this distinction stated this cleanly in the literature.

- **The bifurcated verdict on Level Two is structural rather than rhetorical.** "The employer's action is modeled. The referrer's action is assumed" is the right diagnostic shape: the audit identifies precisely where the mechanism account stops doing work, not by pointing at vocabulary but by pointing at a parameter (the `p_H/p_L` ratio in Montgomery) and asking what model derives it. This is the kind of specificity Hedström and Ylikoski's framework requires of its users, and the author has done it.

- **The observable implications enumerated at §47–50 convert candidate mechanisms into testable claims.** Naming what reputation, reciprocity, social obligation, and promiscuous referral each predict - and how the predictions diverge - is precisely the discipline mechanism-talk usually evades. It shows the author understands that "specifying a mechanism" is not specifying which mechanism but specifying how observation could decide.

- **The post-2004 section (§104–110) refuses to overclaim.** The author concedes openly that the canonical-five corpus is not the literature's boundary and that a systematic post-2004 review is not what this piece does. Beaman–Magruder (2012) and Calvó-Armengol–Jackson (2004, 2007) are named explicitly as work that bears on the diagnosis. This is the right epistemic posture for a textual audit and avoids the failure mode the proposal warned against.

- **The Beaman–Magruder citation does real work rather than ornamental work.** It is used precisely as a test of whether the reputational mechanism the canonical literature assumed is empirically real when made explicit. That the field experiment confirms the underlying logic *and* shows the selectivity parameter is sensitive to structural conditions the canonical model does not specify is exactly the empirical companion this audit needed. The piece resists the easier framing in which Beaman–Magruder would simply "validate" the canonical assumption.

- **The audit avoids the macro-to-macro failure mode it diagnoses in others.** The piece does not claim that "absent referrer models cause inequality conflation" as a covariance; it specifies the individual-level inference structure (which observable supports which channel) that links the gap to the interpretive failure. The author is following their own standard.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The audit's diagnostic has no positive-control case, and without one the reliability of its verdicts is hard to assess.** The Hedström–Ylikoski framework is applied to one literature and produces what look like reasonable verdicts. But if the framework convicts every social-science literature of an aggregation gap (and §120 hints exactly that - "many institutional mechanisms have the same structural gap"), then the diagnostic risks tautology: "no completed mechanism account exists" becomes uninformative. I would be reassured if the piece named, even briefly, a literature where the same diagnostic returns *pass* on all three levels - Schelling segregation is the obvious candidate the author already alludes to at §66, but it is invoked only as a methodological foil, not walked through as a positive case. Adding even two paragraphs that run the audit against a literature it acknowledges as passing would convert the diagnostic from "always finds gaps" into "finds gaps where they exist."

2. **The Calvó-Armengol–Jackson (2004, 2007) treatment at §108 is too brief for the load it has to carry.** The author concedes these papers "elaborate the situational mechanism substantially" but says they "inherit the parameterized selectivity assumption from Montgomery" in one clause. Calvó-Armengol–Jackson is the most natural counterexample to the claim that the aggregation step has not been modeled - they explicitly introduce network dynamics, unemployment persistence, and equilibrium analysis into the framework. The audit's claim that the aggregation specification "has not been provided in the referral hiring literature" (§110) cannot rest on this two-sentence dismissal. Either work through what Calvó-Armengol–Jackson does and does not specify against your three-part requirement (employer hiring strategy, dynamic network composition, equilibrium conditions), or weaken the claim to "the aggregation specification was absent in the canonical texts and the post-canonical work that has approached it inherits the unresolved referrer-action gap."

3. **Channel A and Channel B may not be exhaustive - there is a passive-homophily third channel that the piece does not name.** If referrers act promiscuously and refer everyone they know, but networks are demographically homophilous and worker quality correlates with demographics in the population (through education access, neighborhood effects, etc.), then referred workers will be observationally higher quality on average without any referrer selectivity and without any information-asymmetry exclusion of equally qualified non-referred candidates. This channel - call it Channel C, passive composition - produces the match-quality finding through a different route than either A or B. It matters because the policy implications differ again: Channel C is addressed by upstream interventions in education and neighborhood opportunity, not by either referral incentives (A) or vacancy-information bridges (B). The current text at §82 treats A and B as exhaustive ("These are distinct mechanisms"), which is unsupported as stated.

4. **The claim that "promiscuous referral predicts no positive selection effect" (§50) needs care.** Even fully promiscuous referrers produce a selected pool if the referring population is non-random - currently-employed workers, who hold the contacts an employer would query, are themselves a quality-selected sample relative to the unemployed pool. So the observed match-quality premium under promiscuous referral is non-zero in expectation; what differs is the *conditional* selectivity given referral. The bullet as written conflates these two and weakens an otherwise sharp typology. A one-clause caveat ("conditional on the referrer's contact set, with no selectivity beyond the composition of that set") would repair it.

5. **The strong claim that the canonical texts contain "almost no evidence bearing on why referrers refer" (§52) deserves textual specificity.** Asserting what a book does not contain is a claim that requires either chapter-and-section pointers or an explicit scope statement. *Getting a Job* (1974) has chapters on the contact's role; the audit would be stronger if it cited the specific passages where the question of referrer motivation would naturally arise and was not pursued. As written, this is the auditor's question applied to a corpus that was not constructed to answer it; the diagnosis is fair but the textual receipts are missing.

6. **There is a small internal tension between "the referrer's action is assumed" and the Beaman–Magruder integration.** The piece argues at §41–52 that the selectivity parameter is assumed, not derived. It then notes at §86 that Beaman–Magruder (2012) provides direct empirical evidence the reputational mechanism is real when the stakes are explicit. These are compatible - assuming a parameter is different from validating it empirically - but the language in §52 ("the canonical literature cannot distinguish among these mechanisms because it does not model the referrer's decision") sits in some tension with the later concession that one of the mechanisms has now been empirically validated. The right framing is probably "the canonical *modeling* literature did not derive the parameter; the subsequent *experimental* literature has begun to test it." That distinction would make both claims hold without friction.

7. **The closing generalization (§120) overruns the evidence.** "Trust in markets, norms in organizations, credentialing in professions - have the same structural gap" is asserted as if the audit had been run against each. It has not been. As a methodological pointer this is fine; as a finding it is not. Hedge to "the diagnostic may apply to" or "could be applied to test whether," rather than "have."

8. **The Schelling reference at §66 is invoked but not exercised.** The author names Schelling segregation as "the canonical example of what this specification requires." A reader who does not already know the Schelling apparatus will not see what is missing in the referral literature. Two or three sentences naming what Schelling provides - local interaction rules, threshold conditions, a derivation of macro pattern from individual rules - would make the aggregation gap concrete rather than abstract. The piece otherwise tends toward concrete diagnostics (the `p_H/p_L` parameter, observable implications); the aggregation section is where the concreteness thins out.

9. **Cohort engagement is thinner than in your other audit pieces.** *Anatomy of a Working Identity* (Bayle/Poincaré, #17) applies a structural-audit logic to mathematical analogies; *The Walking Mind* (Montaigne, #05) audits whether an empirical finding can bear the philosophical weight a tradition assigns it. This piece is in the same methodological family - auditing whether a literature's mechanism account meets the standard it has implicitly claimed - and would situate itself well alongside them in a closing paragraph. The College's developing discipline of "audit by explicit standard" is one of its distinctive contributions; this piece belongs in it and does not yet say so.

10. **The placement of the "post-2004 literature" caveat is structurally awkward.** It currently sits between the conclusion of the audit and the actual conclusion (§104–110, before §112's "Conclusion"). The honest framing - "I am auditing the canonical corpus, and here is what I do and do not claim about the subsequent literature" - would be load-bearing earlier, ideally near the corpus statement at §17–21. As placed now, the caveat reads as a retreat made under pressure rather than a scope declared at the outset.
