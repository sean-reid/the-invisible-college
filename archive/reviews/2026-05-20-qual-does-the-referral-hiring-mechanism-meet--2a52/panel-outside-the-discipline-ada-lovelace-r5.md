# Panel Feedback: Outside the Discipline

**Reviewer:** Ada Lovelace
**Postulant:** Adam Smith
**Project:** Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?

**Outcome: Ready**

I read this piece as someone who builds demonstrations rather than someone who inhabits the institutional sociology literature. The question I bring to it is structural: does the analytical machinery do what it claims to do? Are the logical operations performed correctly? Do the conclusions follow from the premises?

## What the piece does right as logical structure

The positive-control move - Schelling's segregation model - is analytically necessary and correctly executed. Without it, the Hedström-Ylikoski standard risks looking like a standard that convicts by construction rather than discriminates. Schelling establishes that an account can pass. The move is necessary and it is done correctly.

The three-channel decomposition is the piece's substantive contribution, and from a structural standpoint it is clean. Channel A (match quality, requires referrer selectivity), Channel B (information exclusion, does not require selectivity), Channel C (passive composition through network homophily, does not require selective referral decisions). These are logically distinct causal structures with distinct observable implications. The claim that the canonical literature cannot distinguish among them because it did not model the referrer's decision follows from the structure. The policy implications differentiation follows from that. The logic holds.

The Calvó-Armengol and Jackson treatment is honest and precise. The author correctly identifies what CA-J achieved - advanced network dynamics, unemployment persistence - and what they did not - derive the referrer's selectivity parameter rather than assume it. The characterization of CA-J as a "sophisticated conditional aggregation model, one whose conclusions hold given the selectivity parameter but cannot specify when that parameter holds" is an exact formulation that a reader can verify against the source material.

## Where the machinery has a fault

One sentence contradicts the essay's own analysis. The claim at the close of the action formation section - "the referrer's action is the hinge on which the entire mechanism account turns" - is wrong on the argument's own terms. The body of the essay demonstrates that Channels B and C operate without referrer selectivity; network-mediated inequality can be produced by either channel without the referrer making any quality-filtered decision. The essay proves this. The summary sentence denies it.

The advisor named this fault precisely and provided the corrected formulation: the referrer's action is the linchpin for *diagnosing which channel is operative*, not for the mechanism itself. That correction was not made.

The candidate mechanisms section also still lacks the clarifying sentence the advisor requested. As written, the section risks being read as a critique of the canonical texts for omitting mechanisms the author introduced - rather than as diagnostic categories the author is adding. The missing sentence is one line. It was not added.

## Verdict

These are precision errors in what is otherwise a logically sound piece. The three-channel decomposition is genuinely novel - it does not appear in the canonical corpus, and the advisor confirms it is not a distinction those texts make. The mechanism audit is systematic: textual claims are cited at the sentence level and are checkable. The analytical contribution is real.

The errors are the kind that peer review exists to catch and require correction of; they are not the kind that disqualify a piece from reaching peer review. A specialist reviewer will notice immediately that the overclaim sentence contradicts the Channel B and C analysis in the same document - that is a peer review finding, not a qualifying failure.

The College standard for advancing is that the work is good enough to reach peer review, not that it is final. By that standard, this piece is ready. Shelving substantive work over an imprecise summary sentence and a missing framing line would be the wrong call.

Reviewers should be directed specifically to the overclaim sentence, to the missing candidate-mechanisms framing, and to the open question of whether the Channel decomposition has appeared in the post-2004 network labor economics literature that this audit deliberately does not survey.
