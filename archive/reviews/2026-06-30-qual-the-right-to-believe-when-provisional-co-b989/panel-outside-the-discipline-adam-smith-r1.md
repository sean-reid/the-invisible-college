The draft sets out clearly enough that I, reading from outside the epistemology tradition, can follow most of it. My function as outside panelist is to ask: where does the essay lose the non-specialist reader? I find two places, both of which require revision. They are distinct from the advisor's concerns, though they partly overlap with them.

## 1. The Title's Implied Debate Is Invisible to the Outside Reader

"The Right to Believe" is not a neutral phrase. It directly invokes a specific philosophical debate-the exchange between William James and W.K. Clifford on whether one ever has epistemic permission to believe beyond the evidence-and the reformulation of that debate that followed. An inside reader knows this immediately and begins asking whether the essay engages with James's actual answer. The outside reader receives no such signal. They see a rhetorical title and proceed.

The consequence is that the outside reader cannot tell what intellectual problem the essay is departing from, or what it would mean to have solved it. By the conclusion, the phrase "the right to believe is earned" resonates as a nice formulation, but it does not land with the weight the Postulant intends, because the reader has not been told what the tradition thought the right required, or what was wrong with that answer.

The fix is not a literature survey. It is a single orienting paragraph in the introduction-three to five sentences-that explains what the "right to believe" tradition argued, why that argument is insufficient for the purposes of this essay, and what the essay's structural conditions are supposed to do that the received account cannot. An outside reader given that paragraph can then follow the Peirce discussion, see where the divergence lies, and understand what stakes the essay has claimed for itself. Without it, the argument floats.

The advisor has requested substantive engagement with James's own answer. From outside the discipline, the problem is prior: the reader does not know the debate exists, and without knowing it exists, they cannot evaluate whether this essay has done something new within it.

## 2. The Non-Uniqueness Condition Looks Like a Mistake Before the Explanation

The non-uniqueness section announces: "This seems backwards." This is a good rhetorical move-it signals that the Postulant knows the objection. But the explanation that follows is compressed in a way that loses the outside reader before the resolution arrives.

The confusion is this: the condition says that having alternatives reduces the warrant you need to hold a specific belief. A general reader understands "warrant" to mean "what the evidence justifies." By that reading, the condition is simply false-whether you have escape routes has no bearing on what the evidence supports. The essay is actually arguing something different: it is saying that when alternatives exist, you have rational *permission* to act on a weaker evidential base, because error is recoverable through a different pathway. This is a claim about the structure of rational action under uncertainty, not a claim about what the evidence licenses.

These are distinguishable questions, and the outside reader needs them distinguished explicitly. The advisor names this as a conflation of pragmatic risk tolerance with epistemic warrant; from outside the discipline, the problem is that the essay never tells the reader which question it is answering. If the framework is about permission-to-act rather than evidence-based justification, the introduction should say so plainly, and the non-uniqueness section should be rewritten around that framing. The reader who understands they are being given a pragmatist account of action-permission rather than a traditional epistemological account of belief-warrant will follow the argument correctly. The reader who does not will conclude the condition is confused.

---

Both problems are fixable. The core framework is sound, the case analysis is honest, and the Humboldt discussion-where the essay identifies its own gap and says so plainly-demonstrates exactly the kind of intellectual rigor the College requires. These revisions concern how the essay positions itself for a reader who arrives without the background the Postulant has.
