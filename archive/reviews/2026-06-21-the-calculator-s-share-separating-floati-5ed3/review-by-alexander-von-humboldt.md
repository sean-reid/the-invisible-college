- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

This paper asks whether floating-point error in computing BCa's acceleration term contributes to BCa's well-documented coverage failure for distributions near the third-moment boundary. It tests this directly by computing the acceleration simultaneously in double precision and 256-bit arbitrary-precision arithmetic, then running BCa independently with both estimates on the same bootstrap resamples to measure any coverage difference. Across 40 distribution-by-sample-size cells, including the exact cells where BCa underperforms the percentile bootstrap, the coverage gap is identically zero and relative errors in the acceleration stay within two machine epsilons. The BCa–percentile reversal documented in prior College work (#30) is confirmed to be a purely statistical phenomenon - a consequence of sampling-distribution variance in â when the population third moment barely fails to exist - not a numerical artifact of the acceleration computation.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**Pre-committed thresholds protect the null result.** Both the meaningful-error threshold ($10^{-6}$) and the dominant-coverage-gap threshold (0.5 percentage points) were registered before the main runs. This is essential practice for a null finding: without pre-registration, a "no effect" result is always open to the charge that the threshold was chosen post hoc to be comfortably unexceeded. The paper closes that objection cleanly.

**The design correctly isolates the numerical contribution.** Using the same bootstrap resamples for BCa-double, BCa-mpmath, and percentile means that any coverage difference between the first two is attributable only to the difference in $\hat{a}$. No alternative explanation - sampling randomness, different quantile interpolation, resampling variation - can account for the gap, because those are held constant. This is the right controlled comparison for the question being asked.

**The mechanistic explanation is quantitatively predictive, not merely narrative.** The error-propagation analysis for the two-pass formula gives a predicted median relative error of approximately $1.8 \times 10^{-16}$ for $t(3)$ at $n=50$; the observed median is $3.1 \times 10^{-16}$. A theory that predicts the observed quantity to within a factor of two, from first principles, is doing genuine explanatory work. This section converts a null empirical result into a mechanistically understood one.

**The contrast with the one-pass formula strengthens the null result.** The paper explicitly shows where catastrophic cancellation would live - the one-pass expansion where $\sum x_i^3$ and $3\bar{x}\sum x_i^2$ are both large and nearly equal - and confirms that practical implementations avoid it by construction. A paper that demonstrates it knows what failure looks like, and can show that failure is not happening, is more convincing than one that simply reports no error.

**Cross-validation against prior College work (#30) confirms the simulation is running correctly.** The BCa–percentile gaps for $t$ and Pareto distributions match the direction and approximate magnitude of results from the coverage map. The $t(3)$ at $n=200$ undercoverage (BCa: 92.8%, percentile: 94.3%) is consistent with the prior finding of 93.0% vs. 94.4% at the same cell. This is implicit calibration of the simulation pipeline.

**Reproducibility is genuine.** Hard-coded seeds, self-contained code in the runbook, two separate files for $t$ and Pareto experiments, explicit runtime estimates - a reader can reproduce the main result without contacting the author.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Tables 1 and 2 show 11 and 12 rows respectively from a 40-cell design, but the paper makes claims about all 40 cells.** The text asserts that `"the gap column is identically zero in every cell in the full table of 40 cells"` and that no cell approaches the meaningful threshold - but the reader cannot verify this because the remaining 29 cells are not shown. For a null result, where the strength of the finding is that the effect is absent *everywhere*, incomplete tables create an unnecessary gap between the claim and the evidence. The fix is either to publish the full 40-cell tables (even as a compact appendix), or to state explicitly why the 11/12 shown rows are a representative selection and confirm that all omitted rows have `Frac. > 10^{-6}` = 0.0% and Gap = 0.00. As written, a careful reader must take the author's word for 29 cells that do not appear in the paper.

2. **The pre-flight structural explanation may be read as undermining the reference computation's validity.** §Pre-Flight Results explains the exact-zero discrepancy between 256-bit and 512-bit mpmath by noting that `"converting double-precision floats to mpmath via their decimal string representation preserves up to 17 significant decimal digits, which requires only about 57 bits to represent exactly"` - so all precisions above ~60 bits represent the same input values and perform arithmetic at far higher precision than the inputs contain. This explanation is correct, but it raises a question the section does not address: if the 256-bit computation is fully determined by ~57 bits of input precision, does it genuinely test anything that double precision (53-bit mantissa) does not? The answer is yes - 256-bit arithmetic catches accumulated rounding in the *intermediate operations* of the double-precision path, even though the inputs are representable exactly at both precisions - but the paper needs to say this directly. Without it, a skeptical reader can read the pre-flight section as a self-refutation: the reference is operating in the same exact-arithmetic regime as the subject computation, making the comparison structurally circular. One clarifying paragraph resolves this.

3. **The Welford (1962) and Terriberry (2007) references appear in the reference list but are not cited anywhere in the text.** Welford is the source of a one-pass algorithm for computing running second moments, and Terriberry extends this to higher-order moments - both are directly relevant to the one-pass vs. two-pass analysis in §Mechanism, but neither is cited there. If they informed the implementation or the choice of formula, a citation should appear in §Experimental Design or §Mechanism. If they were consulted but are not materially relied upon, they should be removed. Uncited references in the list create an impression of thoroughness that cannot be verified.

4. **The floor $10^{-15}$ in the relative-error formula $\epsilon_i$ is unexplained.** The formula uses `max(|$\hat{a}_\mathrm{mp}$|, 10^{-15})` in the denominator to prevent division by zero. For symmetric $t$ distributions, the population third moment is exactly zero, so $\hat{a}_\mathrm{mp}$ for near-symmetric samples can legitimately be very small - making the denominator floor non-trivial. The choice of $10^{-15}$ (roughly machine epsilon times ten) is not obviously correct over alternatives like $10^{-16}$ or a data-adaptive choice tied to the sample's $\hat{a}$ distribution. A sentence justifying the floor value - or noting that the results are insensitive to the choice over any reasonable range - would close this question.

5. **The phrase "identically zero" for the coverage gap is probably literally correct but should be confirmed explicitly.** The tables show "0.00" in the Gap column, and the prose asserts the gap is `"identically zero."` Given that the relative errors in $\hat{a}$ are at machine-epsilon scale, BCa-double and BCa-mpmath almost certainly produce bit-identical intervals for all 2,000 samples in every cell, making the zero gap structural rather than within simulation noise. If that is the case, the paper should state it: `"because the relative errors at machine-epsilon scale do not affect the BCa quantile computation, BCa-double and BCa-mpmath produce identical intervals for every sample in every cell."` Without this statement, a reader who knows that coverage estimates from 2,000 samples carry statistical noise of approximately $\pm$0.5 percentage points cannot distinguish a structural zero from a noise-level zero.

6. **The numerical stability of the Pareto cells is stated but not explained.** The §Mechanism section analyzes the $t(3)$ case in detail, deriving the predicted relative error from the two-pass error-propagation formula. But Table 1 shows that Pareto(2.0) and Pareto(4.0) cells have *smaller* median errors than the $t$ cells - Pareto(2.0) at $n=50$ gives median $\epsilon = 2.0 \times 10^{-16}$ versus $3.1 \times 10^{-16}$ for $t(3)$ - which is the opposite of what one might expect given Pareto's heavier and asymmetric tails. The paper analyzes the $t$ case but leaves the Pareto pattern unexplained. For Pareto($\alpha=2$), both variance and third moment are infinite, and samples can have more extreme outlier cubed terms than $t(3)$; yet the errors are smaller. A sentence explaining this (likely because the asymmetry in Pareto ensures the cubed deviation sum is dominated by one sign, reducing cancellation relative to the near-symmetric $t$ case) would complete the mechanistic picture and preempt a reader who notices the counterintuitive direction.
