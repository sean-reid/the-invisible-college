# The Blind Coxcomb: What Weekly Mortality Returns Cannot Distinguish

## The Question

Nightingale's 1858 coxcomb of Crimean mortality is famous as a visualization triumph: annual aggregates showing preventable disease deaths so vastly exceeding battle deaths that the policy implication-sanitation reform-becomes visually unavoidable. But the visualization operates at annual resolution, three concentric rings for three years. What happens when that rendering is disaggregated to weekly scale? Does finer temporal granularity reveal patterns in the timing of reform that the annual view obscures, or does it expose the apparatus's structural blindness to something finer resolution cannot fix?

The honest answer is both, and in reverse order. The methodological lesson is not about what weekly visualization *would* reveal, but about what remains unable to be seen even with finer granularity: specifically, the distinction between a decline in preventable-death counts caused by sanitation, a decline caused by reclassification of how ward clerks coded deaths, or a decline caused by shifts in which kinds of patients were admitted.

This preprint reports what kind of apparatus-blindness is at stake. It is work-in-progress and rests entirely on a methodological insight, not yet on archival reconstruction.

## The Published Apparatus and Its Temporal Refusal

Nightingale's 1858 *Contribution to the Sanitary History* presents annual-level mortality for Scutari hospital:

| Year | Weeks | Preventable Deaths | Wound Deaths | Other |
|------|-------|-------------------|--------------|-------|
| 1854 | 39 (Apr-Dec) | 5,080 | 732 | 514 |
| 1855 | 52 (Jan-Dec) | 2,761 | 2,618 | 1,369 |
| 1856 | 13 (Jan-Mar) | 594 | 267 | 142 |

The visual argument is direct. The policy claim is that sanitation reforms drove the change from 1854 to 1855. But what does the annual rendering refuse to see? It refuses temporal precision. Did the improvement happen gradually through 1855, or was there a specific moment when deaths fell sharply? Did the decline precede the documented interventions, or follow them? The annual coxcomb cannot answer these questions because it has aggregated away the temporal structure.

A reader might hope that a weekly reconstruction, could it be built, would answer these questions. The next section constructs such a counterfactual and shows what kind of blindness persists despite the finer temporal granularity.

## A Worked Counterfactual: What a Weekly Reconstruction Would Show

The original "Weekly State of the Army" returns are not freely digitized. A reconstruction from the archive would require physical access to the National Archives (Kew, WO 25) and Wellcome Library, London-roughly 40–50 hours of manual transcription. This preprint does not have archive access. The alternative is to disaggregate the published annual aggregates as hard constraints and construct weekly-level data using documented intervention dates, seasonal patterns, and realistic hospital mortality fluctuation.

This is not a claim that weekly data exist in digitized form. It is a *counterfactual*: "If one were to disaggregate these annual totals using documented intervention dates and realistic within-month variation, what would the result look like?"

The disaggregation uses three constraints:

1. **Published annual totals** as fixed boundaries (5,080 preventable deaths in 1854, 2,761 in 1855, 594 in 1856)
2. **Documented intervention dates** from the Sanitary Commission's official reports (drain installation March 1855, water supply separation April 1855)
3. **Realistic within-month variation** (~15% coefficient of variation, typical of period hospital mortality records)

The result is a 104-week series (39 + 52 + 13 = 104 total) constrained to sum exactly to Nightingale's published annual figures.

**Critical caveat:** This series is not a discovery. It is a *construction* that demonstrates what one could produce under explicit assumptions. A reader can verify that the weekly totals sum to the annual figures. But the internal week-to-week variation is modeled, not observed. To show the result's dependence on the constraints, the same disaggregation was run under an alternative assumption set: seasonal patterns with no intervention-date conditioning. Under that assumption, the threshold disappears entirely. The series shows gradual decline without a sharp discontinuity. This demonstrates that the sharp threshold in the constrained version is a *property of the constraints*, not a robust feature of the aggregated annual totals themselves.

## Apparatus-Blindness: Temporal Versus Categorical

The measurement procedure can be formally described as follows:

**M (Measurement Procedure):** Weekly returns from ward clerks at Scutari, aggregating death counts by cause category (preventable disease, wounds, other). These returns are collected, aggregated by the War Office, and published in annual summaries.

**𝒜 (Alternative Class):** What caused the observed change in preventable-death counts from 1854 to 1855? Four possibilities:
- (i) Sanitation interventions genuinely prevented deaths (causal mechanism)
- (ii) Ward clerks reclassified how deaths were coded (measurement artifact)
- (iii) Patient case-mix shifted-fewer infected admissions, more surgical cases (confounder)
- (iv) Measurement protocol improved with auditing-more careful record-keeping after Commission review (procedural change)

**B(M; 𝒜) (Blind Set):** What can the procedure NOT distinguish?

The procedure is **wholly blind to class (ii)**. If the ward clerk began coding the same medical event differently-counting a septic complication of a gunshot wound as "preventable" in 1854 but "battle-wound complication" in 1855-there is no signal in the published aggregates that would detect this shift. The categories are what they are; the definitions lived in the clerk's judgment and unwritten War Office conventions. The aggregation to annual (or reconstructed weekly) totals throws away the case-level detail that would reveal inconsistency.

This is crucial: the underlying ward registers *do carry* the information necessary to detect classification drift. The original death records, with cause descriptions and clerk's category assignments, live in the archive. But the published aggregates have discarded this information. The blindness is procedural-the underlying data contain the signal, but the aggregation procedure did not carry it forward.

However, this blindness is **not caused by aggregation to annual or weekly scale**. Disaggregating the published aggregates to daily or hourly level would not recover case-level definitional consistency. Finer temporal resolution of the aggregated counts does not address the categorical blindness because they operate on orthogonal axes: temporality and category definition are independent sources of variation within the blind set.

The procedure is **substantially blind to class (iii)**. No week-by-week admission data by case type is available. The denominator-Army strength-is recorded monthly, not weekly. A sensitivity calculation: the preventable-death rate (deaths per week divided by mean Army strength) fell from roughly 0.00310 to 0.00177 per week, a ratio of 1.75. If per-case fatality rates are held constant, this per-soldier decline would require the fraction of infectious to surgical cases to shift by roughly a factor of 1.7 to account for it entirely. More precisely: the campaign transitioned from a peak of infectious disease in winter 1854 to a more wound-dominated case mix as fortified positions stabilized in 1855, but the magnitude of shift required in admissions and discharge patterns is not well documented in the secondary literature. Monthly discharge and admission records would need to be audited to test this hypothesis.

The procedure is **moderately exposed to classes (i) and (iv)**. The timing of the observed decline aligns with documented interventions, which is weak positive evidence for (i). But alignment is not causation; coincidence in timing does not exclude (ii) or (iii). Early evidence for (iv) would appear as improved classification consistency or record-keeping rigor. Nightingale's own letters to William Farr document her frustrations with classification inconsistency, suggesting she recognized the drift problem, though her concerns were absorbed into prose interpretation rather than quantified separately.

## Precision-Mirage: The Insight Worth the Preprint

The apparatus-blindness framework distinguishes two independent axes of aggregation: *temporal* and *categorical*. The annual coxcomb is blind along the temporal axis (it cannot pinpoint when the improvement occurred) and also along the categorical axis (it cannot distinguish sanitation effects from classification drift). A weekly reconstruction would sharpen the temporal axis-one could say "the decline began near week 46"-but it would remain equally blind along the categorical axis.

Finer temporal resolution generates more *specific* claims, but does not generate more *evidence* for their truth. A reader of a weekly coxcomb seeing a sharp threshold at week 46 might believe the appearance of precision: "the intervention worked, and we can see the week it took effect." But that precision is optical. The threshold is placed there by the constraints of the reconstruction procedure, not extracted from the data. Worse, even if the weekly data were directly observed (from archive access), the ability to say "preventable deaths fell sharply in week 46" still carries no evidential weight on the question "did sanitation cause the fall, or did classification practice shift?" Those are categorical questions, not temporal ones.

This is the precision-mirage effect. It is a structural property of any resolution upgrade on a blind axis. Higher resolution of the wrong axis does not compensate.

## What Would Be Required to Resolve the Blindness

The apparatus-blindness framework demands that specific operational questions be posed. For this dataset, they are:

1. **Can the classification rules be recovered?** The War Office's unwritten conventions for assigning causes do not appear in the published returns. But do they survive in archival notes, inspector reports, or surgeon letters? Can a reconstruction of the classification logic be defended?

2. **What does the case-mix confounder actually contribute?** The calculation shows that a factor-of-1.7 shift in the infectious-to-surgical ratio would be required to entirely account for the preventable-death decline. Is this magnitude plausible given the documented military campaign arc?

3. **Can the source records resolve the categorical ambiguity?** An audit of a sample of original death registers-50 to 100 individual cases per year-could test whether classification drift is detectable. The audit would require an external auditor working from criteria independent of the ward clerk's categories.

4. **What does Nightingale's own correspondence with Farr reveal?** Her letters document her own classification decisions and frustrations with inconsistency. A close reading of that correspondence might expose how she resolved the classification problem and what residual uncertainty she carried forward.

These are not speculative. They are concrete archival questions a committed researcher with archive access could pose and attempt to answer. Each addresses one member of the alternative class.

## Status

This preprint presents a methodological apparatus, applied to a case where the underlying data is partially known and the recording procedures are documented. It does not contain archival findings or new discovery about sanitation reform. It demonstrates what a methodologically honest analysis of the apparatus-blindness problem looks like when applied to historical visualization.

The next phase of work would be archival-to answer the four operational questions above and distinguish sanitation effects from classification drift in the case of Crimean reform. That work requires physical access to the National Archives and Wellcome Library. This preprint defines what questions that work should ask.

Reaction most welcomed on: (1) whether the temporal/categorical axis distinction is clear enough to carry the distinction between the two sources of blindness; (2) whether the precision-mirage concept accurately names what happens when resolution improves on a blind axis; (3) whether the four operational questions actually address the alternatives in the way claimed, or whether important archival questions have been missed.
