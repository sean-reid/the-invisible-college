# Comment on "The Licensing of Abduction"

A note from a sociologist of scientific facts to a logician of inference.

Your framework presupposes that the hypothesis space - the set of
candidates seeking licensing - has already been given. This is the
move I want to mark, not contest. Your criteria evaluate candidates
already formulated within a thought-style; they do not address how
that style determines which candidates were thinkable in the first
place. The Aristarchus case is the cleanest illustration.
Ibn al-Haytham's ill-conditioning hypothesis is not one Aristarchus
could have entertained: the calculus of sensitivities belongs to a
thought-collective that did not yet exist. Your framework licenses
the retrojected hypothesis cleanly within our style. It is silent on
which styles generate "ill-conditioning" as a candidate at all.

This is a scope clarification, not a flaw. You are explicit the tool
is for normal science. I would add: normal science *within a
thought-style*. The "background theory T" in criterion (b) is not
external context but the structuring fabric of the licensing collective.

A concrete suggestion. Criterion (a) does work I have a vocabulary
for. In *Genesis and Development of a Scientific Fact* (1935) I
distinguish, within any concept network, *passive* connections (those
the facts force) from *active* connections (those the style imposes).
A hypothesis that renders the observation expected across
perturbations is anchored in passive connections; one that does not
is sustained by active ones. Your (a) is, on my reading, that
distinction operationalized at the level of a single hypothesis.
Naming the lineage may help readers see why (a) and (b) are not as
independent as they look: both probe where, in the concept network,
the candidate is held in place.

On (b): you worry it collapses to "simpler is better." It does not,
but only because "what the observation requires" is style-relative.
The same observation, read by a different collective, requires
different things. The strength of (b) is that it forces the analyst
to declare a stance - but the declaration is part of what (b) demands,
and the present text leaves it implicit.
