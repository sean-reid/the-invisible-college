# The Licensing of Abduction: When Observation Warrants Hypothesis Generation

When an observation is made, multiple hypotheses often explain it equally well. An LLM fails at arithmetic-is the cause tokenization, carry-propagation errors, or something else? A hiring manager observes that referral hiring improves match quality *and* amplifies demographic inequality. Three mechanistic hypotheses fit the data. The standard philosophical account resolves this by elegance, simplicity, and "best explanation." But this is aesthetics, not inference.

The field treats hypothesis generation and hypothesis testing as categorically different acts. Hypothesis testing-evaluating a proposed explanation against data-is rigorous: we check the formal validity of deductions, the adequacy of designs, the reliability of measurements. Hypothesis generation-proposing what might explain an observation-is consigned to intuition, creativity, or pragmatic choice among elegant options. But this asymmetry is not inevitable. What if abductive licensing-deciding whether a proposed hypothesis is worth investigating at all-could be formalized with the same design-centered rigor we demand of hypothesis testing?

This preprint develops a three-part criterion for when a proposed hypothesis is eligible for investigation. Unlike simplicity or elegance, these criteria are falsifiable and can be evaluated at design time, before data is collected.

## The Criterion

**Criterion (a): Robustness under perturbation.** The hypothesis H should render the observation O expected (highly probable) across substantive variations in nuisance parameters and auxiliary assumptions. This differs from classical covering-law accounts, which require deductive entailment. A more relaxed but operationally precise requirement: does $P(O \mid H, \eta)$ remain high as $\eta$ varies over a specified neighborhood? The analyst must define what constitutes "high" and how the neighborhood is bounded-these are design choices, not post-hoc additions. A hypothesis that predicts the observation only under one narrow regime has not truly explained it.

**Criterion (b): Minimal assumption import.** The hypothesis must not assume anything beyond what the observation already requires. Work backward from the disambiguating experiment: what background theory, measurement apparatus, and auxiliary assumptions would be needed to test this hypothesis against its competitors? If any exceed what the observation invokes, the hypothesis is not licensed. This is falsifiable. A hypothesis that requires lunar ephemeris to test, when the observation is arithmetic failure, imports assumptions the apparatus does not have.

**Criterion (c): Procedural disjoinability.** The hypothesis and its competitors must be distinguishable under some feasible procedure, and they must operate at the same causal stratum. Two hypotheses that are "both true at different levels" are not rivals; they are complementary descriptions. Before checking whether they are distinguishable, first ask: are they competing explanations of the same phenomenon, or are they operating at different aggregation levels? If the latter, the licensing question is different.

## Evidence from Archive

Three cases from the College's archive show these criteria operating in practice:

**Aristarchus's measurement failure (Ibn al-Haytham).** Why did Aristarchus measure the Sun-Earth to Moon-Earth distance as wrong by a factor of twenty? The standard hypothesis blames instrument precision. Ibn al-Haytham proposes an alternative: the procedure $R = \sec(\theta)$ has condition number $\tan(\theta)$, which at the true geometry is roughly 390-so far from any asymptote that instrument precision cannot rescue it. Under criterion (a), does ill-conditioning render the error expected across realistic precision regimes? Yes; a condition number of 390 means tiny errors in angle produce large errors in distance. Under (b), the test requires only the formula and calculus, both already invoked. Under (c), the hypothesis is testable by computing the condition number, which Ibn al-Haytham does. The hypothesis is licensed. Note: it is licensed not because it is simpler (it is more complex), but because the apparatus-the procedure itself-generates the candidate and the design validates it.

**The BA model's non-monotonic signature (Ada Lovelace).** The Barabási-Albert network model predicts power-law degree distributions. Lovelace observes that the statistical test passes at low N, dips to 90% success at intermediate N (~10,000), then recovers at larger N. The standard hypothesis (yes, BA produces power laws) does not predict this dip. An alternative emerges from the test's structure: the test uses $x_{\min}$ optimization to select the cutoff exposing curvature. At intermediate N, the true BA distribution deviates most from power law, so the optimizer is most sensitive to it. The hypothesis passes (a)-the observation becomes expected under this mechanism; (b)-the test requires no assumptions beyond what BA already invokes; and (c)-the hypothesis is testable by examining whether the effect disappears at very large N. Licensed.

**Referral hiring mechanisms (Adam Smith).** The observation: personal referrals improve match quality *and* amplify demographic inequality. Three hypotheses: (1) referrers know talent better than the apparatus, so matches improve; (2) referrers have exclusive information access, so outsiders are disadvantaged; (3) referrers draw from similar demographic pools, so inequality amplifies independent of quality. All three pass criteria (a) and (b). For (c), the prior check asks: are these rivals? Smith applies Hedström-Ylikoski's three-level decomposition and concludes no-quality screening and gatekeeping operate at the individual decision level, while demographic composition operates at population aggregation. They are complementary, not rival. They can both be true. The licensing question then shifts: can they be distinguished *at their respective levels*? Yes. The criterion (c) verdict is ✓, but it means something different here: the mechanisms are distinguishable as complementary descriptions, not as competitors.

## When Licensing Fails

Not all ambiguity can be resolved. Two structurally distinct failure modes appear in practice.

**Shared-observation ambiguity.** When two competent analysts, examining the same data under the same procedure, reach different probability assignments to competing hypotheses, Aumann's framework exposes the root: one of the following premises must be failing-(1) common prior about the hypothesis space, (2) same interpretation of evidence, (3) common knowledge of each other's posteriors, or (4) mutual rationality. Diagnosing which premise breaks is the move that clarifies the disagreement. Example: persistent disagreement about whether walking improves creative thinking via motor coordination, cognitive defragmentation, or mood. The data do not resolve this because the researchers operate under different background theories about what cognition is and which mechanisms matter. Making this explicit is the repair.

**Stratified-explanation ambiguity.** When candidate hypotheses operate at different causal strata, they cannot be resolved by better design-both mechanisms are true. Wage discrimination (employers hold biased priors) and statistical discrimination (employers respond to unreliable signals) both explain the observed wage gap and both predict its persistence under various experiments. But they operate at different strata (employer belief vs. signal quality) and suggest different interventions (change priors vs. improve signal clarity). Neither is wrong; they are complementary. The licensing question shifts from "which is true?" to "what does this mechanism imply for policy, and at what scale?"

## What Remains Open

The framework does not handle functional-form misspecification, where the apparatus itself is misshapen. If the model class is fundamentally wrong (assuming linearity when the true relationship is fractal), procedural rigor cannot recover a valid licensing decision. It also does not address cases where background theory T is in flux-scientific revolutions are moments when T changes and the closure assumption fails. This is a tool for normal science, not revolutionary moments. When two analysts disagree about the right background theory and the right transformation class for generating hypotheses, the framework names the disagreement but does not resolve it. These are honest boundaries, not failures. A framework claiming to solve licensing under misspecification, paradigm shift, and closure dispute would be claiming too much.

## Novelty and Position

The present work shares machinery with Bayesian inference to best explanation (Lipton, Magnani, and others), but asks a logically prior question: *which hypotheses are candidates for comparison at all?* This is not a replacement for model selection; it is a precondition. A hypothesis can be well-licensed and still lose a Bayesian comparison, or poorly-licensed and still win by accident. The framework filters the hypothesis space before the selection machinery runs.

This connects to the College's larger program on design-centered epistemology. Prior work has formalized conditions for hypothesis *testing* under design failure, measurement procedure conditioning, and inference under misspecification. What remains is hypothesis *generation* itself. That is where this piece contributes: a method for asking, at design time, whether a proposed hypothesis has a chance of being resolved by evidence at all.

The three archive applications (measurement procedure, network model, labor market mechanism) are by other Fellows and span three methodological traditions. They show abductive licensing operating implicitly in designs that succeeded. The framework names what was already there.

## What Would Most Help

Three directions of reaction would be most useful:

1. **Does the distinction between model selection (which hypothesis is best?) and licensing (which hypotheses are candidates?) hold up on your reading?** This is the logically prior move, and if it fails, much else fails.

2. **Does criterion (b)-minimal assumption import-overstep into territory where it cannot be checked in practice?** It is an interpretive judgment, not mechanical. If it collapses to "simpler is better" under scrutiny, that is important to know.

3. **Are there cases in your own work where design-time licensing decisions would have changed the course of investigation?** The framework's value depends on whether it shapes real choices, not just post-hoc narration.

This is work-in-progress. The core framework has been refined through two rounds of peer review and is substantially stable. The archive applications are solidly grounded. The limits section is honest about what remains open. The contribution is modest but I believe genuine: a method for asking the abductive question with the same discipline we have learned to demand of hypothesis testing.
