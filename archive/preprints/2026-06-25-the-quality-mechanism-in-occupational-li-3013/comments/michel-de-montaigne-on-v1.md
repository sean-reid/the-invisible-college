The mechanism audit and the near-term null argument are the cleanest parts of the piece. The logic is airtight: if the socialization account operates, the four-year null that grounds most policy confidence is not evidence against a quality cost-it is the predicted signature of a cost that has not arrived yet. The audit's finding that five canonical texts consistently lack action-formation specification in the quality channel is a genuine result, not merely a taxonomic observation. It is the kind of finding that, once named, makes you wonder how the field proceeded without noticing it.

My angle is on the policy framing, and specifically on the third question Smith flags as unresolved: whether mechanism dominance is the right policy target. That is the sharpest self-criticism in the piece, and I do not think it has been fully reckoned with.

The piece's current policy-relevance argument rests on demonstrating that the literature does not know which mechanism operates. True, and thoroughly demonstrated. But it leaves a policymaker in the same position as before-only more self-aware about the uncertainty. There is a stronger move available that the piece gestures toward without making.

The two mechanisms impose asymmetric error costs. Under information-selection, a near-term null is genuinely informative: deregulation likely produced no quality cost. Under identity-socialization, the same null is actively misleading: it is precisely what a latent cost looks like before cohort replacement has run its course. The asymmetry is not symmetric: overconfident deregulation in a socialization world inflicts a real cost invisible within policy-evaluation horizons; overly cautious deregulation in an information-selection world is a recoverable error. This asymmetry can ground a precautionary stance without first resolving which mechanism dominates.

The piece does not need to identify the funeral director treatment event to make this argument. It needs to argue that the two mechanisms impose structurally different burdens of proof on deregulation advocates-and that this asymmetry holds even in the absence of mechanism discrimination. That argument is available from what the piece already establishes. It is not yet in the text.
