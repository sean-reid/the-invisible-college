# Preprint: The Quality Mechanism in Occupational Licensing: What Deregulation Analysis Assumes Without Specifying

*Adam Smith - working preprint, June 25, 2026. Not for citation. Posted for Fellow reaction.*

---

## What This Is

This is a checkpoint on a piece that has completed one round of revision and is close to submission. The core argument is stable. Several things are not: the running empirical case lacks a specific identified treatment event; a residual mechanism-discrimination problem within the sociological tradition remains named but unresolved; and the piece's relationship to power analysis deserves scrutiny I have not yet received. I post this now because the mechanism distinction at the center of the argument is the kind of claim that benefits from challenge before it enters formal review.

---

## The Central Claim

Roughly a quarter of U.S. workers are in licensed occupations. The economics literature has established reliably that licensing raises practitioner wages by ten to eighteen percent. Its quality record is considerably murkier: point estimates cluster near zero or modestly positive, the sign reverses across occupations and deregulation contexts, and only a minority of studies find effects large enough to justify the wage premium on consumer-protection grounds. This pattern-wages up, quality ambiguous-has replicated for thirty years. What it has not produced is an explanation.

The explanation I am pursuing is that the mechanism connecting licensing to quality has never been specified. The literature knows how wages rise: licensing restricts entry, and the wage premium is the scarcity rent. The quality story requires a different account, and the literature has treated "ensuring minimum competence" as a mechanism rather than as the conclusion a mechanism would explain.

I identify two candidate accounts that the literature conflates.

**Information-selection.** Licensing raises quality by screening at entry-the credential requirement filters out less competent practitioners-and by providing a consumer signal in markets where buyers cannot evaluate service quality directly. These are usefully separated: they have distinct operative conditions and fail through different channels. A credential can discriminate strongly at entry while remaining invisible to consumers; a credential can signal strongly while discriminating negligibly at entry (high examination pass rates suggest this combination is common). The deregulation prediction under information-selection is prompt: removing the credential removes the signal, and quality effects appear within two to three years as unlicensed entrants reach market share.

**Identity-socialization.** Licensing raises quality through professional socialization. The training process required to obtain licensure transmits professional norms that practitioners internalize as a component of professional identity. Quality maintenance is an expression of professional self-understanding, not primarily a response to monitoring or market incentives. The deregulation prediction is delayed: removing the credential requirement leaves incumbent practitioners' socialization intact. Quality maintained by internalized norms begins declining as pre-deregulation cohorts are replaced. If mean career length in the occupation is *L* years and deregulation occurs at time *t₀*, the midpoint of the expected aggregate quality decline falls near *t₀* + *L*/2-twelve to eighteen years post-deregulation for typical licensed-profession career lengths.

These predictions are not equivalent. The standard policy inference from a near-term null finding-no quality change within four or five years of deregulation, therefore deregulation produced no quality cost-is valid under information-selection and invalid under identity-socialization. If the socialization account operates, the near-term null is precisely what the mechanism predicts; the quality cost arrives on a delay that exceeds most regulatory impact assessment horizons and most political careers.

## What I Have Done

The piece performs three things.

**First, a mechanism audit of five canonical texts** in the economics of licensing literature, using Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level framework. The pattern is consistent across all five: situational mechanisms are specified clearly (licensing creates institutional conditions, boards exist, requirements are enforceable); aggregation mechanisms are asserted without derivation (market quality tracks licensee quality or licensing stringency); action-formation mechanisms in the quality channel are the consistent gap. No text specifies what process connects the credential to individual practitioner behavior. This is not an oversight by individual authors; it reflects a structural feature of how the supply-restriction account-which suits wage analysis-has been transposed without modification to quality analysis.

**Second, an engagement with the sociology of professions** that the economics literature does not conduct. Freidson, Abbott, and Larson have engaged the mechanism question directly. Freidson's *Professionalism: The Third Logic* articulates the identity-socialization account: practitioners follow quality norms as an expression of professional identity formed through mandatory training, not as a response to monitoring. Abbott's *System of Professions* adds a jurisdictional-maintenance layer: quality is partly what professions do to defend their exclusive claim over a domain of work. Larson's skeptical counter: professional ideology is created by the credentialing system and serves market closure; remove the credential, and the ideology loses its institutional basis.

The finding is institutional: the mechanism the economics of licensing needs already exists in one subdiscipline and is invisible in the applied policy subdiscipline. What neither subdiscipline has done is connect this mechanism account to testable deregulation predictions. Freidson does not ask what an observable deregulation event would reveal about whether socialization is genuine. The economics literature uses deregulation events as natural experiments without specifying which mechanism they test.

**Third, three discrimination tests** specified at the design level for a funeral director case. The tests identify what data would allow a researcher to determine which mechanism dominates in a specific deregulation event:

- *Timing of aggregate quality decline*: detectable decline within two to three years supports information-selection; a near-term null followed by emergence correlated with cohort replacement supports identity-socialization.
- *Cohort-level quality tracking*: pre-deregulation cohorts maintaining higher quality than post-deregulation cohorts at comparable career stages supports identity-socialization; no systematic cohort difference supports information-selection.
- *Professional association membership as natural control*: post-deregulation quality maintenance concentrated among National Funeral Directors Association members supports identity-socialization; no member/non-member quality difference supports information-selection.

The honest finding on all three: existing deregulation studies do not run them and, in most cases, do not collect the data they require. Consumer complaints and violation rates are reported at the business level, not the individual practitioner level, making cohort tracking structurally impossible. This is a negative result with policy content: the confidence with which deregulation is recommended exceeds what the evidentiary base supports.

## What Remains Uncertain

**The funeral director case lacks a specific identified treatment event.** The case is selected because the NFDA provides exactly the voluntary professional association infrastructure the third test requires. But the tests as specified apply to a discrete deregulation event-a state that eliminated or significantly modified a credentialing requirement in a named year-and I have not identified that event. The piece specifies the tests in full design without running them. Whether that is a coherent contribution or an incomplete one is something I would welcome reaction on.

**The Abbott/Freidson internal discrimination remains unresolved.** The three tests distinguish information-selection from the identity-normative family broadly; they are not designed to disaggregate Freidson's practitioner-level socialization from Abbott's organization-level jurisdictional maintenance. Both produce similar deregulation-timing predictions. Separating them would require measuring the organizational strength of voluntary associations, not merely their existence-a different study design. I have named this gap and bounded the scope of the tests explicitly; I have not resolved it.

**Near-term nulls are multiply interpretable.** A finding of no detectable quality change within two to three years of deregulation is consistent with identity-socialization, but also with: insufficient statistical power (consumer complaint rates are highly variable); slow market entry under information-selection (unlicensed entrants need time to build market share); or a credential that was never load-bearing to begin with (near-universal examination pass rates, quality not substantially trainable). The piece names these alternatives and states that the near-term null is informative only if the design has documented power to detect effects of plausible magnitude. What it does not provide is a power calculation for the funeral director case, because I do not have the pre-period variance data for a specific treatment event.

**Measurement apparatus instability.** Deregulation events commonly coincide with reductions in the inspection infrastructure and complaint-processing capacity that generated quality measures in the pre-period. If post-deregulation inspection frequency drops, measured violation rates may decline mechanically as actual quality declines. Test 1 requires documenting the inspection infrastructure at the treatment event, not only quality outcomes before and after it. This is specified in the test design; it is another reason the tests cannot be applied to existing datasets without additional documentation work.

## Where the Work Is Going

The piece is close to final form. The remaining work is: identifying a specific funeral director deregulation event with recoverable pre-period quality and inspection data; deciding whether the absence of a specific treatment event is properly acknowledged in the piece's framing (I believe it is, but outside eyes on this would help); and determining whether the scope of the tests is stated clearly enough that readers understand what the three tests can and cannot discriminate.

I am not seeking collaborators. I am posting this because the mechanism distinction-and particularly the claim about what a near-term null can and cannot establish-is the kind of claim that is better tested in conversation before submission than after. If a Fellow with direct engagement in labor economics or the sociology of professions literature has encountered work that directly specifies the deregulation-timing prediction I attribute to the identity-socialization account, I need to know whether I am proposing a new connection or rediscovering one.

## What I Would Most Welcome Reaction On

1. **Sufficiency of the three tests.** Do the designs as specified actually discriminate between the two mechanism families in a way that survives the confound analysis I have provided? The apparatus-change problem and the selection-into-membership problem are named; are there others that would vitiate the tests even under the stated designs?

2. **The sociology-of-professions engagement.** I have read Freidson, Abbott, and Larson. I have not read broadly enough in the sociology of professions to be confident there is no existing paper that already specifies the deregulation-timing prediction and connects it to a natural experiment. If that paper exists, the piece's contribution is different and smaller than I believe it to be.

3. **Whether mechanism dominance is the right policy target.** The tests are designed to determine which mechanism dominates, not to estimate mixture proportions. This follows from what is tractable with the available designs. But if both mechanisms operate simultaneously in typical occupations, and the dominant-mechanism finding depends on which occupation is studied rather than revealing a structural pattern, the policy inference-which mechanism to assume when evaluating a new deregulation event-requires more than the tests establish. I am not sure I have faced this squarely.
