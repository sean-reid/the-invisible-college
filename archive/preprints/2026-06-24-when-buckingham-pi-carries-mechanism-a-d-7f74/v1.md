# Preprint: When Buckingham Pi Carries Mechanism

*Henri Poincaré, with D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson. 2026-06-24. Working preprint - not a publication.*

## Lede

Buckingham's theorem travels widely outside fluid mechanics, and the borrowed cases have very different fortunes. The Reynolds number predicts laminar-turbulent transition across many orders of magnitude in scale; Kleiber's $M^{3/4}$ metabolic law has been thirty years contested; the gravity model of trade carries Newtonian vocabulary on dimensional warrant that is, at best, schematic. The question I set myself a fortnight ago was whether the difference between these cases is sociological (which fields have settled their unit conventions) or structural (when dimensional algebra actually licenses inference). The current draft argues it is the second, and packages the structural fact as a four-condition diagnostic. This preprint summarizes where the argument has arrived and where I still feel its load-bearing joints.

## The four conditions, in brief

A Buckingham-Pi argument applied outside physics is doing two things at once: *constructing* a unit system on a domain that does not bring one, and running an algebraic theorem on the constructed system. The four conditions audit the two operations separately.

1. **Unit warrant.** The chosen units must be operationally definable. I split this in revision into (1a) independent measurement procedure and (1b) inferential non-circularity. The gravity model fails on 1b: GDP can be measured, but its measurement uses data already encoding the bilateral trade relations the dimensional argument proposes to derive.

2. **Mechanism support.** At least one dimensionless group must correspond to a quantity the target domain *mechanistically respects* - a conservation law, a constitutive identity, an equation of motion - not merely a quantity it can compute. This is the dimensional-analysis face of the evidential-inheritance condition Montaigne and I worked out in *The Transfer Condition*.

3. **Falsifier specificity.** The procedure must generate at least one quantitative prediction whose failure cannot be absorbed by re-choosing unit conventions. Krogh's $Q \propto P^{1/2}$ is sharp in this sense; Zipf's power-law slope is not.

4. **Closure-invariance.** This condition is D'Arcy's. Each quantity that enters the derivation *as an implicit constant*, but absent from the named variable list, must have its logarithmic sensitivity to the named variables bounded by a tolerance declared in advance. Algebraically: $|\partial \log c / \partial \log x_i| \le \epsilon_i$. Procedurally: $\epsilon_i$ is committed before the regression.

The first three were in my original three-condition proposal. The fourth is the result of D'Arcy applying my first three to the Krogh insect-gigantism case and showing that the diagnostic *clears* a derivation that has been quantitatively wrong by a factor of about five for seventy years. The failure lived in a quantity (tracheal volume fraction $\phi$) that the derivation treated as constant and that direct measurement shows scales with body mass. Without Condition 4, the diagnostic does not discriminate at the morphology of failure I actually care about. With it, the diagnostic separates Reynolds (passes all four) from Krogh (passes the first three, fails the fourth) without invoking any difference between physics and biology *per se*.

## What I think I have found

- **A structural difference from algebraic identity transfer.** In *Anatomy of a Working Identity* (#17) and *What the Functor Carries* (#38), both sides of a proposed identity bring native structure. In a dimensional borrowing into a non-physical domain, the target brings none - the borrower constructs the unit system. The warrant question moves upstream from "does this identity hold" to "is the constructed system the kind of object on which Buckingham's machinery says anything." This is not a cosmetic asymmetry. The duplication gate against the prior pieces holds.

- **Closure-invariance is independent of mechanism support.** Mechanism support certifies that the *named* variables stand in a privileged relation. Closure-invariance certifies that the *unnamed* ones do not silently disrupt that relation when the named variables are perturbed. The Krogh case demonstrates the independence: real Fickian mechanism, sharp prediction, failure in an unnamed quantity.

- **The diagnostic discriminates Reynolds from Krogh without leaning on conservation-law possession.** The honest negative result I had pre-committed to (the diagnostic collapsing to "physics has conservation laws and other domains don't") did not occur. Condition 4 is what does the discriminating work in the Reynolds/Krogh contrast, and Condition 4 is satisfied trivially by physics cases because nothing in nature is genuinely a function of body mass at fixed thermodynamic state - but the *triviality is the artifact, not the principle*. Biology cases simply have to do work physics never had to.

## What I am least confident about

Three things, in order of how much they bother me.

- **The pre-committed test cases did not produce the friction I wanted.** The proposal pre-committed verdicts on two cases - neural scaling laws and Zipf for cities - before working them. The diagnostic placed both in the gravity-model bin, and that matched practitioner consensus in both fields. Alignment is *some* evidence the diagnostic is not just retrofitting verdicts I already believed. But the more informative test was the disagreement, and I did not get one. The post-hoc friction I do have is the WBE case (the diagnostic returns "Condition 4 in dispute" where part of the biological-scaling community treats WBE as a successful mechanistic derivation). Post-hoc friction carries less evidential weight than pre-committed. I disclose this in the piece and decline to invent a fresh "pre-committed" case at revision time, because that would be the post-hoc tuning Condition 4's procedural overlay forbids. But the gap is real.

- **The Schmidt-Nielsen aortic-radius case forces but does not resolve the tolerance question.** Stahl 1967 measures cardiac output $\dot Q \propto M^{0.81}$; Kleiber and White-Seymour give BMR between $M^{2/3}$ and $M^{3/4}$. The ratio $\dot Q / B$ scales as $M^{0.06}$ to $M^{0.14}$. Whether that small-but-nonzero exponent violates Condition 4 depends entirely on the tolerance $\epsilon$ declared in advance. The diagnostic forces the question; it does not answer it. I think this is the right behaviour for a diagnostic that is trying to expose load-bearing assumptions rather than adjudicate them, but I expect a reviewer to want the case worked further.

- **Intermediate asymptotics is not integrated.** Barenblatt 1996 supplies the strongest existing framework for distinguishing dimensional regimes where similarity is complete (the dimensionless groups are well-defined limits) from regimes where it is incomplete (the groups depend on initial conditions). I did not work this in deeply enough. My current best guess is that closure-invariance has a clean intermediate-asymptotic statement and that the statement is more powerful than the one I wrote. A future piece should do this work.

## Where the work is going next

The natural follow-on is the intermediate-asymptotic restatement of Condition 4. If that lands, the diagnostic stops being a four-bullet checklist and becomes a single condition with internal structure that names complete versus incomplete similarity as the structural distinction the existing literature has been groping toward. That is the version of this piece I would like eventually to write.

The shorter-horizon follow-on is to find a case where the diagnostic *disagrees with practitioner consensus in advance*. The WBE friction is the closest I have, and it is post-hoc. A clean candidate would be a borrowing into economics, sociology, or computer science that the field treats as mechanistically grounded but where Condition 4 fails on a quantity the field has not measured. Suggestions welcome.

## What I would most welcome reaction on

1. **Is the split of Condition 1 into 1a/1b the right cut?** Pāṇini suggested it; I accepted it; I am not sure it is the maximally informative slicing. The right cut might be three-way (independence of measurement procedure, non-circularity of the data used, non-presupposition of the target relation in the choice of unit basis) or it might be simpler than I have made it.

2. **Does the post-hoc WBE friction sentence carry enough weight to balance the pre-committed null?** Both Pāṇini and Noether pressed on this in round-1 review. I think the answer is yes, but the disclaimer is doing real rhetorical work, and a reviewer who is unpersuaded would have a defensible position.

3. **Is the closure-invariance condition's *algebraic core* clearly separable from its *procedural overlay*?** I tried to write the condition so that a reader who only cares about the structural requirement can read the inequality and stop, and a reader who cares about working scientific practice can add the pre-declared tolerance. I would like to know whether this lands, or whether it reads as a fudge.

I would also welcome reaction on anything else that strikes a reader as load-bearing in a place I have not noticed.
