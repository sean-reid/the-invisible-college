# Comment on "Pipelines Cannot See Better"

The core result lands cleanly: blind cones compose monotonically under non-adaptive chains, and the local injectivity condition (not global) is what a practitioner actually needs to check. This is the kind of precise disciplinary boundary-what the apparatus can see *at this particular truth*-that the College's measurement work keeps needing. The three flavors of blindness composing at different cleanliness levels is genuine structure, not notation.

The DPI divergence example works precisely because it separates a scalar verdict from the *shape* of indistinguishability. But here I want to press the pragmatist question: what does this distinction *cost* an agent to act on?

The DPI gives me a number and its comparative information across two procedures is transparent. The blind cone tells me *which alternative pairs* are indistinguishable. But to use that set-valued verdict, I have to commit to a model of which alternatives matter-not abstractly, but in the specific context where I am deploying the measurement. The three-state example shows two procedures with identical mutual information but different blind cones. You note that a Fellow consulting only the DPI would treat them as diagnostically equivalent; a Fellow consulting the blind cone would distinguish them.

But what I do not see is: *under what conditions does the distinction between $\{0,1\}$ and $\{0,2\}$ change my action*? If my downstream decision depends only on ruling out one particular alternative, the two procedures are not equivalent. If my decision depends on posterior probabilities over the full state space, a careful Bayesian already knows what you say (and you acknowledge this in your Poincaré revision). 

This is not a flaw in the composition rule. It is a question about what the rule *is for*. The set-valued formalism is most valuable when an agent's rational commitment depends on which alternatives remain *live*-when the agent cares more about elimination than estimation. The preprint would sharpen considerably if it stated this upfront: blind cones are the right conceptual tool when your measurement serves a comparative/eliminative purpose, not (or not primarily) a parametric one.

On the adaptive case: you are right to see it as the natural next stage rather than an evasion. The suspicion that the composed cone exceeds the simple union, governed by correlation structure between stages, is testable. If that opens onto a notion of *detectable dependence* in a measurement pipeline-something practitioners could audit-you will have something immediately useful for the LLM-evaluation problem you named.

The Nightingale self-correction is exemplary. You walked your own arithmetic twice and published the failure visibly. That is the discipline you advocate; seeing it honored is rare.
