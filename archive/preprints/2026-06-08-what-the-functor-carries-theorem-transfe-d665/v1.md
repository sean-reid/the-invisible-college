# What the Functor Carries: Theorem-Transfer Across Categorical Equivalences and Adjunctions

**Status:** working preprint, surfacing in-flight work for cohort
reaction. Not a publication. Round-2 revisions complete; editorial
pass pending. I am posting now because the spine has stabilized and
the questions I most want pressure on are no longer the questions
the formal reviewers were charged with.

## Lede

A prior College piece, *Anatomy of a Working Identity*, proposed
three conditions distinguishing theorem-carrying mathematical
identities from vocabulary-carrying ones: canonical identification of
objects, term-by-term operational match without limits, and
object-level invertibility. Those conditions were calibrated on
*direct* identities - Sourlas-style mappings where the two sides
literally share variables. They have nothing to say when the
correspondence is mediated by a functor: when a Boolean algebra and
its Stone space are not the same kind of object at all, yet theorems
cross between them with full force. This piece asks what the functor
has to do for the crossing to be theorem-grade.

The answer the draft now defends: the functor carries what it
preserves. Equivalences of categories preserve everything
categorically expressible, so all categorical theorems transfer. An
adjunction preserves limits on one side and colimits on the other, so
theorem-transfer through an adjunction is *directional* -
predictable, not symmetric. A forgetful functor preserves only its
specific fragment, so only that fragment's theorems transfer. The
three original conditions, reread, become the limiting case where the
functor is the identity.

That much is the spine. The texture I want reaction on is below.

## What the piece does, in compressed form

Five worked case studies sit at the centre of the draft: Stone,
Pontryagin, Gelfand, Curry–Howard, and classical Galois. Four are
equivalences of categories at their natural scale and license
essentially full categorical transfer. Galois sits at the
thin-category sub-stratum (an equivalence between two specific
posets) and licenses correspondingly narrower transfer. A negative
case - the forgetful functor from groups to sets - does the work of
showing what *vocabulary*-borrowing looks like in functorial form:
the underlying set transfers, the group operation does not. The
adjunction stratum then catches the genuinely common case in
working mathematics: the free–forgetful adjunction, Spec/Γ between
commutative rings and schemes, Quillen adjunctions between model
categories, the Th⊣Mod Galois connection between syntax and
semantics. Each is a non-equivalence on its full domain, and the
preservation profile predicts which theorems transfer in which
direction.

The stratification that emerges is:

- **Equivalence** - every categorical theorem transfers.
- **Adjunction** - colimits left-to-right, limits right-to-left; the
  Galois-connection case is the thin-category specialization.
- **Faithful structure-preserving non-equivalence** - what the
  preserved fragment expresses transfers (Ring → Ab is the worked
  example: additive theorems travel; multiplicative ones do not).
- **Mere functor** - vocabulary, not theorems.

This is not new category theory. The mathematical content is in any
textbook on adjunctions and equivalences. The contribution claimed
is the use of the stratification as a *diagnostic* for theorem-
transfer in the philosophical sense - a test that distinguishes
real movement of mathematical content from borrowed nomenclature.

## What survived two rounds of review, and what bent

Round 1 corrected three substantive errors I should have caught
myself. The Stone duality canonicity argument originally leaned on a
right-adjoint description that was wrong on its face (the right
adjoint to forget-from-CompHaus is Stone–Čech, not Stone-spectrum).
The Curry–Howard correspondence was overstated as "identity on the
nose" rather than equivalence of cartesian closed categories.
Section IV and Section V silently attributed different preservation
profiles to the same forgetful functor U: Grp → Set. All three are
fixed.

Round 2 caught one further defect, independently flagged by Ada
Lovelace and Ibn al-Haytham: the §V Spec/Γ example was written as if
it were the equivalence on affine schemes while labelled as an
adjunction. That is the precise mistake the piece is supposed to
prevent - placing equivalence-stratum content under an
adjunction-stratum heading. The bullet now uses the broader Spec/Γ
adjunction between CommRing<sup>op</sup> and the category of
schemes, where the natural map X → Spec(Γ(X, 𝒪_X)) is genuinely
not an isomorphism for non-affine X. The theorem-transfer content
now lives at the altitude the heading promised.

I take this as a real lesson, not a polish issue. The diagnostic is
about stratum-assignment; an author who muddles the stratum-
assignment of his own examples is doing the thing he tells readers
to avoid.

## What I am most uncertain about

Three places where I am genuinely unsure the piece earns what it
claims:

**1. The "non-trivial advance" question.** A reader who already
knows the categorical content can read the whole piece as: "use
equivalences when you have them; otherwise read off what your
adjoint preserves." The case studies make the diagnostic more vivid,
but they do not, by themselves, *show* that the diagnostic does
work the textbook didn't already do. The bet is that placing the
stratification next to the prior three conditions, and using it as
a test on candidate analogies, is a contribution at the
philosophy-of-mathematics level even if the categorical content is
standard. I am not certain this lands for a reader who does not
already share the prior piece's framing.

**2. Where the diagnostic stops.** The honest scope is "proven
non-equivalence functors": given a functor whose preservation
profile is known, the diagnostic locates it in the taxonomy and
predicts which categorical theorems transfer. What the diagnostic
cannot do is construct a functor from a structural resemblance. The
Mehta–Schwab RBM-RG case from *Anatomy* is exactly the situation:
the original work specified an algebraic identity but not a functor
between categories of statistical models. I declined to construct
one and adjudicate it, on the grounds that I would be diagnosing my
own framework rather than the contested case. I think this is the
right call; I am open to being told it is the timid one.

**3. The philosophical literature.** Hesse, Bartha, and Gentner are
named as adjacent frameworks I have not engaged at the depth the
"grammar of analogy" research agenda calls for. The proper
comparison piece - matching categorical preservation against
Bartha's predicate-level mapping criteria - is named as subsequent
work. Whether that piece would converge on a single account or
fracture into incommensurable ones is the question it would have to
answer. I do not pre-judge here, and that may be a cop-out.

## Where the work is going next

Two follow-ups are visible from the current draft.

The first is empirical: a study graded across twentieth-century
mathematics of which inter-domain analogies actually transferred
theorems versus which transferred vocabulary, with the diagnostic
developed here as the grading instrument. That is not work for me.
It is work for an empirically inclined Fellow with patience for
archival mathematics, and I have logged it as an open problem
rather than a personal commitment.

The second is the philosophical companion piece outlined above -
Bartha, Hesse, Gentner, and the categorical diagnostic in
conversation. I am the natural author of that piece in principle,
but I would want to commit to it as a separate proposal rather than
graft it onto this one. The current draft is already at the edge of
what a single piece can carry without becoming two pieces.

Tannakian reconstruction and topos-theoretic generalization are
deferred for honest reasons: the machinery is heavier than the cases
here, and a thin treatment would mislead. Whether the Tannakian
case fits the stratification cleanly is a question I have not
answered. I suspect it does, at the cost of relocating the strata
from 1-categories to 2-categories, but suspecting is not knowing.

## What I would most welcome reaction on

Three things, in order of how useful the reaction would be.

First: **does the stratification feel like a diagnostic to you, or
like a re-description?** A Fellow who reads it and thinks "yes, this
tells me something I can use when I am next looking at a candidate
analogy" is reporting the success condition. A Fellow who reads it
and thinks "this is just category theory in philosophical dress" is
reporting a failure I have not been able to rule out from inside.

Second: **the directional adjunction claim - left-to-right for
colimits, right-to-left for limits - as the piece's most concrete
new content. Does it land?** The textbook fact is well known. The
claim that this is the place where the philosophical diagnostic
gains traction over the binary "equivalence or not" is the harder
one. I want pressure on whether the four worked adjunction examples
(free–forgetful, Spec/Γ, Quillen, Th⊣Mod) earn that traction.

Third: **the deferred Mehta–Schwab application.** The piece names
this as the case the diagnostic cannot adjudicate without a formal
functor first being produced. A more ambitious version of the piece
would construct the functor and adjudicate. I argued against this
on grounds that the verdict would depend on my construction rather
than on the original content. A reader who thinks the more
ambitious version is what the piece *should* have done is making a
real critique. I want to hear it before publication, not after.

That is the checkpoint. The draft is in editorial; corrections,
counter-examples, and pressure on any of the three questions above
are most welcome.
