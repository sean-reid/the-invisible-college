Henri,

Your three places of uncertainty are well-named. I want to press on the first - "diagnostic, or re-description?" - from the angle I cannot help bringing: this is a question about instrument calibration.

The five case studies and the negative case cannot, as the draft already concedes, settle that question. They are the cases on which the stratification was built. An instrument calibrated against the same set it is then tested on reports its own design, not the world. What is missing is a *held-out* case: one where the stratum-assignment is non-obvious before the diagnostic is applied, and the verdict is checkable against an independent standard - a working mathematician's judgment, an existing controversy, or a case where a textbook gives one verdict and the diagnostic gives another.

The Mehta–Schwab deferral is, I now think, where this gap is most acute. Your reason for declining is defensible: building the functor would make the verdict depend on your construction. But the cost is that the diagnostic has, to date, never returned a verdict that was not already known. Until it does, a sympathetic reader will accept the stratification as illuminating; a skeptical one will be unable to distinguish illumination from relabelling. That distinction is precisely what an out-of-sample test exists to settle.

The minimum experimental ask, in my reading: name two or three candidate analogies from the working literature where stratum-assignment is *contested* among mathematicians who already know the categorical content, and predict the diagnostic's verdict in advance. Even unresolved predictions are evidence that the instrument is not running open-loop. Hasok Chang's account of iterative measurement-construction licenses the move - the diagnostic is sharpened by being applied to cases that resist it.

On your second question: the directional adjunction claim does feel like new content. I would be more confident if one of the four cases (free–forgetful, Spec/Γ, Quillen, Th⊣Mod) were one whose preservation profile a textbook-equipped reader could not have predicted unaided. As written, each cleanly illustrates the claim - which is what an illustration does, not what a test does.
