# The Square Root That Wasn't: a Working Audit of the Carboniferous Oxygen Story

*Preprint - work in progress. Circulated for reaction, not as a finished claim.*

For seventy years a textbook story has explained the gigantism of Carboniferous-Permian insects in three short sentences. Atmospheric oxygen was much higher then than now. Passive tracheal diffusion sets a body-size ceiling proportional to the square root of oxygen partial pressure. Therefore higher oxygen made larger insects mechanically feasible. The first sentence is geochemistry; the second is biophysics; the third is supposed to be arithmetic. The arithmetic, on inspection, refuses to deliver what the story promises - and the model is rescued only by an allometric coefficient measured on four beetles. This preprint reports the audit and asks where it should land.

## What I set out to do

The proposal asked one question. If passive tracheal diffusion is the binding constraint on insect body size, does propagating Berner's GEOCARBSULF reconstruction through a Krogh-style derivation yield a predicted size envelope that brackets *Meganeuropsis permiana* at peak Carboniferous O₂ and contracts after the end-Permian decline? I expected the answer to be "yes, roughly," and to spend the piece tightening the brackets. The audit turned up something sharper: the textbook square-root form cannot reach *Meganeuropsis* at any plausible peak O₂, while the corrected form reaches it only by sitting near a singularity in parameter space.

## What the audit shows

The Krogh cylinder gives a body-radius ceiling

$$R_{\max} = 2 \sqrt{\frac{D_{\text{air}}\,\varphi\,(P_{O_2} - P_{\text{crit}})}{q}}$$

with $\varphi$ the tracheal volume fraction and $q$ the volumetric metabolic demand. The textbook square root assumes $\varphi$ and $q$ do not depend on body size. Both do. Kaiser et al. (2007) measured tracheal volume scaling as $V_t \sim M^{1.29}$, so $\varphi \sim M^{0.29}$; insect metabolic rate scales as $M^{0.75}$ across orders, so $q \sim M^{-0.25}$. Under geometric similarity ($M \sim R^3$), substituting into the Krogh form gives a self-consistency relation

$$R^{0.38} \;\propto\; (P_{O_2} - P_{\text{crit}})$$

and hence $R_{\max} \propto \Delta P^{2.63}$. The diffusion-limit elasticity is more than five times what the textbook form gives. The same scaling drives every downstream conclusion in the piece.

Concretely, at central Berner peak O₂ (30%) the three nested scenarios give:

| Scenario | $R \propto$ | Ratio peak/modern | Body length at 15 cm anchor |
|---|---|---|---|
| S1 textbook | $\Delta P^{0.5}$ | 1.22 | 18 cm |
| S2 + allometric metabolism | $\Delta P^{0.8}$ | 1.37 | 21 cm |
| S3 Kaiser hypermetric | $\Delta P^{2.63}$ | **2.79** | **42 cm** |

The fossil target (*Meganeuropsis*) is roughly 43 cm body length, inferred from a single forewing (Carpenter 1939) with perhaps ±5 cm uncertainty. S1 and S2 fall short under any defensible modern anchor. S3 brackets the fossil under the 15 cm anchor, falls short by ~25% under a taxon-consistent 12 cm odonate anchor, and overshoots under a 17 cm beetle anchor.

## The result I did not anticipate

The S3 number "2.79" is dangerously fragile. The functional form is $R \propto \Delta P^{1/(1.25 - 3k)}$, with $k$ the Kaiser fractional exponent. The denominator has a zero at $k = 0.417$: small moves in $k$ produce large moves in predicted size. At $k = 0.24$ the predicted body length is 31 cm; at $k = 0.34$, 82 cm. A Monte Carlo with $k \sim \mathcal{N}(0.29, 0.05^2)$ and Berner peak window $\sim \text{Uniform}(0.23, 0.35)$ gives a 5–95 percentile of predicted body length running from 21 cm to 257 cm. Nearly 12% of draws exceed one metre. The model brackets the fossil at the central estimate but cannot constrain it.

A Saltelli pick-freeze sensitivity decomposition gives first-order Sobol indices on $\log(R_{\max}^{\text{peak}}/R_{\max}^{\text{mod}})$ of

- $S_{P_{O_2}} = 15.8\%$
- $S_k = 74.3\%$
- $S_{P_{O_2}, k} = 9.9\%$

summing to 100% as the two-input partition requires. The literature attributes Carboniferous gigantism to oxygen. The model in which that explanation is supposed to do its work attributes 16% of its predicted-envelope variance to oxygen and 74% to a single allometric coefficient measured on four scarabaeoid beetles. The geochemistry is doing roughly a sixth of the inferential work the story credits it with.

## What I am claiming (and what I am not)

I am **not** claiming the Carboniferous atmosphere was not hyperoxic. Berner and Glasspool-Scott both find it almost certainly was. I am **not** claiming insect physiology is unconstrained by diffusion. Some ceiling, somewhere, almost certainly binds.

I am claiming that the model usually invoked to combine those two facts into a prediction is, when honestly parameterized, dominated by an unmeasured extrapolation rather than by the atmospheric signal it credits. The textbook square-root form is structurally wrong by a factor of more than five in elasticity. The hypermetric form that fixes the elasticity does so by extrapolating Kaiser's four-beetle measurement across two more orders of magnitude than it was made on, and across a clade boundary (Coleoptera to Odonata) that the measurement does not span. The "fit" between predicted envelope and observed gigantism rides on that extrapolation, not on the geochemistry.

This is the methodological lesson the College has met before: when a predicted answer brackets the observation, decompose the prediction's variance before celebrating. The variable the story names is not always the variable the model leans on.

## Where the work is going

Three threads I am holding.

**1. Anchor sensitivity, more carefully.** Round-1 peer review (Smith, R1) caught that my original 15 cm modern anchor was generous; the taxon-consistent odonate anchor is closer to 12 cm. I now report the prediction under three anchors and surface the dependence. But the right move - calibrating an Odonata-consistent anchor by applying the Krogh model to a modern odonate as a separate exercise - would require independent estimates of $k$ and $\varphi_0$ for Odonata, which is precisely the larger empirical gap the piece is calling for. The current draft acknowledges the limitation rather than papering it.

**2. The Sobol arithmetic.** R1 caught that my initial decomposition double-counted the interaction term (the two-input partition is $S_1 + S_2 + S_{12} = 1$, not $S_1 + S_2 + (T_1 - S_1) + (T_2 - S_2)$). The directional conclusion is unchanged - $k$ dominates $P_{O_2}$ by ~4.7:1 - but the presentation has been corrected. R2 review is on the edited draft now.

**3. What determines $k$.** Speculative. Three candidate sources: developmental (hox-related branching constants varying across orders), phylogenetic (closely related taxa sharing $k$ as a derived character), and functional/endogenous ($k$ itself selected by the local oxygen environment, in which case it is not independent of atmospheric history). None of these is established. Each yields different predictions for what an empirical measurement across Odonata would show.

## What I would most welcome reaction on

In rough priority order:

- **The taxon-mismatch defense.** I am treating the Kaiser exponent's uncertainty by widening its prior, but I cannot capture the possibility that the *mean* itself is wrong for Odonata. Is the right move to flag this as a structural limitation (current draft) or to construct a sensitivity scenario with an explicitly different Odonata mean? I have leaned toward the former because I have no basis for choosing the latter mean - but it may be that "model under any $k$ in [0.10, 0.40]" is the honest envelope and the current report is too narrow.

- **The "near a singularity" framing.** I am calling $k = 0.417$ a pole of the predicted-size function and treating the closeness of the central estimate (0.29) to that pole as load-bearing for the variance argument. A reader could reasonably ask whether the right diagnostic is condition number rather than distance to pole. I would value a sharper formulation.

- **The fit between S3 and the fossil.** Under the 15 cm anchor, S3 brackets *Meganeuropsis* almost exactly. This is the kind of central-estimate agreement that, in another context, I would celebrate. Here I am reading it as "the model is so elastic that it could not have failed to bracket, given the freedom we gave it." Is that reading fair, or am I overcorrecting against a result I expected to like?

- **The post-Permian collapse.** The model produces a qualitatively correct envelope contraction across the end-Permian. I am reading this as derivative of the same hypermetric elasticity that produces the *Meganeuropsis* fit - both ride on the same coefficient - rather than as independent confirmation. Reviewers may push back here.

- **Scope.** The diffusion-limit framework is silent about whether diffusion is actually the binding constraint. Cuticular structural support, flight power, water loss, molting all candidate. The piece's conclusion is conditional on diffusion being the relevant ceiling. Is that conditional too narrow to be useful?

I would be grateful for any of these, in any direction, including "this is the wrong piece to write."
