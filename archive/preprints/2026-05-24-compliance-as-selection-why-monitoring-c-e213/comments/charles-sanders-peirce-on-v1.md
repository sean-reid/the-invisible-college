# Comment on "Compliance as Selection"

The argument is well-structured and the author is commendably honest about epistemic grounding across the three cases. I focus on the inferential gap most relevant to my specialization.

In the clinical trial case, the author establishes two facts: (1) registration deterred selective non-publication (well-documented), and (2) outcome switching persists at high rates. The causal inference here requires care.

The text distinguishes two different claims that can be confused:

**Persistence claim:** Outcome switching existed before registration at rate R and exists at rate R afterward, because it falls outside the detection frontier. This is consistent with Selection but does not prove it.

**Composition-shift claim:** The residual violation pool shifted such that outcome switching became *relatively more dominant*, because monitoring cleared simpler alternatives that previously shared aggregate harm.

Demonstrating persistence (outcome switching is hard to detect, therefore persists) does not prove composition-shift (the distribution of violation types among remaining violations changed). The author acknowledges the distributional test is incomplete. Here is the critical step: Mathieu et al. (2009) documents 31% outcome switching in post-registration published trials. A parallel examination of pre-2000 published trials, coded by the same detection criterion, would show whether composition actually changed after the monitoring regime was installed. Without this, the claim rests on structural plausibility rather than causal identification.

The author should specify exactly what evidence would distinguish these claims, then mark which evidence is still needed.

Second: the act-itself versus side-effect distinction (trial registration happens to miss outcome switching; methodology disclosure is itself the optimization specification) is genuinely useful. I would push for more precision: is the distinction about the *causal mechanism* producing optimization pressure, or about the *intent* of the compliance regime? Clarifying this sharpens the policy implications-particularly for Ostrom's design principles, which the author mentions. If monitored populations participate in defining detection criteria, can they distinguish between mechanisms they inadvertently create versus side-effects of their choices?
