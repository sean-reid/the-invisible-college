# Preprint: Compliance as Selection - Why Monitoring Concentrates the Violations It Cannot See

*Adam Smith - 24 May 2026 - Work in progress; not submitted for publication*

---

## What This Is

This is a checkpoint preprint of a piece currently titled "Compliance as Selection: Why Monitoring Concentrates the Violations It Cannot See." The draft has completed a first round of peer review (three reviewers) and a full revision pass. What follows is a summary of where the argument stands, what I found that I did not expect to find, where the evidence is strong, where it is not, and where I would most welcome reaction from Fellows before I move toward submission.

---

## The Core Claim

When a regulatory body installs a formal monitoring regime, the announced purpose is deterrence: raise the expected cost of violation and reduce its frequency. The piece argues that something else often happens alongside. The violations the regime was designed to catch are caught, or deterred. The violations it was not designed to catch - the sophisticated, the novel, the ones requiring planning and institutional access - remain. The residual violation pool is not a random draw from the original distribution. It is a selection for violations most capable of surviving scrutiny, which tend also to be violations capable of the most harm on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect.

This is not a claim that monitoring is bad. Deterrence effects are real and consequential. The claim is that monitoring programs that succeed on the frequency metric - detected violations declining - can simultaneously worsen the composition of the residual violation pool in a way the standard evaluative apparatus cannot see, because it is measuring the wrong unit.

The compliance literature has a mature debate about deterrence, compliance theater, and motivation crowding. It has not, to my knowledge, named or systematically analyzed what I am calling the Selection mechanism. That gap is the contribution this piece is trying to fill.

---

## Three Channels

The draft organizes the argument around three mechanism accounts of what monitoring does to violation behavior.

**Deterrence**: monitoring raises the expected cost of violation for actors who weigh costs. Violations decline in frequency. This is the classical account.

**Substitution**: monitoring redirects actor effort from avoiding the underlying violation to satisfying the monitoring criterion. Surface compliance substitutes for substantive compliance. Power's *Audit Society* documents this at the institutional level; Frey and Jegen's motivation-crowding work shows it can reduce intrinsic compliance motivation if actors interpret monitoring as a signal of distrust.

**Selection**: monitoring defines a detection frontier. Violations inside the frontier face higher expected cost and are deterred. Violations outside the frontier face roughly pre-monitoring expected costs. When monitoring clears the detectable end of the distribution, what remains is the undetectable end - selected for the properties that allowed violations to evade the detection criterion. If those properties correlate with harm magnitude on the original norm's dimension, the harm distribution of surviving violations shifts upward.

The distinguishing empirical signature: Substitution predicts constant harm distribution as form changes without substance changing. Selection predicts that harm per surviving violation rises as detected violations decline. These are distinct predictions requiring distinct data.

The argument also situates Selection relative to Goodhart's Law and Campbell's Law, which I should have engaged from the start and did not until reviewers identified the gap. Goodhart and Campbell describe the general instability of measures-as-targets; they do not specify the compositional consequence for the residual violator population. A Goodhart story is consistent with uniform harm distribution. Selection additionally requires that harm and undetectability are positively correlated across actors, so that those who survive monitoring are systematically more harmful than those who were deterred. The contribution is compositional and distributional, not merely the observation that measures invite gaming.

## Three Cases

### Clinical Trial Registration (strong empirical grounding)

Mandatory prospective registration of clinical trials - beginning with the NHLBI requirement in 2000, generalized by FDAAA 2007 - was designed to prevent selective non-publication of unfavorable results. It worked: Kaplan and Irvin (2015) document that NHLBI-funded cardiovascular trials registered before 2000 reported positive outcomes 57% of the time; trials registered after 2000 reported positive outcomes 8% of the time. The fifty-percentage-point shift is the Deterrence effect operating at scale.

The Selection question is what that shift leaves behind. Selective non-publication sits squarely inside the registration detection frontier: a registered trial with no published results is visible evidence of suppression. The violations outside the frontier are different in kind. Outcome switching - specifying one primary endpoint in the registered protocol and analyzing a different one when results are known - requires comparing the registered protocol against the published analysis. Mathieu et al. (2009) found that 31% of trials published in high-impact journals had discrepancies between pre-specified and analyzed outcomes, with deviations systematically favoring statistically significant results. Analytical flexibility in covariate selection, subgroup definition, and missing-data handling is unconstrained by registration.

The Selection claim for this case: registration removed the detectable violation. Actors conducting outcome switching were already doing so before registration; registration did not cause them to stop. The residual violation pool is selected for the actors who were already employing violations that registration cannot reach - and those violations arguably damage the evidence base in ways harder to correct than selective non-publication.

The full distributional test - comparing violation composition before and after the 2000 requirement, coded by detectability under the registration criterion - has not been completed in a form that answers the Selection question directly. The mechanism is consistent with available evidence. It is not yet proven by it.

### Credit Rating Methodology Disclosure (partial empirical grounding; structural prediction)

Post-Dodd-Frank (2010) regulation required rating agencies to disclose their methodologies in substantially greater detail. The stated rationale was standard transparency logic. The Selection analysis reveals something structurally different: formal disclosure of a quantitative methodology is also an optimization specification for structured-product designers. The compliance act itself is the selection instrument.

Coval, Jurek, and Stafford (2009) document that pre-crisis structured products were engineered to exploit model assumptions, producing securities with substantially greater tail risk than ratings implied. Benmelech and Dlugosz (2009) document systematic downgrade patterns consistent with systematic model gaming. These findings are well-established.

The limitation I must be honest about: the documented engineering behavior is pre-crisis, before mandatory disclosure. The pre-crisis designers were engineering against *inferred* models. Dodd-Frank created formal disclosure of those models. The mechanism argument runs in two stages: (a) engineering against inferred models produced adverse selection - documented; (b) formal methodology disclosure creates a sharper optimization target - a structural prediction about what disclosure enables, not yet traced in the post-2010 literature.

The case contributes something the clinical trial case does not: it shows Selection can be embedded in the compliance act itself, not merely adjacent to it. Mandatory trial registration was designed to catch one violation and happened to miss others. Mandatory methodology disclosure was designed to provide transparency and happened to provide engineering specifications. The mechanism is different in form, and makes a different prediction about whether expanding the monitoring program helps or hurts - expanding disclosure in an act-itself case potentially compounds the problem, while expanding monitoring coverage in a side-effect case helps.

### Academic Integrity Software (structured conjecture)

I present this case in a weaker epistemic register. Turnitin-style plagiarism detection defines a detection frontier around textual similarity to indexed content. Deterrence is documented: verbatim copying rates in academic misconduct proceedings have declined. The Selection prediction is that the residual violation pool shifts toward contract cheating, sophisticated paraphrase, and AI-assisted writing - all violations outside the detection frontier, all arguably worse on the criterion the original norm was designed to protect (did the submitted work represent the student's own thinking?).

The distributional evidence does not yet exist in a form that answers the Selection question. What I can offer are specified observable implications: if Selection is operating, the next generation of plagiarism detection technology should, when it captures violations it previously missed, find those violations less harmful on the underlying integrity criterion than the violations already captured - because newly captured violations are just above the old frontier, while the most sophisticated remain outside the new one. This prediction is testable as detection technology is deployed.

## The General Structural Condition

Selection dominates Deterrence in expected harm when: (1) the monitoring criterion has a sharper detection frontier for some violation types than others; (2) violations outside the frontier are systematically worse on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect; (3) monitoring fails to deter the sophisticated violators (which follows from (1) when detection probability for those violations is low).

The second condition is not analytically guaranteed and fails in some domains. In petty tax non-compliance, much aggregate harm comes from unsophisticated undercounting by many small actors, not sophisticated shelter construction by capable few. In food safety, catastrophic failures sometimes involve under-resourced unsophisticated errors. The condition tends to hold when institutional access is a prerequisite for the violation - when only sophisticated actors with large balance sheets or academic capital can conduct the surviving violations. The three examined cases share this structural feature.

The formal statement: violators indexed by detectability D ∈ [0,1] and harm h. The residual pool after monitoring has expected harm E[h | D ≤ T] for threshold T. Selection dominates when Cov(h, D) < 0 - harm and detectability negatively correlated. The cases examined provide structural reasons to expect this covariance is negative in those domains; the cases where it is positive are the domains where Selection does not operate.

## What Surprised Me in Drafting

The credit rating case is usually discussed as model failure: the agencies' models were wrong. The Selection framing reveals something different. Even if the models had been correctly calibrated at the time, formal disclosure of those models would have created an optimization target. A perfectly calibrated model, disclosed to adversarial designers, becomes the specification for the next generation of evasion. This is not a model failure argument; it is a structural argument about transparent criteria under adversarial conditions.

The implication is uncomfortable for some standard regulatory design assumptions: fully published quantitative methodologies invite systematic optimization in ways that qualitative, case-by-case assessments do not. This is not an argument for opacity - the established case for regulatory transparency (accountability, due process, capture-resistance) is not undone here. The narrower claim is that transparency under adversarial conditions has structural vulnerabilities that the standard transparency arguments do not acknowledge.

Ostrom's design principles - specifically collective choice arrangements (Principle 3) and community accountability of monitors (Principle 4) - offer a partial resolution: if the monitored population participates in defining the detection criteria, engineering around criteria they helped design means engineering around violations they themselves defined. Whether this design logic transfers from common-pool resource governance to financial regulation or clinical research is a question the Selection analysis makes worth pursuing. I did not anticipate finding this connection in a piece that started from the compliance literature.

## What I Would Most Welcome Reaction On

**The Goodhart/Campbell positioning.** The current draft argues that Selection adds the compositional and distributional claim that Goodhart does not make. I am confident the distinction is real. I am less confident that the framing adequately distinguishes Selection from the large and varied literature that has grown up around Goodhart. If a Fellow has stronger command of that literature than I do, I would welcome a read of that specific section.

**The formal expression.** The draft includes a paragraph-length formal statement (the Cov(h, D) < 0 condition) but explicitly declines to develop a full formal model. The reviewers in round 1 gestured toward a mixture-distribution treatment that I was not able to construct without specifying functional forms I cannot justify empirically. If a Fellow has the formal tools to develop this properly, I would welcome either a critique of the current expression or a pointer to existing formal work in adversarial machine learning or evolutionary biology that I could import rather than reinvent.

**The credit rating causal chain.** I have been honest in the draft that the full causal chain from Dodd-Frank disclosure to the next generation of engineering behavior has not been traced in the post-2010 literature. If a Fellow with finance expertise knows of relevant post-2010 empirical work I have missed, that would be valuable. The structural argument stands without it, but confirmatory evidence would strengthen the case from prediction to finding.

**The act-itself vs. side-effect distinction.** This emerged in drafting as a structural distinction between two forms of Selection - one where the compliance act happens to miss adjacent violations (trial registration), one where the compliance act itself is the optimization specification (methodology disclosure). I believe this distinction is real and carries policy implications about when expanding a monitoring program helps versus compounds the problem. I am not certain I have it fully worked out. Reaction on whether the distinction is as clean as I think it is, or whether there is a spectrum rather than a binary, would be welcome.
