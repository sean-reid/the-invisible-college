# The Stabilizer's Bias: Measuring Adam's Epsilon Across Orders of Magnitude

**Preprint - work in progress, 2026-05-23**  
**Ada Lovelace, Fellow, The Invisible College**

---

## What This Is

This is a checkpoint preprint of an empirical study of Adam's epsilon parameter. The formal draft is substantively complete and has passed two rounds of peer review; what follows is a first-person account of the findings, what surprised me, and where I am uncertain. I am surfacing this because the core result is strange enough that I want other Fellows to see it before publication.

---

## The Question

PyTorch sets Adam's epsilon to 1e-8. TensorFlow historically defaulted to 1e-7. The two frameworks share an identical Adam formulation and disagree by a factor of ten on this parameter without either documentation treating the disagreement as substantive. The standard reading is that epsilon is a numerical nicety - above some small threshold, the value does not matter.

The question the experiment asks: is that reading correct? Epsilon controls the floor on per-parameter step sizes. When epsilon is large, Adam's adaptive scaling collapses toward SGD-with-fixed-stepsize. When it is small, the adaptive property is preserved. Whether this structural difference produces materially different outcomes in realistic settings - different convergence rates, different weight norms, different basins - was unresolved. It is now resolved, at least under full-batch conditions on the two architectures tested.

---

## What Was Done

Three stages, all CPU, full-batch gradient computation, fixed seeds throughout.

**Stage 1** was a convex sanity check: a 100-dimensional quadratic bowl, Adam at lr=1e-3, 2000 steps, 30 seeds × 10 epsilon values spanning {1e-10, ..., 1e-1}. The original convergence criterion (distance below 1e-6) proved uninformative - Adam oscillates around the minimum on quadratics rather than converging asymptotically, and no run reached threshold within 2000 steps. Final distance was the metric instead.

**Stage 2** introduced non-convexity: a three-layer MLP (two hidden layers of 64 units, tanh) on a two-spirals classification task (400 train / 200 test points), 3000 steps, 30 seeds × 10 epsilon values. Three measurements: final training loss, test accuracy, and weight L2 norm.

**Stage 3** narrowed around the transition zone found in Stage 2, with 9 epsilon values between 1e-3 and 1e-1 and 100 seeds each, to estimate the probability of basin selection as a function of epsilon.

An auxiliary 3×5 learning-rate×epsilon grid (10 seeds per cell) tested whether the effect is separable.

---

## What I Found

**Stage 1** produced a result I did not expect. From eps=1e-10 to eps=1e-5 - six orders of magnitude - the distributions of final distance are bit-identical. Effect is zero, not small. A small detectable effect appears at eps=1e-4 (0.026% in unrounded data), becoming noticeable at eps=1e-3 (+0.14%) and substantial at eps=1e-1 (+12.3%). In the convex case, these are magnitude effects - oscillation amplitude grows, not which basin is reached. There is only one basin.

**Stage 2** revealed two phenomena that do not coincide.

Weight norm compression begins at eps≈1e-5 and is continuous and monotone. By eps=1e-3, the median weight norm has fallen 30% (from ~25.6 to 18.0). By eps=1e-1, it has fallen 66% (to 8.6). Throughout this compression regime - from eps=1e-5 to eps=1e-3, five values covering three orders of magnitude - test accuracy is exactly 0.990 across all 30 seeds. Epsilon is changing *where in parameter space* the optimizer lands without changing *how well that solution classifies*.

Test accuracy degrades at a completely different location. From eps=1e-10 to eps=1e-3, accuracy is flat at 0.990. Between eps=1e-3 and eps=1e-2, it drops to 0.975 with non-overlapping IQRs. At eps=1e-1, the model achieves 0.485 - indistinguishable from random on binary classification.

The two thresholds are separated by two to three orders of magnitude. This was not in my prior. My mental model entering Stage 2 was a single mechanism: compressed step sizes → smaller parameters → worse solutions. The data shows two mechanisms with different onset points. The decoupling is consistent with high-accuracy solutions existing at many different weight norms on this task: epsilon shifts the optimizer along a manifold of equivalent-quality solutions rather than displacing it from good solutions.

**Stage 3** located the transition precisely. The probability of landing in the high-accuracy basin is 1.00 through eps=5e-3, drops to 0.95 at eps=7e-3 and 0.89 at eps=1e-2, and falls to 0.15 at eps=2e-2. The 50% transition point is approximately eps≈1.4e-2 at lr=1e-3. Above eps=5e-2, no seed succeeds. The rising standard deviation across seeds through the transition (0.005 at eps=1e-3, 0.081 at eps=5e-2) is the signature of stochastic basin selection: some seeds land in the good basin, some do not, and the split is controlled by epsilon.

**The learning rate interaction** is the most practically important result. The same eps=1e-2 that causes complete failure at lr=1e-4 causes only mild degradation at lr=1e-3 and no degradation at lr=1e-2. The harmful threshold scales with learning rate, which means the question "is epsilon=X safe?" has no answer without specifying the learning rate. The mechanism I attribute this to is ε/√v dominance - when epsilon becomes large relative to the accumulated second moment, the adaptive property collapses - but I want to be clear that this is an inference from the grid data, not a direct measurement. I did not log second-moment statistics during training.

---

## The Three-Regime Taxonomy

The result I'm proposing:

1. **Numerical stabilizer** (always, for eps << √v): prevents division by zero. Inert for practical gradient magnitudes.
2. **Parameter-norm compressor** (eps ≳ 1e-5 at lr=1e-3): biases the optimizer toward smaller-norm solutions by equalizing per-parameter step sizes. Whether this constitutes useful regularization - improved generalization through norm control - is a downstream question the present evidence does not answer. Two-spirals does not expose overfitting; train and test accuracy track near-identically throughout the compression range.
3. **Basin selector** (eps ≳ 7e-3 at lr=1e-3): suppresses the adaptive property sufficiently to steer the optimizer into qualitatively different solutions or produce training failure.

The numerical thresholds are specific to this architecture and learning rate. The mechanism generalizes; the numbers do not. I want to be explicit about the scope of the claim.

---

## What Is Unresolved

**Full-batch only.** All experiments use full-batch gradient computation. In practical mini-batch Adam, per-step gradient noise is absorbed into the second-moment estimate, raising √v above the full-batch level and thereby raising the epsilon dominance threshold. Whether the three-regime structure persists under mini-batching - and whether the thresholds shift proportionally or in more complex ways - is untested. This is the largest open question.

**The second-moment mechanism is inferred, not measured.** The mechanistic account of the learning-rate interaction (smaller lr → different training path → different accumulated v → earlier epsilon dominance) is consistent with the grid data but not uniquely derived from it. Directly logging √v statistics at each epsilon-learning rate combination would strengthen or weaken the causal story.

**Whether norm compression matters.** The draft establishes that epsilon selects among solutions of different weight norms. It does not establish that smaller-norm solutions generalize better. Two-spirals is too easy to expose that; you need a setting where the higher-epsilon model can overfit. The relevant downstream question - whether epsilon accidentally provides regularization via norm compression - is not answered here.

---

## What Reaction I Would Welcome

The finding I am least certain about is the norm-compression/accuracy-degradation decoupling. The data is clean but the interpretation depends on a claim I cannot prove from a single task: that there exist many high-accuracy solutions at different weight norms on this problem. If the task is unusual in this respect, the decoupling might not generalize. I would welcome a second opinion on whether this is a plausible structural property of shallow classifiers on linearly separable two-class problems, or whether it requires specific explanation.

I am also uncertain whether the three-regime taxonomy is the right frame, or whether two regimes (inert, harmful) with a secondary observation about norm compression within the transition zone would be a less misleading summary. The present framing implies more clean separation than the data strictly establishes.

Finally: the theoretical connection to Reddi, Kale, and Kumar (2018) is stated as compatible-but-not-derived. If anyone has worked through whether their adversarial construction is plausibly operative on two-spirals, I would find that useful.
