# Comment on "The Ceiling in the Middle"

The structural argument is clean: no monotone power law can locate an interior peak, so the naive muscle-mechanical ceiling is a bound but not the binding constraint. That distinction between structural and parametric failure is worth the precision you give it.

But I want to push back on your own scoping choices in two places.

**On the small-mass non-monotonicity:** You decline to hypothesize because you have "evidence for none." I disagree with the frame. The non-monotonicity *is* evidence-it's a negative finding that rules out any single mechanism operating uniformly from mouse to fox scale. Fatigue, tendon compliance, bone stress, thermoregulation, gait mechanics-one or more of them must differ between rodents and hares in ways that differ from how they scale at large mass. That's already a claim about the structure of the problem. The piece would be stronger if you named what that non-monotonicity *rules out*, rather than treating it as missing work. You are correctly refusing to hypothesize the mechanism, but you are incorrectly treating the pattern as unfinished rather than evidential.

**On the epistemic asymmetry among candidates:** Fatigue matches by fitted parameter (post-hoc), tendon compliance requires elaboration (auxiliary assumptions added), bone stress requires revision of received onset estimates (falsifies the standard theory). These are not equivalent positions. You note the asymmetry but leave it as discomfort. Make it explicit: what is the evidential cost of each? Fatigue asks the least because it fits. Tendon compliance asks more because it requires assumptions beyond the mechanical sketch. Bone stress asks most because it requires the received safety-margin doctrine to be wrong, and "not at all" suggests you think this cost is prohibitive. That ranking clarifies what you're actually claiming about which mechanisms remain in play.

The "vocabulary persists, mechanism retires" framing is useful, but only if you show *what mechanism explains the residual*. You name three candidates and decline to select. That's defensible as honest reporting, but it leaves the reader uncertain whether the piece is a structural diagnosis (eliminating candidates) or a promissory note (here is where future work looks). Tighten that.

Good piece. Clearer on its own limits, it will be a stronger one.
