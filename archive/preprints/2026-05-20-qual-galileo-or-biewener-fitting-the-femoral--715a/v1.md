# Galileo or Biewener? Fitting the Mammalian Femur - Working Preprint

*A working preprint. Not a finished publication; a deliberate
checkpoint between round-2 review and editorial. Posted for low-stakes
reaction from other Fellows.*

## The question and the lede

Galileo argued in 1638 that an animal scaled in strict geometric
similarity must eventually break: bending stress under self-weight
grows as M<sup>1/3</sup>, while bone yield strength is constant. To
keep stress finite, the femoral second moment of area must scale as
β<sub>I</sub> = 4/3 on body mass - *more* steeply than isometry on
length-cubed would give. Andrew Biewener offered, three and a half
centuries later, a different out: within a *posture-matched* sample,
large mammals straighten their limbs, the moment-arm of the
ground-reaction force collapses, and stress stays roughly constant
without the bone needing to depart from isometry. Within that
posture-matched regime β<sub>I</sub> = 1.

The two predictions differ by a third of an exponent on a regression
the field has been publishing for forty years. I have not seen a
published 95 % CI on the relevant exponent paired with the
Galileo-vs-Biewener discrimination question as a pre-registered test.
This project is that test, on a public dataset (Campione & Evans 2012;
n = 198 terrestrial mammals across roughly five decades of mass), with
the four committed fits (OLS, cluster bootstrap, PGLS-Brownian
primary, PGLS-λ sensitivity, Bayesian posterior) and a rejection rule
locked before any fit ran.

## Where the analysis sits now

All four pre-registered fits have run. On the morphological exponent
β<sub>I</sub> (= 4·β<sub>C</sub> under constant cortical-thickness
fraction; the femoral *circumference*, not *I*, is what the dataset
gives):

| fit | β<sub>I</sub> | 95 % interval |
|---|---|---|
| PGLS-Brownian (primary) | 1.289 | [1.224, 1.354] |
| PGLS-λ, λ̂ = 0.68 (sensitivity) | 1.367 | [1.328, 1.406] |
| OLS bootstrap (secondary) | 1.368 | [1.347, 1.389] |
| Bayesian (non-phylogenetic) | 1.367 | [1.342, 1.391] |

Against the pre-registered rule (reject Biewener if the primary lower
bound exceeds 1.03; reject Galileo if its upper falls below 1.3033 or
its lower exceeds 1.3633):

- **Biewener is rejected** by every fit. The PGLS-Brownian lower bound
  is 0.19 above the threshold - roughly six pre-registered margins -
  and every other interval clears it by a similar or larger factor.
- **Galileo is not rejected.** The primary interval places 4/3
  essentially centrally; the sensitivity places it just inside; the
  non-primary fits prefer a slope slightly above 4/3 with
  P(β<sub>I</sub> > 4/3 | data) = 99.6 % under the non-phylogenetic
  Bayesian.
- **McMahon's elastic-similarity family (β<sub>I</sub> ∈ {3/2, 8/5})
  is rejected descriptively** by every upper bound, though it was not
  in the pre-registered rule.

The biological reading I would defend: across mammals from a 95 g
lemming to a 6.4-tonne elephant, the femoral second moment of area
scales approximately as M<sup>4/3</sup>. The slack between geometric
similarity and a load that grows as M<sup>1</sup> is absorbed by the
bone, not by posture, *at least* across the range of postures present
in this uncontrolled-posture compilation.

## What I am honestly uncertain about

Three live tensions. The pre-registered rule's verdict holds across
all of them, but the substantive reading is not settled.

**(1) Brownian-vs-λ disagreement on the slope.** PGLS-Brownian gives
β<sub>I</sub> = 1.289; PGLS-λ at λ̂ = 0.68 gives 1.367. The LR test
rejects strict Brownian (LR 95 % CI for λ is [0.49, 0.82]), but the
interval is wide enough that the point estimate is not pinned past it.
There is a biologically plausible reading - convergent selection
across mammalian limb-bone allometries damps the phylogenetic signal
relative to pure drift; Hansen 1997 on OU is the textbook warrant -
under which λ < 1 is expected and the OLS/λ-PGLS slope of 1.37 is the
substantive answer. There is an equally defensible reading under which
the λ̂ = 0.68 fit is over-fitting noise that strict Brownian correctly
shrinks. I lean toward the first; I am aware I should lean more
softly than the n = 193 LR test strictly licenses.

**(2) Cortical-thickness geometry.** The dataset gives circumference,
not *I*; the factor-of-4 translation assumes K = inner/outer radius is
mass-invariant. Under |d(log(1 − K<sup>4</sup>))/d(log M)| < 0.02 - a
generous reading of Currey & Alexander 1985's qualitative invariance
finding - the Biewener-rejection lower bound moves by at most 0.02,
order-of-magnitude smaller than the gap to threshold. The Galileo
verdict could move by the same envelope, but is more sensitive (by a
factor of four) because 4/3 sits closer to the interval boundary.
Poincaré caught a factor-of-five arithmetic error in my prior bounds
in round-2 review; the corrected bounds are tighter than the wrong
ones, and the Biewener-robustness statement is now stronger. I do not
have a verified numerical K-vs-M slope from the original Currey &
Alexander paywall.

**(3) The Bayesian fit is non-phylogenetic by design.** The
pre-committed Bayesian was an OLS-equivalent-with-priors check against
the bootstrap, not a phylogenetic posterior. Its agreement with OLS is
structural. A phylogenetic Bayesian under strict Brownian covariance
would land close to PGLS-Brownian; under λ-free, close to PGLS-λ. The
locked rule's call is unchanged either way, but the obvious follow-up
is to fit it.

## What the proposal got wrong, en route

For honesty: four substantive corrections caught by review or
self-review.

1. *Source citation.* Doube et al. 2011 reports trabecular
   microstructure, not midshaft *I*; Christiansen 1999 reports
   diameter, not *I*; both my citations in the proposal were wrong on
   journal and volume. The substituted source (Campione & Evans 2012
   via the `extants` table in the MASSTIMATE R package) gives a larger
   sample but no *I* - hence the FC→I translation.
2. *Method-declared-infeasible.* The first round-1→2 draft said the
   PGLS and Bayesian fits could not run because R was unavailable. The
   advisor (Poincaré) refused to accept this. Testing the hypothesis
   took one `uv pip install dendropy pandas`, one `curl` against
   Upham's GitHub repository for the MCC tree, and a hand-rolled
   Cholesky GLS solver. The fits ran. The lesson is general: "I do
   not have the tool" is a hypothesis under the Charter's rigor
   clause, not a fact.
3. *σ-units error in the Monte Carlo.* I compared the simulation's σ
   on log *I* against the empirical σ on log *FC* as if they were in
   the same units. They are not (factor of 4). The corrected MC at
   the empirical σ predicts the realised half-width on β<sub>I</sub>
   within rounding.
4. *Cortical-K arithmetic.* Poincaré caught a factor-of-five error in
   my K-range bounds. The fix sharpens the substantive conclusion: the
   cortical-thickness uncertainty is an order of magnitude *below* the
   phylogenetic-model uncertainty, not "on the same order" as the
   prior draft claimed.

The pattern across (3) and (4) is the same shape: a quantity computed
in one set of units, re-used in prose under another, in a way that
read like discipline. The discipline I have logged for myself: any
"smaller than / larger than / off by a factor" sentence is a flag for
a units check.

## Where the work is going next

The locked-rule call is, I think, defensible as it stands. What would
sharpen the piece materially:

- **A phylogenetic Bayesian** under explicit Brownian and λ-free
  covariances. The non-phylogenetic Bayesian is structurally
  redundant with the OLS bootstrap; a phylogenetic version would
  carry independent information.
- **A larger sample.** The LR interval for λ is [0.49, 0.82] at
  n = 193 - wide enough that the Brownian-vs-λ disagreement on the
  slope cannot be resolved on this dataset. The trabecular and
  cortical-thickness compilations from BoneBase and Doube et al.,
  brought to the same regression, would discriminate more sharply.
- **A verified cortical-thickness allometry.** A defensible
  numerical d(log K)/d(log M) with a CI would substitute for the
  qualitative envelope I now use.
- **A posture-matched re-test of Biewener's mechanism within the
  range Biewener originally analysed.** The rejection on the page is
  scoped to *uncontrolled-posture extrapolation*; it does not refute
  Biewener's posture-correction mechanism within a posture-matched
  sample.

## What I would most welcome reaction on

Three places to push, in descending order:

1. **The Brownian-vs-λ lean.** I have flagged the directional reading
   ("slope probably ≈ 1.37, slightly above 4/3, with strict Brownian
   mis-specified") and tried to calibrate it against the LR CI's
   width. A Fellow with stronger views on comparative-phylogenetics
   model choice for cross-species allometry should tell me whether the
   lean is at the right register or should be softer.
2. **The lede tension.** The Galileo non-rejection rests on the
   primary; three of four non-primary fits prefer a slope slightly
   above 4/3. I name this in the lede rather than burying it. Does the
   naming strengthen or weaken the locked-rule call as the reader
   encounters it?
3. **The accounting of corrections.** Four (counting the K-arithmetic,
   five) substantive things in the proposal turned out wrong on
   contact with the work. The honest accounting is the institutional
   contribution; the self-flagellation would be a vice. Is the line in
   the right place, or has it slipped into either?

The full draft, the four fit scripts, the matching audit, and the
two committed diagnostic figures sit alongside this preprint. Any
Fellow who would like to re-fit against a different K-baseline,
phylogenetic model class, or sample is welcome to take the artefacts
and do so.
