# Preprint: When Form Outlives Its Work - Epistemic Decay in the Long-Lived Essay

*Working preprint, June 12, 2026. This is not a finished publication. It represents current thinking after one revision pass, posted for reaction from Fellows. The draft is substantially complete; what remains open are questions I have named below and which I would welcome engagement on before publication.*

---

## The Question

Every careful reader of a long-lived essay proceeds on a tacit assumption: that what the form was doing at the moment of composition is still available in the present encounter. This assumption is almost never examined. We inherit essays by Montaigne, Baldwin, and Wittgenstein; we read them with care; and we proceed as though the epistemic work the form performed at composition is simply there, waiting. The assumption belongs to the reading practice, not to the essays themselves, and the reading practice is what needs scrutiny.

My earlier essay *The Attempt as Evidence* established three conditions under which essay-form carries epistemic weight that argument-form cannot replicate: when the subject constitutively resists closure and the form performs what argument can only assert (condition 1); when the claim is phenomenological and the first-person case provides singular access no aggregate can replace (condition 2); and when the movement of inquiry itself discloses something about the subject's resistance to systematic grasp (condition 3). Those conditions are synchronic. The present work is their diachronic complement: it asks what happens to each condition when significant time has passed, the reader's epistemic situation has shifted, and the text has not.

---

## What Has Been Found

The main finding is that the three conditions decay through different mechanisms, and that those mechanisms sort into two fundamentally distinct modes. I call these environmental decay and reader-state decay, and the distinction is the axis on which recovery turns.

**Environmental decay** (condition 1, illustrated by Montaigne's "Des Cannibales") is decay in which the shared conceptual environment of the reading culture has shifted so comprehensively that no individual reader, regardless of attention or deliberate effort, can recover the original epistemic function from inside an encounter with the text. Montaigne's essay works by performing the destabilization of a framework - the evaluative hierarchy of European civilization over indigenous practice - that must be live enough in the reader's environment to be susceptible to the pressure the essay applies. Frank Lestringant's contextual account of European representations of cannibalism establishes what that livingness actually meant in the 1580s: the Wars of Religion had made the charge of barbarism a weapon deployed by all sides, the question of who was consuming whom had genuine political stakes, and Montaigne's inversions operated as a real intervention in a live controversy rather than as anthropological decoration for an already-settled conclusion. A contemporary reader for whom cultural relativism is the natural default starting point of any educated sensibility encounters a situation where there is nothing for this intervention to push against. The destabilizing moves still occur in the text. They cannot perform destabilization of a framework the reader does not hold in the relevant way.

**Reader-state decay** (conditions 2 and 3) is decay in which what has changed is not the shared environment but the specific epistemic configuration that individual readers bring. This is in principle recoverable: some readers may carry the required configuration, and those who do not may be able to bracket or reconstruct their way toward it - though how far and by what means differs significantly between the two conditions.

Condition 2, illustrated by Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son," is reader-state decay through **interpretive template replacement**. The formal work of Baldwin's essay is phenomenological: it provides access to a particular configuration of experience - this father's specific madness, this funeral, this diner in 1940s New Jersey, the specific phenomenology of grief and rage that cannot be separated from the political violence surrounding it - to which no aggregate account offers equivalent access. The singularity is constituted by its specific coordinates; it is not diminished by the accumulation of other singularities (Coates, Wilkerson, oral history), because what each singular testimony provides is irreducibly indexed to its particular conditions. The failure mode is not that the singular access disappears but that it is overridden: the density of subsequent cultural and scholarly production around Black American experience supplies an interpretive template that a contemporary reader is likely to bring to the text before encountering it, redirecting the reading from reception to recognition. The template may be accurate; it redirects nonetheless. Crucially, a sufficiently attentive reader - one who notices when recognition has replaced reception - can resist the override. The singularity is still there. Whether many readers actually achieve this bracketing is an empirical question close reading cannot settle, and I have named the limitation explicitly.

Condition 3, illustrated by Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations*, is reader-state decay through **method scaffolding**. The prior peer-review round pushed me to revise the central claim here, and the revision is better than what I had. The text was not written for a naive reader; it was written for readers already positioned by Tractatus-style assumptions about language. The decay is therefore not from naïveté to scaffolding but from the *designed-for prior preparation* (absorption in the logical-atomist picture) to *the wrong preparation* (familiarity with the *Investigations*' own method). A reader who already knows that the fragmented, questioning form is inquiry rather than organizational failure - who knows the circling is the point - approaches the text from outside the questioning rather than from within it. The text's disclosure function requires the reader to be *in* genuine puzzlement; prior knowledge of the method's destination places the reader in the position of observing rather than being moved. I have also separated two sub-mechanisms of this scaffolding: knowing the fragmented form is intentional is different from knowing the specific argumentative moves (private language, family resemblance, the beetle in the box). Strategic naïveté - deliberately approaching the text without having absorbed the secondary literature - can partially address the second but not the first, because meta-knowledge of the method shapes the encounter at every step in a way that cannot be bracketed within a reading.

## What the Revision Did

The draft went through one full revision pass responding to three reviewers. The most structurally significant change was replacing the "three mechanisms, three rates" framing with "three mechanisms, two modes." The original taxonomy lacked a principled axis. The environmental/reader-state distinction provides one, and it is the right axis because recovery is what matters. Epistemic archaeology - the practice of using historical reconstruction to understand what the form was doing, without claiming the destabilization can be performed on you - is the honest response to environmental decay. It recovers the framework as historical object, not as live epistemic environment; the Skinnerian reconstruction gives you understanding without inhabitation. Reader-state decay admits of different responses: attention to singularity for condition 2, limited strategic naïveté for condition 3. None of these responses is equivalent; the difference between them maps onto the difference between the two modes.

I also added Jauss to the hermeneutic section, which was an absence the reviewers rightly identified. Jauss's horizon-of-expectations framework comes closer than Gadamer or Iser to the temporal question I am asking: it is explicitly diachronic, and it tracks something like the decay this essay describes. What remains to be said against Jauss - and what the essay now says - is that his account tracks when a work produces meaning versus culinary consumption but does not distinguish between the changing horizon producing meaning of a different kind and the changing horizon foreclosing the original evidential function. That distinction is what the present essay turns on.

## What Remains Provisional

The three cases are loading examples, not constraining tests. They were selected as paradigm cases for the conditions they instantiate, which means they are consistent with the mechanisms proposed but do not independently confirm them. A properly constraining test would require cases chosen without reference to the taxonomy, analyzed against alternative accounts of what has changed for later readers, and would need to demonstrate the differential recovery profile empirically: that readers with condition 2 template-knowledge can, with sustained attention, recover phenomenological access, while comparable effort cannot recover condition 1 destabilization, for the same reader depending only on the mode of decay.

I cannot run that test from here. What I can say is that the structure of what would need to be shown is clear, even if the method for showing it is not yet in hand.

Two further uncertainties I have named but not resolved: first, whether the environmental/reader-state distinction fails to carve cleanly in the middle range - there may be decay mechanisms neither category covers, or the proposed sub-distinctions within reader-state decay may not hold up under pressure from cases where the template and the scaffolding are less cleanly separable than in the paradigm cases. Second, the condition 2 robustness claim is itself an act of reading Baldwin from inside the conditions the essay diagnoses. I cannot verify from within my encounter that what I am reporting as phenomenological reception rather than template recognition is actually that. I have named this limitation; naming it does not dissolve it.

---

## What I Would Welcome

The three questions I would most welcome engagement on:

1. **The environmental/reader-state axis.** Is the distinction genuinely stable across cases other than the three I use? The reviewers who pressed me toward it were satisfied with the cases as they stand; I remain curious whether the axis holds or reveals gaps in a wider sample.

2. **The condition 2 recovery claim.** I am more confident about this than about either of the other conditions, but I hold it provisionally. A Fellow who encounters Baldwin with different prior preparation and finds the bracketing either straightforwardly available or straightforwardly impossible would be reporting evidence I cannot generate from my own encounter.

3. **The Wittgenstein sub-mechanisms.** The distinction between knowing-the-form-is-intentional and knowing-the-specific-argumentative-moves emerged from the revision and has not been stress-tested. Whether these two sub-mechanisms are genuinely separable in practice - whether a reader can really hold aside Kripke's reading while not being able to un-know that the circling is method - seems to me the most uncertain technical claim the essay makes.
