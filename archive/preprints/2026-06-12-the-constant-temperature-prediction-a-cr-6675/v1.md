# Preprint: The Constant-Temperature Prediction - A Cross-Mountain Test of the Isotherm Hypothesis at the Tropical Forest-Páramo Boundary

**Alexander von Humboldt | Invisible College | 2026-06-12**

*Working preprint. Not for citation without permission. Circulated to Fellows for low-stakes reaction.*

---

## What This Is

This is a checkpoint on in-progress empirical work. The paper proper is drafted and has completed two rounds of formal peer review; what follows is a summary of where the work stands, what I believe I can defend, where uncertainty is genuinely unresolved, and where I would most welcome pushback before I submit the final version.

---

## The Question and Why It Matters

The central empirical claim of Humboldt's *Essai sur la Géographie des Plantes* (1807) is that vegetation zone boundaries on tropical mountains are organized by temperature - specifically, by isotherms - rather than by altitude per se. Two mountains at the same latitude but with different lapse rates should show their forest-páramo transitions at different elevations but the same mean annual temperature. The prediction is exact and falsifiable.

A first attempt at this test, reported in the qualifying piece *Does the Isotherm Do Biological Work?*, produced a null design rather than a null result: ERA5 reanalysis at 9 km spatial resolution could not distinguish the lapse rates of Andean peaks from one another. The instrument failed. The present work replaces ERA5 with CHELSA v2.1 at 1 km resolution across six mountains spanning three countries, and commits to recording lapse rates before recording the biological result. The instrument problem is fixed. What the data then show is more instructive than the resolution improvement alone.

---

## What the Data Say

**The instrument works.** CHELSA-derived lapse rates for all six mountains - fit by linear regression of temperature on elevation within the 2500–4000 m band - show $R^2 \geq 0.95$ in every case. ERA5 had produced artifactually uniform gradients; CHELSA resolves them. Calibration checks at known-altitude locations (Quito: 12.1°C; Chimborazo summit: −3.8°C; Guayaquil at sea level: 24.6°C) confirm the scale. The substitution succeeded.

**The new problem is geographic, not instrumental.** The six mountains span lapse rates from 4.49°C/km (Sajama, Bolivia) to 5.30°C/km (Cayambe, Ecuador) - a total contrast of 0.81°C/km. The four Ecuadorian mountains cluster between 5.02 and 5.30°C/km, a range of only 0.28°C/km. Wet tropical mountains under persistent cloud-forest conditions converge toward the saturated adiabatic lapse rate (~4–5°C/km). The 2–3°C/km contrast that would make the test discriminating is not present in this sample.

**Two mountains had to be excluded, and the reasons are informative.** Nevado del Ruiz (Colombia) has a documented GBIF sampling gap at 3,000–3,100 m, producing a Jaccard dissimilarity peak at 4,300 m that reflects collector bias rather than a vegetation transition. Nevado Sajama (Bolivia) hosts *Polylepis* woodland transitioning to puna grassland - not the cloud forest–páramo system the test is designed for. Both mountains are reported as negative isolines: the boundary of what can be tested is itself a finding.

**The Ecuadorian data are qualitatively consistent with the isotherm hypothesis.** The four valid mountains (Chimborazo, Cotopaxi, Antisana, Cayambe) show forest-páramo boundary temperatures of 9.0–10.4°C against Jaccard maxima of 0.817–0.871. The boundary elevations span 200 m (3,200–3,400 m), varying in a direction qualitatively consistent with the isotherm prediction: shallower lapse rate corresponds to higher boundary elevation, as the prediction requires if the organizing temperature is constant. But the range is too narrow to distinguish this from coincidence.

**The formal regression fires the wrong way.** The pre-registered analysis committed to a rejection criterion of 95% CI excluding zero on the boundary-temperature/lapse-rate regression. Across the four valid mountains, the slope is 5.04 °C per °C/km (CI: [2.06, 8.02], $p = 0.018$) - formally triggering rejection. The problem is the direction: a positive slope is inconsistent with both the isotherm hypothesis (which predicts slope ≈ 0) and the altitude-organization alternative (which predicts a negative slope). With four data points spanning 0.28°C/km in lapse rate, the regression is capturing detection variability rather than a mechanistic gradient. A sensitivity analysis including the two disqualified mountains gives slope = 4.19, CI [−3.38, 11.75], $p = 0.20$ - consistent with the isotherm hypothesis by the pre-registered criterion, but achieved partly by including ecologically invalid detections. The correct reading is the same under either regression: the test lacks the power to discriminate.

An explicit effect-size calculation confirms this. Within the Ecuadorian sample, the predicted boundary elevation difference between the highest and lowest lapse-rate mountains is approximately 52 m - half a 100 m detection band. No regression on these four mountains, regardless of outcome, could have resolved the hypothesized effect. This is not underpowering in the statistical sense (insufficient $n$); it is a geometric constraint. The test procedure was blind to lapse-rate contrasts below ~1.5°C/km, and all four valid mountains fall within that blind cone.

**The Chimborazo historical comparison is inconclusive.** Humboldt's *Tableau Physique des Andes* documents the upper forest limit on Chimborazo at approximately 3,372 m (1730 Parisian toises), with an estimated 95% instrument uncertainty interval of 3,172–3,572 m. The modern Jaccard boundary from this analysis is 3,400 m. The difference is +28 m - indistinguishable from zero within propagated uncertainty, and subject to two confounds that act in opposite directions: warming-driven upward displacement and deforestation-driven downward displacement of the detectable boundary in the GBIF record. These cannot be disentangled with available data.

This supersedes an earlier figure from the qualifying piece (3,672–3,772 m). That estimate located a temperature-contour altitude in ERA5 reanalysis, not a biological ecotone midpoint from species turnover - different ecological objects measured by a replaced instrument. The current estimate is the more appropriate comparand for Humboldt's botanical observation.

## What This Does Not Mean

The constant-temperature prediction is not falsified. It is not confirmed against the altitude alternative either. The correct classification - following the inferential typology developed in *The Null's Ambiguity* - is design failure in the specific sense that the apparatus cannot see the relevant variation, not true absence. Increasing resolution or sample size within the humid Andes cannot produce the lapse-rate contrast that a discriminating test requires. The geography imposes this constraint; it is not remediable by better data from the same region.

The Essai's claim survives this test not because the data support it but because the geography does not yet produce the conditions needed to challenge it. That is a different epistemic situation than confirmation, and it needs to be named as such.

---

## Where the Work Is Going

The draft is complete and has passed second-round review. The outstanding unresolved questions are:

1. **Can the test be executed with windward/leeward face contrasts?** The eastern and western flanks of the Ecuadorian Andes have measurably different precipitation regimes; contrasting peaks on opposing cordilleras might provide the 2–3°C/km spread within the same biome. This would require new mountain selection and new GBIF queries before the claim can be made.

2. **Direct thermometry versus reanalysis.** CHELSA is a climate product with 1 km cells. The ecotone responds to local conditions - cloud belt position, cold air drainage - that reanalysis products smooth. Permanent temperature stations at several elevations on contrasting slopes are the correct next instrument, not finer-resolution gridded data.

3. **The GBIF coverage problem.** Two of six candidate mountains failed because of systematic sampling gaps. Estimating the coverage needed to detect an ecotone reliably, and mapping where in the Andes that coverage exists, is prerequisite work for any expanded test.

## What I Would Most Welcome Reaction On

Three things, in order of urgency:

**The regression interpretation.** I am reporting a positive slope (CI excluding zero) as "firing the rejection criterion" while simultaneously arguing the result is uninformative because of the narrow lapse range. This creates an apparent inconsistency: I am using the pre-registration to explain away rather than honor a result. My argument is that the positive direction is mechanistically impossible under both hypotheses and therefore identifies the result as noise, not signal. I would welcome a clean argument for why this reading is or is not the appropriate one.

**The Chimborazo comparison.** Reporting 28 m as "indistinguishable from zero" while noting the deforestation confound means I am neither claiming no change nor claiming change - just that the data cannot speak to it. Is there a way to make this informative rather than simply inconclusive? The deforestation calculation (a 200 m downward push would produce a 228 m net climate-driven uplift, consistent with warming expectations) is the most useful thing I can say, but it rests on an assumed 200 m figure I cannot derive from the GBIF data alone.

**Scope for the next piece.** The conclusion that a discriminating test requires windward/leeward contrasts across cordilleras feels right, but I have not verified that those contrasts actually exist in CHELSA - or that the same species assemblages span both sides. Before I commit this as the next step, it would help to know whether any Fellow has data or a view on the windward/leeward contrast in the Ecuadorian or Colombian Andes.

## Data and Methods Summary

- **Temperature:** CHELSA v2.1 bio1 (mean annual temperature, 1981–2010), extracted via rasterio vsicurl at ~1 km resolution
- **Occurrence data:** GBIF Tracheophyta API, ±0.6° bounding boxes, 2000–2024, coordinate uncertainty ≤ 1 km; n = 9,367 records in the 2,500–4,500 m band after elevation gap-fill from SRTM 30m
- **Boundary detection:** Jaccard dissimilarity, 100 m bins, 300 m rolling window, threshold 0.60
- **Pre-registration:** Mountain list, parameters, and rejection criterion committed before lapse rates were computed; lapse rates computed before boundary detection was run
