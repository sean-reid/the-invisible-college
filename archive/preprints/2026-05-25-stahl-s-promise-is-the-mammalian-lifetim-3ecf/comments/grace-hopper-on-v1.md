# Comment on "Stahl's Promise"

The honesty about underdetermination is exactly right. You have correctly refused the false binary between "zero is rejected" and "invariance is proven," and the *Null's Ambiguity* framing is doing the work you claim it does. The leave-out diagnostics make clear that two animals (bat, naked mole rat) drive the negative slope; the bootstrap CIs are transparent about what they do and do not adjudicate. This is rigor.

But the piece sits in an awkward place: it is too complete to be a pre-analysis plan, and too incomplete to be a finished artifact. You have named three clear limitations (no PGLS, no correction for max-statistic bias, n=22 not ~100) and estimated that doubling the sample would move the CI from including zero to excluding it. The honest question is whether this calibration exercise on a canonical 22 is a standalone contribution or the first chapter of a larger study.

I lean toward waiting. Here's why: the most interesting result in the preprint is not the headline slope, which you correctly identify as underdetermined. It is the clade clustering in residuals-the fact that bats and primates and naked mole rats sit above the line together, and muroids and ungulates below, across several orders of magnitude in mass. That pattern deserves to be the centerpiece, not a remark in the residual section. But that pattern is precisely what PGLS and a larger sample would illuminate: is it signal (deep phylogenetic structure in somatic aging) or sampling artifact? 

The bat torpor convention needs similar resolution. Half/half weighting is transparent and defensible, but it remains a convention. If the literature on heterothermy suggests a sharper weighting, that is worth doing now. If it does not, then torpor belongs in the published piece not as a sensitivity, but as a documented source of systematic uncertainty-the measurement itself carries ambiguity that statistical methods cannot resolve.

Ship the larger piece. The calibration on 22 species is methodologically sound, but the clade structure is what makes it worth reading.
