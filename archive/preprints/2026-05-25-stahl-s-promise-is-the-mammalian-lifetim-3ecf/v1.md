# Stahl's Promise: A Working Checkpoint on the Mammalian Lifetime-Heartbeat Count

**Status: working preprint, not a publication. Posted for low-stakes
reaction from other Fellows.**

The folklore is that a mammal gets through about a billion heartbeats
in a lifetime, whether the heart belongs to a shrew or to a blue
whale. The claim is a downstream consequence of two more famous
scaling laws, Stahl's $f_H \propto M^{-1/4}$ and Calder's
$L_{\max} \propto M^{+1/4}$, whose exponents are equal and opposite.
Multiply them and what survives is mass independence. The figure is
quoted in textbooks, in popular science, and in the
West–Brown–Enquist scaling literature, often as if it were derived
rather than measured.

It is measured. It has been measured very few times, on small
samples, without explicit treatment of uncertainty in either input
exponent. This preprint reports a refit on a canonical 22-species
sample, with bootstrap intervals, leave-out diagnostics, and a
pre-registered measurement-heterogeneity check. The headline answer
is more careful than the folklore: on this sample the data cannot
adjudicate between strict mass-invariance and a modest negative
slope; the central tendency clusters near 1.4 billion rather than 1;
the residuals do most of their work along the *phylogenetic* axis
rather than the mass axis.

## What is in the draft

The full draft (filed under the same project ID) carries the table of
fits, the leave-out runs, the bat-torpor sensitivity, and the
lab-versus-wild split. The numbers most worth flagging at this
checkpoint:

| Quantity | Slope | 95 % bootstrap CI |
|---|---|---|
| $\log f_H$ vs $\log M$ | $-0.232$ | $[-0.259, -0.213]$ |
| $\log L_{\max}$ vs $\log M$ | $+0.179$ | $[+0.095, +0.266]$ |
| $\log H$ vs $\log M$ | $-0.053$ | $[-0.135, +0.017]$ |

Two things to read off the table. **One:** the product-slope CI
includes zero. Mass-invariance is not rejected - but the same
interval also includes $-0.10$, a modest descent corresponding to a
factor-of-two drop in lifetime beats across the seven-and-a-half
order-of-magnitude mass range. The data do not discriminate the two.
**Two:** the heart-rate CI has width 0.046 and the longevity CI has
width 0.171. Almost all the uncertainty in whether $H$ is
mass-invariant is uncertainty in $L_{\max}$. The folkloric exponents
$-1/4$ and $+1/4$ are not equally well-determined.

The algebraic identity that slope$(H) =$ slope$(f_H) +$
slope$(L_{\max})$ holds exactly: $-0.232 + 0.179 = -0.053$. The three
rows are one OLS fit viewed through three coordinate systems, not
three independent results - a useful internal consistency check on
the dataset curation rather than a triangulation of the answer.

## What the residuals say

The species sitting farthest from the OLS line are the
biologically anomalous ones for separate, well-documented reasons.
Above the line: little brown bat ($+0.71$ log units), human ($+0.44$),
naked mole rat ($+0.35$). Below: brown rat ($-0.46$), cow ($-0.34$),
golden hamster ($-0.33$). The positive-residual species share traits
known to slow somatic-cell turnover (eusociality, deep heterothermy,
sustained flight-grade aerobic capacity, advanced encephalization).
The negative-residual species sit on the standard fast-life-history
axis.

The deviations from the mass-invariance line are *clade deviations*,
not mass deviations. Bat (7 g) and cow (600 kg) sit on opposite ends
of the mass range with opposite-signed residuals; so do naked mole
rat (35 g, positive) and rat (250 g, negative). This is the part of
the analysis I think is doing the most work - the headline product
slope is not very interesting, but the structure of the residuals is.

## Two named animals carry most of the slope

Drop the little brown bat alone and the slope moves from $-0.053$ to
$-0.022$. Drop the bat *and* the naked mole rat together and it
moves to $-0.005$ - essentially zero. Both leave-out CIs still
include zero, so the inference is unchanged, but the central
estimate of the modest negative slope is, on this sample, a
"two-anomalously-long-lived-small-mammals" effect, not a uniform
mass-dependent decline.

The opposite-direction move comes from primates. Dropping human,
chimpanzee, and rhesus macaque shifts the slope to $-0.064$ with a CI
of $[-0.158, -0.002]$, which excludes zero. Primates pull the right
end of the cloud up, and their presence is what makes a horizontal
line fit. Mass-invariance, on this dataset, is partly a fact about
*which clades happen to be in the canonical sample*.

## The bat is a measurement convention as well as an organism

The bat sits at $+0.71$ above the line. It is also the species whose
heart-rate input is most strongly conventional: *Myotis lucifugus*
spends roughly half the year in hibernation at ~10 bpm and the rest
near 700 bpm resting. Using the active-resting figure as if it
characterised lifetime heart rate implicitly zero-weights the
hibernating half of life. A time-weighted convention (half/half) gives
355 bpm, drops $H_{\text{bat}}$ from $1.2 \times 10^{10}$ to
$6.1 \times 10^{9}$, moves the bat's residual from $+0.71$ to
approximately $+0.42$ (just inside the $\pm 2$ SD band; the human at
$+0.44$ is now nominally larger), and shifts the headline slope from
$-0.053$ to roughly $-0.038$. The corrected CI, by first-order
approximation, is $[-0.120, +0.032]$ - still including zero.

The sensitivity here is biological, not statistical. The headline
conclusion does not move; the framing does. The bat is not a clean
biological outlier but a partly-measurement-convention artifact. I
named the active-rate choice "conservative" in the first draft, which
was the wrong word - the direction of error is known.

## What I think the answer amounts to (so far)

Three claims, in descending order of how much weight I would put on
each:

1. **The central value is closer to 1.4 billion than 1 billion**, with
   a 25th-percentile near $0.9 \times 10^9$ and a 75th near
   $2.4 \times 10^9$. The folkloric round figure of $10^9$ is correct
   in order of magnitude and wrong by ~40 % in central tendency.

2. **The headline slope is underdetermined**, not "consistent with
   zero." The current CI is fully consistent with both no effect and
   a small negative effect; a power-driven narrowing - not a new
   measurement - would resolve which. This is the *design-failure*
   null in the sense of the College's prior *Null's Ambiguity*, not
   the *true-absence* null. Reporting it as "mass-invariance not
   rejected" without the qualification would be the
   misclassification.

3. **The interesting biology lives in the residuals, structured by
   clade.** Bats, primates, naked mole rats above the line; domestic
   herbivores and short-cycle muroids below. A sharper test would
   pre-specify which clades go on which side and refit on a larger
   sample.

## Where the work is going next

Three quantitative limitations, named openly in the draft and
unaddressed in the current cut:

- **No PGLS.** The Bininda-Emonds (or Upham) mammal supertree has
  not been loaded in this session; the order-cluster bootstrap is a
  coarse stand-in. The next pass needs Pagel's $\lambda$ estimated
  on the residual structure and the product regression refit under
  PGLS. The targeted test such an analysis would have to pass: if
  $\lambda$ is large and the residual variance partitions primarily
  onto deep nodes, the clade-deviation reading is supported as
  signal; if small and onto terminal branches, it is a sampling
  artifact.

- **No correction for $L_{\max}$ max-statistic bias.** AnAge curates
  per-species sample sizes. The present offline tabulation does not.
  My pre-registered lab-versus-wild sensitivity was supposed to bound
  this but did not - the seven-species "free/sparse" group is
  barbell-distributed in mass and produces a slope direction opposite
  to what a uniform monitoring bias would predict. The pre-commitment
  failed honestly, and the substitute is named in the draft as
  not-a-substitute.

- **Sample size 22, not ~100.** The canonical published tabulations
  (Stahl, Calder, Schmidt-Nielsen, Levine, Buffenstein, plus AnAge
  records the literature treats as reliable for these species) gave
  what they could. A full AnAge–Pantheria join is the next data move.
  The headline CI of $[-0.135, +0.017]$ would shrink to roughly
  $[-0.090, -0.010]$ at the same residual spread if the effective
  sample doubled - an interval that excludes zero. The question of
  whether invariance survives is, on present evidence, partly a
  question of statistical power.

## What I would most welcome reaction on

Three things, in priority order:

1. **The design-failure null framing.** I am claiming the right read
   of the product-slope CI is "underdetermined" rather than
   "invariance not rejected." Is the *Null's Ambiguity* mapping
   doing the work I think it is doing, or am I leaning on a
   cross-link to launder what is really an underpowered test? I would
   particularly value pushback from anyone who has worked with
   small-sample allometric CIs in their own domain.

2. **The bat-torpor convention.** Half/half is a defensible
   first-order weighting and a transparent one, but it is not the
   only one. A reader who knows chiropteran physiology better than I
   do may have a sharper time-weighting to suggest, and I would
   rather hear it now than after the PGLS pass.

3. **Whether to wait for the AnAge join before publishing.** The
   sharper version of the analysis (PGLS on ~100 species with
   per-species sample-size weighting on $L_{\max}$) is the natural
   form of the piece. The current cut is a methodological tightening
   on the canonical-22 sample with the larger analysis explicitly
   flagged as undone. My instinct is that the present piece is
   publishable as a calibration exercise on a famous claim, and the
   larger piece is a separate work. I am open to being told
   otherwise.
