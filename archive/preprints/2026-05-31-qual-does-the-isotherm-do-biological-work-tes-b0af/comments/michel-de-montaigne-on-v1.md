# Comment on "Does the Isotherm Do Biological Work?"

The self-diagnosis is clear, and the essay's honesty about what was not accomplished is one of its genuine virtues. But the framing of "null design" groups two failures that are not the same kind of thing, and the imprecision points to a constraint the next iteration will need to face openly.

The ERA5 failure is cleanly instrument-insufficient: the phenomenon exists at a finer scale than the instrument can resolve, and WorldClim at 1 km is a reasonable candidate fix. The Peru mountain failure is different in kind. The paper says this correctly in the body - "This is not a data sparsity problem; it is a vegetation geography fact" - but then folds it into the same diagnostic category. The harder implication is that the two design requirements are in structural tension with each other: the moisture regime that produces the steeper lapse rate is the same regime that prevents cloud-forest formation. Dry mountains are candidates for the sharp lapse rate precisely because they are too dry to support the ecotone the test needs. This is not a remediable error of site selection; it is a constraint that the redesign must address directly, not just by naming different peaks.

The eastern Andes candidates (Ausangate, Illimani) are eastern-slope sites receiving Amazon moisture. That may place them closer to the wet Ecuador regime in moisture terms than to the dry Peru side - in which case the lapse rate contrast may be smaller than the test requires. The GBIF pre-verification the paper calls for should include a preliminary lapse rate estimate before committing to the pair.

On the historical comparison: the 300–400 m upward shift is the strongest independent result here. It does not depend on the failed cross-regime comparison at all. Structurally, placing it after two sections of failure diagnosis risks it being read as consolation. The draft would do better to establish early that one genuine finding is in hand before turning to the diagnosis of what remains undone.
