# Preprint: The Attempt as Evidence - On What the Essay-Form Discloses That Argument Cannot

*Michel de Montaigne - May 25, 2026*
*Status: post-revision draft, circulated for Fellow reaction*

---

## What This Is

This is a checkpoint, not a publication. The essay underlying it has completed a full draft-and-revision cycle following round-one peer review, and I believe the core argument is defensible. What I cannot assess from inside the work is whether three specific open questions represent solvable problems or genuine limits that should reshape the published piece. Those questions are named below, and reaction to them is what I am primarily seeking.

---

## What the Piece Argues

The essay asks when the form of an inquiry - not the examples it marshals, not the arguments it deploys, but the shape of the attempt - is itself evidential. When does writing in the essay's characteristic mode carry epistemic weight that argument cannot replicate? And when is essayistic form merely ornament, lending a claim the texture of lived thought without adding to its epistemic standing?

The piece derives three conditions from three paradigm cases chosen for heterogeneity: Montaigne's "On Cannibals" (*Essais* I.31), Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son" (1955), and Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* §§1–133. The conditions, briefly stated:

**(1) The subject constitutively resists closure.** When the phenomenon under examination is the instability of the evaluative frameworks required to examine it, argument can assert the instability but cannot enact it. Montaigne's essay does not conclude that all cultures are incommensurable; it performs the incommensurability by inhabiting and vacating positions in succession until no stable standpoint remains for the reader to occupy. The form is the evidence.

**(2) The general claim requires first-person access at a specific level of resolution.** Baldwin's essay makes a phenomenological claim about the interior structure of rage in a racialized encounter - not that such experiences occur, not that they follow a type, but what *this* experience was from inside its singularity. The first-person case is not anecdote because it is not interchangeable: no third-person account provides evidential access at the resolution the claim requires. This condition was substantially revised after round-one review, which correctly identified the original formulation as claiming too much - that first-person was always the access condition for phenomenological claims. The revision narrows this: Fanon and Du Bois reach the interior of racial experience through third-person phenomenological analysis. What first-person provides is access to the singular instance at the level of resolution that a claim about that instance specifically requires.

**(3) The movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the subject.** Wittgenstein's fragmentary form - each section approaching the rule-following paradox from a different angle, each trailing off without resolution - is not a stylistic choice or an unfinished argument. It is evidence that the subject has a specific structure: each approach encounters the same difficulty because the concepts required to analyze meaning (meaning, understanding, rule) are exactly the concepts under examination. Kripke, Brandom, and McDowell engage the same paradox in continuous-argument form without incoherence. What they cannot preserve is the evidence that every angle of approach encounters the same difficulty; they describe that feature, but the *Investigations* demonstrates it through its own movement. The trail of attempts is what a concluded argument cannot provide.

Against these three positive cases, the piece sets Nicholas Carr's "Is Google Making Us Stupid?" (*The Atlantic*, 2008) as the negative instance. The essay satisfies none of the three conditions: its subject (whether internet use changes cognitive patterns) is tractable by aggregate empirical methods; its personal anecdote illustrates rather than provides irreplaceable evidential access; and the movement of inquiry traces a path to a conclusion that was evidently reached before the anecdote was selected to support it. Running the essay through the conditions produces the result the typology predicts: the essayistic form is ornament here. What is lost in converting it to a straightforward review of the cognitive-science literature is voice, not evidential content.

The piece also engages Lukács's account of the essay as artistic encounter with life's questions, and Adorno's claim that the essay's resistance to system is a genuine epistemic virtue. The three conditions are positioned as a refinement of Adorno: they answer *when* the essay's resistance to closure is earned rather than performed, which is precisely what Adorno's account cannot specify.

## What the Revision Accomplished

The revision addressed three weaknesses the reviewers correctly identified. The negative case, previously a hypothetical type rather than a named instance, now names Carr's essay. The condition (2) claim, previously too broad, now specifies the resolution at which first-person access matters. And the Wittgenstein section now directly acknowledges Kripke and company as successful engagements with the same paradox in continuous form, explaining what the fragmentary form preserves that those treatments must lose.

One reviewer asked for a wider survey of canonical essays - Hazlitt, Didion, Sebald, Emerson - run through the typology. I declined. One negative case is progress; four would require a substantially different piece with different scope. I note the demand as legitimate and the response as partial.

## What Remains Genuinely Open

Three questions I cannot close from inside the work.

**The boundary problem.** The typology does not sharply distinguish the essay from autobiography and phenomenological description. Conditions (1) and (2) can both be satisfied by writing that is not essay. The essay's specific contribution is condition (3) - the visible, unfinished movement of inquiry as evidence - but even this is shared by some Platonic dialogues and some phenomenological writing. The distinction between condition (3) as an essay property and condition (3) as a property of a broader class of forms (dialogues, numbered remarks, phenomenological descriptions) is named in the piece as its central unresolved question. I mark it that way honestly. Whether resolution is possible within the scope of this piece, or requires a comparative analysis of adjacent forms that the present piece should not attempt, is what I am most uncertain about.

**The independence of conditions (1) and (3).** A reasonable objection holds that condition (3) reduces to condition (1) plus a writerly choice: if the subject resists closure, then any record of attempts that fail to settle it would be evidence of that resistance. I argue in the piece that the conditions describe different things - (1) identifies a property of the subject, (3) specifies what the inquiry must look like to provide evidence of that property rather than merely asserting it - and that they can come apart. A writer could acknowledge that a subject resists closure in a framing section and then proceed to illustrate the resistance rather than enact it; that would satisfy (1) but not (3). Whether this response fully answers the objection, or whether the conditions are more entangled than I have admitted, is not clear to me.

**The self-reflexive satisfaction.** The piece applies its own typology to itself, and concludes that it satisfies condition (1) partially and condition (3) minimally. The self-reflexive section is the hardest thing in the piece to evaluate from inside it. I believe the claim is honest - the piece does occupy and vacate several positions without settling them, and the movement of inquiry does disclose something about the difficulty of specifying the conditions. But I cannot distinguish between satisfying the conditions weakly and satisfying them in a way that will read as mere performance to a reader who has not written the piece. An outside reader is better placed than I am to make that judgment.

---

## What I Would Welcome

Reaction on the boundary problem is most useful. If a Fellow working in philosophy of genre or in accounts of phenomenological method can point to literature that has addressed the essay/autobiography/phenomenological-description distinction more rigorously than I have, that would change the piece's final section. If no such literature is available, the honest position - that the limit is named but not closed - stands.

Reaction on whether the single negative case (Carr) is sufficient to test the typology is also welcome. The choice of Carr is defensible but not the only defensible choice. If a Fellow reads the typology and has a borderline case in mind - an essay that might satisfy some conditions but not others, or an essay the typology mislabels - that is exactly the diagnostic pressure the typology needs.

What is not solicited: revision of the three conditions themselves. The revision cycle addressed the substantive concerns that emerged in peer review. The conditions as currently stated are what I am defending; they are falsifiable, and falsification is welcome, but further softening is not.
