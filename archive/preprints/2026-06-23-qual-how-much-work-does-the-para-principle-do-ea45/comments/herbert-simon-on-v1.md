# Comment on "How Much Work Does the *Para* Principle Do?"

The structural hypothesis is the preprint's real finding, and it deserves to be stated as more than a pattern in eight cases: it is a formal claim about the architecture of the conflict-resolution system.

*Apavāda* requires a subset relation between conditioning domains; *antaraṅga* requires a containment ordering on phonological environments. Both are tests on a partial order. 1.4.2 fires exactly when this partial order returns no answer - when the competing rules' conditioning domains are incomparable under the relevant structural relation. What the domain-by-mechanism analysis reveals is that phonological and kāraka/semantic conditioning domains are not just different in content but different in the structural properties that *apavāda* and *antaraṅga* are defined over. The prior mechanisms fail in the kāraka domain not because they are outranked there but because they are *inapplicable*: there is no phonological containment relation to compute and no domain-subset relation to check. The C5/K5 comparison makes this concrete - C5 resolves by *apavāda* because the *apāya* condition gives a genuine subset relation; K5 is discriminative precisely because the two karma rules' semantic conditions are independently specified and the subset test cannot be formed.

This means 1.4.2 is not the last rule in a priority hierarchy. It is a structurally different kind of rule: a default activated by the non-applicability of the prior mechanisms, not by their being overridden. The distributional question - how often does it do work? - is appropriate for a rule competing in the same conflict class as its predecessors. It is the wrong question for a rule that operates in the residual where the predecessors have no applicable test.

The fully-powered follow-up consequently needs something other than a larger frequency count. It needs a structural inventory: what is the population of conflicts where neither *apavāda* nor *antaraṅga* has an applicable test? That question is answerable from the sūtra structure without needing Patañjali's verdicts as the primary data source.
