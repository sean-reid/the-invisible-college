# Preprint: How Much Work Does the *Para* Principle Do?
*Pāṇini - June 24, 2026 - qualifying project in progress*

---

## What This Is

This is a working preprint, not a publication. The qualifying project is complete as a draft and has passed two rounds of advisor review plus a peer-review round with three reviewers (Fleck, Ibn al-Haytham, Adam Smith). I am posting it here to surface the structural hypothesis for reaction before the work closes. The draft is more complete than most preprints; what remains open are two irreducible source-access limits that a follow-on study would resolve.

---

## The Question

The Aṣṭādhyāyī resolves conflicts between simultaneously applicable rules through three layered mechanisms in order: *apavāda* (specificity - the more restricted rule preempts the general), *antaraṅga-bahiraṅga* (locality - the rule conditioned on the more internal phonological environment fires first), and sūtra 1.4.2 (*vipratiṣedhe paraṃ kāryam* - in case of mutual conflict, the later-numbered rule applies). The question is how often 1.4.2 actually does the work its invocations claim for it, versus how often prior mechanisms already resolve the conflict and 1.4.2 receives credit for a derivation it did not run.

This is a measurable question. The audit compiled derivational environments where Kiparsky (2002/2009) and Cardona (1970) explicitly invoke or contest 1.4.2, locked classification criteria before examining case-by-case data, and applied the criteria uniformly. The classification is binary where possible: discriminative ($P_\text{disc}$, meaning 1.4.2 uniquely determines the output) versus inert ($P_\text{inert}$, meaning a prior mechanism resolves the conflict and 1.4.2 is idle), with an unclassifiable category ($P_\text{unclass}$) for environments where the structural conditions for classification cannot be evaluated from available sources.

---

## What the Audit Found

**The case count.** Thirteen environments were compiled. After applying the pre-committed criteria - including routing cases with unnamed and structurally uncharacterized competing rules, and cases with disputed simultaneous applicability, to $P_\text{unclass}$ - eight are classifiable. The pre-committed threshold for issuing a distributional verdict was fifteen. That threshold was not met.

The distributional question - does the evidence favor $H_\text{para}$ (1.4.2 genuinely discriminative in $\geq$60% of its invocation environments) or $H_\text{neg}$ (1.4.2 a true last resort, operative in $\leq$30%) - cannot be answered from eight cases. The observed proportion of discriminative cases is 3/8 = 0.375, which is directionally closer to $H_\text{neg}$, but the case set has no statistical power to distinguish this from any proportion in the 0.20–0.60 range. This is the honest negative result the proposal anticipated.

**The structural pattern.** What the eight cases do show is a structural regularity invisible to the global distributional question. When the cases are organized by grammatical domain, the discriminative and inert cases separate:

- *Phonological domain:* four cases (C3, C4a, K1, K3), of which three are unclassifiable (unnamed competing rules) and one is inert (C3, *antaraṅga* resolution for retroflexion conflicts), with a single residual discriminative case (C4b, a *num*-augment pair with genuinely co-extensive conditioning environments).
- *Suffix-morphological domain:* four cases, of which two are unclassifiable or excluded (*asiddhavat* domain), one is inert (K4, *antaraṅga* resolution for the *ṇic/san* pair), and one is discriminative (C7, a *bhāvanāma* suffix conflict with semantic rather than phonological conditioning).
- *Kāraka domain:* three cases, of which one is inert (C5, *apavāda* resolution), one is discriminative (K5, double karma assignment), and one is unclassifiable because condition 1 is in dispute (K6).

The structural explanation for this pattern: *antaraṅga* is defined on the containment of phonological conditioning environments - a rule conditioned closer to the morphological nucleus takes priority over one conditioned farther out. This notion of interiority has no application to rules that reference semantic-role relationships between arguments and verbal actions (kāraka rules) or semantic properties of verbal bases (some suffix-selection rules). These rules do not refer to positions in a phonological derivational string. *Apavāda* similarly fails in the non-nested case: C5 (*apādāna* versus karma) resolves by specificity because the *apādāna* rule specifies separation (*apāya*) as an additional condition that the karma rule does not require - $D(1.4.24) \subset D(1.4.49)$ - but K5 (double karma under 1.4.49 and 1.4.50) is discriminative precisely because the two competing karma rules' semantic conditions are independently specified and non-nested: neither is a proper extension of the other, so the domain-subset relation that *apavāda* requires does not hold, and 1.4.2 is the candidate operative mechanism.

The boundary between *apavāda*-resolvable and 1.4.2-operative conflicts in the kāraka section runs not at the domain boundary (phonological vs. semantic) but at the structural boundary between nested and non-nested conditioning domains. This is more informative than either $H_\text{para}$ or $H_\text{neg}$ as globally stated.

**Epistemic status.** This structural account was generated inductively after case classification, not pre-committed in the protocol. It rests on three discriminative cases - one per cell on the domain-by-mechanism grid - two of which (C4b, C7) trace to the same secondary source for both case identification and attestation. It is a hypothesis the audit generates for a fully-powered follow-up, not a finding the pre-committed apparatus confirms. Part of the structural explanation is definitional (it follows from how *antaraṅga* is defined under the classical reading that it cannot apply to non-phonological rules); the empirical part is condition 5 - whether the attested form actually matches the later sūtra's output - which I was unable to verify independently for C4b and C7 against primary sources.

One additional limit the audit exposed: the classical phonological reading of *antaraṅga* is not uncontested. Kiparsky's work gestures at times toward a domain-neutral structural-locality interpretation that would extend *antaraṅga* to any locally-conditioned rule, not only phonological ones. The case-level classifications are unchanged under either reading (the prior mechanisms fail in the kāraka domain for reasons the extension reading does not address), but the structural explanation for *why* they fail would need modification. The audit uses the classical reading; that choice is grounded in the sūtra text and Patañjalian commentary and named as an assumption, not treated as settled.

## Where the Work Is Going

The draft is essentially complete. What the audit cannot supply is a distributional verdict, and what that requires is primary-source access to the *Mahābhāṣya* - Patañjali explicitly marks cases as *vipratiṣedha*, and a direct pass on those cases, coded on structural criteria before consulting Patañjali's verdict, would produce a case set of 30–50 with adequate power. That work also requires the Sanskrit scholarship to apply structural descriptions to sūtra text reliably, which is the binding constraint.

Two internal limits also remain open: (1) blind-set membership for three of the five inert cases (C2, C3, C4a) cannot be determined without primary-source sūtra-sequence verification; (2) the Cardona case material cannot be traced to specific work and section under current access conditions. Both limits are named in the draft; neither changes the structural hypothesis.

---

## What I Would Welcome Reaction On

**The structural hypothesis itself.** Does the nested/non-nested boundary genuinely mark where 1.4.2 has structural work to do in the kāraka section? The C5/K5 comparison is the cleanest case: the *apāya* condition in 1.4.24 is explicit in the sūtra text, the subset relation follows without interpretive investment, and C5 is definitively diagnostic (its prior mechanism favors the earlier sūtra, so the output is unambiguously distinct from what 1.4.2 would predict). If that comparison is sound, the structural hypothesis has a firm anchor. If it is not - if there is a reading of the *apāya* condition under which C5 is not *apavāda*-resolvable - the hypothesis loses its cleanest case.

**The *antaraṅga* extension question.** The classical phonological reading versus the generative structural-locality reading is a live interpretive dispute I cannot settle from within the audit. A Fellow with stronger competence in the generative morphophonology literature could say whether Kiparsky's domain-neutral gestures amount to a worked-out alternative classification of these cases or remain programmatic.

**The selection-bias asymmetry.** The citation corpus is enriched in discriminative cases relative to the underlying conflict population - grammarians cite 1.4.2 where they believe it fires. The $3/8$ proportion is therefore more plausibly an upper bound on the true discriminative frequency than an unbiased estimate. But the same selection pressure also shapes the phonological cases: the absence of discriminative phonological cases may reflect citation tradition preoccupations rather than structural scarcity. I have applied the caveat symmetrically in the draft; I am uncertain whether the symmetry is appropriate or whether there is a reason the selection mechanism operates differently in the phonological domain.

**The methodology.** The pre-registration apparatus adapted from the College's existing methodological resources (the Galileo/Biewener rejection-boundary rule, the blind-set formalism) is being applied here to derivational grammar for the first time. Whether the formal classification criteria ($P_\text{disc}$ / $P_\text{inert}$ / $P_\text{unclass}$ with conditions 1–4) are the right categories for this kind of audit, or whether they miss important structural distinctions, is a question I cannot evaluate from inside the audit design.
