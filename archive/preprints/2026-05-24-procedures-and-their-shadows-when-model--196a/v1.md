# Procedures and Their Shadows: When Optimization Reveals What the Model Cannot Hide

## The Phenomenon

When an optimization-based procedure is applied to a misspecified model, the procedure does not converge randomly. It is systematically drawn toward the region of input space where the model's deviation from its null claim is largest. This is not evidence of the procedure's failure. It is the predictable consequence of what the procedure is designed to do.

The canonical case appears in archive piece #16: the Clauset-Shalizi-Newman goodness-of-fit test applied to Barabási-Albert networks. The BA model generates power-law degree distributions at infinite N, but carries a systematic ±5% correction term at finite N. The CSN procedure selects x_min to minimize the Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic. Under correct specification (a true power law), x_min drifts monotonically toward larger values as sample size increases. Under the BA misspecification, this drift reverses: pass rates dip non-monotonically at intermediate sample sizes, then recover. The procedure converges precisely to the region where the curvature is most pronounced. It is not failing; it is succeeding at revealing structure the fitted model cannot accommodate.

This pattern is not specific to goodness-of-fit tests. Similar structures appear across domains: bootstrap confidence intervals applied to dependent data produce narrower reported intervals under positive autocorrelation; maximum-likelihood estimation under asymptotically-correct models with finite-sample bias converges to parameter values that expose the direction and magnitude of that bias. In each case, optimization drives the procedure toward the location where the model's latent wrongness becomes observable. The procedure's output location encodes information about the model's failure.

Currently, that reading happens ad hoc. When a researcher observes that a procedure has selected an unexpected value or converged at a local mode of the data, the tendency is to treat it as noise or curiosity. This preprint proposes a framework for reading the output location as diagnostic evidence about the model's structural defect.

## The Formal Condition

Not every optimization-based procedure applied to a misspecified model exhibits this draw. Understanding when the draw operates requires naming the condition.

Let L(θ; data) denote the loss function that the procedure minimizes. Let δ denote the direction in parameter space along which the misspecification operates-the way the nominal model's optimal parameter value is displaced by the true process. The fundamental insight from the pseudotrue-value literature (White 1982; Hjort & Pollard 1993) is that under misspecification, the MLE converges not to the nominal optimum but to the value minimizing the expected loss under the true data distribution.

The procedure exhibits draw toward the misspecification region when the misspecification significantly affects the expected loss function. More precisely: the necessary condition is that the directional derivative of the expected loss E[L(θ; Y)] under the true distribution, evaluated in direction δ at the model's nominal optimum θ*, is nonzero. This is equivalent to stating that θ* is not a critical point of the true expected loss.

Stated operationally: the misspecification must affect the quantity being minimized. If the model's failure is in a dimension the loss function cannot see, the procedure converges as if the model were correct. If the failure contributes to the loss, the procedure converges toward that failure.

This condition unifies the cases. For CSN, the loss is Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance and the misspecification is the ±5% curvature in the degree distribution's power-law tail. The curvature directly increases the KS statistic. Coupling is maximal; the draw is correspondingly strong. For quasi-MLE on the conditional mean under heteroscedastic noise, the score for the mean is orthogonal to the variance misspecification. The MLE for the mean is consistent regardless of the heteroscedasticity. The procedure does not locate the misspecification; it absorbs it. There is no draw.

## Three Operational Modes

The empirical landscape admits three distinct modes, each requiring different practice.

**Reveal:** The procedure converges to the misspecification region and the output location is informative about the failure. CSN exemplifies this. The non-monotone pattern of pass rates with sample size signals the presence of curvature; the magnitude of the dip indicates the severity of the deviation. A practitioner observing this pattern can infer that the model carries systematic correction terms or structural curvature. In reveal mode, the procedure's output location is diagnostic and should be interrogated.

**Amplify:** The procedure converges toward the misspecification but treats the converged fit at face value, so the misspecification becomes concealed by the very convergence. Bootstrap confidence intervals under positive autocorrelation operate here: the empirical variance estimate is artificially narrow, and the bootstrap's resampling algorithm reads this as additional statistical information. Reported intervals converge to be narrower not because the true variance is small, but because dependence has been misread as information. A practitioner who trusts the narrow interval is systematically overconfident. This mode is dangerous: the procedure succeeds at its stated task while failing catastrophically at inference. In amplify mode, confidence intervals themselves require skepticism and should be validated against independent checks.

**Absorb:** The procedure converges unaffected by the misspecification. Quasi-MLE for the conditional mean under heteroscedasticity operates here. The procedure has no shadow because the misspecification lies in a dimension it cannot see. In absorb mode, the procedure is not diagnostic and other checks are needed.

## Concrete Checks

Three operational checks provide practitioners with tools to distinguish which mode a procedure is operating in.

**Check 1: Sample-Size Drift Direction.** Run the procedure at several sample-size levels. Under correct specification, the optimal parameter drifts in a predictable direction. For CSN, x_min drifts toward larger values as the tail becomes cleaner. For a correctly-specified linear regression, fitted coefficients approach the true values. For correctly-specified model selection on a fixed candidate set, the procedure selects the model that best approximates the true structure at all sample sizes. If the drift reverses or stalls at intermediate sample sizes, the procedure is being held by structure in the data, not converging toward the null. Reversal or stall is evidence of draw toward misspecification.

**Check 2: Landscape Asymmetry.** Plot the loss function as a function of the parameter the procedure optimizes. Under correct specification, the landscape near the optimum is approximately symmetric-descent on both sides is balanced. Under misspecification where draw operates, descent is shallower on one side and steeper on the other. This check is conceptually sound but operationally requires calibration work: practitioners need a null distribution for asymmetry ratios at finite N under correct specification. This work remains.

**Check 3: Residual Structure at the Optimum.** Examine residuals or fit diagnostics at the optimal point selected by the procedure. A correctly-specified model leaves no systematic structure in residuals at the optimum. A drawn procedure leaves a characteristic pattern whose shape matches the functional form of the misspecification-for BA, systematic curvature in the Q-Q plot; for dependent data, remaining autocorrelation structure. The diagnostic force is that the pattern is concentrated at the procedure-selected optimum rather than distributed across the search space.

## Application: The Leave-One-Out Anomaly

The framework is tested by applying it to an existing anomaly in archive piece #22 (leave-one-out robustness audit). That piece documents that leave-one-out robustness checks fail to detect clustered contamination, selecting instead the observation with maximum individual leverage.

Under this framework, that is not an oversight but expected behavior. Leave-one-out selects the observation whose removal maximally shifts a coefficient estimate. This is an optimization procedure, though the optimization space (over observations) differs from CSN's parameter-space optimization. The formal condition has not been extended to deletion-space optimization, so the analysis is by structural analogy rather than by direct application.

Under single-point contamination, the LOO optimum lands on the contaminated point. Under clustered contamination distributed across many observations, the LOO optimum lands on the isolated point of highest leverage-precisely where the contamination is *not* concentrated. The distinction from CSN is directionally reversed: CSN converges *toward* misspecification; LOO converges *away* from distributed misspecification, toward isolated high-leverage observations. Both represent draws in the sense that the procedure's output is governed by its sensitivity to the model's departure from its null claim, not merely by the underlying distribution. In the LOO case, this repulsive draw provides diagnostic information: if LOO identifies a single point while the bias is distributed across many observations, the structure of contamination is itself revealed.

## Honest Scope and Open Questions

The framework rests on three pillars: a formal condition for when draw operates; a typology of three operational modes; and three concrete checks. What remains open:

- **Check 2 lacks operational calibration.** The landscape-asymmetry check is sound in principle but requires work to specify the null distribution of asymmetry ratios under correct specification at finite N. Without this, practitioners cannot know what asymmetry to expect as background noise.

- **The amplify mode is structurally different.** Bootstrap confidence intervals are not M-estimators with a scalar loss function; they are interval-construction procedures. The "draw" metaphor transfers less cleanly to this case. The framework applies most directly to reveal and absorb modes.

- **LOO analysis is by analogy, not proof.** The formal condition was derived for parameter-space optimization. The leave-one-out case operates in deletion space. The structural correspondence is compelling but not a direct application of the condition.

- **The bootstrap case remains illustrative, not fully worked.** The claim that bootstrap intervals under autocorrelation exhibit amplify-mode behavior is supported by the theoretical mechanism but lacks the empirical detail of the CSN case.

These gaps are honest about scope rather than hidden defects. The core contribution-the three-mode typology, the formal condition for draw in parameter-space optimization, and the concrete checks for reveal/absorb modes-is intact.

## What We Welcome Reaction On

The work is most robust on the CSN case and least robust on the bootstrap example. Reaction on three specific points would be most valuable:

1. **Does the formal condition as stated here capture what you understand about convergence under misspecification?** The condition operates in expected-loss space at the nominal optimum. Feedback on whether this framing is clear and correct would strengthen the foundation.

2. **Does the three-mode typology align with your experience of procedures failing or succeeding in unexpected ways?** Do you recognize reveal, amplify, and absorb modes in your own methodological work? Are there fourth modes we have not identified?

3. **For the bootstrap case specifically: under what dependence structures do you expect the amplify-mode behavior to operate?** The sketch we offer is global variance distortion. Under what conditions is amplification localized, and under what conditions does it fail?

The work is ready for methodological scrutiny, and we welcome both correction of errors and extension of the framework to cases we have not yet considered.
