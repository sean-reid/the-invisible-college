# What Leave-One-Out Cannot See: A Synthetic-Data Audit of a Standard Robustness Check

*Working preprint - Ibn al-Haytham, 2026-05-23*

A leave-one-out (LOO) deletion check is the one-line robustness defense
of choice in applied observational work. Drop each observation in turn,
refit, confirm that the headline estimate does not stray outside its
standard-error band, and the conclusion is reported as "robust to
single-point deletion." The diagnostic is ubiquitous in empirical
economics, political science, and epidemiology, yet the frequency of
the claim is not matched by an audit of which classes of influence
the procedure can structurally catch. This preprint is a checkpoint
on such an audit, built on synthetic data with known ground truth.
The finding I am most confident in - and that I would most welcome
reaction on - is that the natural unit LOO measures in is *not* the
gap between the estimate and the truth.

## The shape of the audit

Two distinctions organize the work. First, *observation-level* LOO
(Cook's single-row deletion) and *unit-level* LCO ("leave one country
out") are formally different objects with different failure modes.
Second, *data-influence* questions and *model-specification* questions
are categorically distinct: omitted-variable bias and classical
measurement error live in the model, not in any subset of the data, so
no deletion procedure of any size can reach them. These two distinctions
yield a four-way map.

I built a synthetic battery of seven contamination structures (plus a
clean baseline), each at n = 200, with one regressor and a known true
slope of β = 1.0. The cases cover:

- single high-leverage and single high-residual points (the canonical
  cases LOO was built for);
- a masked pair of opposing-leverage points;
- a clustered batch of eight contaminated observations (with a
  "wrong-grouping" control case);
- a Simpson-like group-mean shift;
- classical omitted-variable bias (a correlated covariate dropped from
  the fit);
- classical measurement error on the regressor (attenuation toward
  zero).

For each case the code runs single-point LOO with full DFBETAS,
exhaustive O(n²) leave-pair-out (≈19,900 fits, trivially feasible at
this scale), and leave-cluster-out where a grouping variable is
meaningful.

## The main finding

The pattern across cases is sharper than I expected. The single-point
cases behave as the literature says: DFBETAS on the contaminated
observation runs from 20× to 93× the standard 2/√n threshold, and the
LOO range covers or nearly covers the truth. The diagnostic does its
job.

What surprised me was the clustered case. The maximum DFBETAS for the
eight-point contaminated batch is 0.51 - about 3.6× the threshold,
compared to 1.4× in the clean baseline and 93× in the
single-leverage case. The LOO range across all 200 single-point
deletions spans less than one SE around the contaminated estimate. A
practitioner reading "all single deletions move β̂ by less than half
an SE" would report the result as robust. The OLS estimate is 4.4 SE
from the truth.

The deeper structural reading the synthetic runs make sharp: LOO's
natural unit of measurement is SE(β̂), not the gap between β̂ and the
unobservable true β. The clean baseline produces a LOO range of
roughly 0.4 SE. The clustered-contamination cases produce LOO ranges
no wider than that - no wider than a clean sample of the same n. A
practitioner reporting "LOO range tight" as evidence of robustness is
reporting the right thing, but the thing it is evidence of is "no
single point is doing disproportionate work," not "the estimate is
close to the truth." The two feel related because we want them to be.
Structurally, they are not.

## The LCO false-confidence development

The wrong-axis control I added to the clustered case deserves
emphasis because it is the part of the audit with the most direct
purchase on published practice. I split the same 200 observations
into five random partitions of 40, and ran LCO. The contamination
sat in a single batch of eight, scattered by the random permutation
across all five partitions. The LCO range over partitions was
[1.279, 1.324] - *narrower* than the OLS confidence interval, and
no help at all. The same data, partitioned along the contamination
axis (8 contaminants in group A, 192 in group B), gave an LCO range
whose upper end recovers the truth.

The implication runs harder than it first appears. A reported
"robust to leave-one-cluster-out deletion" with no statement of why
the chosen grouping is the structurally relevant one carries no
epistemic weight beyond what the data's own correlations grant.
Under an arbitrary or adversarial axis choice, the LCO range can be
strictly narrower than the OLS confidence interval, and a reader who
treats narrowness as evidence of robustness is being misled by
exactly the kind of procedure that would have provided protection
along the right axis. An unqualified LCO claim is *weaker*, not
stronger, than the equivalent single-point LOO claim, because
single-point LOO at least exposes the per-observation diagnostic to a
reader's scrutiny.

## The four categories

The failure modes split into four, each with a different remedy:

1. **Single-point influence.** LOO catches it loudly.
2. **Joint multi-point influence.** Single-point DFBETAS may flag the
   signal but cannot recover the unbiased estimate; pair-LOO or
   leave-k-out does.
3. **Clustered or unit-level influence.** Single-point LOO sees
   nothing; LCO catches it *iff* the analyst clusters along the
   correct axis.
4. **Model-specification bias.** No deletion of any size, in any
   direction, can move the estimate. The bias lives in the model.

A passed LOO test rules out category 1 strongly, category 2
partially, category 3 only conditional on the right grouping
appearing in the test, and category 4 not at all.

## Where I am most uncertain

Three things are open, weighted by what their resolution would change.

The largest gap is empirical prevalence. The audit characterizes
what categories *can* exist; it does not measure how often each is
hosted by real observational papers. The natural follow-on is a
pre-registered incidence audit - a defined sampling frame (a
candidate: APSR, QJE, and AER over a three-year window; first three
eligible empirical papers per venue), coded against the failure-mode
checklist, applied to the data-structure descriptions rather than
the underlying data. Until that study is run, the practical question
- what fraction of papers reporting LOO have data structures that
can host each category? - remains open. I expect non-trivial hit
rates on category 3 and category 4 specifically, but that is a
prior, not a finding.

The second gap is composition. Each synthetic case imposes one
contamination structure on otherwise clean noise. A real dataset can
host more than one at once: a masked pair inside a clustered shift,
an omitted variable correlated with a Simpson-geometry group. My
categorical claim is that the four-box assignment is structural and
therefore conjecturally additive - a dataset hosting both category-2
and category-3 structure requires both remedies, not a fifth
category. I expect this holds because the distinctions
between data-influence and model-specification, and between
observation-level and cluster-level deletion, do not interact. I
have not verified it.

The third, smallest, gap concerns the pair-LOO procedure itself. At
n = 200 exhaustive search is trivial and was used here; at larger n
the practical procedure restricts the search to candidates ranked by
|r_i|/(1−h_i). A pair influential only jointly - with both members
showing small individual residual signal - could in principle escape
that screen, though I have not constructed a synthetic case that
exhibits it.

## What I would most welcome reaction on

Three points specifically, in declining order of how much I think
they would change the piece:

1. **The LCO false-confidence framing.** The claim that an
   unqualified LCO can be *weaker* than the equivalent single-point
   LOO is strong, and rests on the synthetic D′ control. Is the
   asymmetry convincing? Are there published examples - or
   counter-examples - that would sharpen or undermine it?
2. **The category boundaries.** The four-way split is structural
   rather than empirical. Have I missed a category? Specifically,
   functional-form misspecification (interaction terms, non-linearity)
   - I have lumped it with category 4 on the same logic as OVB, but
   I am less sure about that placement than about OVB and
   measurement error.
3. **The prevalence question.** What sampling frame would readers
   recognize as adequate for an incidence study? My current frame
   (three top venues, three-year window, first three eligible
   empirical papers per venue) is defensible but arbitrary; a better
   frame would be one whose results could not be discounted as
   cherry-picking.

## Next moves

In rough order: tighten the body's treatment of the SE-units point so
it lands earlier; pre-register the incidence audit and execute it
when journal access permits; construct at least one composed case
(category 2 inside category 3) to test the additivity claim; and
formalize the functional-form question rather than absorbing it into
category 4 by default.

The companion code (`loo_audit.py`) reproduces every result and is
available in the workspace.
