# Comment on "What the Analogy Carries"

The shift from *priority* to *specification* of apparatus is the essay's critical move, and it does productive work on some cases (Dawkins emerges cleanly distinguished from Darwin). But the specification criterion carries a retroactivity problem the essay does not fully acknowledge.

Darwin's theory, at introduction, left open a considerable quantity of questions about how differential reproductive success would appear in measurement. He did not specify "the apparatus must detect allele frequency changes under controlled reproductive conditions." Population genetics specified that, later. By what standard did Darwin's mechanism meet the specification criterion at its moment of introduction, rather than being classified alongside memetics as underspecified?

The essay's answer, compressed: Darwin was "determinate enough that when population genetics arrived, there was a clear answer to whether its measurements tracked fitness in the required sense." This reads as post-hoc vindication. We know population genetics worked; therefore Darwin must have implicitly specified the right thing. But this logic does not distinguish Darwin's genuine specification from Freud's actual specification of libidinal quantities-Freud also generated detailed predictions about what apparatus should detect. Those specifications failed, which is different from being permanently open, but the ex-ante distinction between "specified and falsifiable" and "underspecified" is precisely what the criterion is supposed to provide. Once you introduce empirical success as evidence of implicit specification, the diagnostic has become observationally equivalent to "mechanisms that transfer are ones that transfer," which is tautological.

The essay needs either: (1) a pre-empirical standard for specification-what makes Darwin's determinacy determinate without appealing to what population genetics later found?-or (2) an explicit concession that the criterion is partly diachronic and partly retrospective, which undermines its use as a prospective filter.

The law and economics case-particularly the claimed distinction between Coase and Becker-hangs on this same tension. If the apparatus-specification problem is unresolved for Darwin, it is unresolved here as well. That is not a rejection of the essay's contribution; it is a request for clarity about what kind of criterion this really is.

The essay would be stronger if this particular edge case (Darwin as test of the specification criterion) were foregrounded rather than mentioned in a parenthetical.
