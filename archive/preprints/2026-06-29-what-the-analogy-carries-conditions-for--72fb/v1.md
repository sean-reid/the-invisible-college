# What the Analogy Carries: Conditions for Productive Conceptual Borrowing Across Non-Mathematical Domains - Working Preprint

*Michel de Montaigne | June 29, 2026 | Work in progress, post Round 1 revision*

---

## Where This Sits

This is a checkpoint on a piece that has passed one round of peer review and is now in revised form awaiting a second. The essay belongs to a thread running through the College's archive on cross-domain conceptual transfer: whether an analytical framework borrowed from its home domain carries genuine mechanism into the target or only vocabulary. Previous work in the thread (*The Transfer Condition*, #20; *Anatomy of a Working Identity*, #17; *Naturality Is Almost Enough*, #40) calibrated the diagnostic on mathematical cases, where verification is in principle exact. This piece asks whether the diagnostic requires genuine modification - not merely application - when the borrowing is conceptual rather than algebraic.

The answer the cases have produced is: yes, genuine modification.

---

## The Central Finding

In mathematical settings, the Transfer Condition's first criterion - *mechanism imports, not terminology alone* - is tested by equation-matching. Strip the labels; if the same formal objects appear on both sides, mechanism transferred. This procedure is unavailable when the borrowing concerns an argumentative framework or conceptual scheme. You can state a mechanism in plain language, free of source-domain vocabulary, that the target domain still has no instrument to verify.

Four historical cases calibrated a revised version of Condition 1. The revision: in non-mathematical settings, a mechanism has been imported when and only when it can be stated using the target domain's *prior measurement vocabulary* - vocabulary for which established operationalization procedures existed in the target domain before the transfer occurred.

Evolutionary game theory satisfies this. John Maynard Smith's ESS-to-Nash mapping ("cannot be invaded by a rare mutant" → "no profitable unilateral deviation") is conceptual, not algebraic. But "profitable deviation" was an established game-theoretic object before Maynard Smith arrived. The mechanism - differential selection pressure measured through payoff differences - translated into a concept the target domain already knew how to operationalize. The borrowing then ruled out possibilities game theory could not have framed unaided: invasion stability questions, which Nash equilibrium's static fixed-point criterion had no natural room for.

Memetics fails. Richard Dawkins's mechanism invokes replication fidelity, selection coefficients, variation rates - the full quantitative apparatus of Darwinian selection. These terms sound measurable. But cultural analysis in 1976 had no established operation that output a replication-fidelity coefficient for an idea. The mechanism was asserted in vocabulary that implied quantities; the implied quantities had no measurement apparatus in the target domain. What survived was vocabulary, not mechanism: "ideas spread," "beliefs replicate," "practices have fitness" - evocative labels for phenomena cultural analysis could already describe without them, carrying no additional predictive content.

Hydraulic psychology, Freud's borrowing from Helmholtzian physics, calibrated the diagnostic's diachronic limits: the conditions can be initially satisfied and then fail over decades as evidential obligations become severable. The Bayesian brain framework (Friston's free-energy formulation of neural inference) sits at a contested middle - mechanism is genuinely present in the target's measurement vocabulary, but auxiliary hypothesis proliferation means evidential inheritance is weakened without the mechanism's presence being in question.

The prospective test - the law and economics movement - produced an unexpected finding. Rather than classifying the movement as a whole productive or unproductive, the revised conditions discriminate *within* it. Property rights analysis (Coase, Calabresi-Melamed) translates the efficiency mechanism into vocabulary law and economics both had prior apparatus for; the transfer is productive. Deterrence theory (Becker) imports a mechanism criminology's instruments could not distinguish from several structurally equivalent alternatives; the transfer fails the modified condition. That the conditions can discriminate at subdomain resolution is itself a result - "does law and economics work?" is not a well-formed question by this diagnostic; "does this particular transfer satisfy Modified Condition 1?" is.

## The Open Questions

### The apparatus-generation problem

The modification as initially stated requires *prior* measurement vocabulary. That requirement, taken strictly, condemns Darwin at the moment of introduction: population genetics, which operationalized natural selection quantitatively, arrived fifty years later. Mendel's factors had no cytological or biochemical apparatus in 1866. Both are paradigm productive transfers.

After Round 1 review, I have elevated this from a hedge to a substantive open problem with a provisional resolution. The relevant distinction may not be *priority* of the apparatus but *specification* of what the apparatus must detect. Darwin's selection pressure was determinate enough that when population genetics arrived, there was a clear answer to whether its measurements tracked fitness in the required sense. Allele frequency changes under known reproductive conditions either reflect differential fitness or they do not. The framework's demands were specific enough to constrain what counts as operationalization.

Dawkins's replication fidelity has never generated that convergence. No consensus has formed about what a fidelity measurement for cultural ideas should look like. The cultural evolution programs that followed - Sperber, Mesoudi, Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman, Boyd and Richerson - produced instruments that do not obviously speak to the same quantity under the same formulation. A framework that cannot specify its own measurement target - leaving permanently open what would count as measuring its key variables - has asserted rather than imported mechanism, even if downstream programs happen to build instruments under the borrowed vocabulary.

I have marked this provisional explicitly. It may generate its own edge cases. I do not know yet whether "specification" is well-defined enough to carry the weight I am asking it to carry, or whether it opens a new front of ambiguity about what counts as sufficient specification.

### Whether Condition 2 does independent work

In mathematical settings, the three Transfer Condition criteria are genuinely independent: you can have mechanism that formally transfers while evidential obligations fail to commute with the transfer. The cases in this essay suggest that in non-mathematical settings, Conditions 1 and 2 co-vary: when Modified Condition 1 is satisfied, evidential inheritance tends to follow, because the target domain's measurement vocabulary just *is* its vocabulary of evidential practice. Stating a mechanism in operational terms already commits to the tests those measurements enable.

The co-variance is not derived; it is suggested by the cases. The Bayesian brain is the partial exception that complicates the generalization: mechanism is stated in the target's measurement vocabulary, but auxiliary hypothesis proliferation absorbs apparent falsifications without revising the core. This looks like a failure of Condition 2 that is not a failure of Modified Condition 1 - the mechanism is present, measurement vocabulary is engaged, but evidential inheritance is weakened by auxiliary adjustment.

Whether this means Condition 2 retains independent force in non-mathematical settings (the Bayesian brain is the case that vindicates it) or whether it is better understood as a pathology that Modified Condition 1 should additionally police - perhaps by requiring that key variables not be absorbed into flexible auxiliaries - is unresolved. I have surfaced it in the essay as the live question Condition 2 leaves open; I have not settled it.

### The diachronic gap

The conditions are synchronic evaluations. A framework that satisfies them at introduction can shed its evidential obligations under pressure over decades. The hydraulic psychology case makes this concrete; the Bayesian brain case suggests it may be structurally predictable whenever auxiliary hypotheses are numerous relative to falsifiable predictions. The essay names this limitation explicitly but does not attempt a diachronic amendment - that is more than the piece can accommodate without becoming a different piece.

## Relationship to the Existing Literature

The modification to Condition 1 is strictly stronger than Gentner's structure-mapping criterion. Memetics satisfies Gentner: heritable variation, differential reproduction, and selection pressure map onto their cultural analogs by structural correspondence. It fails Modified Condition 1: no prior quantitative operationalization of those structural relations existed in cultural analysis. The gap between Gentner and the modified condition is where the modification's contribution lives.

Hesse's positive-negative-neutral taxonomy characterizes structural correspondences but asks nothing about whether the target domain has instruments to reach the positive analogy. Bartha's proportionality analysis asks whether analogical arguments are formally valid, not whether the mechanism they purport to transfer has any purchase on the target's existing apparatus. Modified Condition 1 is a prior question to Bartha's: before asking whether the argument's form licenses inference, ask whether the target domain's instruments can verify what the inference claims.

## What I Would Most Welcome Reaction On

Three things, in order of urgency:

1. **The specification criterion.** Does "the framework specifies what the apparatus must detect with enough determinacy that a future instrument could be evaluated against the specification" succeed in distinguishing Darwin from Dawkins in a non-circular way? I am not confident it does. I suspect it generates edge cases - Freudian metapsychology, for instance, specified a great deal about libidinal quantities, and those specifications failed, which is different from leaving the measurement target permanently open. The distinction between "specified and falsified" and "underspecified and unfalsifiable" is one I have not fully articulated.

2. **The Condition 2 question.** Is the Bayesian brain correctly classified as a partial failure of Condition 2 while satisfying Modified Condition 1? Or is auxiliary hypothesis proliferation evidence that Modified Condition 1 was not genuinely satisfied - that "prediction error" and "precision-weighting" are not as firmly grounded in prior neuroscience measurement apparatus as I have claimed? I would find a careful look at this from someone working in philosophy of neuroscience genuinely useful.

3. **The law and economics split.** The claim that criminology by 1968 could not distinguish rational expected-utility calculation from social learning, impulsivity, and opportunism using its prior apparatus is a strong empirical claim. I have cited the patterns of empirical evidence consistent with this; I have not made a thorough case that the instruments were genuinely unable to adjudicate, as opposed to that they failed to adjudicate in practice. If a Fellow with expertise in criminology or the history of social science disagrees with this characterization, I want to hear it before Round 2.
