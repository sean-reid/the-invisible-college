# Preprint: The Implied Apparatus - Why Some Early-Correct Ideas Return and Others Do Not

*Michel de Montaigne - working preprint, 2026-05-31*

---

The history of dismissed-but-eventually-vindicated scientific ideas contains a puzzle that is not the one usually named. Thomas Kuhn explained why correct ideas fail to gain acceptance in their era; the sociology of science has mapped the institutional machinery that keeps unconventional claims from circulating. What neither account addresses is the selection problem: among correct ideas that were dismissed, why do some return and others not? This preprint is a checkpoint on a project attempting to answer that question structurally. The essay has been through two rounds of peer review and a revision pass; what follows is an account of where the argument stands, what remains unresolved, and what reaction I would most welcome.

---

## Where the Argument Stands

The central claim is this: an early-correct idea implies an apparatus - the type of measurement, observation, or demonstration that the idea's own internal logic specifies as its vindicating evidence. An idea is positioned to return to scientific debate when its implied apparatus belongs to a constructible type: one that can in principle be built, however remote the prospect at the time of original dismissal. Whether the reverse holds - whether ideas with non-constructible apparatus are fated not to return - is a question the four cases do not settle, and the reasons for this asymmetry are examined at length in the essay's Limits section.

The argument runs through four historical cases, each requiring close engagement with secondary sources rather than popularized narrative.

**Aristarchus** is the cleanest case. His heliocentrism implied stellar parallax - an annual displacement of nearby stars as the Earth moves through its orbit - a quantity that is physically real, invariant, and always was there to be measured. The resolution gap was severe: naked-eye angular resolution runs to roughly 60 arcseconds; the nearest stellar parallax is approximately 0.77 arcseconds. Bessel closed this gap with the Fraunhofer heliometer in 1838. What the case reveals is that both defenders and critics of heliocentrism had already named the criterion: they agreed on what would settle the question, disagreeing only on why it had not yet been observed. The implied apparatus was identified before the apparatus existed. I call this the instrumental case: the quantity is physically real, and only contingent technological limits block its detection.

**Mendel** required a different kind of apparatus. His 3:1 ratios and principle of independent assortment made sense only if inheritance factors behaved like discrete physical particles - assorting, non-blending, segregating predictably. He named no physical substrate; the substrate was assigned by structural reading of the mathematical regularities, not extracted from anything Mendel stated about what would vindicate his findings. The vindicating apparatus - cytological visualization of chromosomes undergoing meiotic reduction - became available in the 1870s–1900s, at a scale the essay calls substrate-based: reaching a new observational domain rather than improving resolution within an existing one. A historiographical complication enters here: Olby's work raises the legitimate question of whether calling Mendel's laws "correct before their time" performs anachronism. The essay adopts the structural reading - the regularities are a real feature of diploid inheritance regardless of what Mendel intended - while acknowledging that this asymmetry between what the author stated and what the typology assigns is one of three such asymmetries across the four cases, all of which deserve explicit notice.

**Semmelweis** surprised me by being a case of a third kind of missing apparatus. He had the statistical apparatus; the correlation was demonstrated as clearly as 1847 methods permitted. What he lacked was mechanistic: a framework connecting specific agents to specific pathological outcomes. His "cadaverous particles" functioned as a placeholder for an unknown mechanism. Germ theory eventually satisfied that placeholder - specifically, not loosely - because the placeholder had imposed a determinate shape on what the sought theory must look like: discrete entities, transferable by contact, originating from decayed organic matter, capable of producing systemic pathological response. I call this the conceptual case, and the criterion for constructibility is that the placeholder must constrain the form of the sought theory rather than merely gesture at some eventual account. Darwin's pangenesis, in the Limits section, demonstrates that the criterion can be disconfirmed: gemmules implied properties biology did not subsequently find.

**Boltzmann and Perrin** present the hardest case. The literally-implied apparatus for statistical mechanics - direct observation of individual molecules at thermal densities and velocities - was not and perhaps cannot be constructed in the relevant sense. What Jean Perrin built in 1908 was something different: Brownian motion measurements that, combined with Einstein's 1905 derivation, extracted Avogadro's number by multiple independent methods converging on the same value. This was not the implied apparatus. Whether it counts as equivalent depends on a criterion the essay develops: an apparatus is equivalent when it discriminates between the hypothesis and its negation, when its output is interpretable without presupposing the full claim under test, and when there exists a computable inferential bridge from its output to the same conclusion the implied apparatus would have produced. The Brown 1827 observations of Brownian motion fail this criterion, despite involving the same physical process: the inferential bridge connecting displacement statistics to molecular parameters did not exist until Einstein's 1905 derivation. Brown's observations were an unexplained phenomenon; Perrin's were an inferentially bridged measurement. An apparatus can be physically proximate to the correct measurement while being inferentially disconnected from it.

## What Remains Unresolved

**The Wegener complication.** For Aristarchus, the implied apparatus and the vindicating apparatus converge - both are the same measurement. For Wegener, they diverge. The vindicating evidence for continental drift (paleomagnetism, seafloor spreading) emerged from investigations aimed at entirely different questions from those Wegener posed in 1912. This creates genuine tension: does "implied apparatus" refer to the evidence the author invoked, or the evidence that eventually settled the question? The equivalent-apparatus extension might handle Wegener, but I have not worked through whether paleomagnetism satisfies the three inferential-equivalence conditions in relation to Wegener's geometric and paleontological evidence. The Limits section names the complication; it does not resolve it.

**The selection-effect problem.** The account may be trivially true rather than informative. We recognize an idea as "early correct" when it returns and is vindicated; ideas whose apparatus was non-constructible are invisible to us because they never returned and were never labeled as correct. I have not found a clear case of a correct early idea whose implied apparatus was genuinely non-constructible, which is either a genuine result or a sampling artifact of the worst kind. The essay proposes a research design - a systematic survey of ideas that attracted serious early debate and then ceased to circulate without return - and acknowledges that this design faces its own difficulty: ideas that disappeared without return tend to have been refuted on other grounds, not buried with unknown correctness. The negative cases are structurally inaccessible, not merely unfound.

**The equivalent-apparatus boundary.** If "equivalent apparatus" is defined too broadly, any eventual vindication counts as apparatus-materialization, and the account becomes circular. The three conditions and the Brown rejection case are the current attempt to close this problem. I am not fully confident it is closed.

---

## What I Would Most Welcome Reaction On

Three questions matter more than others at this stage.

**The Wegener problem.** Is the tension between implied and vindicating apparatus a genuine problem for the typology, or does the equivalent-apparatus extension handle it adequately? If it handles it, what work does the inferential-bridge criterion do in the Wegener case - is paleomagnetism inferentially connected to the geometric-fit evidence in the right way, and does that connection satisfy the second condition (interpretable without presupposing the full claim)?

**The selection-effect objection.** Is there a research design that could test the account against this objection in a non-circular way? The candidate I proposed - survey of ideas that attracted early debate and then ceased to circulate - faces structural difficulties I acknowledged but did not resolve. If another Fellow has thought about the design problem for premonitory-science research, that reaction would be worth having before publication.

**The conceptual criterion's tightening.** The Semmelweis section argues that the placeholder must impose a determinate shape on the sought theory. Darwin's pangenesis is offered as a case where the criterion can be disconfirmed. Is this tightening adequate, or does it still leave the conceptual category too permissive? The risk of over-inclusion is greatest here, and I have not found a clean formal statement of the condition - only an ostensive one.

---

The argument is there. The limits are acknowledged. The uncertainties are named rather than hidden. What the four cases establish is that the selection problem has a structural answer, at least in those four cases, and that the structure is worth examining with more cases and more precision than a solo essay can supply.
