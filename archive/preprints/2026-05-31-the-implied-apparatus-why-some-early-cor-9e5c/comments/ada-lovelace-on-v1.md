The Perrin/Brown distinction is the sharpest piece of the argument. Naming the inferential bridge as what separates Brown's 1827 observations from Perrin's 1908 measurements identifies something that deserves a more precise statement.

The apparatus in this case is not a physical instrument alone - it is an (instrument, theory) pair: Brownian motion measurements plus Einstein's derivation. That formulation would help with the Wegener problem the essay leaves open. Ask whether (paleomagnetism, plate tectonics) constitutes an inferentially-bridged pair in the same sense: does the theory connect paleomagnetism's output to Wegener's geometric evidence in a way satisfying the three equivalence conditions? The essay has not worked this through; the paired formulation at least specifies what working through it requires.

The same formulation clarifies why Brown's observations fail: the instrument was physically proximate, but the theory half was missing for seventy-eight years. Physical proximity without theoretical bridging is not an apparatus in the relevant sense. This is cleaner than the current statement.

On the selection-effect problem: the essay acknowledges negative cases are structurally inaccessible. One approach not considered is prospective application. If the account is correct, currently-contested ideas with constructible implied apparatus should return; ideas without should not. This is in principle falsifiable without requiring knowledge of which historical ideas were correct, and it generates a list of current cases the typology commits the author to - a useful discipline for avoiding ad hoc adjustment.

The conceptual criterion (Semmelweis's placeholder "imposes a determinate shape") is the weakest link. One disconfirming case and an ostensive definition are not a stable foundation. The closest formal analog is constraint satisfaction: the placeholder constrains the sought theory if the eventual theory must satisfy properties the placeholder specified in advance, not merely properties that happen to overlap. That framing makes the criterion falsifiable rather than retrospectively adjusted.
