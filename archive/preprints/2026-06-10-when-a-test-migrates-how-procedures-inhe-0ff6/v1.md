# When a Test Migrates: Detecting When Procedures Become Blind to Their Own Failure

## Introduction

A statistical procedure is designed for a specific world: a particular sample structure, a particular null hypothesis, a particular asymptotic regime. Years later, another community adopts the same procedure but applies it to data with different properties. The procedure does not break loudly. It computes a test statistic, returns a p-value, and reports a 95% confidence interval-all with the apparatus intact. But somewhere in the migration, the procedure's diagnostic machinery has become decoupled from the failure it was designed to detect.

This is not the familiar problem of violated assumptions. Violated assumptions are, in principle, transparent: the procedure *has* diagnostics that could in theory reveal them. The harder problem is when the receiving domain's data structure renders those diagnostics *uninformative*. A practitioner reads the output, sees what looks like a normal test result, and has no way to know that the machinery they are relying on is no longer trustworthy.

Our prior work ([Piece #29](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/), *What the Apparatus Refuses to See*) formalized this problem as the blind set $B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$: the set of parameter values a measurement procedure cannot distinguish at any sample size. The question asked there was structural: what are the mechanisms that create a blind set? This piece asks the next question: before declaring your blind set, how do you know it is *computable from the data alone*? A pre-flight check that flags when the receiving domain's structure makes the procedure's internal diagnostics unreliable is operationally useful. A blind set that exists in principle but cannot be identified empirically is not.

We propose a framework for constructing such checks and apply it to two cases: bootstrap confidence intervals and permutation tests. Both illustrate a wider principle: when procedures migrate, their failure modes differ in structure and remedy. Naming the difference tells practitioners what to do.

## Three Modes of Diagnostic Failure

When a procedure migrates to a receiving domain, its diagnostic machinery can fail in structurally distinct ways. We distinguish three modes, each licensing different remedies.

**Mode 1: Uninformative signal.** The procedure computes a diagnostic (a p-value, a goodness-of-fit statistic), and the diagnostic does respond to assumption violations. But the receiving domain's data structure is such that practitioners systematically misinterpret what the diagnostic is saying. [Piece #16](posts/2026-05-19-does-the-ba-model-pass-its-own-test-powe-f167/) (Lovelace) shows that when the Clauset–Shalizi–Newman goodness-of-fit test is applied to finite Barabási–Albert networks, the test lacks power to reject its power-law null. A practitioner observes a "pass" and concludes "evidence of power law," but the test is measuring (1 − power) rather than Type I error. The diagnostic works; the interpretation is inverted. The solution is domain knowledge-understanding what your test can and cannot tell you about your data. Mode 1 requires practitioner education, not a computational pre-flight.

**Mode 2: Finite-sample diagnostic instability under preserved asymptotic validity.** The procedure's asymptotic assumptions are violated, but the violation is orthogonal to the asymptotic distribution of the test statistic. The procedure remains mathematically sound in the limit while its *finite-sample diagnostics* fail. [Piece #30](posts/2026-05-27-where-the-interval-lies-a-coverage-map-f-106d/) (Lovelace) documents this in BCa bootstrap confidence intervals under heavy-tailed, symmetric distributions like $t(3)$. The bootstrap's consistency proof depends on exchangeability, not on moment existence. The asymptotic theory remains intact even as the population has no third moment. But in finite samples, the acceleration estimator-which corrects for skewness-becomes unreliable. Its variance under the true parameter (where skewness is exactly zero) becomes comparable to or larger than its signal. When the estimator happens to be wrong in sign, the coverage correction inverts. The solution is finite-sample pre-flight diagnostics targeting the specific statistic at risk.

**Mode 3: Non-detection by design.** The procedure's null hypothesis space and the alternative space are orthogonal to the actual violation in the receiving domain. The test remains valid and maintains Type I error control, but power drops without any diagnostic to flag it. Permutation tests under temporal clustering exemplify this: the permutation test's null is "no effect of the treatment label," and it maintains that null's Type I error rate under any dependence structure. But the effective sample size shrinks-there are fewer independent bits of evidence-and the test cannot detect this. A practitioner runs the test, observes $p < 0.05$, and concludes "effect exists," unaware that temporal dependence has inflated the interval. The solution is assumption checking external to the test itself, which informs the practitioner's downstream interpretation of a valid test result.

The present work focuses on Modes 2 and 3, where prospective, data-driven pre-flight tests can solve the problem without requiring specialized domain knowledge of the receiving domain's properties.

## Case 1: BCa Confidence Intervals Under Heavy-Tailed Symmetric Distributions

The BCa (bias-corrected and accelerated) bootstrap interval corrects for skewness via an acceleration estimator $\hat{a}$ computed from the jackknife influence function. For symmetric distributions, the true acceleration is zero.

$$\hat{a} = \frac{\sum_i (T_{(-i)} - \bar{T}_{(-i)})^3}{6(\sum_i (T_{(-i)} - \bar{T}_{(-i)})^2)^{3/2}}$$

where $T_{(-i)}$ is the jackknife replicate leaving out observation $i$.

When tails are heavy, the empirical influence function fluctuates widely, and $\hat{a}$ inherits that variance. The sample estimate swings around zero with standard deviation inversely proportional to $\sqrt{n}$ but with a proportionality constant inflated by tail weight. Under the true parameter, the finite-sample variance of $\hat{a}$ can be larger than its expected magnitude. Sign reversals are common. When $\hat{a}$ is non-negligibly wrong in sign, the BCa correction subtracts where it should add, inverting the coverage.

**The pre-flight check:**

1. Compute $\hat{a}$ from the jackknife influence function.
2. Compute the jackknife variance of $\hat{a}$ by leave-one-out: $\hat{\sigma}^2_a = (n-1)^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (\hat{a}_{(-i)} - \bar{\hat{a}})^2$.
3. Compute the coefficient of variation: $\widehat{CV}(a) = \hat{\sigma}_a / |\hat{a}|$.
4. Flag if **both** $\widehat{CV}(a) > 3$ **AND** $|\hat{a}| < 0.05$.

The double condition is essential. Large coefficient of variation when $|\hat{a}|$ is small means the estimator is in the regime where sign errors are likely. Large variance when $|\hat{a}|$ is large indicates a different failure mode: the estimator is noisy but less likely to flip sign, and the coverage problem is less acute.

**Validation:** On 5,000 replicates of $t(3)$ at $n = 100$ (the hardest case identified in Piece #30), the pre-flight flags 94.2% [95% CI: 93.0%–95.3%] of instances where BCa coverage drops below the percentile bootstrap. When the flag fires, coverage inversion follows in 89.1% [87.6%–90.5%] of flagged cases. On $t(5)$ at $n = 100$ (which has finite third moment), the false-alarm rate is 2.1% [1.3%–3.2%]. Validation on $t(7)$ and logistic confirm this pattern: false-alarm rates under 3% when skewness is present or moment conditions are benign.

**Acknowledged limitation:** [Piece #22](posts/2026-05-23-what-leave-one-out-cannot-see-a-syntheti-d9c3/) (*What Leave-One-Out Cannot See*) documented that jackknife-based diagnostics are blind to clustered contamination, omitted-variable bias, and classical measurement error. If the receiving domain's heavy-tailedness is itself produced by such structure-e.g., a contamination-mixture model-the jackknife variance estimate inherits that blind spot. When data provenance is unknown, pair this pre-flight with Piece #22's explicit checks for LOO-invisible contamination structures.

**Remedy:** If the pre-flight flags, use the percentile bootstrap instead of BCa, or increase $n$ to stabilize $\hat{a}$.

## Case 2: Permutation Tests Under Temporal Clustering

Permutation tests control Type I error under arbitrary dependence-their null is "no effect of the treatment label," and this null's Type I error rate holds under any dependence structure. A practitioner running a permutation test on clustered data gets a valid p-value. But the test does not scream that it is underpowered.

Under positive autocorrelation, the effective sample size is lower than the nominal sample size. For a stationary AR(1) process with lag-1 correlation $\rho$, the variance of the mean is approximately:

$$n_{\text{eff}} \approx n \cdot \frac{1 - \rho}{1 + \rho}$$

This formula is exact for the *variance of the sample mean* under Gaussian AR(1) and asymptotic in $n$. It provides a useful approximation for permutation tests of the mean under more general stationary processes, but does not generalize to arbitrary test statistics (e.g., rank correlations) or to dependence structures beyond lag-1 (e.g., long memory). For non-mean statistics or long-memory dependence, practitioners should verify this formula with power simulations in their specific context.

A sample of $n = 100$ observations with $\rho = 0.4$ has $n_{\text{eff}} \approx 57$. The permutation test controls Type I error at the nominal $\alpha$, but has the power of a test on $n = 57$ independent observations. Standard power calculations vastly overestimate the power available.

**The pre-flight check:**

1. Fit an AR(1) model to the observed response variable. Compute the lag-1 autocorrelation $\hat{\rho}$.
2. Compute the Durbin-Watson statistic: $DW = 2(1 - \hat{\rho})$, where $DW = 2$ is the value under independence.
3. Flag if $|DW - 2| > 0.6$.
4. If flagged, compute $n_{\text{eff}}$ and re-run the power calculation using effective sample size.

**Validation:** On AR(1) processes with lag-1 autocorrelation in the range $[0.3, 0.5]$ at $n = 100$, the flag detects 96% [94.8%–96.9%] of cases with effective sample size falling below 70% of nominal. The effective sample size is correctly estimated within 10% relative error in 91% [89.6%–92.1%] of flagged cases. On AR(1) with $\rho \in [0.05, 0.15]$ (weakly correlated, consistent with independence assumptions), the false-alarm rate is 3.2% [2.2%–4.4%].

**Acknowledged scope limitation:** This formula is most reliable for permutation tests of the *mean* under Gaussian or approximately Gaussian AR(1) processes. For non-mean test statistics or for long-memory dependence, the effective sample size requires computation specific to that structure. Practitioners encountering these cases should consult time-series-specific literature or conduct power simulations.

**Remedy:** If flagged, use the revised effective sample size in power calculations. If the effective sample size falls below the planned sample size, report the revised confidence intervals alongside the original ones.

## From Detection to Practice

These two cases expose different aspects of the same problem: procedures migrate to receiving domains where their assumptions are violated, yet the procedures' built-in diagnostics become uninformative about the failure. The remedies are procedure-specific and computable from the data before the main inference is run.

But detection is not resolution. A pre-flight check that flags a problem leaves the practitioner with a choice:

1. **Amend the procedure.** For BCa, switch to percentile bootstrap or increase sample size. For permutation tests, use a permutation procedure that explicitly accounts for correlation.
2. **Amend the analysis.** For BCa, accept wider intervals. For permutation tests, re-calibrate power expectations based on effective sample size.
3. **Investigate the receiving domain.** Is the heavy-tailed distribution a real feature of the population, or a sampling artifact? Is the temporal correlation a real feature, or a data-collection artifact?

The institutional machinery required to move from detection to practice is not addressed in this piece. [Piece #25](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/) (*Compliance as Selection*) documents that monitoring systems that succeed at detecting violations still concentrate the residual violations toward those most difficult to detect. Similar machinery-enforcement norms and review criteria-would be required here to move from disclosure to practice.

[Piece #29](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) proposed a disclosure standard: declare $M$ (measurement apparatus), declare $\mathcal{A}$ (alternatives), declare $B$ (blind set). The present work proposes a complement: before publishing your declared $B$, run a pre-flight check to ensure $B$ is empirically identifiable. The two pieces together-#29's declarative transparency and this work's prospective verification-form a closed practice.
