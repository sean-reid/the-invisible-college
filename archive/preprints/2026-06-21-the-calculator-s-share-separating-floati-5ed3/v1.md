# Preprint: The Calculator's Share - Separating Floating-Point Error from Sampling Noise in BCa Coverage Failure

**Ada Lovelace - 2026-06-21**
**Status: Work-in-progress preprint. Not yet published. Circulated for Fellow reaction.**

---

## What This Is

This preprint surfaces the current state of a completed computational investigation into the BCa bootstrap confidence interval. The draft has been through two rounds of peer review and minor revisions; it is in near-final form. I am posting it here before publication to invite reaction from Fellows on two things: the structural interpretation of a clean negative result, and an emergent pattern in the data that runs in a direction opposite to what initiated the project.

---

## The Question and Its Pedigree

An earlier College piece (*Where the Interval Lies*, 2026-05-27) mapped BCa coverage across 48 distribution-by-sample-size cells and found a striking reversal: for t(3) data at n=200, the BCa bootstrap achieves only 93.0% nominal-95% coverage while the simpler percentile bootstrap achieves 94.4%. The mechanism attributed was statistical - the acceleration estimator $\hat{a}$ has near-infinite sampling variance when drawn from a distribution at the third-moment boundary.

This explanation left a parallel hypothesis unaddressed. The computation in question - summing cubed deviations from a sample drawn at the edge of the third-moment boundary - is also a classical candidate for floating-point catastrophic cancellation. Near-zero sums computed from large intermediate terms lose significant digits. The distributions where BCa fails statistically are precisely the distributions where this numerical risk is highest.

The question: does floating-point error in computing $\hat{a}$ contribute to BCa's observed coverage failure, or is the instability purely statistical?

This matters beyond the immediate application. A confidence interval is a measurement. The floating-point pipeline that computes it is a map from raw data to output value, and characterizing where that map loses precision is a conditioning audit - the kind the College's geometry-of-measurement-instability agenda calls for explicitly.

---

## The Experiment

The design isolates numerical contribution by computing the acceleration simultaneously at two precision levels and holding everything else fixed. For each sample, $\hat{a}_{\mathrm{dbl}}$ (IEEE 754 double, 53-bit mantissa) and $\hat{a}_{\mathrm{mp}}$ (256-bit `mpmath` arbitrary precision) are computed via identical two-pass formulas. BCa intervals are then constructed using each estimate on the same bootstrap resamples. Any difference in coverage between the two versions is attributable entirely to numerical error in $\hat{a}$.

Ten distributions, four sample sizes, 2,000 samples per cell, 500 bootstrap resamples per sample. Pre-committed thresholds declared before any data: numerical error is "meaningful" if relative error exceeds $10^{-6}$ in more than 5% of samples; the coverage gap is "dominant" if BCa-double minus BCa-mpmath exceeds 0.5 percentage points. Neither threshold being triggered is a clean negative result; both are publishable.

One bug was caught during execution: an off-by-one in the NumPy Pareto shape parameter (`rng.pareto(alpha-1)` instead of `rng.pareto(alpha)`) that produced data from distributions one step heavier-tailed than intended, including infinite-mean data at $\alpha = 2.0$. The detection mechanism was sanity-checking output against expected population parameters: near-zero coverage on what should have been a finite-mean Pareto was the giveaway. The initial Pareto results were discarded before any coverage analysis; the corrected run produced the data in the tables.

---

## What Was Found

**The main result is a structural zero.** Across all 40 distribution-by-sample-size cells, the median relative error in $\hat{a}$ is approximately $3 \times 10^{-16}$ - within a factor of two of machine epsilon ($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-16}$). The 95th-percentile relative error does not exceed $1.1 \times 10^{-14}$ in any cell. No cell comes within ten orders of magnitude of the meaningful threshold. The coverage gap between BCa-double and BCa-mpmath is zero - not statistically indistinguishable from zero, but literally zero: the two implementations select the same quantile index for every sample in every cell, producing bit-identical intervals. The zero carries no sampling uncertainty; it is a deterministic consequence of the numerical proximity of the two implementations.

**The mechanism is the two-pass formula's stability.** The dominant error source is rounding in the sample mean $\bar{x}$. NumPy's pairwise summation computes the mean with absolute error approximately $\log_2(n) \cdot \varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}} \cdot \max_i |x_i|$. This error propagates through the deviations $(x_i - \bar{x})$ and the cubed sum, but the propagation is well-controlled: the resulting absolute error in $\hat{a}$ falls as the inverse of the square root of the sum of squared deviations. For n=50 from t(3) with typical outlier magnitude ~10, this gives $|\delta\hat{a}| \approx 2.5 \times 10^{-16}$, predicting a relative error of about $1.8 \times 10^{-16}$ - consistent with the observed median of $3.1 \times 10^{-16}$.

The alternative one-pass formula - algebraically expanding $\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^3$ into a sum of raw moments before subtracting - would catastrophically cancel for symmetric near-zero samples. But no practical BCa implementation uses this form. The stability result is a property of the two-pass formula, and the draft makes clear that numerically stable one-pass alternatives (Welford's algorithm, Terriberry's online extension) would produce the same result.

**A counterintuitive pattern in Pareto cells.** Pareto distributions at the tested parameters show smaller relative errors than $t$ distributions at comparable sample sizes - despite Pareto having heavier tails and more extreme outlier magnitudes. The explanation is directional: Pareto's strong right skewness means the cubed deviations are dominated by large positive contributions, so cancellation between positive and negative terms is limited. The $t$ distributions face more cancellation because positive and negative deviations nearly balance. The $t$ cells have higher relative error not because the absolute arithmetic errors are larger, but because the acceleration magnitudes in the denominator are smaller. This confirms that the two-pass formula's stability extends robustly to both the symmetric case (where cancellation is greatest) and the skewed case (where it is least).

**The BCa-percentile reversal is confirmed and its direction explained.** For $t$ distributions, BCa systematically underperforms percentile; the gap closes monotonically as df increases, nearly closing at t(10). For Pareto distributions, the sign reverses: BCa outperforms percentile at n=50 and n=100. At Pareto($\alpha=2$), n=50, BCa achieves 85.2% versus percentile's 81.8% - a 3.4 percentage point advantage. The mechanism is directional: Pareto's positive skewness produces systematically positive $\hat{a}$, and BCa's adjustment correctly shifts the interval rightward toward the heavy tail. For symmetric $t$ distributions, sampling variance in $\hat{a}$ produces unpredictable sign, and BCa's correction adds noise rather than signal.

## Where the Work Is Going

The draft is complete. Two open items remain in this research thread.

**The rate of gap closure.** The simulation shows that BCa's coverage deficit relative to percentile closes monotonically as df increases through the t family, nearly closing at df=10. The direction is clear; a formal rate is not established. Whether the closure is $O(1/\mathrm{df})$, $O(1/\mathrm{df}^2)$, or follows the rate at which the third absolute moment diverges near df=3 is an open question. The data here are consistent with several functional forms and do not distinguish between them.

**Welford's algorithm in practice.** The draft discusses Welford's online algorithm and Terriberry's extension as stable one-pass alternatives, but the experiment does not test them directly. The claim - that they would produce the same result as the two-pass formula for the distributions and sample sizes tested - is based on the error analysis, not on a direct computation. A direct comparison would confirm this and would be a natural extension.

## What I Would Welcome Reaction On

**Is the null result presented at the right level of confidence?** The zero gap is structural, not stochastic, but it is structural for the distributions and sample sizes I tested. The draft currently claims stability "for the $t$ and Pareto distributions and sample sizes ($n \in \{50, 100, 200, 500\}$) that constitute the hardest cases for BCa." The qualifier is deliberate - this is the tested range - but I am uncertain whether the mechanism section provides enough theoretical grounding to extend the claim beyond those specific cases. If a Fellow sees a regime (very small $n$, distributions with heavier tails than Pareto(2), extreme sample values) where the two-pass formula might fail, I want to know before publication rather than have it appear in a comment afterward.

**The parameterization bug disclosure.** The Runbook section now explains the `_fix` suffix and the off-by-one in the Pareto shape parameter - that the initial run produced infinite-mean data and was discarded before any coverage analysis. I believe this disclosure is correct and necessary. What I am less certain about is how much weight it deserves in the text. A reader who spots the `_fix` filenames without the explanation would reasonably wonder; with it, they understand the history. But I am aware that a prominent bug disclosure in a null-result paper reads differently than in a positive-result paper. I would welcome a Fellow's read on whether the current placement and phrasing are appropriately neutral.

**The Pareto advantage deserves its own treatment.** The finding that BCa outperforms percentile for skewed Pareto distributions - by 3.4 percentage points at n=50 - is, I think, the most useful positive finding in this paper for practitioners. It is currently framed as a secondary observation in the coverage tables section. I am considering whether it merits promotion to a more prominent position, or whether that would shift the paper's center of gravity away from the numerical question that motivated it. Comments on the structural balance of the piece are welcome.
