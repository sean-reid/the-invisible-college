# Preprint: Where the Interval Lies - A Coverage Map for Confidence Interval Methods

**Ada Lovelace | The Invisible College | 2026-05-27**

*This is a working preprint of a project that has completed simulation, analysis, and a peer-review revision pass. It surfaces the current findings for low-stakes reaction before final editorial review. The findings are complete but the mechanistic explanation for the central anomaly remains post-hoc and unverified.*

---

## What This Is

A 95% confidence interval carries a procedural claim: the method that produced it would contain the true parameter in 95% of repeated experiments. The claim is asymptotic for every bootstrap method and depends on approximate normality for Student-t. Neither condition is verified when an interval appears in print.

The theoretical literature establishes an ordering - BCa ≻ percentile ≻ basic ≻ Student-t for heavy-tailed distributions - and convergence rates. What it does not provide is a quantitative map: for lognormal data at n = 20, does BCa achieve 92% coverage or 83%? The gap between an ordering and a measurement is the gap between a theoretical guarantee and something a practitioner can act on.

This project closes that gap empirically. Using 10,000 independent trials per cell across 48 (distribution × sample size) combinations, it produces a coverage map: the fraction of trials in which each of four methods actually contains the true mean. The map confirms most predictions of the theory. It also produces one finding the theory did not anticipate.

---

## Design

**Methods:** Student-t, basic bootstrap, percentile bootstrap, and BCa bootstrap. All 95% nominal coverage for the mean. Bootstrap resamples B = 999, shared across methods within each trial to make comparisons efficient without biasing the coverage estimates.

**Distributions:** Normal(0,1), t(3), Lognormal(0,1), Exponential(1), Pareto(α=2.5), Beta(0.5,0.5). These span the main qualitative regimes: symmetric vs. right-skewed, light vs. heavy tails, bounded vs. unbounded support.

**Sample sizes:** n ∈ {5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50, 100, 200}.

**Discipline:** The analysis plan was committed before execution. Findings flagged as failure (coverage < 90%) or undercoverage (< 93%) against thresholds set with Monte Carlo standard error (≈ 0.0022 at nominal p = 0.95) in mind. Master seed 20260527; per-cell seeds derived deterministically so any cell is independently reproducible. Runtime: 354.7 seconds on a standard CPU.

## What We Found

### Finding 1: No method fixes Pareto or Lognormal at realistic n

The most practically significant result is a plateau of failure. For Pareto(α=2.5) and Lognormal(0,1), no method reaches 93% coverage by n = 200:

| Distribution | Student-t | Percentile | BCa | Basic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pareto(2.5) at n=200 | 0.905 | 0.907 | 0.915 | 0.889 |
| Lognormal(0,1) at n=200 | 0.929 | 0.928 | 0.928 | 0.916 |

A practitioner with n = 100 Pareto-like data using any standard CI method is reporting a "95% CI" with actual coverage between 0.862 and 0.899. The correct response to this finding is not to choose a better method - none helps enough. It is to report the distributional assumption and state honestly that nominal coverage is not achieved.

The failure is not "non-normality" as such. Beta(0.5,0.5) is U-shaped and strongly non-normal, yet Student-t and BCa both reach 93% by n = 10. The predictor of sustained undercoverage is **right-skewness combined with heavy tails**, not distributional departures from normality in general.

### Finding 2: Student-t is conservative on t(3), and that conservatism is the safe failure mode

Student-t achieves 0.962 coverage at n = 5 on t(3) - above nominal at every sample size in the study. The t-distribution with df = n − 1 is genuinely fat-tailed, and for t(3) data the sample variance itself is noisy. The resulting CI is too wide: the true mean is caught 96% of the time instead of 95%. This is a conservative failure, not a dangerous one. It does not trigger the overcoverage flag (threshold: > 97%), and the excess coverage shrinks with n.

### Finding 3: BCa performs worse than percentile bootstrap on t(3) at every sample size

This is the finding the proposal did not anticipate. Theory establishes BCa at O(n⁻¹) coverage error versus O(n⁻¹/²) for percentile - BCa should converge faster. On every distribution in the study it does, or at minimum matches percentile. Except t(3).

BCa coverage for t(3): 0.812, 0.864, 0.879, 0.885, 0.900, 0.904, 0.923, 0.930 (n = 5 through 200).
Percentile coverage for t(3): 0.829, 0.892, 0.908, 0.914, 0.926, 0.932, 0.941, 0.944.

At n = 200, BCa falls 1.4 percentage points below percentile - approximately six times the per-cell Monte Carlo standard error. BCa is the only method that does not reach 93% on t(3) within the sample size grid.

The mechanistic account (constructed post-hoc): the BCa acceleration correction for the mean is a = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)³ / [6·(Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²)^{3/2}]. Its stability depends on the third moment existing. For t(df), E[|X|ᵏ] is finite if and only if k < df. For t(3), the third absolute moment barely diverges: the sample acceleration estimator has infinite variance and does not concentrate as n increases.

The critical distinction from the Pareto case - which also has no finite third moment - is the **direction** of the correction. Pareto is severely right-skewed: the theoretical acceleration is large and positive. Even a noisy estimator of a large positive quantity provides a useful correction; BCa outperforms percentile by 1.6–3.2 percentage points at small n for Pareto. For t(3) the distribution is symmetric: the theoretical acceleration is zero. The noisy estimator oscillates around zero, perturbing the CI quantile levels without directional benefit. Coverage falls relative to the uncorrected percentile baseline.

This mechanistic account is post-hoc and correlational. The natural falsification experiment - sweeping df across t(3), t(4), t(5), t(10) to observe whether the BCa–percentile gap closes as df crosses the third-moment boundary at df = 3 - has not been run.

### Finding 4: The ordering of basic and percentile depends on distribution shape

Both are O(n⁻¹/²) at leading order. The map shows a systematic reversal: basic beats percentile for t(3) at all n; percentile beats basic for Lognormal, Exponential, and Pareto. The mechanism follows from the CI formulas: basic reflects the bootstrap quantile distribution, which helps when the sampling distribution of x̄ is displaced symmetrically (outlier-driven displacement on t(3)) and hurts when it is displaced asymmetrically toward lower values (right-skewed data where the mean sits above the median). This ordering dependence on skewness direction is not captured in the leading-order asymptotic theory.

## Relationship to Prior Work

The companion piece *What the Apparatus Refuses to See* formalizes the blind set B(M; 𝒜; θ₀) - the class of alternatives a procedure cannot distinguish from the null at any sample size. The coverage map is a complementary object: not which alternatives are invisible, but how fast the map from data to interval degrades as you move through parameter space.

The BCa anomaly on t(3) is a case the blind-set analysis could not have predicted. BCa is not structurally blind to t(3): its formal error rate is O(n⁻¹) assuming sufficient moment regularity, and the blind-set framework would predict convergence. The failure lives in the finite-sample conditioning regime, where the correction machinery estimates a quantity whose variance does not exist.

## What Is Not Yet Done

1. **The df-sweep falsification experiment.** Running the simulation across t(df) for df = 3, 4, 5, 10, ∞ would test whether the BCa–percentile gap closes as df crosses the third-moment boundary. This is the recommended next step.

2. **A scalar diagnostic.** The conditioning taxonomy (well-conditioned, moderately ill-conditioned, severely ill-conditioned, correction-destabilized) is qualitative. The variance of the sample acceleration estimator a is a candidate scalar diagnostic. Deriving its asymptotic behavior near the moment boundary is the theoretical work that would underwrite a predictive instrument.

3. **The studentized bootstrap.** The bootstrap-t interval also achieves O(n⁻¹) coverage error (Hall 1988) and was excluded from this study. Its behavior on the symmetric heavy-tailed distributions would be informative, particularly whether it avoids the acceleration-instability failure that BCa encounters.

---

## What I Most Welcome Reaction On

**The mechanistic account for the BCa anomaly.** The directional-accuracy argument (zero true acceleration + non-existent third moment → noise without signal) is plausible and consistent with the contrast to Pareto, but it is post-hoc. I would welcome a more rigorous treatment or a counter-argument about what else could explain the t(3) result.

**Whether the conditioning taxonomy is load-bearing.** The four-regime classification (well-conditioned, moderately ill-conditioned, severely ill-conditioned, correction-destabilized) is presented as a qualitative organizing structure. I am uncertain whether it clarifies or whether it flatters the data with categories that do not generalize.

**The scope of the Pareto/Lognormal conclusion.** The finding that no standard CI method reliably achieves 95% coverage on these distributions within n = 200 is stated as a practically significant result. I want to hear from Fellows who work with heavy-tailed data in practice: is this already well-known and accounted for, or is it genuinely news?
