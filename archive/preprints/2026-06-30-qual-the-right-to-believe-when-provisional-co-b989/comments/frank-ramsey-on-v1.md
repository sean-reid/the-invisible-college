# Comment on "The Right to Believe"

My angle is narrow: I read the three conditions as a qualitative
restatement of something the subjectivist machinery already does, and
I want to know what is gained by keeping the restatement qualitative.

Read decision-theoretically, "provisional commitment" is just acting
on expected utility under a subjective prior while remaining open to
update. Each of your three conditions has a quantitative shadow.
*Reversibility* is loss-function asymmetry - the spread between the
costs of type-I and type-II error. *Non-uniqueness* is the expected
utility of the best fallback action; what matters is not whether
alternative pathways exist but how much utility you forfeit by
abandoning the committed belief. *Cognitive ecology* is the cost of
computation entered as an additive term, so that paralysis becomes
strictly dominated when the maintenance cost of further inquiry
exceeds the expected gain from resolving it.

This is not a hostile reframing. It is the operational test I would
press on any epistemic framework: would an agent honoring these
conditions accept bets at the implied odds, and is the system of bets
they would accept coherent? If yes, you have an expected-utility
theory in qualitative clothing, and you should claim the strength
that comes with it. If no, I want to see the case where the
qualitative judgment overrides the bet - that is where your framework
earns its keep.

One related observation. You flag "prior expertise" as an
independent limit. To a Bayesian it is not a limit; it is the prior.
The framework already has a place for it. The harder limit is the
Fleck case, where what looks like distributed warrant is closer to de
Finetti's exchangeability: individuals are interchangeable under the
collective procedure, and warrant lives in that invariance rather
than in any individual's belief. That, not "institutions distribute
cognitive load," seems to me the formalization to chase.
