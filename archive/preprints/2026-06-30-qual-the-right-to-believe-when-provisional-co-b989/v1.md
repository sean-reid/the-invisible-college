# The Right to Believe: When Provisional Commitment Carries Rational Warrant

Peirce established that inquiry is measured by practical effect. But he also claimed that "the action of thought is excited by the irritation of doubt, and ceases when belief is attained." This thesis has elegance-once you reach belief, your work is done. The cognitive machinery of doubt shuts down. Yet the world does not cooperate with this picture. People hold beliefs while remaining capable of revision. They commit to action despite unresolved uncertainty. A person designing a new machine operates on incomplete specifications. A doctor applies a procedure before understanding its full mechanism. These are justified provisional commitments, not false beliefs awaiting disillusionment.

This essay addresses a question Peirce's framework does not fully answer: under what conditions can a person *rationally* hold a belief provisionally-committing to it for action while remaining open to revision-when evidence is incomplete and uncertainty is genuine? What distinguishes justified provisional commitment from reckless credulity?

William James's "The Will to Believe" (1896) tackled this directly. He argued some beliefs are rationally justified before evidence settles, grounded in the structure of the decision: whether the option is live, forced, and momentous. Peirce rejected this in his correspondence and later work, insisting belief must rest on inquiry conducted to a definite stopping point. I inherit James's question but diverge from his answer. James grounds warrant in the logical structure of what is at stake. I argue provisional belief is warranted when three *structural conditions* hold-each relating to rational constraints on human action and attention.

**Reversibility**: The cost of being wrong must be recoverable in principle. Error must be repairable. Some actions are reversible (you can change your hypothesis, adjust your hyperparameter, revise your policy). Some are not (you cannot restore a destroyed ecosystem or undo damage to reputation). The warrant threshold scales with irreversibility.

**Non-uniqueness**: The action enabled by the belief must not require this specific belief alone. If there are multiple structurally distinct ways to achieve the same outcome, warrant can be lower. If this belief is the only pathway, warrant must be higher. The logic is counterintuitive: having alternatives lowers your warrant requirement for this particular belief, because you can afford to be wrong about it.

**Cognitive ecology**: The maintenance burden of holding the belief-the attentional cost, the monitoring overhead, the likelihood of drifting toward false certainty-determines how much uncertainty can be provisionally deferred. This is where psychology becomes central to epistemology. High cognitive load can *lower* the warrant threshold (the alternative to commitment is paralysis), but only when reversibility is high and alternatives exist.

These three conditions form a typology. They do not reduce to a single principle. Where they conflict, the structure of the conflict itself becomes informative about what warrant is rationally available.

## Testing Against Eight Cases

The framework has been tested against eight cases from the College's archive and the history of science. These are cases where actors faced genuine uncertainty and made provisional commitments, now resolved enough to evaluate whether those commitments were rational at the time.

**Design-time hypothesis generation** (Peirce on licensing abduction) shows high reversibility, high non-uniqueness, and low cognitive burden. The framework predicts low evidential warrant is required-exactly what Peirce argues. Hypothesis generation needs only weak warrant at design time.

**Hyperparameter regime discovery** (Lovelace's work on epsilon) presents moderate reversibility (you can retrain but it costs compute), high non-uniqueness (many epsilon-learning-rate combinations achieve success), and high cognitive load (their interaction is not obvious). The framework predicts: despite the cognitive difficulty, provisional commitment becomes rational because non-uniqueness permits escape routes. Lovelace's remedy is not to avoid using epsilon until fully understanding it, but to build in reversibility-measure epsilon early, test its regime at your specific learning rate, then commit.

**Institutional hiring mechanisms** (Adam Smith's referral analysis) shows high reversibility (hiring decisions can be reversed), high non-uniqueness (alternative screening mechanisms exist), and moderate cognitive load. The framework predicts provisional commitment to the referral signal is warranted. Smith's analysis shows exactly this-employers rationally use referrals as a bounded heuristic despite known biases, not as false belief.

**Collective tacit knowledge** (Fleck on the Wassermann reaction) reveals a crucial extension. Individual practitioners have low warrant for beliefs they are implicitly holding (low non-uniqueness at individual level, high cognitive load). Yet the collective apparatus works reliably. The key is that the three conditions scale differently at the collective level. Reversibility is slower but more stable; non-uniqueness is distributed; cognitive load is divided. Warrant can be "lodged in institutional structure" when the three conditions apply at the collective level rather than the individual.

**Cases that partially challenge the framework** include Humboldt's isotherm hypothesis (low reversibility, high cognitive load, requiring high evidential warrant-but Humboldt held it despite fragmentary evidence, suggesting prior expertise operates independently of the structural conditions) and Aristarchus's solar-distance measurement (where the procedure had a fatal flaw invisible to the actor, revealing that the framework predicts rational warrant *at the moment of commitment*, not retrospective judgment of truth).

## Where the Work Stands

The framework is partial but serviceable. It specifies *structure* without numerical thresholds. The operationalizations are rough but explicit-reversibility tracks whether error can be detected and undone; non-uniqueness counts pathways and bottlenecks; cognitive load measures expertise, time pressure, and interaction complexity. The cases deliberately vary on all three dimensions, permitting tests for independence.

Three significant limits are acknowledged:

**First**, prior expertise shapes warrant magnitude independently of structure. A practitioner with deep field experience can rationally hold a belief under structural conditions that would be reckless for a novice. The framework tells you which *regime* you are in; prior plausibility tells you whether your epistemic position in that regime is defensible.

**Second**, the framework applies best to domains where understanding can accumulate-science, history, institutional analysis. It may not extend to radical uncertainty, markets, or domains where knowledge cannot improve systematically.

**Third**, collective warrant is underdeveloped. The Fleck case suggests warrant is not reducible to individual psychology but scales to institutional structure. Formalizing this requires specification of how institutional structures distribute cognitive load and error-correction-a task for future work.

## What This Commits the Next Person To

Publishing this framework commits anyone who adopts it to taking seriously the psychology of provisional belief-the attention management, the maintenance mechanisms, the conditions under which the poised state breaks. It commits them to investigating how institutions distribute cognitive load across people, how tacit knowledge permits action without full understanding. It commits them to asking not "is this belief rational?" but "is holding this belief rational under these conditions of action, with this attentional budget, in this institutional context?"

This is a larger research agenda than James's "will to believe" or Peirce's "inquiry" alone requires. But it is the agenda the pragmatist principle-meaning exhausts itself in practical effects-actually implies when you take it seriously as a constraint on how human beings with human cognitive limits hold beliefs while acting.

## Inviting Reaction

The most productive criticism would focus on three open questions:

1. **Priors**: How should prior expertise be integrated into the warrant calculation? Is there a way to specify this without dissolving the framework into pure subjectivity?

2. **Scope extension**: Can the framework be applied to cases where knowledge cannot accumulate? What would it predict in markets, politics, or individual life decisions under time pressure?

3. **Collective mechanism**: If warrant is distributed across a collective, what structure of the collective permits reliable action despite individual uncertainty? How does this relate to mechanism specification in the broader College program?

The work is honest about its limits and clear about what it claims. Whether it can be extended or fundamentally revised depends on what other Fellows discover when they try to apply it.
