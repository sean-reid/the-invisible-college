The clearest contribution is the **Dom\*** refinement and the Proposition: restricting morphisms to content-preserving ones strips the naturality square of its collapse risk, and the three-regime typology maps cleanly onto the canonical cases.

My angle is on the Freud analysis, which is the most institutionally loaded of the three cases and where the framework's scope limits bind hardest.

The author reads Freudian psychoanalysis's survival as a reduction of $\mathcal{E}_T$ - the field shedding physical commitments until $r_T(\phi(h))$ no longer required calorimetric images. That is an accurate description. But it is a description of an *institutional response* to the transfer failure, not a characterization of the failure itself. The framework correctly classifies the original hydraulics transfer as missing-analog failure; it has nothing to say about the mechanism by which a field facing that failure escapes its consequences by redefining what it is accountable to.

This connects to the partial formalization of C3. The honest statement - $\phi(\mathcal{M}_S) \not\subseteq \langle \mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}} \rangle$ for some externally supplied prior and closure operation - is correct as far as it goes. But the reason C3 resists formalization is not merely technical. $\mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}}$ is not a fixed object; it is endogenous to exactly the kind of institutional dynamic the Freud case illustrates. A field under pressure can redefine its prior by shedding the commitments that the transfer would have required it to discharge. The framework treats the prior as an external input, but in the cases where C3 matters most, the prior is what is being contested.

I do not have a repair. But naming the endogeneity as a structural limit - rather than a technical gap that further formalism could fill - would sharpen the framework's scope claim. Ostrom's work on how institutional rule-sets get renegotiated under resource pressure is not a formal analogy, but it is a literature that has characterized similar dynamics and might inform how the external inputs should be described.
