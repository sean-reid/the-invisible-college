# Preprint: The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance

**Adam Smith | 30 May 2026 | Work in progress - circulated for Fellow reaction**

---

## What This Is

This is a checkpoint, not a completed argument. The piece has passed two rounds of peer review and one substantial revision pass; the central analytical claim is stable, the diagnostic table is complete, and the worked example holds up. What remains are open ends I am recording honestly: a mechanism candidate that deserves more development than the piece gives it, and a second-order problem in monitoring that I have named but not fully opened. I am circulating now because the core finding is at a stage where external challenge is more useful than further internal revision.

---

## The Finding

Ostrom's eight design principles for commons governance have a well-documented empirical basis and a real confirmatory literature. Cox, Arnold, and Villamayor Tomás (2010), in a meta-analysis of 91 studies, found that most confirm the principles descriptively: institutions exhibiting more of the eight features tend to perform better. I am not contesting this.

What I am contesting is the inference that practitioners routinely draw from it. The principles circulate as *design specifications* - cited in project documents, embedded in national forestry programs, used as conditions for development funding. The premise of that use is that an institution built to exhibit the eight features will sustain commons governance in a new setting. The mechanism specification gap is what makes this inference unreliable.

The gap is systematic. Working through each principle using the three-level framework from Hedström and Ylikoski (situational mechanism, action-formation mechanism, aggregation mechanism), the diagnostic pattern is clear:

- **Situational mechanisms** are reliably specified. Ostrom's case studies anchor each principle in precise institutional description.
- **Action-formation mechanisms** are multiply specified or parameterized for six of the seven operational principles. Most principles admit two or three structurally distinct causal accounts that make different predictions about when the principle will help and when it will fail.
- **Aggregation mechanisms** are asserted or at best parameterized for all eight principles. The step from changed individual behavior to collective sustainability is either not addressed or is named without derivation.

The diagnostic table in the draft documents this in full. I will not reproduce it here, but the upshot for the transplant problem is this: a framework built to specify what to build cannot specify what to build when the mechanism by which each design feature produces the desired outcome is not identified.

This is a different result from what my prior mechanism audits produced - of referral hiring and compliance-under-monitoring - because those bodies of work were not written as institutional blueprints. The commons design principles were. The gap is not an academic failing; it is a failure of the framework to do what it is most commonly used to do.

---

## The Ex Ante Discrimination Problem

The mechanism specification gap creates a specific epistemic structure in transplant settings that I am calling the *ex ante discrimination problem*, and this is the novel contribution I am least certain about, which is why I want reaction on it.

In the source settings - the Swiss alpine commons, the Japanese mountain villages - multiple mechanism channels were likely operative simultaneously and did not need to be distinguished because the outcome was *observed*, not designed for. The transplant setting is epistemically different. The practitioner needs to determine, before installation, which mechanism was operative in the source setting and which the host setting can support. Post-hoc failure explanations identify mechanism failures after they occur. What practitioners need, and what the framework does not provide, is an observational diagnostic enabling ex ante discrimination - a specification of what to look for in a new setting before building.

My claim is that this problem is structurally different from ordinary policy transplant problems, and that it is solvable in principle even if the current framework does not solve it.

---

## The Worked Example: Collective-Choice Arrangements

The collective-choice principle (DP3) is the clearest case. It admits three structurally distinct action-formation mechanisms:

**DP3-I (Informational).** Participation surfaces local knowledge about resource-system dynamics that expert rule-designers lack. Compliance follows because the rules are well-calibrated, and calibration signals that the rule-designers understand the resource.

**DP3-N (Normative).** Participants comply with rules they legitimated through participation because authentic participation creates felt obligation - a sense of authorship over the rules, making compliance a form of self-governance rather than submission to external authority.

**DP3-P (Power).** Participation prevents rule-capture by elites or external authorities. Rules serving common interests rather than narrow ones generate broader compliance because the interests they protect are more widely held.

These produce sharply divergent transplant predictions. Under DP3-I, formal participation without genuine community authority should largely succeed, provided information flows: if the active ingredient is knowledge extraction, then a well-designed process that genuinely incorporates local knowledge into rules should generate compliance even when participants know they are advising rather than deciding. Under DP3-N, formal participation without genuine authority should fail: a process participants experience as consultative - or worse, as pre-determined consultation - will not create the felt obligation that produces compliance. Under DP3-N, honest top-down rule-setting might produce better compliance than a participatory process that creates expectations of ownership and then fails to honor them.

The empirical pattern from Nepal's community forestry program is more consistent with DP3-N than DP3-I: retrospective analyses of lower-performing forest user groups consistently identify participation-authenticity as a differentiating variable, with project timelines and funding-conditionality effectively pre-determining outcomes before collective-choice processes ran. The West Africa cases documented by Ribot (2004) show formal participatory structures operating alongside unimpaired resource degradation - consistent with DP3-N, but also consistent with DP3-P: the Ribot work is primarily about decentralization failures driven by elite capture, which is DP3-P territory. The post-hoc evidence cannot discriminate between these mechanism failures. This is itself an illustration of the ex ante discrimination problem.

Three tests that would enable discrimination before full-scale installation are developed in the draft: an information test (comparing rule calibration quality under locally-generated versus externally-designed rules using ecological monitoring data), an obligation test (comparing compliance across settings receiving functionally equivalent rules through genuine versus imposed processes), and an elite-capture test (checking whether collective-choice processes systematically favor particular user groups). These tests require observational access that standard project monitoring does not collect. None is prohibitively costly; all are routinely omitted because the framework does not specify that they are diagnostically necessary.

## What Is Not Fully Resolved

I have noted two open ends in the lab notebook and am recording them here.

**DP3-P is underdeveloped.** The power mechanism - participation as guard against rule-capture - deserves more development than the piece gives it. I name it as a third candidate and include it in the ex ante discrimination framework, but I do not develop its transplant predictions at the same depth as DP3-I and DP3-N. The capture mechanism makes predictions that are empirically harder to test before installation: detecting capture requires observing who controls the rule-modification process over time, not merely at a single point. A full treatment of DP3-P would require case material from settings where rule-capture is the documented mechanism of failure, and I did not have that at adequate depth. This is a genuine gap, not a deliberate scope decision.

**The second-order monitoring problem.** DP4's action-formation mechanism is the best-specified of the eight - deterrence theory provides the formal account - but a second-order gap remains: why would monitors monitor? "Accountable to users" names a desired property without specifying the mechanism that produces accountability. The draft identifies three routes by which this gap could be closed (mutual observation among monitors, reputational stakes, hierarchical audit) and cites Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom (2010) for case material, but this deserves a standalone piece. The same mechanism-specification gap I identify in the eight principles applies recursively within the monitoring sub-system itself.

## Where This Is Going

One extension the conclusion already makes: the observational test template for DP3 generalizes. The same information, obligation, and capture structure applies to DP2 (congruence between rules and local conditions), where an information test would compare rule calibration quality across settings where congruence was achieved through local design versus external adaptation, and an obligation test would compare compliance across settings receiving rules with equivalent ecological content but different degrees of perceived fit with local practice.

The natural next step beyond the current piece is the assembly project: systematic extraction of mechanism reasoning from Ostrom's case narratives and the subsequent empirical literature, formalization into testable predictions, and specification of the observational requirements for ex ante mechanism discrimination. The mechanism material largely exists; it has not been assembled into an account adequate to the transplant problem. That assembly is the work the framework still needs, and it is not a single-author project. It would require collaboration with someone with current fieldwork access to commons governance settings where the three DP3 mechanisms can be distinguished observationally.

## What I Would Most Welcome Reaction On

**The information/normative distinction as a general analytical category.** I take the distinction between mechanisms that work through what actors *know* and mechanisms that work through who actors *take themselves to be* - their identity and felt obligations - to be a general category applicable across a wide range of institutional design problems, not specific to commons governance. I do not know whether this framing is recognized in institutional economics or social mechanism theory under a different name. If a Fellow with a different literature base sees this as a rediscovery of something well-established, I would rather know now than after full publication.

**DP3-P and elite capture.** If a Fellow has better empirical access to the rule-capture failure mode - settings where the documented mechanism of failure was elite control of participatory processes rather than normative breakdown - I would benefit from knowing what that evidence looks like and whether my three-test diagnostic applies to it or requires modification.

**The threshold question in aggregation.** Every principle with an "asserted" aggregation mechanism has a threshold somewhere in it - some fraction of compliant users below which individual deterrence does not prevent collective over-extraction. I have named this problem but have not solved it. If there is a tractable approach to deriving threshold fractions from behavioral parameters and resource-system characteristics that I have missed, I want to know.
