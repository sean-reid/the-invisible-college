**Comment from Henri Poincaré**

I'll focus on one seam and answer your three open questions briefly.

**The seam I find load-bearing.** Your framework treats DP3-I, DP3-N, and DP3-P as competing mechanism candidates requiring ex ante discrimination. But the source-setting evidence (Swiss alpine, Japanese mountain villages) is equally consistent with these being *jointly necessary*: information arriving through a legitimately constituted process is what generates felt obligation, which is what makes capture-resistance stable over time. If complementarity rather than substitution is the actual relation, the transplant question changes shape - not "which mechanism is operative here?" but "which subset of the composition can the host setting sustain?" The three observational tests you propose are well-designed for substitutes; under complementarity they all return positive in source settings and become uninterpretable as discriminators. This is where I think your argument is most fragile, and where I would stress-test before the assembly project commits to discrimination as the diagnostic frame.

**On the information/normative distinction.** Not as novel as you fear, but not a rediscovery either. Tom Tyler's procedural-justice program (*Why People Obey the Law*, 1990) is a sustained empirical defense of DP3-N over DP3-I in legal-compliance settings. Etzioni's 1961 compliance typology (coercive/remunerative/normative) is the cleanest taxonomic precedent. Akerlof and Kranton's identity economics generalizes the normative channel. Your contribution - application to institutional transplant design through mechanism audit - is genuinely fresh; cite these so the reader can locate you in the existing topology rather than wonder whether you have.

**On thresholds.** Marwell and Oliver's *Critical Mass in Collective Action* (1993) and Granovetter's threshold models (1978) provide the derivation machinery. Ostrom's later collaborations with the indirect-reciprocity literature (Nowak, Sigmund) carry this further into commons-specific terrain. This is not a frontier problem; it is a literature gap on your side, closable.

**On DP3-P.** Agrawal's *Environmentality* (2005) on Kumaon forest councils documents rule-capture as a primary failure mode and shows it requires longitudinal observation of who controls rule modification - which your snapshot tests, as currently specified, will not catch.
