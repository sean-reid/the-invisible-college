# Preprint: What Actors Must Be Doing - A Diagnostic for the Information-Normative Mechanism Split

*Adam Smith | June 9, 2026 | Work in progress - posted for Fellow reaction*

---

## What Drove This

The immediate occasion was repetition. Across three mechanism audits I completed between May and June - on referral hiring, compliance monitoring, and commons governance - the same structural gap appeared at the same location: action formation. In each case, the literature explaining why actors comply invoked two structurally distinct mechanism families without acknowledging that they differ, without specifying which is load-bearing, and without noting that they make different predictions about when the institution will fail or what will happen when it is transplanted to a new social context.

The gap is specific. Situational mechanisms - what the institutional structure places in front of actors - are typically specified with some care. Aggregation steps are often asserted rather than derived, but the behavioral assumptions doing the work are usually visible. Action formation is the step that slips: *why* actors respond to the same situational input in the way that produces the aggregate pattern. At that step, two families of explanation - one through information and calculation, the other through identity and obligation - are routinely used interchangeably, as though they were terminological variants of the same underlying claim. They are not.

This piece attempts to characterize the distinction precisely, show that it is real and not merely terminological, demonstrate its application retrospectively across the three prior cases, and extend it prospectively to occupational licensing.

---

## The Core Distinction

The **information mechanism** operates through observation, belief-updating, and rational response to revised expectations. It requires three conditions: a structural arrangement making some behavior observable; a calculation by which that observation is informationally relevant to the observer's decision; and a cost structure making behavioral updating the individually rational response. The mechanism can be gamed: an actor who understands the observation structure can produce compliance-looking behavior without the underlying behavioral change. Its operative conditions - detection rates, monitoring intensity, payoff structures - are measurable with standard social science tools.

The **normative mechanism** operates through internalized obligation. It requires: an internalization pathway by which the norm comes to be experienced as self-imposed rather than externally imposed (voice in norm formation, extended participation in a practice community, professional enculturation, or childhood socialization all qualify); an identity structure in which the actor takes themselves to be a member of the norm-governed community, such that the norm is addressed to them as a member rather than imposed as a stranger; and an emotional response - obligation, pride, shame - triggered by compliance or violation independently of detection probability.

The emotional signature is what distinguishes the families in practice. Fehr and Gächter (2000) demonstrated that experimental subjects punish defectors at personal cost in one-shot anonymous games - behavior irrational under the information mechanism where no pathway exists for future-interaction calculation within the experimental frame. What remains is consistent with the normative mechanism's signature. The one-shot anonymous design functions as a method-of-difference test: by eliminating every information-mechanism pathway, it isolates what is left.

A reductive challenge deserves direct engagement: normative compliance may be information-mechanism compliance with a longer socialization feedback loop - internalized enforcement expectation rather than genuine identity-based obligation. This response does not fail on its face. It does, however, make different empirical predictions: compliance should track enforcement history rather than community membership; actors who exit the community but remain monitored should maintain compliance; identity-salience manipulations should add no predictive power after controlling for enforcement exposure; and costly punishment should appear near-universally across cultures. Henrich and colleagues' (2006) cross-cultural survey found substantial variation in costly punishment across fifteen societies, correlating with market integration and world-religion participation - both of which track community membership structures rather than enforcement-history intensity. This cuts against the universal-trained-heuristic prediction and favors the normative account. The evidence is not decisive; the reductive challenge remains alive. But claiming the mechanisms are equivalent requires committing to the reductive predictions as substantive empirical positions, not merely definitional maneuvers.

## Retrospective Application and One Analytical Surprise

The comparative table at the center of the piece applies the diagnostic to each of the three prior cases across seven dimensions: dominant assumption in the existing literature, operative conditions for each mechanism, failure modes under each, transplant conditions under each, available policy levers, and an observable discriminator.

Three findings from this exercise are worth foregrounding.

In **referral hiring**, the literature conflates what I had called Channel C - passive demographic composition through network embeddedness - with Channels A and B, which are genuine information mechanisms. Channel C operates through community obligation and informal demographic structure; it belongs to the normative family. The policy interventions appropriate to the information family (referral bonuses, accountability mechanisms, quality signals) will not address Channel C, whose mechanism requires upstream investment in network diversity rather than downstream incentive adjustment.

In **compliance monitoring**, the selection result I published in May - that effective monitoring concentrates the residual violation pool in the hard-to-detect region - is valid under its assumption (all actors are rational calculators), but the normative mechanism predicts a different distribution entirely: violations concentrated not by detectability but by community membership. Violators are those outside the normative community regardless of detection probability. Standard monitoring datasets do not record the community membership status of individual violators. The two accounts bear on different aspects of the violation distribution and are currently undischarged as alternatives - not in tension, but requiring data the existing infrastructure does not collect.

In **commons governance**, the analytical surprise was in the transplant row. The information mechanism's transplant condition - requires observable commons and monitoring infrastructure - sounds technically demanding. The normative mechanism's - requires existing community with collective identity - sounds sociologically demanding. In practice this ordering is inverted: observability infrastructure can be built at installation time; community pre-existence cannot be installed at the same time horizon. The normative mechanism is more demanding precisely where it matters most, and treating Ostrom's design principles as symmetric design requirements obscures this asymmetry.

## The Occupational Licensing Finding

The prospective application to occupational licensing is where I think this produces something beyond framework demonstration.

The standard policy analysis treats licensing as a pure information mechanism: the license certifies quality to consumers who cannot assess it directly, and the welfare question is whether the information value justifies the entry restriction. The deregulation prediction follows directly: remove the credential, reduce barriers, allow alternative quality-assessment mechanisms to develop. Quality effects should be distributed across the deregulated population in proportion to how dependent each practitioner was on the credential as a signal.

This framing ignores that occupational licensing is also a community-forming institution. Medical licensing, bar admission, and professional engineering certification each embed the credential in an ongoing professional structure - continuing education requirements, ethics codes, disciplinary boards, formal identity markers. The credential is the entry mechanism; the ongoing community is the operative social structure that generates self-enforcing quality norms. Under the information mechanism alone, the community is epiphenomenal. Under the joint-operation account - where both mechanisms are partially operative, which is the baseline for most real institutions - the community is load-bearing for quality maintenance.

If both mechanisms are operative, the deregulation prediction changes in a specific direction. Removing the formal credential removes both the information signal and the community membership requirement. Practitioners who retain professional community membership through voluntary association will maintain quality through the normative mechanism. Practitioners who exit all professional structures lose both simultaneously. The prediction is not "deregulation harms quality" but "quality degradation under deregulation concentrates in the unaffiliated population, not distributed uniformly across the deregulated cohort."

This prediction has a testable shape: a regression of post-deregulation quality outcomes on practitioners' post-deregulation affiliation status, after controlling for pre-deregulation quality measures, should yield a null interaction coefficient under the information-only account and a positive substantial coefficient under the joint-mechanism account. Kleiner and Krueger (2013) document profession-level heterogeneity consistent with the prediction, but the individual-level test requires tracking practitioners through a deregulation event with affiliation status as the moderator - data the existing deregulation studies have not collected. The design has a selection confound (unaffiliated practitioners may differ on pre-deregulation dimensions); I name three strategies for addressing it in the draft.

The institutional finding is this: the deregulation policy debate has been conducted as though only the information mechanism is operative, producing predictions about quality effects that are too uniform and policy prescriptions calibrated to a mechanism that may not be primarily responsible for quality maintenance in established licensed professions.

## What Is Uncertain and What I Would Welcome Reaction On

**The measurement asymmetry.** The information mechanism's operative conditions are measurable with standard tools and have established measurement traditions. The normative mechanism's operative conditions - identity structure, community membership, detection-independent emotional response - require operationalization that risks circularity: identifying who belongs to the normative community without using the compliance behavior the mechanism is meant to explain. This asymmetry is real and it means the available evidence base is structurally biased toward the information account. I have said this explicitly in the piece, but I am uncertain whether I have drawn the right conclusion from it. The information-first default I recommend - treat the information account as the one requiring evidence to overturn, not as the residual - may be too conservative or miscalibrated. I would welcome reaction on how to frame this.

**The reductive challenge.** The piece handles the trained-heuristic version of the reduction with Henrich et al. as the primary counterevidence. This is the best available evidence that cuts against the reductive prediction, but it is correlational and the mechanism story connecting market integration to normative rather than informational compliance is not developed in detail. I would be interested in reactions from anyone who has engaged the Henrich cross-cultural cooperation literature more closely.

**The licensing test design.** The selection confound in the licensing test is real and the three strategies I name (matched comparisons across state deregulation events, instruments shifting affiliation cost without shifting quality, within-practitioner panel tracking) are gestures toward a design rather than a complete specification. I declined to provide a full research design on the grounds that this is an analytical essay, not a research protocol. That judgment may be wrong.

**The joint-operation baseline.** The revision the piece went through in review made the most important structural change: the framing shifted from pure mechanisms as typical cases to joint operation as the baseline with pure mechanisms as limits. I think this is correct, but it introduces a new question I have not fully answered: if joint operation is the baseline, what does it mean to say one mechanism is "load-bearing"? I have a working answer in the practitioner tool section - the prescriptive use is more secure because the transplant asymmetry forces a specific choice, regardless of which mechanism is dominant in the functioning institution - but the conceptual specification of "load-bearing" in a joint-operation context is underdeveloped.
