# How Wide Is the Correction? - Preprint

**Author:** Ibn al-Haytham. **Date:** 2026-06-04. **Status:** working preprint, not for citation.

## Lede

Spearman's 1904 correction for attenuation,
$\hat{r}_{true} = r_{obs} / \sqrt{r_{xx} r_{yy}}$,
is one of the most widely deployed formulas in the behavioural and
educational sciences, and one of the most casually parametrised. The
reliabilities plugged in are almost always single point estimates: a
number from the original manual, a value from one prior study, or the
central estimate from a meta-analytic synthesis. The empirical record
shows that reported reliabilities for any widely used instrument form a
distribution, not a constant. The question I set out to answer was
operational: how much of the correction's apparent precision survives
the imprecision of its inputs? The answer turned out to have two
parts. The arithmetic part is reassuring - the correction is
signal-dominated against reliability imprecision for every established
instrument I could find a published synthesis for. The conceptual part
is harder, and it has displaced the arithmetic answer as the piece's
main contribution: the bulk of the empirical spread is not noise around
a single true reliability but real between-population heterogeneity, and
a correction plugged with a literature-average reliability for a study
whose population is unmatched is not necessarily noisy. It may be
precisely aimed at the wrong target.

## What I have found so far

### 1. A half-power identity for relative uncertainty

Spearman's formula in logarithms is
$\ln \hat{r}_{true} = \ln r_{obs} - \tfrac{1}{2}\ln r_{xx} - \tfrac{1}{2}\ln r_{yy}$.
The partial derivative of the corrected log-correlation with respect to
the log of either reliability is exactly $-1/2$, everywhere in the
domain. Under independent reliability errors the delta-method gives
$\mathrm{SD}(\ln \hat{r}_{true}) \approx \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{(\sigma_{xx}/\mu_{xx})^2 + (\sigma_{yy}/\mu_{yy})^2}$.
The standard deviation of the corrected log-correlation is exactly half
the quadrature-combined relative SD of the two reliabilities. This is
not new mathematics - the derivative is elementary - but I had not
previously seen it stated as an identity, and stating it shrinks the
operational question to a one-line calculation. The relative
uncertainty of the corrected correlation does not depend on $r_{obs}$;
it depends only on the relative spread of the reliability inputs, and
at fixed half-weight.

### 2. Established instruments are signal-dominated

I compiled empirical means and spreads from the reliability-
generalisation literature for PHQ-9, GAD-7, BDI-II, and BFI-44. Defining
the signal-to-noise ratio of the correction as the ratio of the
correction's magnitude in log-space to the delta-method SD, every
named-instrument profile lands at SNR between $3.6$ and $7.3$. A 50,000-
draw Monte Carlo across 28 cells matched the analytical SD within
$1\%$. The four-regime map I had planned to draw is, for these
instruments, occupied entirely by the signal-dominated regime. The
boundary where the correction would lose its margin (SNR = 1) is
explicit: $\sigma_{\mathrm{crit}}(\mu) = |\ln \mu|\, \mu \sqrt{2}$.
At $\mu = 0.95$ the margin is thinnest ($\sigma_{\mathrm{crit}} =
0.07$); at $\mu = 0.70$ it is most generous (~$0.35$).

### 3. Most of the empirical spread is heterogeneity, not noise

This is the substantive finding the operational question alone would
have missed. Within-study sampling SDs computed from Feldt's (1965)
asymptotic approximation for Cronbach's $\alpha$ and from Fisher-$z$
for test–retest correlations give predicted SDs much smaller than
reported between-study SDs at typical study sizes. For PHQ-9 alpha
($\alpha = 0.86$, $k = 9$), within-study sampling accounts for ~70%
of reported variance at $n = 100$, ~35% at $n = 200$, and under one
quarter at $n \geq 1000$. The crossover is at $n \approx 140$. The
test–retest case is sharper: at $r = 0.81$, $n = 200$, within-study SD
is $0.025$; reported between-study SD is typically $0.06$ or larger,
implying a residual between-population SD of $0.055$. The bulk of the
reported spread is real heterogeneity. A correction that plugs in a
literature-average reliability is computing the right number for an
imaginary average population, not the population in front of the
practitioner.

### 4. The arithmetic is robust, but its meaning is not

The same $\sigma$ propagates through the same delta-method formula
whether it represents measurement noise around a single true $r_{xx}$
or genuine between-population variation. Under the noise reading, SNR
is the correction's signal against the uncertainty of estimating one
true reliability. Under the heterogeneity reading, SNR is the
correction's signal against the spread of corrected values one would
obtain by drawing the reliability from different populations. The
first kind of uncertainty is reducible by larger samples; the second
is not. A high SNR under the heterogeneity reading does not certify
that any one $\hat{r}_{true}$ is the right corrected value for any one
population.

## Where the work is going next

The deferred audit is the next step, and it has been redesigned in
light of the heterogeneity finding. The proposal anticipated a sample
of 15–20 recently published papers, with each correction recomputed
under the empirical reliability distribution. The SNR result renders
most of that audit's expected output predictable ($\geq 90\%$ of
published values inside the empirical interval), and the audit is
blind to the mis-targeting concern by construction. The right
question for the audit is not "does the published value lie inside
the empirical interval" but: from what population was the plugged-in
reliability drawn, does that population match the study sample on the
dimensions most likely to vary the construct (language, clinical
status, age band, mode of administration), and how does the corrected
correlation shift when the reliability is re-drawn from a
population-matched subset? The audit's expected difficulty is the
extraction of population metadata from primary reports, which is
inconsistently disclosed. I expect this to be 2–3 days of work and
to produce a small structured dataset releasable alongside the piece.

Two smaller follow-ups: (a) the high-reliability case ($\mu = 0.95$,
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{crit}}$ is thinnest) deserves a closer look,
because the syntheses I consulted do not isolate a published example
where the margin is breached but the parameter region is close to the
empirical envelope; (b) the correlated-reliabilities case where the
same sample reports both $r_{xx}$ and $r_{yy}$ is currently handled by
a bounded sensitivity analysis over $\rho \in [0.3, 0.7]$, and a
reliability-generalisation study reporting actual same-sample $\rho$
would replace the bound with a direct number.

## What I would most welcome reaction on

Four things, in descending order of how much they would change the
piece.

1. **Is the heterogeneity reframing the right lead?** The
   operational SNR finding is clean and complete, and a short
   technical note could stop there. I have promoted the within-vs-
   between decomposition to the piece's contribution on the grounds
   that the SNR result is robust as arithmetic but, under the
   heterogeneity reading, answers a question that does not match the
   practitioner's problem. A Fellow who disagrees with this
   prioritisation should say so; the structural choice is not
   forced by the data.

2. **The "three independent estimates" working rule.** Section 7 of
   the draft proposes that a population is "characterised" for an
   instrument when at least three independent reliability estimates
   exist from samples matching the study on language, clinical
   status, age band, and mode of administration. "Three" is a
   working number, not a theorem. I would welcome reaction on
   whether this threshold lands at roughly the right operational
   level or whether it is too lenient (or too strict) for the
   instrument literature as it actually exists.

3. **The within-vs-between subtraction.** I subtract within-study
   sampling variance (from Feldt for alpha, from Fisher-$z$ for
   test–retest) from total reported variance to estimate the
   between-population residual. The subtraction is the
   reliability-generalisation literature's standard move and the
   conclusion is robust to the heuristic of treating reported ranges
   as 90% intervals, but the subtraction is a moment-matching
   estimate that does not propagate its own uncertainty. A more
   careful hierarchical decomposition (e.g., a random-effects
   meta-analysis on the alphas with item count and sample size as
   covariates) would tighten the claim. Is this worth doing for the
   present piece or for a follow-up?

4. **The redesigned audit's feasibility.** Population-metadata
   extraction from primary reports is the audit's pacing item. I
   would welcome any pointer to a curated dataset where this work
   has already been done - by the original reliability-generalisation
   authors or anyone else - that would save the audit from
   constructing its own population coding from scratch.

## What I am not asking

The half-power identity itself does not need defence; the derivative
is elementary and the Monte Carlo cross-check is recorded. The
substantive structure of the piece (SNR map, decomposition, worked
example, disclosure rules) survived two rounds of formal peer review
without challenge to its substance. This preprint is surfaced
one layer earlier than that, to give the cohort an opportunity to
reshape the audit and the heterogeneity framing before the next phase
of work begins.
