## Round 1 - ada-lovelace (outside) - `minor`

# Review by Ada Lovelace

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay proposes a three-part taxonomy for confessions of ignorance in intellectual prose - sincere uncertainty, conventional hedging, and strategic uncertainty - and tests three diagnostic questions against Plato's *Apology*, Montaigne's "Apology for Raymond Sebond," and Frankfurt's *On Bullshit*. The diagnostic works cleanly on unmixed cases (Plato's strategic prosecution, Frankfurt's sincere definitional labor) but correctly identifies an indeterminacy in Montaigne, where the writer's genuine epistemic state and rhetorical strategy are fused in a single act. The essay's central structural finding is that this indeterminacy is not a failure of the diagnostic but a feature of the Montaigne-style essay as a form - which by its nature tends to align sincere and strategic not-knowing - and it extends the author's prior diagnostic work on examples (#07) by showing that the two tests (falsifiability and rhetorical function) diverge precisely in the most interesting cases.

## Strengths

## Strengths

**The three-mode taxonomy is genuinely novel and well-constructed.** The distinction between sincere uncertainty, conventional hedging, and strategic uncertainty is not merely a relabeling of existing categories. The specific positioning of strategic uncertainty as a third option in Frankfurt's liar/bullshitter space - "the writer cares about truth and has a view, but uses the forms of uncertainty as a shield against commitment" - is precise and philosophically interesting. It is not a category the existing literature has.

**The Plato analysis is the essay's sharpest close reading.** Three diagnostic features are identified (comprehensive surface skepticism coexisting with firm positive commitments; increased rather than decreased aggression after the confession; argument-already-built rather than inquiry opened), and each is traceable in the text. A reader who has read the *Apology* can check each claim independently. This is exactly what a diagnostic is supposed to demonstrate.

**The Montaigne analysis is intellectually honest in the right way.** The essay refuses to force a clean verdict where none exists. The finding that Montaigne's "Apology" is simultaneously strategic (the skepticism clears exactly the terrain Catholicism needs cleared) and sincere (decades of revision deepens rather than resolves the uncertainty) is not hedged away - it is named as the diagnostic's correct output when the input functions are aligned. A lesser essay would have forced one reading. This one earns its indeterminacy.

**The meta-finding is the essay's genuine intellectual contribution.** The observation that the diagnostic's failure is "a structural feature of serious essayistic writing" - that the form Montaigne invented requires both genuine confession and argumentative function, and that the best confessions arise where these are the same act - is the kind of structural result that earns the piece its place. It goes beyond cataloguing cases to say something about why the indeterminacy exists.

**The two-diagnostic comparison in "What the Failures Reveal" is the most original passage in the essay.** Bringing the falsifiability test from #07 into contact with the rhetorical-function test here, and showing that the two tests diverge in the same cases and that those cases are the most intellectually interesting ones, is a real contribution to how diagnostic tools in this genre should be evaluated. The observation that Montaigne's "Que sçay-je?" fails *both* tests - and that this double failure is informative rather than merely disappointing - is well-executed.

**References are correctly cited and doing real work.** Hyland's two studies are cited appropriately. Frankfurt is engaged as a primary source, not just invoked. Montaigne is cited in the Frame translation with full bibliographic information. The cross-reference to #07 is internally linked. No citation appears merely ornamental.

## Concerns

## Concerns

1. **The Hyland mechanism-transfer claim is asserted, not demonstrated.** The essay makes a crucial methodological move: it says Hyland's specific markers - "the modal verbs ('may,' 'might,' 'could'), approximators ('around,' 'roughly'), and attribution shields" - do not transfer from scientific to literary prose, but that "the underlying mechanism Hyland identified holds in any discourse." This is plausible, but the essay does not say what the mechanism is at a level of abstraction that would make the transfer claim verifiable. Something like: "the mechanism is that uncertainty language is governed by social function rather than epistemic state, and this holds wherever discourse communities exist and wherever career position affects how writers present claims" would ground it. As written, Hyland functions as authority-citation in a piece whose thesis is precisely about distinguishing sincere claims from strategic ones. The essay is using Hyland's name as a hedge; it should use his argument.

2. **The Frankfurt analysis does not apply the essay's own diagnostic recursively where it could.** The essay correctly identifies Frankfurt's hedges as sincere definitional labor rather than conventional performance. But Frankfurt's authorial position contains a move the essay's taxonomy is designed to catch: Frankfurt opens *On Bullshit* with explicit disavowal of any systematic theory ("I propose to begin the development of a theoretical understanding of bullshitting... I do not have a very clear idea of how to do this, so I shall just try to do it") and then proceeds to produce a precise and structured taxonomy. This is formally identical to Socrates' structure: comprehensive ignorance on the surface, specific positive commitments in practice. Whether this is sincere, conventional, or strategic is a question the essay's own diagnostic should at minimum attempt. The omission is not fatal - Frankfurt may simply be less interesting than Plato on this axis - but it leaves the essay's most analytically sophisticated case study doing less work than it should.

3. **The three tests lack an integration function, and the essay does not name this as a limitation.** When tests 1, 2, and 3 give different verdicts - as they do for Montaigne - the essay concludes that "the cases happen to be the most interesting." This is correct, but it sidesteps a practical question: what should a reader do when the tests diverge on a case they are actually trying to diagnose? Is a text strategic if any one test says so? Only if all three agree? Does test 3 (what immediately follows) have priority over test 2 (does behavior change) because it is more local and less dependent on biographical knowledge? The essay is honest that the tests "give partial verdicts," but it presents this as a feature of the difficult cases rather than as a design gap in the diagnostic itself. A sentence naming this - that the tests are not a decision procedure but a set of probes that require judgment to integrate - would be more accurate and more useful to a reader who wants to apply them.

4. **The claim that the essay form "aligns sincere and strategic not-knowing by design" is underargued.** This is the essay's most ambitious structural claim, appearing in the penultimate section: "The essay as a form, in the tradition Montaigne established, requires writers to think in public - to show uncertainty in motion rather than paper over it. That requirement aligns sincere and strategic not-knowing by design." But why does that particular combination produce alignment rather than tension? Many essayists - Bacon, Lamb, Orwell - work in the Montaigne tradition and manifestly separate inquiry from argument, confession from prosecution. The claim that the alignment is built into the form rather than being a feature of Montaigne's particular use of the form needs at minimum a sentence of argument. What in the form creates the alignment? The first-person voice? The refusal of the thesis-first structure? The iteration over examples rather than deductive development? Naming the mechanism would convert the claim from assertion to evidence.

5. **Conventional hedging gets no extended case study, which unbalances the taxonomy.** The essay introduces three modes and gives extended close readings only to strategic (Plato) and aligned sincere-and-strategic (Montaigne), with Frankfurt serving a different function - demonstrating sincere hedging that resembles but is not purely conventional. The conventional hedging mode - which the essay correctly identifies as "the commoner case, empirically" - has no corresponding case study where it is demonstrated at length. The essay describes what conventional hedging is via Hyland, then uses Frankfurt to show what it is *not*. But the mode itself goes undemonstrated in a full passage analysis. A paragraph of close reading - even a brief one, from an economics paper or a philosophy review - where hedging is clearly performing community membership rather than reporting epistemic state would ground the third mode the way Plato grounds the strategic one. Without it, conventional hedging has the appearance of a category without a specimen.

6. **Minor: the cross-reference to #07 would benefit from a one-sentence gloss for readers encountering this essay independently.** The essay says: "In my earlier essay on the evidential function of examples, I proposed a diagnostic test for examples: what would it take to defeat this claim? The test is about epistemological status - whether a claim is falsifiable in principle." This is enough to follow the comparison, but a reader who hasn't read #07 may not see why "what would defeat this claim?" is the correct formulation for a falsifiability test rather than simply asking "is this claim falsifiable?" The essay's own answer to this question - that defeat conditions must be *named*, not merely possible - is what makes the #07 test interesting. One sentence of clarification here would make the two-diagnostic comparison land more precisely.


## Round 1 - henri-poincare (primary) - `major`

# Review by Henri Poincaré

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** major
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The draft argues that confessions of ignorance in serious essayistic prose serve three distinct functions - sincere uncertainty, conventional hedging (taking Hyland's linguistic analysis of scientific writing as ground), and strategic uncertainty (a third position alongside Frankfurt's liar–bullshitter distinction) - and proposes a three-test diagnostic for telling them apart from the text alone. Through close readings of Plato's *Apology* (clearly strategic), Montaigne's "Apology for Raymond Sebond" (the aligned case where sincere and strategic are inseparable), and Frankfurt's own *On Bullshit* (sincere-under-construction with conventional residue), the piece reaches a structural conclusion: the diagnostic handles unmixed cases cleanly and finds a real, irreducible indeterminacy at the center, which the author argues is a property of the essay form rather than a defect of analysis. A late section returns to the author's prior essay on the evidential function of examples and uses it to contrast an epistemological diagnostic ("what would defeat this claim?") with the rhetorical one developed here ("what is the not-knowing doing in the argument's architecture?").

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The Frankfurt extension is the piece's sharpest move.** Naming a third option in the liar–bullshitter space - "the writer cares about truth and has a view, but uses the forms of uncertainty as a shield against commitment" - is not a paraphrase of Frankfurt. It is a genuine addition. The line "The liar works against the truth; the strategic uncertain works around commitment" is the kind of compression that earns the reader's attention. The category is also doing work in the rest of the essay rather than sitting decorative - it is what Test 3 actually catches.

**The Plato seam is the right seam.** The observation that "the uncertainty is comprehensive on the surface" while "the positive commitments that follow are specific and firm" is exactly the diagnostic move the rest of the essay is built around. Pairing this with the second observation - that the confession produces aggression rather than caution - is a real piece of textual evidence rather than ambient assertion. A reader can apply the same pair of tests to other texts and get traction.

**The refusal to tidy up the Montaigne case is intellectually honest.** A weaker version of this piece would have either declared Montaigne strategic (cheap) or declared the diagnostic failed (defeatist). Instead, the draft argues that the indeterminacy is the structural shape of the most interesting essayistic writing - that "when sincerity and strategy point in the same direction, the text gives us nothing to triangulate against." The image of "trying to unbraid a rope at its fibers, when the rope's strength is precisely in the braiding" is doing real work; it gives the reader a way to hold what is otherwise a slippery finding.

**The self-citation to "The Exemplum's Epistemology" is structural rather than ornamental.** The contrast set up in §"What the Failures Reveal" - that the earlier essay's "what would defeat this claim?" is a test of epistemological status, while the present essay's "what is the not-knowing doing in the architecture?" is a test of rhetorical function, and these two tests can diverge - is a genuine result. The author has now constructed a small mutually-aware apparatus across two essays, which is the kind of accumulation the College should reward.

**Hyland is invoked correctly and at the right load.** The acknowledgment that Hyland's specific markers do not transfer to philosophical or literary prose, but that the underlying mechanism (uncertainty language governed by community norms rather than epistemic state) does, is the right way to use empirical linguistics in an essayistic register. The author is not pretending to apply Hyland's apparatus; they are taking his finding and asking where it generalizes.

**Prose discipline is consistent.** "The skepticism is the argument." "Those are the same sentence." The piece earns its sentences. There is almost no padding.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The conventional-hedging mode is announced but never engaged by the diagnostic.** The three modes are introduced as parallel - sincere, conventional, strategic - but the three diagnostic tests in §"A Diagnostic, Tested" really only distinguish sincere from strategic. Test 1 (does the confession narrow options?) does not obviously catch conventional hedging, which can be either narrow or broad. Test 2 (does behavior change?) is closer - purely conventional hedging should be inert - but the piece never makes the connection. Test 3 (what follows?) likewise fires on argument-vs-inquiry, not on convention. The result is a three-mode taxonomy supporting a two-mode diagnostic. Either the diagnostic should be extended to triangulate conventional hedging specifically, or the conclusion should be honest that the diagnostic is really a sincere/strategic discriminator with conventional hedging as background noise.

2. **The Plato reading is asserted as obvious where it is not.** The phrase "by any diagnostic reading, a strategic one" papers over a significant scholarly debate about Socratic irony, the depth of Socratic ignorance, and whether the *Apology*'s confession is a sincere expression of a Socratic philosophical position. Vlastos's "Socratic Irony" and the secondary literature it generated are not adjudicated here; they are not even named. The textual evidence the author gives (comprehensive skepticism alongside substantive ethical certainties; aggressive prosecution; pre-built argument structure) is good evidence, but it does not warrant "by any diagnostic reading." Soften this to "the diagnostic offered here reads it as strategic, against a tradition that has variously read it as sincere or ironic" and the claim becomes defensible rather than overconfident.

3. **The Frankfurt section is the thinnest of the three case studies - and Frankfurt is the easiest case to do well, because the text is short and the apparatus is the author's own.** The whole treatment is two paragraphs. The reader is told that Frankfurt "qualifies his definitions in real time" with examples like "I mean, roughly..." and that the definition "tightens" with each qualification, but no specific qualification is quoted and tracked through to its effect on the definition. The piece concludes that the verdict is "sincere, or at minimum sincere-under-construction," but the case for that verdict is given in one sentence. Walking through two or three actual Frankfurt hedges, showing where the definition was before and after, would make the diagnostic visible at work on a third kind of case rather than merely asserted.

4. **The structural-finding move at the end risks unfalsifiability.** "The diagnostic fails precisely on the cases that are most interesting, and the failure is a feature of the genre" is an elegant move, but a reader could mount the same defense for any diagnostic that struggles with hard cases. The piece would be strengthened by naming what a counterexample would look like: a piece of essayistic writing of the first rank where sincere and strategic are clearly separable. If such a case exists and the diagnostic produces a clean verdict, that supports the structural claim. If no such case can be imagined, the structural claim is doing too much work. A paragraph addressing this would convert the closing flourish into a finding.

5. **The piece slides between "the function in the text" and "the writer's intention" at the load-bearing moment.** §"What the Failures Reveal" says: "The distinction between sincere and strategic not-knowing is real at the level of authorial intention - Plato's Socrates knows more than he claims to; Montaigne genuinely doesn't know what he claims not to know - but the textual evidence for that distinction is weak precisely where the question is most interesting." This is doing serious work, but it concedes that the diagnostic has not actually been textual all along - it has been textual evidence used to infer authorial mind. The piece should make this explicit. The diagnostic is not neutral between "what the text does" and "what the writer intends"; it is a method for reading from one to the other, and that method has limits the piece glosses.

6. **The diagnostic is recapitulated rather than derived.** §"A Diagnostic, Tested" presents the three tests as if formulating them, but the reader has been watching them be applied implicitly through the three case studies already. The effect is that the tests read as a summary of moves already made rather than as the apparatus that generated the readings. Two possible repairs: (a) move §"A Diagnostic, Tested" earlier, before the cases, so it does the analytic work prospectively; (b) keep the order but flag in the introduction that the diagnostic will be derived from the cases and stated formally only afterward. Either is honest; the current order is neither.

7. **"The three modes coexist and overlap" is stated once and then dropped.** The piece treats each of its three case studies as if one mode dominates - Plato strategic, Montaigne aligned-sincere-and-strategic, Frankfurt sincere-with-conventional-residue. But the most empirically common case in serious prose is probably a single sentence in which all three are operative simultaneously. The diagnostic is not built to address that case. The author could either acknowledge the diagnostic's sentence-level limits (it operates at paragraph or essay scale, not sentence scale) or take up one mixed sentence and show what the diagnostic can and cannot say about it. Either would close the gap between the announced overlap and the test-by-test treatment.

8. **A small textual matter.** The reference to "Michel de Montaigne. 'The Exemplum's Epistemology...'" in the References section is the author citing themselves under the persona name; the in-text reference at §"What the Failures Reveal" reads "In my earlier essay on the evidential function of examples." That is fine, but the cross-reference should use the markdown link format the archive index specifies so the blog renders an internal cross-reference with backlinks: `[the earlier essay](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/)`. The current bracketed slug at line 99 is close but not in the link form that produces the "Cited by" backlink.


## Round 1 - ibn-al-haytham (secondary) - `minor`

# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay distinguishes three modes of confessed ignorance in serious prose - sincere uncertainty, conventional hedging (after Hyland), and strategic uncertainty (a third position between Frankfurt's liar and bullshitter, in which true forms of uncertainty are used as cover against commitment) - and proposes a three-test diagnostic for telling them apart from the text alone: does the confession narrow options or hold everything open; does the writer's behavior change after the confession; and what immediately follows it. The diagnostic is applied to Plato's *Apology* (read as cleanly strategic), Montaigne's "Apology for Raymond Sebond" (read as a case where sincere and strategic are inseparable because aligned), and Frankfurt's *On Bullshit* (read as sincere-or-sincere-under-construction with a conventional layer the diagnostic cannot strip away). The piece's structural finding is that the diagnostic gives verdicts on the unmixed cases and a principled indeterminacy on the mixed ones, and that this indeterminacy is itself a feature of the essay form Montaigne invented - where genuine doubt and argumentative function are the same act.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The naming of "strategic uncertainty" as a third position in Frankfurt's taxonomy is a real contribution.** Frankfurt's liar/bullshitter pair has been generative for two decades, and the author has added a category that fits exactly the gap I have felt reading polished philosophical and essayistic prose: a writer who cares about truth, holds a view, and uses the forms of uncertainty to avoid commitment. The category is not a synonym for any of the existing terms, and once named it is hard to unsee. This is the piece's central original move and it lands.

- **The Plato reading is the strongest case study.** The contrast between Socrates' comprehensive surface skepticism and the substantive ethical certainties that follow ("better to obey God than men," "the unexamined life is not worth living") is drawn with the precision of a controlled comparison: same speaker, same speech, two epistemically incompatible registers. The observation that a sincere admission of ignorance should produce *greater* tentativeness while the *Apology* produces vigorous prosecution is a specific behavioral prediction the text fails to meet, and it is the kind of evidence I want a diagnostic to rest on.

- **The author refuses the easy verdict on Montaigne.** A weaker version of this essay would have forced the diagnostic to deliver a clean reading of the "Apology for Raymond Sebond" - either calling the doubt strategic (and losing the textual evidence that it is real) or calling it sincere (and losing the evidence that it does selective argumentative work). The author holds both and lets the indeterminacy stand. "When doubt is simultaneously real and useful, the confession cannot be read against the argument it supports, because they are the same act." This is the right epistemic posture for the evidence available, and it is the move I would not have predicted from the round-1 outline.

- **The structural finding is genuinely novel.** Framing the indeterminacy not as diagnostic failure but as a structural feature of the essay form Montaigne invented - that the genre *requires* sincere and strategic to align - converts a limitation into a positive claim about the genre. I have not seen this argued cleanly in the literature I know.

- **The Hyland citation is used carefully.** The author quotes Hyland's empirical finding about scientific hedging without overclaiming the transfer to literary prose, and explicitly distinguishes Hyland's surface markers (modal verbs, approximators) from the underlying mechanism that travels. The line "the mechanism transfers even when the markers do not" is the right move.

- **The "unbraid a rope at its fibers" image** in the closing section earns its place: it does work, not ornament, by picturing why the diagnostic fails exactly where the writing is most accomplished.

- **The cross-reference to the prior essay on examples is integrated, not gestural.** The observation that the "what would defeat this claim?" diagnostic and the "what is the not-knowing doing?" diagnostic can diverge in the same cases - and that the divergence is informative - is the kind of method-building across pieces the College ought to encourage. It earns the citation rather than decorating with it.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Mode-versus-function equivocation runs through the entire essay and is load-bearing.** The piece names "three modes of not-knowing" - sincere, conventional, strategic - and a "mode" most naturally reads as a state of mind: what the writer actually believes, intends, or knows. But the diagnostic the essay constructs operates on the *text* and recovers *functions* - what the confession does in the argument's architecture. The author is locally aware of this - the Plato section says "the text gives us no purchase on the sincere component - we cannot recover it from the argument's structure. What the structure shows us is the strategic function" - and yet the rest of the essay continues to speak of modes, of writers who "have a view" and use uncertainty "as a shield," of intent and concealment. If the diagnostic is genuinely working from the text alone, the categories ought to be renamed: *sincere function*, *conventional function*, *strategic function* - what the not-knowing is doing in the argument, irrespective of what the writer privately holds. As written, the essay slides between two object-level claims (a claim about writers' minds; a claim about textual effects) and the reader cannot tell which is being defended in any given paragraph. This is the single most important fix.

2. **The diagnostic is unfalsifiable as currently specified.** When the three tests give a clean verdict (Plato), the diagnostic "works." When they fail to give a clean verdict (Montaigne), the diagnostic is said to "correctly identify an indeterminacy." There is no specified outcome under which the diagnostic would be *wrong*. This is the same structural problem the author flagged in *The Exemplum's Epistemology* about claims that "absorb every disconfirmation" - the diagnostic here is currently in that posture with respect to itself. I would like to see at least one of the following added: (a) a case where the diagnostic gives a confident verdict that the author thinks is wrong, and the author says what would correct it; (b) named conditions under which the diagnostic's verdict on Plato could be defeated (what could one find in or about the *Apology* that would force a re-reading?); (c) the prediction that the diagnostic should give different verdicts on hypothetical neighboring texts, and a worked example of the neighbor.

3. **Selection bias: three pre-selected exemplars, each chosen to illustrate a thesis already in mind.** The diagnostic is tested only against cases the author selected because they exhibited the patterns the diagnostic is supposed to detect. There is no positive control - no case selected on independent grounds where we expect *sincere* uncertainty and where the diagnostic should return that verdict. Frankfurt is the closest, and even there the author concedes the diagnostic cannot separate sincere from conventional. So the diagnostic has been demonstrated to identify strategic (Plato), to identify alignment (Montaigne), and to give a partial verdict (Frankfurt). It has not been demonstrated to identify *cleanly sincere* uncertainty. Either name a fourth case where the diagnostic should and does return "sincere," or concede in the conclusion that the sincere category has not been textually demonstrated and may not be diagnostically recoverable on its own.

4. **The Frankfurt verdict is treated as a success where the text supports treating it as a mixed case.** The author writes that the diagnostic "gives a verdict (sincere, or at minimum sincere-under-construction) but cannot fully separate the sincere component from the conventional one." Read carefully, that is two unmixed cases (Plato strategic; Montaigne aligned) and *one more* mixed case, not a clean sincere reading. The Test 1 / Test 2 / Test 3 summary on §61–63 nonetheless lists Frankfurt as showing "genuine development," which understates the residual conventional component the body of the section names. If Frankfurt is also a mixed case, the diagnostic's record is *one* clean verdict and two indeterminacies, which sharpens the structural finding (the genre aligns sincere with what supports the argument) rather than weakening it - but it does change the bookkeeping.

5. **The Hyland transfer is asserted at the level of "mechanism" without specifying what the mechanism is.** The essay says: "But the underlying mechanism Hyland identified holds in any discourse: uncertainty language can be governed by convention, by political environment, and by career position rather than by epistemic state. The mechanism transfers even when the markers do not." What is the mechanism, stated as a falsifiable claim? "Uncertainty language is governed by social factors as well as epistemic ones" is true everywhere, but it does not give the reader anything to test. A tighter formulation - for instance, that the *distribution* of hedges across writers in a discourse should covary with social variables (seniority, institutional position) and not only with epistemic ones - would make the transfer claim defeasible. As written it is the kind of move the author's *Exemplum* essay would name as a structural claim that has not specified its defeat conditions.

6. **"What comes immediately after the confession?" (Test 3) is doing more work than the text shows.** The test is well-formed on Plato - the confession of ignorance is followed by an already-built argument - and weaker on Montaigne and Frankfurt. It would be worth specifying what *counts* as "immediately after." A paragraph? A page? In Plato's *Apology* the gap from confession to prosecution is short. In the "Apology for Raymond Sebond" the relevant material is dispersed across hundreds of pages. The test's resolving power depends on the textual window being applied, and the window is not declared. (This is the kind of concern I raise in my own optical work - what is the aperture of the instrument, what does it admit, what does it exclude?)

7. **No engagement with the case where the three modes contradict the diagnostic's verdict.** The author considers cases where modes align (Montaigne) but not the case where, say, a writer is sincerely uncertain but writes in a way that the diagnostic flags as strategic, or vice versa. This is the experimental design question: false positives and false negatives. What does the diagnostic do when it is wrong? The piece would be stronger for naming at least one plausible false-positive case - a sincerely uncertain writer whose argumentative architecture the diagnostic would misread as strategic. Even a sketch of such a case would constrain the diagnostic's scope.

8. **A modest note on cohort placement.** The piece sits in interesting territory next to Pierre Bayle's brief in the College, and the structural finding about the essay form ought, I think, to acknowledge that some readers will hear it as a defense of evasion. The author already half-anticipates this - "the indeterminacy is not evasion; it is the honest shape of the inquiry" - but a single sentence specifying when alignment *would* slide into evasion (presumably when the writer's authentic epistemic state is more resolved than they let the form show) would forestall the obvious objection.


## Round 1 - pierre-bayle (secondary) - `accept`

# Review by Pierre Bayle

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** accept
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay argues that confessions of ignorance in serious writing operate in three distinct modes-sincere reports of actual uncertainty, conventional hedging that signals discourse-community membership independent of epistemic state, and strategic deployment of uncertainty language as a shield against commitment. The author develops three diagnostic tests to distinguish these modes (narrowing of options, behavioral change after confession, what follows the confession), applies them to Plato's *Apology*, Montaigne's "Apology for Raymond Sebond," and Frankfurt's *On Bullshit*, and finds that the diagnostics succeed when sincere and strategic uncertainty diverge but fail-or rather, correctly identify a genuine indeterminacy-when a writer's authentic doubt and rhetorical strategy are aligned. The central finding is that this alignment is not a defect in the diagnostic but a structural feature of the essayistic form Montaigne established, which requires thinking in public about unresolved questions.

## Strengths

**The three-part taxonomy is genuinely novel and extends usefully beyond its sources.** Hyland's work on hedging in scientific discourse is well-known; this essay's contribution is to distinguish strategic uncertainty (a third category with its own logic) and to show that this distinction operates across genres-philosophical, literary, analytical-not just scientific writing. The separation of "strategic" from both "sincere" and merely "conventional" is the intellectual work the piece does.

**The close readings deliver specific, textual evidence for the diagnostic claims.** The analysis of Plato's *Apology* is particularly strong: the identification of three markers (comprehensive skepticism on the surface but specific ethical certainties in practice; behavior shifting from tentative to aggressive; confession followed by fully-formed argument rather than inquiry) is precise and traceable. The reader can check each claim against the text and see why the confession functions strategically rather than sincerely.

**The intellectual honesty about the diagnostic's failure is exemplary.** Rather than hiding the fact that the tests give no clean verdict on Montaigne, the essay makes that failure its central finding and argues-persuasively-that the failure is informative rather than disqualifying. The claim that "when the writer's genuine epistemic state and their rhetorical strategy are aligned, the text cannot tell us which is generating the confession" is a real observation about how language works, and the essay earns it through careful reasoning rather than assertion.

**The Frankfurt case clarifies the relationship between convention and sincerity in hedging.** The distinction between Frankfurt's hedges (which develop the concept in real time) and merely performative hedges (which signal circumspection without moving the argument) shows that convention and genuine uncertainty are not synonymous-they can coexist and do different work simultaneously. This is the kind of precision the essay's taxonomy promises.

**The structure and prose are clear without being reductive.** The essay's argument is visible in its organization, assumptions are explicit, the architecture of the diagnostic tests is transparent, and the cases are marked as clear, partial, or indeterminate rather than forced into a single frame.

**The piece engages appropriately with prior College work**, citing #07 ("The Exemplum's Epistemology") in its final reference list and building on the distinction between illustrative and loading exempla at line 69-72, which extends naturally into the current work's concern with rhetorical function.

## Concerns

1. **The claim about where sincere uncertainty appears in published writing (line 9) lacks empirical grounding.** The draft states "Pure sincere uncertainty in published writing is mostly found at the edges: in a conclusion's final paragraph, in a footnote that acknowledges a gap, in a methodological aside." This is plausible but presented without evidence or citation. A piece built on diagnostic rigor should either cite a study that measured this distribution or present it more tentatively as an observation rather than a documented finding. As written, it reads like the kind of fabricated specificity (the location categories given with such assurance) that I would flag in other work. The hedge "rarer in polished writing than it appears" (line 9) softens it appropriately, but the claim would be stronger if it either cited evidence or was framed more explicitly as an inference from the case studies rather than a general claim.

2. **The generalization about the essay form as Montaigne established it (lines 74-76) could be more tightly bounded.** The draft claims "The form that Montaigne invented - thinking in public about questions one cannot resolve - requires confessions of ignorance that are simultaneously sincere reports and argumentative moves."
