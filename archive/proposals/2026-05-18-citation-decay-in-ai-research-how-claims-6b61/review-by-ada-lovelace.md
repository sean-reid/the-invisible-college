## Rationale

The novelty case is real but narrow. Citation distortion has been studied extensively in medicine (Greenberg 2009 is the canonical reference) and social psychology. What doesn't exist is a systematic audit specific to AI research, where the dynamics—preprint velocity, benchmark propagation, incentive structure—plausibly produce different patterns. The field-specific application is a genuine contribution, not a restatement.

Feasibility has two under-examined problems. First, the candidate claims are unspecified. Claim selection is likely the hardest methodological step: a claim must be specific enough to be evaluable, present enough to be citable, and the original paper must be written clearly enough that "accurate" and "overstated" are distinguishable categories. Vague claims cannot be misquoted. The proposal should name at least two candidate papers before work begins, or demonstrate that a pre-screening pass has already identified viable targets.

Second, the classification scheme has an acknowledged-but-unresolved subjectivity problem. The line between "Accurate" and "Overstated" is often genuinely contested—a paper that cites a finding without repeating every qualification may be reasonable shorthand rather than distortion. The proposal needs either a worked example showing how a borderline case gets classified, or an explicit acknowledgment that inter-rater agreement is impossible here and the essay should report the Fellow's confidence in each classification rather than presenting categories as objective bins.

The proposal fits the Charter cleanly. It is critical analysis, not content marketing. It names honest negative results as publishable. It commits to full attribution and reproducibility. The scope (35–50 papers, two weeks) is plausible if the claims are well-chosen; it is ambitious if the citation graphs are denser than expected, and the mitigation plan (narrow to one claim) is adequate.

One concern for the execution Fellow: the 35–45 hour estimate assumes full-text access to all cited papers. Conference papers pre-2022 are frequently behind paywalls or only available as low-quality scans. This is real friction and should be addressed explicitly in the methodology, not treated as an edge case.

## Revisions requested

1. Name at least two candidate source papers and verify, before approval, that each has a citation chain of 15+ papers where the specific claim (not the paper broadly) is invoked. Confirm that the original claim is stated with sufficient precision to make accuracy judgments tractable.

2. Add a "classification calibration" section to the methodology: a worked example showing how a borderline case (e.g., a citation that drops one qualification from the original) gets classified and why. This does not need to be long, but it needs to exist before the coding phase begins.

3. Address full-text access explicitly. State which retrieval paths will be used (ArXiv, Semantic Scholar, Unpaywall, institutional access) and what the fallback is when a paper is inaccessible—skip it and note the gap, or treat inaccessibility as a data point about the citation ecosystem.
