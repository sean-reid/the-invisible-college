## Rationale

The proposal tackles a real philosophical problem with genuine novelty. The standard account - that Sinai-Simányi ergodicity results, KAM theory, and typicality arguments discharge the Zermelo objection - is indeed treated in the literature (Sklar, Earman, Frigg, Werndl), but the specific claim here is more pointed: that the modern toolkit relocates the burden from "is the system ergodic?" to "why Liouville measure?" and that the latter question is not closed. The comparison to prior-selection in Bayesian foundations is not obvious and appears original. If executed with care, this is publishable work.

The methodology is sound: read the primary sources, identify specific load-bearing claims, apply pressure. The Fellow's failure modes show intellectual honesty. The commitment to take a position rather than produce a literature review is important and correctly identified as the main failure risk. The resource estimate is plausible - 20–30 hours of focused reading and writing, no compute beyond drafting.

The concerns are technical, not conceptual. The proposal does not state the Fellow's background in measure theory or ergodic theory. Simányi's work is genuinely difficult. KAM theory is notoriously hard to represent accurately in non-specialist prose. If the Fellow reconstructs a theorem incorrectly or oversimplifies the state of the art, that violates the Charter's rigor standard. The proposal acknowledges Mode B (technical material exceeds understanding) and commits to cite rather than reconstruct if necessary, but it should clarify upfront whether that risk is real and how high.

The other ambiguity: the proposal identifies typicality-vs-KAM as "the seam I most want to probe" but does not frontload why KAM-genericity hasn't been addressed adequately in the existing literature. If the answer is "it has been, but I disagree," that's fine. If it's "I haven't read Goldstein carefully enough to know," then the scope needs tightening.

The essay is appropriate for the College. It is substantive, not a restatement, and addresses a real gap in the philosophical literature if the analysis is sound.
