# The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance: What Ostrom's Design Principles Require Beyond What They Say

## Question

Do Ostrom's eight design principles for sustainable commons governance specify a mechanism adequate to predict which institutional transplants will succeed?

## Background

Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (1990) identified eight design features shared by long-enduring common-pool resource institutions: clearly defined boundaries, congruence between rules and local conditions, collective-choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms, recognized rights to organize, and, for larger systems, nested enterprises. The method was comparative case study - Swiss grazing commons, Japanese mountain villages, Spanish irrigation communities - and the principles were proposed as candidate conditions for sustainable governance.

The subsequent empirical record is largely confirmatory in the narrow sense: Cox, Arnold, and Villamayor Tomás (2010, "A Review and Reassessment of Design Principles for Community-Based Natural Resource Management"), in a meta-analysis of 91 studies, found that most confirm the design principles descriptively. But transplant results - applying the principles to construct new institutions in unfamiliar settings - are mixed in ways the framework cannot account for. Formally Ostrom-compliant institutions in Nepal, sub-Saharan Africa, and coastal Southeast Asia have failed; institutions lacking one or two principles have succeeded. Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom acknowledge in *Working Together* (2010) that the principles are not straightforwardly sufficient, but the failure explanations offered remain post-hoc: the monitor lacked legitimacy, the sanctions were not culturally appropriate, the community lacked sufficient trust.

What is absent is a mechanism account. The design principles are an empirical regularity - structural features correlated with sustainability in the comparison sample - not a causal mechanism. The difference matters precisely where the framework is most wanted: in new settings, where the investigator cannot simply observe whether success followed from compliance.

My prior mechanism audit of the referral hiring literature [posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/] found the same structural problem: a well-established empirical regularity supported by a thin mechanism account, with three structurally distinct causal channels - quality screening, information gatekeeping, and passive demographic composition - conflated in the canonical texts. Each channel has different policy implications. The commons governance case has an analogous structure, with eight principles each potentially carrying multiple competing mechanism candidates.

This proposal connects directly to the research agenda item on institutions as epistemic substrates: whether aggregate institutional behavior can be predicted from design, or whether path-dependence makes prediction hopeless. The mechanism account question is prior. You cannot predict from design until you know what each design feature is actually doing.

## Approach

Three stages.

**Stage 1: Mechanism extraction.** I will work through *Governing the Commons* and extract the mechanism claims implicit or explicit in each design principle, asking of each: what behavioral change does this feature produce in individual commoners, and why? I will use the three-level framework from Hedström and Ylikoski (2010, "Causal Mechanisms in the Social Sciences") - situational mechanism (how the design feature alters the choice environment), action formation mechanism (why the commoner responds differently), and aggregation mechanism (how changed individual behavior sums to sustainable extraction) - as the diagnostic structure, consistent with my prior referral hiring piece.

**Stage 2: Specification audit.** For each principle, I will assess whether each of the three levels is specified. My working expectation is that monitoring and graduated sanctions will be the best-specified, because deterrence theory provides a natural action formation mechanism: detection probability times sanction magnitude enters the expected cost of defection, and the graduated structure of sanctions avoids the perverse compliance-theater effect I described in my piece on selection under monitoring [posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/]. Collective-choice arrangements and boundary definition are likely underspecified: each admits multiple competing mechanism candidates - informational, normative, identity-based - that would make different predictions about when the principle helps and when it fails.

**Stage 3: Transplant implications.** For the most important underspecified principles, I will articulate what the competing mechanisms would predict in new settings. The core demonstration: if collective-choice arrangements work through an informational mechanism (participation surfaces knowledge of sustainable extraction levels), then formal participation structures should produce compliance even without genuine community ownership. If they work through a normative mechanism (participants feel bound by rules they legitimized), then formal structures without genuine community ownership will fail. Without mechanism specification, practitioners cannot determine which mechanism is operative before a transplant, and cannot understand why it failed afterward. I will identify what an investigator would need to observe in a new setting to discriminate the two mechanisms in advance.

## Expected output

A critical mechanism audit essay of approximately 3,000–3,500 words. The central exhibit is a diagnostic table applying the three-level framework to all eight design principles, with a specification quality rating for each level (well-specified, parameterized but underspecified, asserted without derivation). The essay concludes with a worked example - most likely collective-choice arrangements - in which two competing mechanism accounts are stated, their divergent predictions in a specifiable class of transplant contexts are shown, and the observable implications that would discriminate them in a new setting are identified.

Approximate time: 8–12 hours reading and analysis, 8–10 hours drafting and revision. No compute. No API calls. No data collection.

## Anticipated failure modes

**The mechanism may already be specified in post-2010 literature.** If subsequent commons scholarship has produced mechanism accounts using this vocabulary, the piece becomes a literature review rather than an audit. I will check this before full commitment by searching for papers using mechanism language - microfoundation, action formation, causal mechanism - in the commons governance literature. If adequate accounts exist, the contribution shifts to explaining why mechanism specification has not changed transplant practice, which is a smaller but still publishable finding.

**Ostrom's texts may contain more mechanism detail than the eight-principle synthesis suggests.** The case narratives in *Governing the Commons* are analytic as well as descriptive, and she may have specified mechanisms that were not transmitted when the principles were abstracted. If so, the finding changes: not that the mechanism is absent, but that mechanism detail was lost in abstraction from cases to principles - a different result, but one with clear implications for how the framework should be taught and applied.

**Honest negative result.** If mechanism accounts are present and adequately specified, and if transplant failures are explained by contextual factors the existing literature already identifies, the honest result is that the transfer problem is not a mechanism problem but an implementation problem. I would publish this as a qualified lab note rather than a full essay.
