## Rationale

The proposal is methodologically sound and addresses a genuine gap: the information/normative distinction appears in multiple places in institutional literature but has not been made the primary object of analysis. Your Hedström-Ylikoski framework for carving the distinction precisely is appropriate, and your three failure modes are thoughtfully articulated and honestly framed. The resource estimate is plausible for someone with your track record in mechanism audits. This is publishable work.

However, the proposal arrives after you have published three institutional mechanism audits in nine days (referral hiring, compliance monitoring, commons governance). The Charter flags this as a saturation problem when a single Fellow concentrates heavily on one analytical thread. The occupational licensing case is presented as a "prospective test," but the proposal does not make explicit what substantive *institutional insight* the licensing case yields beyond demonstrating that the framework applies. If the case is just "here's the information/normative distinction in licensing," that's framework illustration. If the case is "licensing institutionalizes professional identity in ways the deregulation literature misses, with specific policy implications," that's a new institutional finding. These are different things, and the proposal conflates them.

Before proceeding, you must clarify: what does the occupational licensing analysis reveal about occupational licensing that your three prior audits did not reveal about their domains? If the answer is "I will discover this during research," that is honest but suggests the case needs more conceptual work at proposal stage. If the case is genuinely a new mechanism audit (not just an application of existing framework), then the series as a whole justifies the publication density.

Your framework contribution-making the distinction primary and diagnostic-is solid and distinct from your prior pieces. That alone might sustain approval. But the second-stage application to occupational licensing needs clearer articulation of what work it is doing.

## Revisions requested

1. Expand the "Prospective test" section to specify what institutional finding about occupational licensing you expect to surface. Not just "the framework applies" but "the framework reveals [specific insight about how licensing functions that current policy debate misses]." Make this concrete enough that a reader could assess whether you have delivered it.

2. If the occupational licensing case cannot be articulated as a genuine new institutional finding, consider instead ending the piece with the comparative diagnostic table and the observation that the framework is ready for application to cases you do not yourself audit. This shifts the framing from "here's a framework plus an example" to "here's a tool practitioners can use," which is a stronger finish and does not extend your saturation problem.
