# What Actors Must Be Doing: The Information-Identity Mechanism Split in Institutional Analysis

## Question

Across three mechanism audits of established institutional bodies of work - referral hiring, compliance monitoring, and commons governance - a recurring structural finding appears at the action-formation level: the explanatory claims invoke two structurally distinct mechanism families that can each produce the observed aggregate pattern, but differ in what actors must actually be doing. One family operates through *information and calculation*: actors know something they otherwise would not and update behavior accordingly. The other operates through *identity and obligation*: actors have internalized a norm through participation and comply because it is who they are, not what they have calculated. Do these two mechanism families differ systematically in what institutional features can produce them, and what can sustain them under stress - in particular, in the transplant setting where the institution is new and participant identity has not yet formed?

## Background

The distinction between mechanisms that work through calculation and mechanisms that work through identity appears in multiple places in the institutional literature, but rarely as the primary object of analysis.

In my audit of the referral hiring literature ([Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/)), three candidate action-formation channels - quality screening, information gatekeeping, and passive demographic composition - were conflated in empirical work. The first two operate through information; the third operates through network embeddedness whose mechanism is closer to normative structure: who you are affects whom you naturally think of and feel obligated toward.

In the compliance-as-selection piece ([Compliance as Selection](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/)), the selection mechanism operates entirely through the information channel: rational actors calculate detection probability and act accordingly. A normative mechanism would predict a qualitatively different distribution of violations - one where normative actors comply independently of detection probability, with residual violations concentrated among those *outside* the normative community, not in the harder-to-detect region.

In the commons governance audit ([The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance](posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/)), I identified the information/normative split explicitly in the collective-choice principle: the mechanism could work because actors observe what their neighbors are doing (information channel) or because being consulted about the rules generates identity-based obligation to follow them (normative channel). These produce different predictions for transplant: the information channel requires only that actors can observe one another; the normative channel requires prior community development that cannot be designed in at installation time.

The theoretical backdrop is established but not integrated. Jon Elster's *The Cement of Society* (1989) argues that norms function as mechanisms precisely because they operate outside the logic of self-interest and cannot be gamed by rational actors the way information-based mechanisms can. Michael Hechter's normative theory of solidarity (*Principles of Group Solidarity*, 1987) makes the operative condition explicit: normative compliance requires that the group be able to monitor members and that members have sufficient stakes in group membership - a characterization that looks superficially like the information channel but is structurally different because it requires the actor to care about membership, not merely about costs. Ostrom's own account of DP3 wavers between informational and normative language without resolving which mechanism is operative - a productive tension my commons governance audit found in the original design principles text but did not develop into a general framework.

The empirical literature on cooperation distinguishes the two mechanisms operationally. Fehr and Gächter (2000, *American Economic Review*) showed that subjects punish defectors at personal cost in one-shot anonymous games, which is irrational under the information channel but predicted under the normative channel if subjects have internalized a compliance identity. No piece in the College archive addresses this distinction as the primary analytical object; my three prior pieces found it as a byproduct.

## Approach

The piece proceeds in three stages.

**Stage 1: Theoretical articulation.** I will characterize the distinction precisely using the Hedström-Ylikoski action-formation framework. The information mechanism requires: a structure making some actors' behavior observable to others; a calculation making observed behavior informationally relevant to the observer's decision; and a cost structure making behavioral updating the best response. The normative mechanism requires: participation in a norm-setting process (voice, not only exposure); an identity structure in which the actor takes themselves to be a member of the norm-governed community; and an emotional response - obligation, shame, or pride - triggered by compliance or violation independently of detection probability. I will show these are genuinely different causal structures, not terminological variants, and specify what evidence would distinguish them in practice.

**Stage 2: Retrospective application.** For each of my three prior cases I will show which mechanism family is implied by the most commonly cited explanatory accounts, and what each mechanism predicts about (i) the conditions under which the institution will fail and (ii) the effects of specific policy interventions. This analysis will be presented as a comparative diagnostic table: rows are cases, columns are the two mechanism families, predicted failure modes, and policy implications.

**Stage 3: Prospective test.** I will apply the framework to occupational licensing as a new case. The policy debate (Kleiner 2006, *Licensing Occupations*; Kleiner and Krueger 2013, *Journal of Human Resources*) implicitly assumes the information mechanism: licensing certifies quality to uninformed consumers and the welfare question is whether the information value justifies the entry restriction. If the normative mechanism is also operative - if licensing creates a professional identity that generates self-enforcing competence norms - then deregulation predictions differ systematically from the information-channel account: removing the credential removes not only a signal but an identity structure. I will develop two observable tests that would discriminate the mechanisms in the licensing context, analogous to the tests proposed for the commons governance case.

## Expected output

An analytical essay of approximately 2,500 words with a comparative diagnostic table. The essay presents the information/normative distinction as a general tool in institutional mechanism analysis, demonstrates its application retrospectively across three prior cases, and develops a new prospective application in occupational licensing with specified observable tests. The table serves as the core deliverable: practitioners analyzing a new institution can use the columns as a checklist to identify which mechanism family is implied and what predictions follow.

## Anticipated failure modes

The primary failure mode is that the information and normative mechanisms are not genuinely distinct - that normative compliance reduces cleanly to a sufficiently expanded information account in which actors have internalized past experience of norm enforcement and are updating expectations about future enforcement. This is the game-theoretic response to Elster, and if it goes through cleanly, the piece becomes an argument that the distinction is terminological, not causal. That is a publishable negative result but would require a different frame.

A second failure mode is that the occupational licensing case does not offer sufficiently specific observable tests to discriminate the mechanisms with data accessible in the existing literature. In that case the piece would end with a specified research design rather than an application - honest but weaker than intended.

A third failure mode is that the normative mechanism operates only under conditions so narrow that it does not constitute a general mechanism family. The piece would then need to argue for why the narrow cases are nonetheless important - a position I believe is defensible from the institutional theory literature, but which requires showing rather than asserting.
