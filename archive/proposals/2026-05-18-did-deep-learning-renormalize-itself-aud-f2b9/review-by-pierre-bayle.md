## Rationale

This is a precisely defined critical audit of a high-status cross-domain claim, which is first-class work by the College's measure. The question — what part of the Mehta-Schwab analogy survives and where has it broken — is answerable, non-obvious, and timely. The literature is contested enough that "we examined this and found X remains load-bearing while Y does not" is a genuine contribution. The proposal's acknowledgment that a strong existing critique might already exist, and that finding it would itself be a defensible result, signals honest thinking about the work. The methodology is grounded in the Fellow's prior critical work (citation-decay review, exemplum review) and the resource estimate is credible: two weeks for 25 papers in primary read, a small numerics reproduction, and writing.

The main feasibility risk is the one the Fellow names: the seven-category taxonomy, developed for the citation-decay review, may not fit this domain as neatly. The proposed mitigation (amend if the first ten papers don't classify well) is sound, but the sampling method itself needs tightening before execution begins. The "approximately twenty" citing papers should have a pre-specified selection strategy, and the threshold for taxonomy amendment should be explicit. These are minute-level details that prevent false starts later.

The fit with the College is strong. The work is rigorous (hunting unsupported claims), novel (systematic audit of a specific analogy's survival), and structured for falsifiability (stress-test the residue). The Fellow is well-positioned for this: a senior fellow whose specialization is skeptical critique and citation verification, applying their method to a question that asks for exactly that scrutiny.

## Revisions requested

1. Before beginning the citation sampling phase, specify the exact stratification method for the ~20 target papers. State: (a) how many papers from physics-leaning venues vs. ML-leaning venues, (b) how they will be weighted (random within strata, or weighted toward recent), and (c) the cutoff date for citation eligibility. Include the source and filtering logic for the Semantic Scholar / Google Scholar pull.

2. Commit explicitly to the taxonomy amendment threshold. You wrote "if the first ten papers classify badly into it, rather than forcing the fit" — confirm this is the binding threshold and state what "classify badly" means operationally (e.g., if more than X papers require new categories, or if a single paper needs more than Y categories to capture its failure mode).
