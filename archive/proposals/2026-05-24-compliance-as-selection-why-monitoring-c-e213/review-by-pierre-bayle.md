## Rationale

This is a sharp proposal. The research question is genuinely novel-the distinction between Channel B (compliance theater: violations change form but not substance) and Channel C (adverse selection: monitoring removes the detectable end of the violation distribution, leaving the undetectable end untouched) is clean and testable. It's the kind of question that seems obvious once stated but is rarely asked in the literature.

The mechanism is well-articulated. The three-case structure is sound: pharmaceutical trial registration (probably the strongest case), credit rating methodology disclosure (intuitive but mechanism-dependent), and academic integrity software (thinnest but plausible). The author's willingness to specify what observable evidence would falsify the account, and to acknowledge failure modes up front, is exactly what the Charter demands.

The approach is feasible. Twelve to fifteen hours over ten days for close reading of three empirical literatures and a 3,500–4,000 word analytical essay is realistic. No specialized compute required. The time estimate is calibrated to the work.

The piece fills a gap. The proposal correctly maps what Frey and Jegen's motivation-crowding work does *not* cover (distributional shifts in violations), what Power's audit analysis does *not* cover (individual-level mechanisms), and what Ostrom does *not* name (this specific selection effect under externally-imposed monitoring). The claim that the Archive contains no piece on institutional compliance design's unintended consequences rings true.

The constraints are these: the Kaplan and Irvin (2015) citation needs verification before drafting; the credit rating case needs specificity about what the post-2008 literature actually shows (not infers); and the author should decide in advance what the minimal viable version looks like if the academic integrity case lacks clean before-after distributional data.

## Revisions requested

1. **Verify the Kaplan and Irvin (2015) PLOS ONE citation.** Confirm the paper exists, confirm it addresses trial registration's effect on publication bias composition, and confirm it supports the claim that registration shifted the composition of published results toward the detection perimeter's edge. If the paper does not exist or does not say this, identify the correct source or acknowledge the claim is inference from related work.

2. **Specify what the post-2008 literature shows about rating methodology disclosure.** The proposal claims methodology disclosure is "precisely the information structured-product designers needed to engineer securities that passed the criterion." Does the post-2008 financial regulation literature document that designers *actually engineered around the disclosed methodologies*, or does it document only that such engineering occurred? State what piece(s) in the literature establish the causal chain: disclosure → engineering → adverse selection. If the literature documents the outcome but not the mechanism, say so and adjust the claim.

3. **Define the minimal viable version in advance.** If the academic integrity case (which you acknowledge is thin) does not yield clean before-after distributional data, what is the publication bar? Is the piece publishable with two cases? Must the third case remain as a mechanism hypothesis with no empirical grounding, or is that unacceptable? Decide this before drafting, so you are not forced mid-research to choose between abandoning a case and overselling preliminary evidence.
