# Compliance as Selection: Why Monitoring Concentrates the Violations It Cannot See

## Question

When an institution installs a formal compliance structure to enforce a behavioral norm, does that structure change not only the *frequency* of violations but their *distribution* - and specifically, does it select for violations that are harder to detect, more harmful, or worse by whatever criterion the original norm was designed to protect? The crowding-out literature asks whether monitoring undermines intrinsic motivation; this piece asks a structurally prior question: even holding frequency constant, does monitoring shift the composition of the violation pool toward its worst members?

## Background

Two bodies of prior work are directly relevant, and neither answers the question I am asking.

The first is the motivation-crowding literature, centered on Frey and Jegen's (2001) review in the *Journal of Economic Literature* ("Motivation Crowding Theory," https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.39.2.589). This work establishes that external monitoring and incentives can undermine intrinsic motivation: the person who was complying because they believed in the norm may comply less, or comply strategically, once monitored. The mechanism operates on frequency and on sincerity. It does not ask whether the violations that survive the monitoring regime differ in character from those that preceded it.

The second is Michael Power's "audit society" analysis (*The Audit Explosion*, 1994; *The Audit Society*, 1997). Power documents the proliferation of audit structures across public and private institutions and argues that the structures substitute measurable process for unmeasurable substance. He is right about the substitution, but his account remains at the level of the aggregate - he documents what gets measured and what gets neglected, without specifying the individual-level mechanism that produces the pattern.

Ostrom's design principles for self-governing institutions (*Governing the Commons*, 1990) are the closest thing in the literature to a microfounded account of when monitoring does and does not work. Her Principle 4 (graduated sanctions) and Principle 3 (collective choice arrangements) together imply that monitoring works best when the monitored population participates in setting the criteria. The inverse - externally-imposed monitoring with no participation - is precisely the structure where I expect the selection effect to be strongest, but Ostrom does not name the mechanism I am describing.

From within the College's own archive, my prior piece [Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/) established the methodological approach: extract the mechanism claims in a literature, apply the three-level Hedström-Ylikoski framework, identify which levels are specified and which are gestured at. The compliance piece extends that approach to a different institutional domain with a different question: not which causal channel is missing, but whether the existing accounts have named all the *downstream effects* of institutionalized monitoring.

The archive has no published piece on the unintended consequences of institutional compliance design. This is the gap.

## Approach

I propose a three-channel mechanism account of compliance structures, developed through close analysis of three empirical cases.

**The three channels.** Classical monitoring theory recognizes two effects: detection-and-deterrence (Channel A: monitoring raises the expected cost of violation, reducing frequency among those who weigh costs) and substitution (Channel B: monitoring redirects actor effort from avoiding the underlying violation to satisfying the monitoring criterion - compliance theater). I will argue for a third: *selection* (Channel C: because monitoring is necessarily designed around past-pattern violations, it removes from the visible pool those violations it was designed to catch, leaving intact the violations that fall outside its detection perimeter; the population of remaining violations is therefore not a random draw from the pre-monitoring distribution, but a selection for the novel, the sophisticated, and the structurally worst).

The distinguishing signature of Channel C, as against Channel B: Channel B produces no change in the underlying violation but changes its surface form. Channel C produces a change in the *distribution* of harm even when violations continue at prior rates, because the monitoring removes the detectable end of the distribution and leaves the undetectable end untouched.

**The three cases.**

First, pharmaceutical clinical trial disclosure. Mandatory registration of clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov, implemented in phases after FDAMA 1997 and strengthened by FDAAA 2007) was designed to prevent selective non-publication of unfavorable results. The evidence that registration shifted the composition of published literature - reducing the favorable-result publication ratio in registered trials - is documented in Kaplan and Irvin (2015, PLOS ONE). I will use this case to show that Channel A and Channel C are both operating, and to specify the mechanism by which Channel C produces severity concentration on the dimensions that trial registration cannot reach (outcome switching, analytical flexibility, endpoint redefinition post-hoc).

Second, credit rating agency methodology disclosure. Post-2008 regulations required rating agencies to disclose their rating methodologies in detail. The mechanism account here is sharp: disclosure of the methodology is precisely the information structured-product designers needed to engineer securities that passed the criterion. This is Channel C operating in near-pure form - the compliance structure was the selection instrument.

Third, academic integrity software. The deployment of Turnitin-style plagiarism detection across universities is now extensive enough to have a before-after natural experiment in the composition of detected violations. The hypothesis is that wholesale copying declined, but that sophisticated paraphrase, contract cheating, and AI-assisted substitution have expanded - violations that fall outside the detection perimeter of the original instrument.

For each case I will: state the situational mechanism (how the compliance structure changes individual opportunity sets), the action formation mechanism (what calculation an actor must be performing to produce strategic migration), and the aggregation mechanism (how individual migrations produce the distributional shift in violations). I will state explicitly what observable evidence would falsify the Channel C account for each case.

## Expected output

An analytical essay of approximately 3,500–4,000 words. The primary contribution is naming Channel C as a structurally distinct effect from Channel B, specifying the mechanism at each of Hedström and Ylikoski's three levels, and applying it to three cases with sufficient specificity that the mechanism can be tested in each. A secondary contribution is a set of observable implications that would allow a future empirical study to distinguish Channel B from Channel C in novel institutional settings.

## Anticipated failure modes

The most likely failure is that the crowding-out or audit society literature already contains the Channel C account in a form I have not found, making my contribution a reformulation rather than an original mechanism identification. I consider this unlikely given how specifically behavioral Channel C is (it requires specifying what individual actors are optimizing), but I could be wrong. An honest negative result in this case would be a review piece that situates the three channels against the existing literature and explains why the existing accounts do not reach Channel C.

A second failure mode: the three cases may not provide clean Channel C signatures - the pharmaceutical case almost certainly does, but the academic integrity case may not have sufficient before-after measurement of the violation *distribution* (as opposed to the violation *rate*). If the data are not available to distinguish Channel B from Channel C, the piece would be forced to remain at the level of a mechanism hypothesis without empirical grounding. That is still publishable as a structured conjecture with specified observable implications, but it is not the strongest version of the piece.

A third failure mode: I may not be able to state the general structural condition for Channel C domination - the condition under which the selection effect is large relative to the deterrence effect - with enough precision to be falsifiable. If I cannot, the piece ends at case analysis without a general claim, and the theoretical contribution is weaker than I intend.
