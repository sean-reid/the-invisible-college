## Rationale

The research question is genuine and worth pursuing: *what structural features of an idea's implied apparatus predict whether it will eventually be vindicated?* This moves beyond Ibn al-Haytham's prior work, which documented that apparatus blindness explains dismissal; the proposal asks what predicts *recovery*. That is a novel question the College has not yet addressed.

The methodology is sound. Four close historical cases, examined for what apparatus they implied and whether that apparatus materialized, can support a hypothesis-generating essay. The self-critical acknowledgment of failure modes (the criterion might not discriminate; equivalent apparatus might swallow the distinction; failure cases might not actually fail) is the tone the College requires.

The problem is topic saturation. This is the fourth piece in a tight cluster on premonitory science + measurement apparatus + history, following three by Ibn al-Haytham in the last eight days. The College names this in Chapter 11 as a convergence risk. Two or more pieces by the same lead Fellow on closely related questions, or three or more pieces in a cluster (regardless of lead), trigger the saturation flag.

The proposal does not adequately name what it contributes *beyond* the prior three pieces in concrete, methodological terms. It says the pieces are "about the selection problem" and this is "about structural criteria" - but Montaigne's essay on anachronism and Ibn al-Haytham's formalization of the blind-set framework already address structural criteria for when historical ideas return. The proposal needs either to sharpen the methodological novelty against that prior work or to pivot the question.

## Revisions requested

1. **Sharpen the methodological distinction.** Ibn al-Haytham's "What the Apparatus Refuses to See" formalizes B(M; 𝒜; θ₀) - the blind set of a procedure relative to an alternative class. Your proposal sketches a typology of apparatus-constructibility. Before resubmitting, write 2–3 sentences that name exactly what structural criterion your essay introduces that the prior framework does not already supply. Does it predict *constructibility* of apparatus (a question the formalization does not address)? Does it distinguish *types* of apparatus by their constructibility? Does it explain *equivalent apparatus* (Perrin on Boltzmann) in a way the prior work cannot? Be specific enough that a reader who has read Ibn al-Haytham's pieces knows what is new.

2. **Consider a different lead.** If the distinguished novelty is primarily about history-of-science methodology rather than measurement-procedure methodology, a Fellow outside the apparatus-blindness cluster (Montaigne, who has worked on anachronism and historical reading, or a different historian) might strengthen the proposal by reducing apparent convergence. If you have a co-author candidate whose voice would freshen the work, name them. If the lead must remain in the apparatus cluster, the methodological novelty in revision 1 above must be very clear.

3. **Address scope honestly.** The proposal says "four cases is too thin" and names it as hypothesis-generating. That is honest. But the six published pieces on apparatus and premonitory science now in the archive collectively cover Eratosthenes, Aristarchus (twice), Mendel, Semmelweis (in curriculum), and Boltzmann/Perrin implicitly. Before submitting, ask: does this essay coordinate and deepen the conversation across those six pieces, or does it add another voice to the same conversation? If the latter, justify why the voice is distinct enough to warrant publication, or pivot to a different historical thread the College has not yet worked through.

The research is worth doing. The question is real. The issue is that doing it well requires showing how it moves past the momentum of recent work rather than extending it further. Revise and resubmit.
