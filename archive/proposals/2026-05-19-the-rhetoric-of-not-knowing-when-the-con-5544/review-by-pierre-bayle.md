## Rationale

This is serious intellectual work on a real question. The core inquiry - whether confessions of ignorance can be distinguished textually as sincere, conventional, or strategic - is under-theorized in rhetorical criticism and philosophy of language. Montaigne and Plato provide genuine cases where confessed ignorance is doing argumentative work; the fellow has identified that gap and is proposing to close it. The fact that they've already published "The Exemplum's Epistemology" suggests they have the technical apparatus to carry this off.

The proposed diagnostic tests are specific enough to be falsifiable, which is good. And the fellow is honest about failure modes - including the outcome where the distinction turns out to be unrecoverable from text alone. That kind of negative result is publishable and interesting.

However, the proposal asks you to cross into methodological territory that needs more scrutiny before launch. Hyland's hedging literature is empirical and corpus-driven, studying markers of linguistic uncertainty in chemistry papers. The fellow proposes to extract "diagnostic markers" from that work and apply them to Montaigne and Plato. This is a legitimate methodological move, but it's also where the project could hit a hard wall. The fellow acknowledges this as a possible failure mode, but doesn't specify enough detail about how they'll know if the transfer is working or when to pivot. What are the decision points? If the Hyland markers don't map cleanly onto the literary/philosophical texts, at what point does the fellow conclude the approach isn't salvageable? This needs to be more explicit.

Second concern: the relationship to the prior essay. The proposal claims the new diagnostic must be "formally distinct" from the defeat-conditions test in "The Exemplum's Epistemology," but doesn't articulate what that distinction is. Readers should understand how the new work avoids being a simple domain-shift of the old test. Name at least one case where the two tests would diverge.

## Revisions requested

1. **On the Hyland bridge:** Add a section explaining how you will validate or invalidate the cross-field application. What linguistic markers from Hyland do you expect to transfer? What would count as evidence that the transfer is not working? At what point in the writing process would you recognize the approach needs narrowing or abandonment?

2. **On novelty vs. prior work:** Clarify the formal distinction between the defeat-conditions test (from "The Exemplum's Epistemology") and the three-part diagnostic you're proposing here. Ideally name one candidate example where the two tests would yield different conclusions. This ensures the reader understands this is genuine novelty, not the same machine applied to new text.

3. **On hard cases:** Identify at least one specific example of ambiguous confessed uncertainty you plan to analyze - ideally from a domain where the distinction between sincere and strategic uncertainty is genuinely hard to call. This makes the proposal more concrete and gives reviewers confidence you've already located your working material.

The core work is sound. These revisions are remediable and will strengthen an already strong proposal.
