## Rationale

This is a genuinely interesting question, and the proposal shows real philosophical sophistication. The author correctly identifies that the example — a fundamental tool in essay writing — has an ambiguous epistemic status: it persuades, but does it prove? The background work is solid, the textual choices are sensible (Montaigne through Baldwin through contemporary essayists represents real range), and the author is admirably clear about what might go wrong. The work fits the College's mission: it is intellectually substantive, makes a non-obvious contribution, and could teach a thoughtful reader something they did not previously understand.

The proposal has three specific problems, all conceptual, none fatal. First, it distinguishes "genuine evidence" from "rhetorical effect" without defining what makes something count as genuine evidence in an essay context. The typology turns on this distinction — a constraining example is one that carries evidential weight, a loading example is one that does rhetorical work without evidential force — but the proposal never says what framework is being used to separate them. Is evidence defined by logical necessity? By robustness to counterexample? By something else? Before the close readings begin, this should be explicit. Otherwise you won't know what you are looking for in the texts.

Second, the proposal commits to examining "whether the essayists I read are aware of which kind they are using." Inferring authorial intentions from text alone is speculative and difficult to verify. You can infer that Montaigne used a constraining example or a loading example, but inferring that he *knew* he was doing so is another matter. Either ground this in authorial statements (interviews, prefaces, self-commentary within the essays) or drop the awareness claim and focus on whether the text's *structure* signals control. The revised phrasing might be: "whether the best essayists signal awareness of this distinction through textual markers."

Third, the typology itself (constraining, illustrative, loading) may not survive contact with actual examples, as the author acknowledges. But the proposal is unclear about what you will do if the categories break down under close reading. The author's fallback is to "complicate or abandon the typology rather than defend it," which is honest. But before committing 20+ hours to close readings, it might be wise to have a Fellow with training in analytic philosophy (the proposal mentions looking for this collaborator anyway) stress-test the framework: Are these categories real divisions in kind, or are you imposing them? What makes constraining and loading examples fundamentally different? This conversation should happen before the essay drafting starts, not after.

## Revisions requested

1. Before drafting, define your epistemological framework. What counts as "genuine evidence" in an essay? What makes a constraining example count as evidence and a loading example not? This is the conceptual foundation of your typology; it should be explicit.

2. Revise the claim about authorial awareness. Either (a) ground it in authorial statements (interviews, prefaces, self-commentary), or (b) reframe it as examining whether the text's structure signals awareness and control over the distinction, or (c) drop the authorial-intention claim and focus solely on the function of the examples in the text.

3. Before you begin the close readings, recruit a philosophy Fellow to stress-test your typology. Have them review whether constraining, illustrative, and loading are real kinds or whether you are imposing categories on a spectrum. This will save you from building 20+ hours of analysis on a foundation that may not hold.
