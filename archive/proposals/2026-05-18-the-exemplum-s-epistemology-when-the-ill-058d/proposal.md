## Title

The Exemplum's Epistemology: When the Illustrative Example Proves Something, and When It Only Pretends To

## Question

Can a single well-chosen example serve as genuine evidence in an essay-length argument, or does its persuasive power derive from rhetorical rather than epistemic force — and if the latter, does that disqualify it, or merely describe it?

## Background

The example — the *exemplum* — is one of the oldest instruments in the writer's kit. Aristotle distinguishes it from the enthymeme in the *Rhetoric* (Book I, Chapter 2) as a mode of inductive rather than deductive persuasion: the example invites the reader to generalize from one case rather than deduce from premises. Montaigne knew this machinery and used it constantly: nearly every essay in the *Essais* turns on an anecdote lifted from Plutarch, a memory from his own experience, or a story about some Roman general who behaved well or badly under pressure. The example is the unit of currency in the essayistic tradition.

But examples are epistemically treacherous. Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's work on the availability heuristic (Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D., "Availability: A Heuristic for Judging Frequency and Probability," *Cognitive Psychology* 5, 1973, pp. 207–232) shows that vivid, specific, emotionally resonant cases are systematically overweighted in probability judgments — they *feel* more evidential than they are. A single airplane crash makes air travel feel more dangerous; a single brilliant dropout makes college feel unnecessary. The essay exploits this mechanism. It does not merely exploit it by accident: the skilled essayist selects the example that will resonate, that will make the reader feel the force of the claim before the argument has technically been made.

Anthony Grafton's *The Footnote: A Curious History* (Harvard University Press, 1997) and Ann Blair's *Too Much to Know* (Yale University Press, 2010) trace the commonplace tradition — the systematic collection of examples, anecdotes, and *sententiae* for deployment in arguments. In that tradition, the example was interchangeable: any one of fifty anecdotes about Roman virtue would serve equally well to illustrate Roman virtue, which suggests that the example's function was ornamental or mnemonic, not evidential. Yet Montaigne's examples do not feel interchangeable. The specific Gascon peasant he describes in "To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die," the particular form of his father's paralysis, the precise quality of his own memory's failures — these feel load-bearing. The question is whether that feeling tracks something real.

More recent essayists have worked this ground without resolving it. Joan Didion's *The White Album* (1979) opens with the claim that "we tell ourselves stories in order to live," then proceeds by accumulated example — vignettes of California in 1968–1971 — that are intended to make the claim feel inevitable rather than demonstrated. James Baldwin in *The Fire Next Time* (1963) uses his own adolescence as extended example for claims about the psychology of American racism. Neither writer pretends to have proven anything statistically. Both succeed in making their readers believe them. The epistemological status of that success is unexamined.

## Approach

I will conduct close readings of four or five essayists chosen to represent range across time and register: Montaigne (specifically "On Experience," "To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die," and "On the Inconstancy of Our Actions"), William Hazlitt ("On the Pleasure of Hating"), James Baldwin (*The Fire Next Time*), Joan Didion (*The White Album*), and one contemporary writer — likely Marilynne Robinson or Zadie Smith — to test whether the pattern holds in recent prose.

For each, I will map the structural relationship between examples deployed and claims they are meant to support. The mapping produces a working typology: (1) *constraining examples*, where a specific counterexample would genuinely defeat the claim, meaning the example has real evidential weight; (2) *illustrative examples*, interchangeable with many others, functioning as mnemonic or rhetorical amplification; (3) *loading examples*, where the argumentative work is done by the example's emotional or narrative content rather than its logical relation to the claim.

I will then examine whether the essayists I read are aware of which kind they are using, and whether the best essayists signal this distinction in some way — marking loaded examples as such, or using constraining examples at the moments when the argument most needs genuine support.

The essay will not merely describe this typology. It will enact a position on it — arguing either that loading is a legitimate move the essay form licenses, or that it is a form of epistemic bad faith that careful essayists should resist, or something more qualified than either. The examples I use in making my own argument will be chosen with the typology in mind.

## Expected output

A long-form essay of approximately 3,500 to 4,500 words. The essay will be self-exemplifying in the sense that its own use of examples will demonstrate whatever it argues about the proper use of examples — or, if the argument yields that exemplary self-demonstration is itself a rhetorical trick, the essay will say so plainly and bear the awkwardness. The piece is suitable for the blog's audience: a thoughtful general reader willing to spend thirty minutes with a question they have not consciously formulated before but will recognize once it is posed.

Total elapsed time: eight to twelve days of intermittent work, well within a two-week window.

## Anticipated failure modes

The typology I propose may prove too clean. Real examples, read carefully, may not sort into categories — a single anecdote in a single essay may function as constraining evidence from one angle and emotional loading from another simultaneously. If so, the essay may need to complicate or abandon the typology rather than defend it. That is an acceptable outcome, provided the complication is substantive rather than a retreat from precision.

A second failure mode: the essay becomes tediously recursive — writing about examples using examples to a degree that collapses into self-parody. The solution is to write the essay and then check whether the recursion is illuminating or merely clever, and cut what is merely clever.

A third: I may find that the epistemic/rhetorical distinction does not hold up philosophically — that once you accept that essays are not arguments in the formal sense, the question of whether examples are "really" evidential dissolves rather than resolves. If that is where the evidence leads, I will say so. A negative result here is publishable: demonstrating that the distinction is confused is itself a contribution.
