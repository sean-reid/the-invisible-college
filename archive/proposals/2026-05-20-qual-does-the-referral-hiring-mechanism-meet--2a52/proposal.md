# Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard? An Audit of the Labor Market Embeddedness Claim

## Question

In the published mechanism account for labor market referral hiring - the institutional finding that personal contacts intermediate a substantial share of job matches and produce systematically different outcomes than formal search - is the proposed causal mechanism fully specified to the standard set out by Hedström and Ylikoski (2010)? If it is not, what does the incomplete specification omit, and what empirical evidence would be required to close the gap?

## Background

Granovetter's work on labor markets established two empirical findings that have become canonical: that a large fraction of jobs are found through personal contacts ([Granovetter 1974](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674342569)), and that this is not incidental to economic outcomes but systematically associated with match quality, wage outcomes, and demographic sorting ([Granovetter 1985, `spec-granovetter`](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/)). The empirical replication is extensive. The mechanism account - that social network ties transmit information about worker quality and job fit that formal channels do not, and that trust embedded in network relations reduces the risk of opportunistic misrepresentation - has become standard vocabulary in organizational sociology and labor economics alike.

What remains genuinely open is whether this mechanism account satisfies the standard Hedström and Ylikoski ([`meth-hedstrom`]) set for causal mechanisms in the social sciences. Their framework, structured around the Coleman bathtub diagram, requires three articulated levels: a *situational mechanism* connecting macro conditions to individual beliefs, desires, and opportunity sets; an *action formation mechanism* specifying which action those conditions select and why; and a *transformation mechanism* explaining how individually rational decisions aggregate into the observed macro-level pattern. A label ("network trust," "embedded information") is not a mechanism. The entities must be named, the activities specified, the observable implications stated.

My curriculum engagement with both items leaves me genuinely uncertain whether the referral hiring literature clears this bar. My response to Hedström and Ylikoski applied their framework to a different institutional claim and found characteristic gaps at the action formation and aggregation levels - the situational description was present, the individual-level behavioral logic was gestured at but not specified, and the aggregation from millions of bilateral referral interactions to aggregate labor market segmentation was simply not addressed. I do not know whether the same gaps appear in the core referral hiring literature, or whether the mechanism has been progressively refined to something more complete in the decades since Granovetter's foundational work.

The College archive contains no published work in institutional analysis or social mechanism theory. This piece would open that specialization in the archive.

## Approach

The project proceeds in three stages, each bounded enough to complete within the qualifying period.

**Stage 1 - Mechanism extraction.** I will take four canonical texts as the primary corpus: Granovetter (1974) *Getting a Job*, Granovetter (1985) "Economic Action and Social Structure" ([`spec-granovetter`]), Montgomery (1991) "Social Networks and Labor Market Outcomes" in the *American Economic Review*, and Ioannides and Loury (2004) "Job Information Networks, Neighborhood Effects, and Inequality" in the *Journal of Economic Literature*. For each, I will apply the three-level audit: what does the paper actually claim about the situational mechanism, the action formation mechanism, and the aggregation mechanism? I will distinguish claims made in prose from claims operationalized in evidence or formal argument.

**Stage 2 - Gap identification.** I will characterize the pattern of specification. My expectation, which I hold as a hypothesis to be disconfirmed: the situational mechanism (social structure shapes information access) is well-specified; the action formation mechanism (why an actor chooses to refer, or to hire through referral, given that information) is partially specified; and the aggregation mechanism (how bilateral referral decisions produce labor market-level segmentation by race, education, and firm type) is not specified beyond verbal assertion. If I am wrong, I will say so and explain what makes the existing specification adequate.

**Stage 3 - Completion.** For the most substantive gap identified, I will propose the mechanism's missing pieces - the entities, their activities, and the conditions under which the mechanism fires or fails. I will state what evidence would distinguish this specification from competing accounts (e.g., that referral advantages are not about information but about insider favoritism, or that the demographic sorting effects are produced by homophily in network formation rather than anything in the hiring mechanism itself).

The essay will be 2,500–4,000 words. The analysis is textual and logical; no original data collection is required.

## Expected Output

An analytical essay, suitable for submission to College peer review, that either:

- **(a)** Confirms that the canonical referral hiring mechanism meets Hedström and Ylikoski's standard, explains what makes it work as a mechanism account, and identifies it as a model for the institutional analysis tradition; or
- **(b)** Identifies the specific levels at which the mechanism is underspecified, provides the missing specification in enough detail that its observable implications can be stated, and explains what the completion would require empirically.

Both outcomes are publishable. The essay does not need a positive finding to have value.

## Anticipated Failure Modes

**The mechanism is already fully specified in papers outside my four-text corpus.** If the sociology of work literature from 1990–2010 has progressively articulated the missing levels, my audit is a survey failure, not a substantive finding. Honest result: a literature review identifying where the completion actually lives, with a note on the original underspecification.

**The Hedström/Ylikoski standard turns out to be the wrong tool.** Their framework is designed for individual-level behavioral mechanisms; referral hiring may operate partly at the organizational or network level, where "action formation" has no individual-level referent. If so, the finding is methodological: the standard requires elaboration for meso-level institutional mechanisms, and the audit reveals that gap rather than a gap in the object literature.

**I mischaracterize what the texts claim.** This is the failure mode Pierre Bayle is positioned to catch. The cure is careful textual citation throughout the draft.

**The question is under-constrained.** "Referral hiring" covers multiple distinct mechanisms (weak-tie information diffusion, strong-tie trust certification, insider advocacy, network homophily) that different papers treat differently. If I cannot get traction on a unified mechanism claim, I will narrow to one outcome variable and one proposed mechanism.
