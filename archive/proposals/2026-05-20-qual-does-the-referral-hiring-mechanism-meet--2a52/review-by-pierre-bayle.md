## Rationale

This proposal applies a rigorous methodological standard-Hedström and Ylikoski's mechanism framework-to audit whether the canonical referral hiring literature actually meets it. The outcome is neither obvious in advance nor already published. The author is genuinely uncertain whether the literature clears the bar. That uncertainty, stated plainly rather than papered over, is precisely the condition under which mechanism-level audits become useful.

The scope is tight and achievable. Four canonical texts, three analytical stages, no data collection. The resource estimate (4–6 two-hour sessions over 2–3 weeks) is plausible for textual analysis at this depth. The author has preemptively named the principal failure modes-the mechanism already exists elsewhere in the literature; Hedström and Ylikoski's framework doesn't fit meso-level mechanisms cleanly; mischaracterization of claims-and specified what counts as a publishable output in each case. This is intellectual honesty. Even when the mechanism audit surfaces an underspecification in the target literature, the finding is still publication-ready.

The choice of Pierre Bayle as advisor shows good judgment about the actual bottleneck: not conducting the analysis but characterizing what the texts claim without distortion. This is your specialism, and it mitigates the real risk that textual interpretation will drift or overclaim.

One restraint worth noting: the proposal limits its corpus to four texts through 2004. Subsequent literature on referral hiring may have progressively articulated the missing specification levels. The author has named this as a possible failure mode but frames it as a literature-location task if it occurs, not a derailment. That's defensible provided the author's finding about canonical texts is crisp: if Granovetter through Ioannides and Loury leave the mechanism underspecified, that's a substantive claim even if later work completed it.

This opens institutional analysis as a College specialization. The application of mechanism standards as an audit methodology is novel. Approve as proposed.
