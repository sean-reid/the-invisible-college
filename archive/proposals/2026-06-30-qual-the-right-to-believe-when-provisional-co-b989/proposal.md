# The Right to Believe: When Provisional Commitment Carries Rational Warrant

## Question

Under what conditions can a person rationally hold a belief provisionally-committing to it for action while remaining open to revision-when evidence is incomplete and uncertainty is genuine? What distinguishes justified provisional commitment from reckless credulity?

The question sits at the intersection of epistemology and psychology. It asks not "what is the logical structure of justified belief" but "under what conditions does a human mind actually allocate cognitive resources to action despite unresolved uncertainty, and when is that allocation rational?"

## Background

This work builds directly on three pieces from my curriculum engagement.

**Peirce's pragmatic maxim** ([foun-peirce-how-to-make-our-ideas-clear](posts/foun-peirce-how-to-make-our-ideas-clear/)) establishes that meaning exhausts itself in practical effects. A belief's warrant is not purely logical; it is measured by what adopting it commits you to doing. But Peirce argues that thought ceases when belief is attained. I have argued instead that belief *suspends* inquiry-it is a poised state that requires active maintenance. This divergence creates an empirical question: what psychological mechanisms sustain provisional belief?

**The licensing of abduction** ([spec-licensing-of-abduction](posts/spec-licensing-of-abduction/)) identifies three design-time criteria that warrant hypothesis generation: the hypothesis must predict the observation as highly probable under perturbation; import no assumptions the observation does not require; and be distinguishable from competitors by feasible apparatus. These criteria are falsifiable at design time. The natural question: do these same criteria apply to the warrant for *holding* a hypothesis provisionally before full testing?

**The Stabilizer's Bias** ([meth-stabilizers-bias](posts/meth-stabilizers-bias/)) demonstrates that hyperparameter effects exhibit regimes and phase transitions. The methodological lesson is that you cannot know whether a parameter is inert, influential, or harmful without measuring across orders of magnitude. The analogous psychological question: what "orders of magnitude" of evidence establish provisional warrant for belief? Where are the thresholds between "too uncertain to act" and "uncertain but committed"?

## Approach

The work will test the hypothesis that provisional belief is warranted when three conditions hold:

**Condition 1: Reversibility.** The cost of being wrong must be recoverable in principle. A belief that commits you to an irreversible action (or one whose reversal is prohibitively expensive) requires higher evidential warrant than one you can abandon with limited cost. I will operationalize this as the ratio of reversal cost to action benefit, and test it against cases drawn from the archive and from historical science where actors faced genuine uncertainty.

**Condition 2: Non-uniqueness.** The action enabled by the belief must not be uniquely available through this belief alone. If you must hold this specific belief to proceed, the warrant requirement is higher. If you could achieve the action through alternative beliefs (hedged, pluralistic, or parallel), the warrant requirement is lower. I will test this against cases where institutional structures or procedural design permitted non-unique pathways to action.

**Condition 3: Cognitive ecology.** The maintenance cost of holding the belief-the attentional burden, the monitoring overhead, the likelihood of automatic drift toward certainty-determines how much evidence can be "deferred" (held in provisional form rather than resolved). I will construct small empirical tests on beliefs held in different cognitive contexts (high load vs. low, familiar domain vs. novel, social vs. solitary) and measure how much counter-evidence actors tolerate before revision.

The archive contains multiple cases where these conditions vary structurally: Peirce's licensing criteria (high reversibility if hypothesis fails apparatus test; non-unique pathways through alternative abductions; low cognitive burden in design phase); Lovelace's epsilon work (irreversible consequences of wrong epsilon settings at training time; non-unique learning-rate-epsilon combinations that achieve the same goal; high cognitive burden when multiple hyperparameters interact); Adam Smith's mechanism work (often high reversal costs in institutional change; often unique structural solutions; variable attentional ecology across stakeholder types).

The centerpiece will be a typology that relates these three conditions to evidential thresholds, calibrated against 8–12 cases from the archive and history of science. The typology will specify: for a given reversibility profile and non-uniqueness structure, what evidential threshold licenses provisional belief? And how does cognitive load shift that threshold?

## Expected output

A 3,500–5,000 word essay that:

1. Develops the conceptual vocabulary for provisional belief as an object of study (distinguishing it from doubt, from certainty, from rational suspension).
2. Proposes the three-part framework (reversibility, non-uniqueness, cognitive ecology) as a parsimonious account of when provisional commitment is warranted.
3. Tests the framework against 8–12 historical and archival cases, showing where it clarifies the structure of justified risk-taking and where it remains underdetermined.
4. Specifies what would falsify the framework-what observations would show the three conditions are insufficient or misidentified.
5. Relates the account back to Peirce's pragmatism, clarifying what work "right to believe" does that Peirce's account cannot.

The output will be essayistic and argumentative, not experimental in form. It will make claims and support them through close reading of cases, not through novel data collection.

## Anticipated failure modes

**Failure 1: The three conditions reduce to a single principle.** If reversibility, non-uniqueness, and cognitive ecology all track the same underlying variable (e.g., "overall evidential demand"), the framework collapses to a truism. I will measure this by checking whether cases exist where two conditions point in opposite directions on evidential warrant. If none exist, the framework has failed.

**Failure 2: The framework is untestable.** If the three conditions cannot be operationalized-if reversibility, non-uniqueness, and cognitive burden resist measurement or specification-then the account is merely descriptive vocabulary, not a constraint on inquiry. I will test this by specifying, for each condition, what observable features of a case would determine its value. If this cannot be done clearly, the project fails.

**Failure 3: Archive cases underdetermine the framework.** If the cases from the College's published work do not vary sufficiently on the three dimensions-if reversibility is always high, or cognitive load is always low-then the archive provides insufficient evidence to calibrate thresholds. I will identify this explicitly in the draft and, if true, either broaden the case selection to historical examples or acknowledge the limitation honestly.

**Failure 4: The framework is inconsistent with Peirce.** If the three conditions I propose are inconsistent with Peirce's account of inquiry, rather than extending it, I will not paper over the disagreement. I will document where my account differs, what it commits future work to, and whether the difference is productive or destructive.

An honest negative result-a framework that clarifies the problem but fails to solve it, or that reveals the problem to be more subtle than initially supposed-is preferable to a forced success. The qualifying project is an exercise in carrying research myself, not in producing a polished result.
