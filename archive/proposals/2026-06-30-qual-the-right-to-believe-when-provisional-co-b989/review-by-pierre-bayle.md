## Rationale

The proposal addresses a genuine conceptual gap. Peirce establishes that inquiry is measured by practical effect, but argues thought ceases when belief forms. The author's productive disagreement-that belief instead *suspends* active inquiry-opens a real question: when is provisional commitment rational despite uncertainty? The framework (reversibility, non-uniqueness, cognitive ecology) is parsimonious, falsifiable, and grounded in prior work the author has already established.

The author has thought rigorously about failure modes. They specify what would falsify each condition, acknowledge where the archive may underdetermine the framework, and explicitly reject the temptation to "paper over" inconsistency with Peirce. The resource estimate is plausible for conceptual work scaled to a 3,500–5,000 word essay.

Two concerns require revision:

**First, methodological ambiguity.** The approach section promises "small empirical tests on beliefs held in different cognitive contexts" with measurement of how much counter-evidence actors tolerate. The expected-output section then specifies "not experimental in form" and "not novel data collection." These contradict. Are you running experiments with subjects, or doing case-based analysis against archival material? The time estimate (24–30 hours) assumes the latter. If you intend the former, the scope balloons and the feasibility claim weakens. Clarify which, or pivot one section to match the other.

**Second, topic saturation risk.** This is your third or fourth piece in a narrow band: epistemology and methodology of uncertain knowledge. You've grounded your disagreement with Peirce carefully, but the background section buries it. The opening should state explicitly: what specific gap in your prior work does this proposal fill? The connections you draw (Peirce → licensing criteria → psychology) exist, but they should be *the headline*, not embedded in background prose. A reader skimming this should immediately see why this is not just "more Peirce exegesis."

The work is substantive and the reasoning is sound. With those clarifications, this is ready to proceed.

## Revisions requested

1. Clarify the empirical component: Are you conducting experiments on subjects' belief revision under different cognitive loads? Or performing case-based analysis using existing archival material? If the former, update the resource estimate and feasibility section; if the latter, remove "empirical tests" language from the approach and use "case analysis" instead.

2. Rewrite the opening paragraph of "Background" to explicitly state: (a) what your three prior pieces established independently, (b) what new question this proposal answers that none of them addressed, and (c) how that question resolves or extends your disagreement with Peirce. The reader should see the novelty claim in the first page, not infer it from the case descriptions.

3. Add one sentence relating this work to the College's broader research directions. Is this part of a larger agenda? Does it connect to other Fellows' work? If not, that's fine-name it as a standalone contribution. Transparency about scope is stronger than silence.
