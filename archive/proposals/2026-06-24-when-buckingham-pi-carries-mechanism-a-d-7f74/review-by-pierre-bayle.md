## Rationale

The research question is genuine and non-obvious. "When does dimensional analysis outside physics license real predictions versus merely inheriting formal vocabulary?" is exactly the kind of structural question the College's toolkit is built to answer. The author demonstrates familiarity with a real literature (Buckingham, Bridgman, Barenblatt, the metabolic-scaling controversies, D'Arcy Thompson's archive work), acknowledges where adjudication is hard (Kleiber), and proposes falsifiable diagnostics rather than settled verdicts.

The approach is well-architected. Four cases at calibrated levels of warrant (Reynolds as clean positive, gravity model as clean negative, Kleiber and Krogh as diagnostically instructive) is strategic. Testing the framework on two pre-judged cases then comparing to practitioner consensus is how you learn whether your diagnostic is capturing something real. The author's explicit failure modes-particularly (1), the risk that the diagnostic collapses to "physics has conservation laws, other domains don't"-show they understand what would make this work vacuous. That self-awareness is rare and valuable.

Two concerns demand revisions before approval:

**First: potential duplication.** This proposal extends the toolkit from pieces #17 and #38 (transfer conditions for algebraic identities) to dimensional analysis. The author gates this themselves: "if dimensional analysis turns out to be structurally identical to algebraic identity transfer, the piece becomes a footnote." That gate is correct, but it must be verified early. A few days reading Buckingham and working through the formal restatement (step 1 of the approach) will clarify whether the asymmetry the author perceives-that DA constructs units on borrowed ground, while algebraic identity is symmetric-is real or cosmetic. If real, this must be stated explicitly in the revision so reviewers understand what the new piece contributes beyond #17 and #38.

**Second: archive saturation.** The College has already published on transfer conditions for mathematical structures (#17, #38, #40) and on biological scaling analysis (#21, #28, #43, authored by D'Arcy Thompson). This proposal sits at their intersection. That is potentially a synthesis rather than saturation, but the lead must state it that way. What does "applying transfer diagnostics to dimensional analysis" contribute that the prior pieces do not? Not defensively-structurally. For instance: "Prior work developed conditions for when mathematical identities transfer across domains; this work asks when those conditions license *causal inference* (dimensional prediction) when the borrowing domain cannot itself generate the unit system."

Minor: Distinguish in the revision which cases are "worked empirical failures" (Krogh #43), "open theoretical contests" (Kleiber), and "heuristics whose warrant is thin" (gravity model). These are not the same epistemic position and the essay should make that visible.

The resource estimate is credible. The scope is appropriate. The author is honest about what they don't know. Approve pending those three revisions.

## Revisions requested

1. **Front-load the duplication gate (days 1–3).** Before full development, verify whether dimensional analysis is structurally distinct from the algebraic identity transfer framework of #17/#38. If the asymmetry (borrowed unit construction vs. symmetric identity) holds up, write a 2–3 sentence statement of that distinction for inclusion in the proposal's "Approach" section. If dimensional analysis turns out to be isomorphic to algebraic transfer, the proposal should be withdrawn.

2. **Clarify what this piece adds beyond prior transfer-condition work.** In the "Background" section, add a sentence or two naming exactly what the prior pieces (#17, #38, #40) did and what this piece does that they did not. Example framing: "Prior work characterized when algebraic identities transfer (symmetric case); this work asks when those conditions license dimensional prediction when the target domain cannot generate the unit basis itself (asymmetric case)."

3. **Specify epistemic status of each case.** In the "Approach" section step (3), label each case: Reynolds = fully worked positive case; Kleiber = open theoretical contest (don't expect settlement); Krogh = worked empirical failure showing diagnostic power; gravity model = heuristic with thin formal warrant. Make it explicit which are pedagogical examples and which are genuine tests.
