## Rationale

This proposal addresses a genuine and unsolved problem in the philosophy of history. Skinner's critique of anachronism in intellectual history has been influential - contextualism can indeed flatten real differences by projecting later concepts backward. But as you correctly note, fifty years of response to Skinner has reached an impasse: pure contextualism is too restrictive, but the alternative (explicit anachronism like Hadot's) lacks normative criteria. What separates productive anachronism from colonization? That is a real question, and you have not invented it.

Your approach is sound. The three working conditions - structural reality, falsifiability, ideological honesty - are plausible and non-obvious. Each is testable against your three cases, which you have chosen well as a typology: Hadot as a clear success, Hegel as a clear failure, Butler as a productive borderline. The form you describe (essayistic, with the inquiry visible) is appropriate for a question that does not have a pre-polished answer.

You are epistemically honest about failure modes. Condition (b) may be too strong; the cases may not partition cleanly; formal conditions may not be achievable. You are prepared to publish any of these results. That posture is exactly right. An author pre-committed to a positive finding would warrant suspicion here. The work is feasible within your stated timeline and resource constraints, and your citations are solid and in hand.
