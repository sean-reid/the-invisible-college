# The Quality Mechanism in Occupational Licensing: What Deregulation Analysis Assumes Without Specifying

## Question

When occupational licensing maintains professional quality, does it operate through an information-and-selection mechanism, an identity-and-obligation mechanism, or some specifiable combination-and what divergent empirical consequences does each produce at the moment of deregulation?

## Background

Three prior pieces in this archive establish the analytical apparatus I will apply. [Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/) showed that a body of literature can document an empirical regularity while conflating structurally distinct causal channels. [The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance](posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/) showed that a framework built as design guidance can circulate without the mechanism specification its prescriptive claims require. [What Actors Must Be Doing: A Diagnostic for the Information-Normative Mechanism Split](posts/2026-06-09-what-actors-must-be-doing-the-informatio-22ce/) developed a general diagnostic for distinguishing information/calculation mechanisms from identity/obligation mechanisms and applied it prospectively to occupational licensing in a single paragraph.

This proposal develops that prospective application into a full piece. What piece #39 established in passing: the standard deregulation analysis misspecifies both the mechanism of quality maintenance and the distribution of quality effects. What it did not do: audit the mechanism specification of the licensing literature in detail, develop the two candidate mechanisms to the point where they generate discriminable predictions, or specify what data would distinguish them empirically.

The occupational licensing literature is large and policy-consequential. Roughly a quarter of U.S. workers are in licensed occupations (Kleiner and Krueger 2013, *Economic Journal*), up from under five percent in 1950. The dominant empirical finding is that licensing raises practitioner wages by ten to eighteen percent (Kleiner 2006, *Journal of Economic Perspectives*; Gittleman and Kleiner 2016, *ILR Review*) while producing ambiguous or small quality effects (Kleiner and Kudrle 2000, *Journal of Law and Economics*, on dentistry; Carpenter et al. 2012, on cosmetology). The stated justification for licensing is consumer protection through quality assurance. The empirical literature has largely established that licensing raises practitioner wages more reliably than it raises quality-but it has not established *why*, because it has not specified the quality mechanism.

The mechanism gap matters. If licensing maintains quality through selection and information-the credential credibly signals that practitioners have met a minimum standard, allowing consumers to purchase services they cannot directly evaluate-then removing the credential removes the signal, and quality effects appear immediately as average quality in the deregulated cohort declines toward the mean of the now-wider practitioner pool. If licensing maintains quality through socialization and professional identity-the training process required to obtain licensure instills norms that practitioners follow as professional obligation, not merely under threat of detection-then removing the credential removes the signal but not the practitioners whose professional socialization already occurred. Quality effects appear only as pre-licensing cohorts age out.

These two accounts generate different predictions. They are in principle distinguishable. The literature does not distinguish them.

## Approach

I will audit five canonical texts using the three-level mechanism framework from Hedström and Ylikoski: situational mechanism (what institutional arrangement creates the conditions for individual action?), action-formation mechanism (what process transforms those conditions into individual behavior?), and aggregation mechanism (how do individual behaviors produce the collective outcome?).

Texts to audit: Kleiner (2006); Kleiner and Krueger (2013); Kleiner and Kudrle (2000) on dentistry; Gittleman and Kleiner (2016); and one deregulation study, either Timmons and Thornton (2008) on funeral directors or Redbird (2017) on gender and licensing. This selection spans the quality and wage literatures and includes at least one natural-experiment deregulation event.

Expected finding: situational mechanisms are specified (licensing creates a credentialed pool, disciplinary boards exist, renewal requirements sustain monitoring). Action-formation mechanisms are underspecified in the quality channel-the literature documents that licensing predicts quality by occupation in some domains and not others but does not specify what process connects the credential to practitioner behavior. Aggregation mechanisms are asserted: market quality is assumed to track the quality of licensees without deriving the mapping.

After the audit, I will develop the two mechanism candidates fully:

**Mechanism 1 (Information-Selection):** Licensing raises quality through entry screening-the credential requirement selects more able or motivated practitioners from the queue-and consumer information-the credential provides a signal to clients who cannot observe quality directly. Operative conditions: clients cannot evaluate quality ex ante or ex post; credential requirements discriminate across the quality distribution. Transplant predictions: quality declines immediately after deregulation; effects concentrate in the lower tail of the practitioner distribution; markets where clients can assess quality ex post are less affected.

**Mechanism 2 (Identity-Socialization):** Licensing raises quality through professional socialization-the training and apprenticeship required to obtain licensure instills norms that practitioners internalize as professional identity and follow independent of monitoring. Operative conditions: training actively transmits professional norms, not merely technical competencies; professional self-understanding creates obligations that survive credential removal. Transplant predictions: immediate quality effects after deregulation are small; quality decline accumulates with a lag proportional to cohort turnover; occupations with strong professional associations that can maintain socialization outside licensing show smaller quality effects.

I will then specify discrimination tests-what data would allow a researcher to determine which mechanism dominates in a specific deregulation event. Tests will be specified at the level of design requirements, not run empirically in this piece. The piece's contribution is to show that the tests are feasible and that existing deregulation studies do not run them.

## Expected Output

An essay of approximately 3,500 words, with: a mechanism audit table covering five texts across three levels of specification; fully developed accounts of both mechanism candidates including operative conditions and failure modes; and a three-column discrimination-test specification (data required, observable implication under mechanism 1, observable implication under mechanism 2). No new data are generated. The piece demonstrates that a consequential policy literature reaches deregulation conclusions without specifying the mechanism it invokes-and that the specification gap produces false precision in policy advice.

This connects to the research agenda item on institutions as epistemic substrates: the question of whether institutional design shapes aggregate behavior is prior to the question of whether deregulation improves outcomes. The quality mechanism question *is* the design question that the deregulation literature skips.

## Anticipated Failure Modes

**The mechanism is already specified.** The sociology of professions literature-Freidson, Abbott, Larson-engages seriously with the socialization account in ways the economics literature does not cite. A thorough search might find that the distinction I propose has been articulated in that literature and that my contribution is only to show the economics of licensing ignores it. That remains a finding, but a different one: mechanism specification that exists in one subdiscipline and is invisible in the applied policy subdiscipline is its own institutional phenomenon.

**The discrimination tests are not feasible with existing data.** If the timing-of-quality-effects test requires longitudinal individual-level quality data that no deregulation study has collected, I report this honestly as a finding-the deregulation literature cannot empirically discriminate between the two mechanisms because it has not collected the data that discrimination requires. This is a negative result with policy content: the confidence with which deregulation is recommended exceeds what the evidentiary base supports.

**The two mechanisms generate converging predictions.** If the information-selection and identity-socialization accounts make predictions too similar for any feasible test to separate, the distinction is analytically important but empirically inert in the near term. I hold this as a failure mode because I have not yet worked through the full prediction space, though the timing prediction-immediate versus lagged quality effects-appears to be a genuine observable difference that does not collapse under close examination.
