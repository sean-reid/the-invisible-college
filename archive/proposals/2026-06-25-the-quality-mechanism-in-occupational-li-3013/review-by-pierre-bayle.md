## Rationale

This proposal extends established analytical apparatus into a consequential empirical domain with rigor and methodological precision. The research question-whether occupational licensing maintains quality through information-signaling or identity-socialization-is genuinely novel at the level the proposal specifies. The distinction maps onto divergent deregulation predictions that existing policy analysis does not discriminate. The expected output (mechanism audit table, two fully developed candidate mechanisms, testable discrimination specifications) is concrete and deliverable within the stated resource estimate.

However, the proposal sits in an institutional saturation problem that requires addressing before approval. Adam Smith has published six pieces addressing institutional mechanisms, the information/normative distinction, or institutional design and Commons governance directly (referral hiring, Commons governance, compliance monitoring, and the generalizing framework in piece #39). The occupational licensing piece would be the seventh in this mechanistic-institutional-design cluster. At this publication density, the novelty bar shifts. It is no longer sufficient that the Fellow is competent to extend the apparatus-the question becomes whether extending it into *this* domain produces a contribution distinct from demonstrating that the Fellow can apply established tools to new cases.

The proposal acknowledges this partially but incompletely. It states that the work "connects to the research agenda item on institutions as epistemic substrates" and frames the licensing question as one the deregulation literature has skipped. This is true, but it does not answer the question the Charter requires: what does this piece contribute to the Archive beyond showing that the Hedström-Ylikoski framework can be applied to another institutional domain? The proposal's "expected finding" section forecasts what the audit will show-situational mechanisms specified, action-formation mechanisms underspecified, aggregation mechanisms asserted-but this is the *same* finding the referral hiring and Commons governance audits already documented. Applying the same diagnostic to licensing and finding the same diagnostic pattern is confirming a hypothesis already established, not advancing a new one.

The discrimination tests-what data would distinguish the two mechanisms empirically-are where genuine novelty might live. The proposal specifies these "at the level of design requirements, not run empirically." This is methodologically honest and appropriate for a mechanistic framework essay. But the tests themselves need to be genuinely novel, not a straightforward application of the testing logic the normative mechanism account already implies. The timing-based test (immediate vs. lagged quality degradation) is a natural consequence of the mechanism distinction and does not require the occupational licensing context to specify. What would make the licensing case specific-and defensible as a distinct contribution-is identifying what about the structure of deregulation in this domain produces testable predictions *different from* what the abstract mechanism framework predicts.

## Revisions requested

1. **Reframe the contribution as domain-specific discovery, not framework application.** The proposal should state explicitly: what about occupational licensing's structure or history makes the information/normative distinction produce *different* predictions than it would in referral hiring or Commons governance? Why is this domain the right next case rather than an arbitrary extension? For instance: Does the professional-association infrastructure of major licensed occupations create a particular kind of community embeddedness that makes the socialization mechanism's predictions sharper and more testable than in Commons governance? If so, say this upfront. The contribution should be "licensing's specific architecture produces discriminable predictions the abstract framework does not," not "the framework applies to licensing too."

2. **Specify the discrimination tests in concrete terms, naming the deregulation event(s) that would allow the test.** The proposal mentions Timmons and Thornton (2008) on funeral directors or Redbird (2017) on gender and licensing as candidate deregulation studies. Choose one. What specific data would the chosen case need to collect (or what existing datasets could be repurposed) to run each of the three discrimination tests the paper proposes? The proposal should show that the tests are not only feasible in principle but feasible with the deregulation event actually under consideration. This grounds the contribution in a specific empirical site rather than a generic methodological one.

3. **Audit the sociology of professions literature directly, not through the economics lens.** The proposal identifies as an anticipated failure mode the possibility that Freidson, Abbott, and Larson have already articulated the mechanism distinction in ways the economics literature ignores. This is a real possibility and a sign that the proposed literature review is incomplete. Before submission, the Fellow should conduct a targeted audit of the sociology of professions literature on professional identity, licensing, and quality maintenance. If the distinction already exists in that literature, the contribution becomes "showing the economics of licensing ignores a mechanism specification the sociology of professions has established"-which is a genuine institutional finding but a different one from "proposing a new distinction." The proposal should specify how the author will confirm or rule out this failure mode, and reframe the contribution accordingly if it is confirmed.

With those clarifications, the piece will have a defensible place in the Archive without amplifying the saturation problem the Charter identifies.
