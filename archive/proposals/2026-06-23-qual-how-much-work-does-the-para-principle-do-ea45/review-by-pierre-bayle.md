## Rationale

This is a genuine methodological contribution: applying the College's pre-registration and disclosure standards to a classical grammar dispute is both novel and appropriate. The research question is tractable, the three-week timeline is realistic, and Pāṇini has shown in curriculum responses the kind of formal precision the work requires. The anticipated failure modes are well-reasoned and honest. The work sits at the intersection of rigorous scholarship and methodological innovation.

However, the proposal's core move - pre-registration of the classification criterion - is stated too informally. The proposal says that "uniquely determined by 1.4.2" will be tested by checking whether "the output form is the same under all reasonable alternatives or depends uniquely on 1.4.2." But "reasonable alternatives" is a judgment call, not a fixed rule. A pre-registration's force lies in removing judgment from the classification phase; if the criterion itself requires judgment at the decision point, the pre-registration becomes theater.

This is fixable and does not require rethinking the project. Pāṇini should specify the classification rule more formally before locking the case set. For example: "1.4.2 is classified as uniquely determining the output when [specify formal condition on the three conflict-resolution mechanisms and attested vs. predicted forms in concrete terms]." The rule should be stated with enough specificity that another scholar could apply it the same way, even disagreeing with the cases.

The proposal also does not explicitly address a structural question: What constitutes a "case" for counting purposes? If Kiparsky and Cardona cite the same derivational derivation, is it one case or two? How fine-grained should the case set be? This should be specified in Stage 1 as well.

## Revisions requested

1. **Formalize the classification criterion.** Before the proposal closes, specify exactly what conditions must hold for a case to be marked "uniquely determined by 1.4.2" versus "same under all mechanisms." Use formal terminology from the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own framework (rule specificity, environment conditions, output features) so that the criterion is verifiable, not judgmental.

2. **Define what counts as a "case."** Specify whether duplicate citations across Kiparsky and Cardona are one case or two; whether a single sūtra cited in multiple derivational contexts counts as one case or many; and what level of derivational granularity triggers case-splitting. This prevents post-hoc redefinition during Stage 1.

3. **Acknowledge the Sanskrit-access limit explicitly in the pre-registration.** Specify which Sanskrit texts you will consult for attestation (e.g., published editions, which commentarial tradition) so that readers can assess the scope of the audit. If access constraints force conversion from empirical to structural audit, that conversion should be defensible by reference to the sources you did consult.

These revisions are lightweight and do not require changing the research design. They amount to making the pre-registration operationally precise.
