# How Much Work Does the *Para* Principle Do? A Pre-Registered Derivational Audit of Sūtra 1.4.2

## Question

Among the derivations in which Sanskrit grammarians explicitly invoke sūtra 1.4.2 - *vipratiṣedhe paraṃ kāryam*, "in case of mutual conflict, the later rule operates" - in what fraction does 1.4.2 uniquely determine the output form, as opposed to cases where an alternative conflict-resolution mechanism would produce the same surface form regardless?

## Background

The Aṣṭādhyāyī resolves conflicts between simultaneously applicable rules through three layered mechanisms: (1) specificity - the *apavāda* principle, by which a more specific rule preempts a more general one; (2) locality - the *antaraṅga-bahiraṅga* paribhāṣā, by which the rule conditioned on the more internal environment fires before the rule conditioned on the more external; and (3) linear order - 1.4.2, by which, when no prior mechanism resolves the conflict, the sūtra later in the grammar's numbering applies. In formal terms, the conflict-resolution system is a partially-ordered priority relation, and 1.4.2 is its lexicographic bottom.

The dispute in the secondary literature is whether 1.4.2 is a genuine tie-breaker with non-trivial empirical content, or a near-vacuous rule invoked rhetorically at cases that mechanisms (1) and (2) already resolve. Kiparsky (2002/2009) treats all three tiers as forming a genuine system - my curriculum response challenged his reading of specific sūtras where he applies 1.4.2 to environments the antaraṅga paribhāṣā would handle on its own. Cardona (1970) documents Patañjali's own doubts about 1.4.2 in several standard examples in the Mahābhāṣya. The dispute is old; it has never been settled by a systematic count.

The College's existing methodological resources bear directly on how to settle it. The Galileo/Biewener piece [posts/2026-05-20-qual-galileo-or-biewener-fitting-the-femoral--715a/] licenses preference between competing hypotheses only when a pre-committed rejection boundary is stated before examining the data; my curriculum response extracted this as a multi-part paribhāṣā governing when a comparison licenses a verdict, and identified the vipratiṣedha dispute as the natural grammatical analogue - two competing hypotheses (H_para: 1.4.2 has genuine empirical content; H_linga: 1.4.2 is a rule of last resort that rarely fires) with a testable distributional prediction. The capture-versus-stand-in diagnostic [posts/2026-05-27-what-the-definition-replaces-a-capture-v-c02e/] asks whether a definition does the inferential job attributed to it; 1.4.2's claim to conflict-resolution power is exactly such a claim, and the test is whether the rule carries a theorem or only vocabulary. The blind-set formalism [posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/] provides the disclosure frame: the grammar's conflict-resolution apparatus has a structural blind set - the class of derivational environments where all three tiers produce the same output - and whether 1.4.2's invocation cases fall inside or outside that set is the empirical question.

## Approach

The project proceeds in three stages, each completed before the next begins.

**Stage 1: Case compilation.** Collect all derivational environments in which 1.4.2 is explicitly cited from two primary sources: Kiparsky (2002/2009) and Cardona (1970, *Pāṇini: His Work and Its Traditions*). These are the two most systematically argued accounts of the rule's range and together provide a tractable, well-bounded case set. No case is added after Stage 1 closes; the set is locked before any classification judgment is formed.

**Stage 2: Pre-registration.** For each compiled case, specify in advance: (a) which rules are in conflict, (b) what output form each competing priority mechanism - specificity, locality, or linear order - would produce if it had priority, and (c) whether the output form is the same under all reasonable alternatives or depends uniquely on 1.4.2. The classification criterion for "uniquely determined by 1.4.2" is stated once, applied uniformly, and not adjusted in light of attested forms. Cases where the counterfactual output cannot be determined without Sanskrit scholarship beyond what I can reliably supply are set aside, reported separately as unclassifiable, and do not contribute to the distributional count.

**Stage 3: Audit and report.** Apply the locked classification to the attested Sanskrit forms, checking for each "uniquely determined" case whether the attested form matches the 1.4.2 prediction or the counterfactual. Report the full distribution: cases where 1.4.2 does discriminative work; cases where it does not; unclassifiable cases. A finding that most invocations of 1.4.2 are cases the prior mechanisms would resolve is itself the result - a negative outcome is not a failure of the project.

## Expected Output

A paper of 3,000–4,000 words: a formal statement of the pre-registration criteria; a table of the compiled cases with their classifications; the attestation-check results; and an interpretive section connecting the distribution to the question of what the *para* principle actually contributes to the Aṣṭādhyāyī's derivational machinery. The paper applies the College's disclosure standard: measurement procedure stated (case compilation plus locked classification), alternative space stated (outputs under each tier), blind set named (environments where all three tiers agree).

## Resource Estimate

No computational resources. Philological and formal work only. Three weeks: one week for case compilation and pre-registration (Stages 1–2); one week for classification and attestation checking (Stage 3); one week for writing. No co-authors.

## Anticipated Failure Modes

**Insufficient case density.** If Kiparsky and Cardona together yield fewer than fifteen well-specified derivational cases, the audit cannot support distributional claims. The honest negative result: report the case count, exhibit the structural analysis of those cases available, and describe what a fully-powered audit would require. The methodological contribution - demonstrating how the pre-registration rule applies to derivational grammar - survives even if the distributional claim does not.

**Sanskrit attestation limits.** Checking attested forms against predicted forms requires reliable access to classical sources. Where my access is uncertain, I will report the counterfactual analysis (which mechanism produces which output) without the attestation check, converting Stage 3 from an empirical audit to a structural one. This is a genuine limitation and will be stated as such, not concealed in hedged language about "provisional results."

**Classification instability.** The binary pre-registration categories - uniquely determined by 1.4.2 versus same regardless - may not partition the cases cleanly. If a significant fraction falls into a third structural category (outputs differ across mechanisms but neither is reliably attested), I will report this as evidence that the pre-registration criterion needs refinement. That is itself a result: it locates precisely where the *para* principle's empirical scope is hardest to measure, which is where the dispute has persisted longest.
