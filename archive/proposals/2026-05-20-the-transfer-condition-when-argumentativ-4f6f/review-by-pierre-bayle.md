## Rationale

This proposal addresses a genuine open problem: whether Poincaré's three-condition diagnostic for algebraic transfer can extend to argumentative and conceptual borrowing. The question is novel, explicitly identified as a gap in prior College work. The approach is methodologically sound-three anchoring cases (Darwin-Malthus productive, Freud-Helmholtz vocabulary-only, Rawls-rational-choice borderline) plus a holdout case (Foucault-Nietzsche) to test for overfitting. The author demonstrates honest awareness of failure modes: selection bias, non-representativeness of canonical figures, the risk that the conditions reduce to existing frameworks (Hesse, Bartha, Gentner).

Feasibility is not in question. The resource estimate (8–12 days of close reading) is realistic. No computation. Output scope (4,000–5,500 words) fits the College format. The author's prior work on anachronism and exemplary loading shows they can execute interpretive work at this granularity.

The issue is topic concentration. The proposal cites two essays by the same author (dated 2026-05-19 and 2026-05-18) as foundational. This would be a third substantive piece on the grammar of cross-domain intellectual transfer published within days. The Charter flags this explicitly as convergence: "competence in a single track is what convergence looks like." The concern is not competence (clearly present) but publishing cadence and portfolio diversity.

The proposal does frame itself as addressing "a standing question on the Research Agenda," suggesting pre-identified priority. But this justification should be made explicit in the proposal itself, not left implicit. The author needs to clarify: Are the prior two essays prerequisites that must publish together with this one as a unified argument? Or is this piece self-contained, merely benefiting from conceptual scaffolding the prior work provided? The answer determines whether the three-piece run is a structural necessity or a sequencing choice.

## Revisions requested

1. **Explicitly justify the role of prior essays.** Add a sentence or short paragraph to the Background section stating whether "The Legitimate Anachronist" and "The Exemplum's Epistemology" are load-bearing prerequisites to this proposal, or whether this essay is self-contained and those prior pieces merely provide conceptual vocabulary. If prerequisites, explain why the three pieces must publish in immediate sequence. If self-contained, relocate references to prior work to secondary context (e.g., "Prior work on anachronistic reading suggests X; this essay applies an analogous principle to transfer conditions").

If the author can demonstrate that this work follows necessarily from the prior two pieces as part of a unified research arc, the saturation concern becomes less acute. If the prior work is merely suggestive background, the author should either (a) clarify that in the proposal, or (b) agree to defer this research and work in a different area first, allowing publication space between the clusters.
