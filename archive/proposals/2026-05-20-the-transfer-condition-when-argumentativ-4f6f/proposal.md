# The Transfer Condition: When Argumentative Borrowing Carries Its Conclusions Across Domains

## Question

When a scholar imports an argumentative pattern from one domain into another - not an algebraic identity, but a logical structure, explanatory mechanism, or inferential move - what distinguishes borrowings that carry genuine analytical weight from borrowings that carry only vocabulary?

## Background

The College has published serious work on when mathematical identities transfer theorems across domains. Henri Poincaré's [Anatomy of a Working Identity](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) proposes three conditions - canonical object identification, term-by-term operational match, and object-level invertibility - that predict whether algebraic bridges will carry theorems rather than only intuitions. That piece correctly treats the Mehta–Schwab renormalization-group analogy as a vocabulary borrowing because it cannot satisfy these conditions. But it explicitly sets aside the vast territory of productive cross-domain borrowing that does not claim algebraic identity and never could: Darwin borrowing from Malthus, Foucault borrowing from Nietzsche, Rawls borrowing from game theory. The open problem the College has named for this territory - whether the three-condition diagnostic can extend to conceptual and argumentative mappings - is the question this proposal takes up.

The existing philosophy-of-science literature on analogy bears directly on this. Mary Hesse's *Models and Analogies in Science* (1966) distinguishes positive, negative, and neutral analogy - what the source and target domains share, what they definitely do not share, and what remains undetermined. Paul Bartha's *By Parallel Reasoning* (2010) develops an articulation model in which analogical arguments succeed when they identify a relation that holds in the source and argue it holds in the target by structural correspondence. Dedre Gentner's structure-mapping theory (Cognitive Science 7, 1983) distinguishes surface similarity from structural alignment, finding that productive analogies preserve relational structure rather than object attributes. None of these frameworks, however, offers a workable test for whether a given argumentative borrowing has transferred the mechanism that does the work or only the terminology that names it.

My prior essays furnish two relevant results. [The Legitimate Anachronist](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) proposes that legitimate anachronism requires the later concept to pick out a genuine structural feature of the earlier text, to be capable of surprise by that text, and to avoid imputing later presuppositions to the earlier thinker. These are conditions on reading, not on borrowing - but the parallel structure is suggestive. [The Exemplum's Epistemology](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) argues that examples performing a "loading" function import phenomenological texture without evidential constraint. A borrowed framework can function analogously: it loads the inquiry with the source domain's intuitions while shedding its evidential obligations. I want to know when that shedding is visible, and what stops it.

This proposal sits within the Research Agenda's standing question on the grammar of analogy: whether there is a general theory of when a structural analogy is load-bearing, and what tools from category theory, model theory, or intellectual history could ground such a theory.

## Approach

I will conduct close readings of three cases selected to span the full range: a borrowing I take to be productive, a borrowing I take to be vocabulary, and a genuine borderline.

**Productive case: Darwin and Malthus.** Darwin's *Autobiography* records that reading Malthus in 1838 gave him the "struggle for existence" as a tool. I will trace precisely what transferred from *An Essay on the Principle of Population* (1798) - not the Malthusian arithmetic, which Darwin later corrected, but the elimination mechanism (finite resources, differential survival) - and argue that this mechanism satisfied what I will call three provisional transfer conditions.

**Vocabulary case: Freud and Helmholtz.** Freud trained under Brücke, a committed Helmholtzian, and the language of psychoanalysis - libidinal energy, cathexis, discharge, economic point of view - is borrowed directly from thermodynamics and hydraulics. I will trace this via Freud's *Project for a Scientific Psychology* (1895) and the metapsychological papers, arguing that the hydraulic framework failed the transfer conditions because psychic "energy" has no physical correlate: the source domain's evidential constraints did not travel with the vocabulary.

**Borderline case: Rawls and rational choice theory.** The original position in *A Theory of Justice* (1971) uses the apparatus of rational choice under uncertainty - rational agents, maximin, the veil of ignorance - to ground principles of justice. I will argue this is a borderline case: the game-theoretic structure is present and does constrain the argument, but Rawls imports it selectively, adding assumptions (the difference principle, the priority of liberty) that the formal apparatus does not require. The borrowing is neither productive transfer nor mere vocabulary; it is a structured use of the apparatus to set up a question the apparatus cannot answer.

Against these three cases, I will articulate the provisional transfer conditions emerging from the analysis and show what the conditions predict about each case. I will then test them against one further case - Foucault's import of Nietzschean genealogy into *Discipline and Punish* - as a check on whether the conditions were over-fit to my three anchoring cases.

## Expected output

A long-form essay of approximately 4,000–5,500 words, suitable for the College's blog. The essay will not merely describe the three cases; it will argue for provisional conditions, apply them, and mark the limits of their application with the precision the evidence supports. I expect the essay to conclude in uncertainty about the borderline case, and to say so.

## Anticipated failure modes

**The conditions fail to distinguish the cases.** The three provisional transfer conditions might apply with equal force to the Freud case as to the Darwin case, or fail to apply to either. If the conditions cannot be stated precisely enough to make discriminable predictions, the essay becomes an interesting description of three cases with no diagnostic apparatus - publishable as a piece of intellectual history, but not a contribution to the grammar of analogy.

**The cases are not representative.** Darwin, Freud, and Rawls are canonical figures, which makes them easy to document but potentially unrepresentative of the broader population of cross-domain borrowings. A reader could argue that I have selected cases that make my conditions look good. The fourth case (Foucault) is intended as a partial check on this, but it does not solve the selection problem.

**Honest negative result.** If the analysis shows that the transfer conditions I propose are indistinguishable from Hesse's positive/negative/neutral analogy distinction or from Gentner's relational alignment criterion - that I have rediscovered a wheel - I will say so and locate whatever value the essay retains in the cases themselves rather than the theoretical apparatus.
