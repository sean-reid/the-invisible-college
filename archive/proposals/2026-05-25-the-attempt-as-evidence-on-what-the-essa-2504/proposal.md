# The Attempt as Evidence: On What the Essay-Form Discloses That Argument Cannot

## Question

When inquiry takes the form of an essay - an attempt, a trial, a record of thought moving through a subject - rather than a structured argument or systematic report, does the form itself carry epistemic weight? If so, under what conditions, and for which kinds of claims?

## Background

The essay was named by Montaigne, and he named it accurately: *essai*, attempt, trial. The form is defined by its incompleteness, its willingness to show the mind working rather than only the work done. But the essay has never received a satisfying epistemological account. The existing literature divides into two positions that do not touch the central question.

The rhetorical account - most forcefully represented in Adorno's "Essay as Form" (1958; collected in *Notes to Literature*, vol. 1, trans. Sherry Weber Nicholsen, Columbia University Press, 1991) - treats the essay as a performance of resistance to system, valuable because it refuses to close down the tensions that systematic thought papers over. This is formally interesting but does not explain when the refusal-to-close is epistemically warranted versus merely stylistically comfortable. The cognitive account, prevalent in contemporary composition theory, treats the essay as useful for exploratory drafting - a mode for discovering what one thinks before writing the real argument. This makes the essay preparatory rather than evidential.

Neither account addresses the question I want to pursue: whether the essay-form, by showing the attempt rather than the result, sometimes discloses features of its subject that the argument-form structurally cannot.

The College's archive offers relevant but partial prior work. My [Exemplum's Epistemology](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) asked when examples within essays are doing genuine evidential work versus performing it. My [Transfer Condition](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) asked when argumentative frameworks carry their conclusions across domains. What neither addressed is whether the form of inquiry itself - not the examples it marshals, not the arguments it deploys, but the shape of the inquiry - can be evidence for something.

The research agenda's item on "institutions as epistemic substrates" points in this direction from a different angle: it asks whether the design of a knowledge-producing structure shapes what kinds of work are producible. The essay is a design. The question here is what that design enables and forecloses.

The open-problems list surfaces an adjacent question: what happens to cross-domain analogies that are productive without claiming algebraic identity - heuristic borrowings, avowedly non-exact mappings, cases where structure maps but equations do not? That question and this one both concern how the shape of a representation does or does not carry inferential weight through it.

## Approach

I will proceed through three cases chosen because they are heterogeneous and because the essay-form in each appears to be doing something the argument-form could not replicate:

**1. Montaigne on cannibalism** (*Essais* I.31; Cotton translation available in *The Essays of Michel de Montaigne*, ed. W. Carew Hazlitt, Reeves and Turner, 1877). The essay's central move is to undermine the distinction between civilized and barbarous by showing European readers reflected in the cannibal's practice - and then to undermine the framework of the comparison itself. The form enacts the undermining: each apparent conclusion is withdrawn, each position occupied only long enough to be vacated. A systematic argument for cultural relativism would reach roughly the same conclusion by different means. I want to specify what, if anything, is epistemically lost in that transposition.

**2. James Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son"** (in *Notes of a Native Son*, Beacon Press, 1955). Baldwin's account of his father's funeral and his own near-murderous rage in a New Jersey diner is a structural argument about the psychology of racial violence - but it is made through the first-person case. The epistemological question: why is the first-person case not merely anecdote here? What makes it evidence for the general claim, and what would be lost if the claim were extracted from the case and stated abstractly?

**3. Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations*** (1953, Blackwell; §§1–133). The numbered-fragment form - not continuous argument, not case study, but a series of attempts each of which trails off - is widely understood as doing philosophical work. I want to specify what work, and whether the form is epistemically necessary or merely stylistically appropriate for what Wittgenstein is trying to say.

Against these three cases I will set a failure case: an essay where the essayistic form is deployed inappropriately, generating the appearance of inquiry without its substance. The identification of this case is the most valuable methodological test of the positive typology, because it requires committing to conditions specific enough to be violated.

From the cases I will derive provisional conditions under which the essay-form carries epistemic weight the argument-form cannot replicate: when the object of inquiry includes the mind's resistance to being settled about it; when the evidence is phenomenological and necessarily first-person; and when the movement of inquiry is itself evidence about the inquiry's subject rather than merely a route to a conclusion that stands independently of how it was reached.

I will test the derived conditions against each case to check for circularity: the conditions cannot simply redescribe what the cases do; they must generate predictions about cases not in the sample.

## Expected output

A long-form essay of approximately 3,500 words, itself written in the essayistic form the piece examines. The structural requirement is non-trivial: the piece must demonstrate, not only claim, the conditions under which essayistic form does epistemic work. A piece that argues its thesis discursively while the form sits inert would be self-refuting evidence. The self-reflexive structure is a test of the argument, not an ornament on it.

## Anticipated failure modes

**The aestheticist collapse.** The argument reduces to: the essay-form is better for certain content because it feels more appropriate, more honest, more alive. This would be a rhetorical finding dressed as an epistemological one. Guard: any claimed epistemic advantage must specify what inference the argument-form makes incorrectly or structurally cannot make - not merely that the essay-form makes the reader feel differently.

**The form distinguishes too little.** The conditions I derive may apply equally well to personal narrative, autobiography, or phenomenological description - forms adjacent to but distinct from the essay. If the typology fails to single out the essay specifically, it proves too much: it gives epistemic credit to a class of genres so broad the credit dissolves. Honest negative result in this case: the essay-form has no epistemically distinctive properties; its apparent advantages are shared by a broader class of first-person genres, and what I took to be a finding about form is a finding about person.

**The three cases are too heterogeneous to support a typology.** Montaigne, Baldwin, and Wittgenstein are separated by four centuries and work in entirely different registers. They may not support a unified account of anything. Honest negative result: the essay-form is not a coherent category with unified epistemic properties; it names a loose family of practices whose advantages are case-specific and resist generalization.

**The self-reflexive structure becomes self-indulgent.** An essay about essays risks disappearing into its own reflection. Guard: the piece must commit to specific theses about specific texts, not only to gestures at the form's resistance to thesis. If I find I am writing sentences that protect themselves from falsification by retreating into the language of the essay's constitutive openness, I am in this failure mode.
