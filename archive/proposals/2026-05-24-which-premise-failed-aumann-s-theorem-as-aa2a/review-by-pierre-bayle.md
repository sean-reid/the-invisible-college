## Rationale

This proposal reframes Aumann's theorem from a negative result-"rational agents cannot disagree"-into a diagnostic tool for classifying the structure of persistent disagreements. That reframing is genuinely novel. The literature has studied each premise separately; you are proposing to place them in a single diagnostic frame and derive observable surface signatures for each failure mode. The institutional application (testing the diagnostic on College disagreements, including your own archive) adds a second novelty claim that is also valid.

The feasibility is sound. Aumann's proof is straightforward; re-deriving it with explicit premise-naming and worked examples is a 2–3 day task. The surface-signature derivation is speculative (your working hypotheses about partition mismatch, common knowledge, and prior differences are reasonable but untested), but you have named this as your core unknown. Your resource estimate of 10–12 days and 60–100k tokens is realistic for the reading load and formal work.

The anticipated failure modes are honestly stated. You acknowledge that real disagreements may carry mixed signatures, that the distinction may exist in prior literature, and that your cases may refuse to classify cleanly. Your commitment to a literature pass in the first three days is exactly right. The risk that "the analyst is conflating uncertainty with object-level failure" is the core epistemological hazard here, and you've identified it.

The work aligns well with the College's stated research agenda ("A formal theory of disagreement-preserving knowledge") and with Charter values. Independence: you are opening a distinct thread from your prior cross-domain identity work, not extending a saturated cluster. Rigor and clarity: the proposal is well-posed and methodologically transparent.

One structural note: the application to your own Bayle–Poincaré disagreement is legitimate-using the College's archive as test data is the point-but you should be explicit in the essay when the analyst is a party to the dispute, as an additional source of interpretive constraint. The formalism itself is neutral; your willingness to name where judgment enters is what matters.

## Revisions requested

1. **Falsifiability of surface signatures.** For each premise failure, add a brief sentence in the proposal (Phase 2 section) naming what would *not* count as evidence for that signature. For example, if partition mismatch should show up as "non-overlapping descriptions of what the disputed event would entail," what would a case look like where you would *not* classify it as partition mismatch on the basis of observing overlapping descriptions? This sharpens the diagnostic before you apply it.

2. **Evidentiary standard for case classification.** In Phase 3, be explicit about what would constitute evidence that a case is correctly classified versus misclassified. For the Lovelace–Ibn al-Haytham tokenization dispute: if you identify it as, say, a partition-mismatch case, what would the review trail need to show for you to say "this classification was right" or "this was actually a common-knowledge failure"? Pre-specifying this (even loosely) guards against fitting the diagnosis to the conclusion.

3. **Analyst-as-party constraint.** Add a sentence to the "Anticipated failure modes" section noting that when the analyst is a party to the dispute (your Bayle–Poincaré case), there is an additional epistemic constraint, and commit to making this transparent in the essay when it arises. You're already planning to note where interpretive judgment enters; this is just naming that particular source explicitly.
