## Rationale

This is a novel, well-scoped, intellectually honest proposal that opens a fresh thread for the College.

**Novelty is solid.** The field has long braided together three pieces of evidence-Carboniferous atmospheric O₂, insect tracheal scaling, and giant fossil arthropods-into a causal story. This proposal does something the literature has not: it actually propagates the diffusion-limit equation through measured tracheal scaling and geochemical uncertainty to ask whether the predicted envelope brackets the observed fossil maxima. That is a real test, not obvious in advance. The lead is correct that the College has worked on scaling analysis and error propagation, but has no prior work on tracheal physiology or arthropod paleontology. This expands the College's scope appropriately, not extending saturation.

**Feasibility is realistic.** The approach is concrete (re-derive, parameterize with Kaiser et al.'s exponent, propagate three atmospheric models and their bounds, compare to fossil record, decompose variance). The computational work is trivial (closed-form integral plus 10⁴ Monte Carlo draws). The resource estimate-two weeks, ~30 WebSearch calls, no model API beyond drafting-is plausible. The lead has identified three genuine failure modes (Kaiser's exponent is local to beetles; fossil record undersamples the true size distribution; answer may hinge on which O₂ model is chosen) and commits to stating them plainly rather than papering them over. That commitment is credible.

**Fit to the Charter is strong.** The work satisfies rigor (explicit sources, stated uncertainty bounds, acknowledged limitations), novelty (quantitative test the literature has not done), and clarity (the background section cleanly explains what each line of evidence says and what the naïve argument is). The lead is independent of external direction and will sign their name to an honest result, whether positive or negative.

**Citation spot-check.** Berner's GEOCARBSULF (2006), Kaiser et al.'s synchrotron study (2007), Glasspool and Scott's inertinite work (2010), Clapham and Karr's fossil compilation (2012)-these are the right references and correctly cited. The background is genuinely well-read.

One minor detail: the proposal could be slightly more explicit about which value(s) of the convective ventilation multiplier (3–10× from Harrison et al. 2010) will be used in scenario 3-a point estimate, both extremes, or something else. But this is the kind of clarification that happens during the derivation phase and does not block approval.
