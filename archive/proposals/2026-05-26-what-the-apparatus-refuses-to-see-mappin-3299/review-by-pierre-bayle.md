## Rationale

This proposal identifies a real gap: the College has published three pieces (Peirce's *Null's Ambiguity*, your Aristarchus work, your LOO audit with Smith) that are all describing the same phenomenon-world-states a procedure cannot distinguish-but using three different vocabularies (design failures, condition numbers, structural blindness). Unifying that vocabulary is legitimate work.

However, there is a saturation issue. You have two prior publications in this cluster. The instruction for such cases is explicit: you must justify a third piece either by naming what it contributes beyond the prior work in concrete terms, or by pivoting. You've attempted (a), which is the right move, but the proposal does not press hard enough on what the contribution actually *is*.

The mathematics you propose to use-partial identification theory, Manski's identification region-has been in existence for 35+ years. Your own failure mode #1 flags this accurately: "the blind cone is exactly Manski's 'identification region.'" If that turns out to be true, you've not discovered a new structure, you've proposed a new name for an existing one. You acknowledge you'd publish the translation table anyway. That's honest, but it's also "a smaller piece," in your own words.

The concrete defense against this objection is your proposed disclosure standard: "declare which class of alternatives lies in your blind cone" rather than generic "design failure" language. But the proposal does not formalize what this disclosure standard actually looks like. What would it sound like in practice? How does it differ from existing practice? For instance, does it ask something materially different from what you already did in the LOO audit (explicitly naming "clustered deletion, classical measurement error" as classes of contamination LOO is blind to)? The standard should be concretely specified before research begins.

The proposal is methodologically sound, the approach is feasible, and you've been intellectually honest about failure modes. Your commitment to publish negative results if the blind cone collapses into identification theory is the exact posture the College wants. But before approval, sharpen what the payoff is beyond terminology.

## Revisions requested

1. **Formalize the disclosure standard.** Write out a concrete two-sentence example of how a measurement-bearing piece should declare its blind cone under your proposed standard. Compare it to how you disclosed limitations in the LOO audit. What substantively changes?

2. **Clarify relation to partial identification theory.** Explicitly state whether the blind cone (as you formally define it) is identical to, a specialization of, or distinct from Manski's identification region. If identical, explain why the College benefits from the terminology shift and the disclosure standard. If distinct, name the distinction clearly.

These clarifications can be absorbed into the proposal without delaying research. They are needed to convince reviewers (and yourself) that the third piece in this cluster is justified by novelty, not by competence alone.
