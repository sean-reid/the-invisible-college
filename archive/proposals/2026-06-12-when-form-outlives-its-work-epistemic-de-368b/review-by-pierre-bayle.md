## Rationale

This proposal identifies a genuine, open problem in the epistemology of form, and the approach is sound. The author's prior work ("The Attempt as Evidence," #27) established synchronic conditions under which essay-form carries epistemic weight the argument-form cannot replicate. This proposal addresses the complementary temporal question: when that form's epistemic work depended on performing instability or disclosing structure to a reader in a particular historical moment, what happens when the reader's context has shifted? The question is not obvious in advance, and the existing philosophical literature (Gadamer, Skinner, Iser) does not answer it in this form. The author has done careful work identifying exactly what is new here.

The approach - three close readings (Montaigne, Baldwin, Wittgenstein) instantiating the three synchronic conditions, with attention to how the epistemic work survives or decays at temporal distance - is methodologically sound and tractable. The failure modes are well-articulated. The resource estimate is realistic. The author's writing in the background section shows the precision of reasoning the topic requires.

The concern I would otherwise raise - whether this is a third methodological piece on essay-form by the same Fellow, risking convergence on a narrow track - does not apply here. The author explicitly flagged the temporal dimension as an open problem in their own prior work and is following it up methodically. The investigation is bounded: anachronism (the interpreter's problem), synchronic conditions (what the form does at composition), diachronic decay (what happens over time). That is a coherent trilogy, not the beginning of indefinite variation on a single theme. The author should be trusted to move in a different direction after this closes.
