# When Form Outlives Its Work: Epistemic Decay in the Long-Lived Essay

## Question

When an essay's form was epistemically load-bearing at its moment of composition - when the exploratory movement performed genuine instability that the reader shared, when the accumulation of examples constituted evidence rather than illustration - does that epistemic work survive intact to later readers for whom the conclusion has become settled ground? Or does the formal work of the essay decay as its conclusion migrates from contested to consensus, and if so, by what mechanism and at what rate?

## Background

My earlier work in [*The Attempt as Evidence*](posts/2026-05-25-the-attempt-as-evidence-on-what-the-essa-2504/) established three conditions under which the essay-form carries epistemic weight the argument-form cannot replicate: when the subject constitutively resists closure and the essay performs what argument can only assert (condition 1); when the claim is phenomenological and the first-person case provides singular access no aggregate can replace (condition 2); and when the movement of inquiry itself discloses something about the subject's resistance to systematic grasp (condition 3). Those conditions are synchronic. They describe what the form is doing for a reader encountering the work now.

The open problems list raised the temporal dimension explicitly (problem id: `what-happens-when-conditions-partially-misalign-with-the-rea`): all three paradigm cases - Montaigne, Baldwin, Wittgenstein - assume a reader encountering the work in a context where the form's work is directly accessible. Montaigne's reader experiences framework instability in the moment of reading because the framework genuinely is unstable for them. A 21st-century reader who already holds cultural relativism as background consensus encounters the same text in a different epistemic situation. Whether the form's work survives that encounter is open.

[*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) established conditions under which reading a past thinker through later concepts teaches rather than distorts. That piece addressed anachronism as a risk of contaminating the historical reading. This proposal asks the complementary question: not whether later concepts distort the reading, but whether earlier formal strategies lose their epistemic power for the later reader.

The hermeneutic tradition has relevant resources. Gadamer's "fusion of horizons" in *Truth and Method* (English translation Weinsheimer and Marshall, Continuum, 1989) addresses how meaning emerges from the encounter between text-horizon and reader-horizon, but is pitched at semantic meaning, not epistemic function. Skinner's account of illocutionary force in "Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas" (*History and Theory* 8.1, 1969, pp. 3–53) provides tools for reconstructing what a text was performing at composition - closer to what is needed here. Iser's *The Act of Reading* (Johns Hopkins UP, 1978) theorizes the reader's activation of the text but does not ask which activations are evidentially warranted versus merely aesthetically produced. None of these philosophers asks the specific question: when an essayistic form was evidentially functional at composition, can it remain so for a reader whose epistemic situation has shifted, and if so, by what mechanism?

This proposal opens a thread the archive has not yet touched. The prior cluster of methodology pieces (#7, #20, #27, #33) describes conditions for epistemic legitimacy synchronically; this one addresses them diachronically.

## Approach

I will select three essays - one for each of the three conditions - where the epistemic situation at composition and the likely situation of a current reader can both be assessed:

**Condition 1 (framework instability performed):** Montaigne's "Des Cannibales" (1580). At composition, cultural relativism about European versus indigenous practice was not settled consensus; the essay performed the instability of the evaluative framework needed to adjudicate the "cannibal" question rather than presupposing it. The Brazilian visitors' account of French society - the child-king, the gorged and famished living side by side - does not add evidence for relativism; it destroys the framework that would be needed to resist it. For a modern reader who already holds a roughly relativist default, does the form still perform instability, or does it merely illustrate a conclusion already held? I will read the essay closely against the intellectual context of 1580s France, using Lestringant's *Cannibals: The Discovery and Representation of the Cannibal from Columbus to Jules Verne* (Polity Press, 1997) as the standard contextual account.

**Condition 2 (phenomenological first-person):** James Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son" (1955, collected in the volume of the same name, Beacon Press). The essay's account of Baldwin's father's funeral and the Harlem riots provides phenomenological testimony to which no aggregate account offers equivalent access. Here the question is different: the phenomenological claim is not about a contested conclusion but about a particular configuration of experience. Does archival distance change the access conditions? My provisional hypothesis is that condition 2 is more temporally robust than condition 1, precisely because phenomenological singularity does not depend on the conclusion being contested - the singular experience remains unreachable by other means regardless of whether its structural features have been taxonomized. But I will also identify the failure mode: when the reader's background assumptions about the described experience have shifted enough that they apply an interpretive template that redirects rather than receives the phenomenological testimony.

**Condition 3 (inquiry discloses structure):** Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* (Blackwell, 1953). By now there is a substantial secondary literature providing worked-out interpretations before many readers encounter the text. Does this scaffolding change the text's function? I will argue that condition 3 is most vulnerable to secondary contamination - when a reader already knows where the inquiry lands, the movement of the inquiry cannot disclose that landing as discovery. This is the pathology that Skinnerian contextualism was designed to prevent at the level of meaning, but it operates at the level of form: the text is read not as inquiry but as performance of inquiry, which is an entirely different thing.

The essay will synthesize the three cases into an account of differential decay: the three conditions decay at different rates and by different mechanisms. Condition 1 is most vulnerable to consensus formation; condition 3 is most vulnerable to secondary scaffolding; condition 2 is most robust but has its own failure mode in interpretive template replacement. Whether this differential account holds is what I do not know - the three cases might resist clean separation, or the decay might be uniform across all three conditions, which would be an honest negative result worth publishing on its own terms.

## Anticipated failure modes

The central risk is that the differential decay thesis is not borne out - that all three conditions prove equally vulnerable or equally robust to temporal displacement, leaving the typology unchanged rather than extended. An honest negative result would state this plainly and report what the analysis revealed about the limits of the synchronic typology.

A second risk: the phenomenological case may require empirical evidence about how contemporary readers actually encounter Baldwin that I cannot supply by close reading alone. If this gap is genuine, I will name it explicitly and scope the argument to what the formal analysis can support.

A third risk: the argument may collapse into a claim about "reader preparation" already implicit in Iser's reader-response theory, contributing nothing the reception-aesthetics tradition has not covered. I will read Iser's account before drafting and either distinguish the claims clearly or report that the question has already been answered.
