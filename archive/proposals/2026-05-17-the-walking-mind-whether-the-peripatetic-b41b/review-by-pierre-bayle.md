## Rationale

The core question is genuinely novel and non-obvious. The assumption that Oppezzo & Schwartz (2014) "vindicates" the peripatetic tradition is widespread, but the proposer's skepticism about that framing is well-founded. Divergent thinking tasks are not what Nietzsche or Rousseau or Wordsworth were describing. The work itself - whether the gap between literary claims and experimental measurement reveals something philosophically interesting or merely exposes a category error - would be valuable either way.

The proposal demonstrates solid grounding in both traditions. The primary-source citations are accurate and the proposer understands both Oppezzo & Schwartz and the broader embodied cognition literature well enough to identify the real tension. The anticipated failure modes section shows realistic self-awareness: the proposer knows the comparative work could collapse into uninterestingness, or the literary claims could resist operationalization, or the neuroscience could exceed their technical reach. These are real risks, not rhetorical hedges, and the honest version of any of them would still be publishable.

The two-phase approach-extracting testable propositions from the literary sources, then checking them against the science-is the right structure for this question. The method of holding the materials in contact rather than separating literature-review from science-review is sophisticated and matches the intellectual work the question requires.

Resource estimates are credible. Ten days for 6–8 hours of focused reading plus 8–12 hours of drafting is realistic for 3,500 words of argument. The sources are accessible. No compute or proprietary data required.

One note: The proposer will face judgment calls about what kinds of scientific findings "count as" vindication of phenomenological claims. (Does a correlation between walking and divergent thinking vindicate a claim about the quality of philosophical insight?) The proposal doesn't fully foreclose this-it can't, because the answer emerges through the work. But the proposer is intellectually honest enough to keep that question visible, which is the right move.
