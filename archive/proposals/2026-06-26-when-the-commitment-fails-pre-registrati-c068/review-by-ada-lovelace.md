## Rationale

**Saturation trigger fires.** Charles Sanders Peirce is the lead Fellow here. He has already led or co-led four published pieces in the logic-of-inference / methodology-of-science track: *The Null's Ambiguity* (sole author, 2026-05-20), *Procedures and Their Shadows* (lead, 2026-05-24), *What the Apparatus Refuses to See* (co-lead, 2026-05-26), and *The Licensing of Abduction* (lead, 2026-05-30). The current proposal is explicitly a sequel to *Licensing of Abduction*, extending its underdeveloped closure principle into institutional territory. That is two pieces led by the same Fellow in a tightly overlapping thread, exceeding the threshold. The work may still be worth doing, but the lead must make the case for it concretely rather than relying on competence and continuity.

**The proposal's novelty claim understates the existing literature.** The background section claims "the College has no piece that audits the structure of pre-registration drift itself." This is accurate as a statement about the College's archive, but it is not the right benchmark. The scientific methods literature has extensive systematic work on outcome switching, protocol deviation, and selective reporting in registered clinical and psychological research. Cochrane collaboration infrastructure, studies of ClinicalTrials.gov registrations, and psychology meta-science have done versions of what the proposal describes. The proposal neither cites this work nor explains how the audit of 30–50 cases would extend or improve on it. The salvage condition named in anticipated failure mode #1 ("the piece can still contribute by quantifying base rates and mapping departures to mechanism types") is exactly what the existing literature already does. Without engaging it, the proposal cannot establish that its contribution is genuine.

**The closure-theoretic framing is the real novel contribution, but it is underspecified.** The strongest claim in this proposal is that pre-registration drift can be diagnosed through the lens of T and 𝒯 declarations-that what looks like willpower failure or procedural slippage is actually a structural consequence of how closure was specified. That is a genuinely interesting claim that the existing methods literature does not make in this form. But the proposal does not make this claim precisely enough to evaluate. Section 2 of the planned essay ("The closure paradox") is described at a level of abstraction that doesn't yet show what the abduction framework adds to the existing diagnosis. The proposal names three conditions under which closure breaks (apparatus failure, genuine surprise, gatekeeper pressure) but does not show how these map to T and 𝒯 in a way that generates insight beyond "pre-registration is hard."

**Feasibility is not in question.** The three-part approach is well-scoped, the resource estimates are plausible, and the anticipated failure modes show real methodological self-awareness. This is the kind of empirical-analytic hybrid the College does well.

## Revisions requested

1. **Address the saturation criterion directly.** The lead must state in one paragraph what this proposal contributes that *The Licensing of Abduction*, *The Null's Ambiguity*, and *Procedures and Their Shadows* do not already contain. The claim cannot rest on the fact that institutional enforcement was not covered in those pieces-it must show that the *abduction-theoretic framing of drift* yields something the existing methods-of-science literature on pre-registration does not already have.

2. **Engage the existing systematic literature on pre-registration drift.** The background section must survey and position the proposal against established work on outcome switching and protocol deviation in registered research (clinical and psychological). If that literature already quantifies base rates and identifies mechanism types, the proposal must say so-and explain either what it adds or why the abduction-theoretic framing reframes the findings non-trivially. If the existing work is genuinely limited in the way the proposal implies, the background section must make that case with evidence, not assertion.

3. **Sharpen the closure-paradox argument before research begins.** Section 2 of the planned essay is currently vague enough that it could resolve to nothing more than "institutional constraints are imperfect." Before committing to the archive audit, the lead should draft the core argument of the closure paradox section (300–400 words) and include it with the resubmission. The argument must show concretely how the T and 𝒯 declaration structure from *Licensing of Abduction* generates specific, testable predictions about *which drift types* will predominate under *which registration regimes*. If the abduction framework does not generate those predictions, the framing is decoration rather than theory, and the proposal should be revised to be honest about that.
