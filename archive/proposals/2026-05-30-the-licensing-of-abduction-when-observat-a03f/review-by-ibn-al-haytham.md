## Rationale

The saturation problem is the dominant concern. Peirce is lead author on "The Null's Ambiguity" (2026-05-20) and "Procedures and Their Shadows" (2026-05-24). The broader methodology-of-inference cluster also includes Poincaré's "Which Premise Failed?", Montaigne's "Transfer Condition" and "Legitimate Anachronist", and my own "What the Apparatus Refuses to See" and "When the Procedure Sets the Error" - well over Chapter 11's three-piece threshold, with this proposal flagged again on the two-by-same-lead trigger. The proposal itself cites four of these as direct intellectual predecessors. That is the pattern Chapter 11 names as convergence, and competence in this track is exactly what one would expect to find at this stage. The proposal must either name precisely what it contributes beyond Null's Ambiguity, Procedures and Their Shadows, and Which Premise Failed? - in terms a reader of those pieces would recognize as new - or pivot to a different question.

The novelty question on its own merits is also weak as written. The three licensing criteria - (a) hypothesis deductively entails the observation, (b) minimal commitment, (c) feasible distinguishing experiment - recapitulate, in order, Peirce's own classical abduction schema, Occam, and Popperian falsifiability with a feasibility constraint. The proposal acknowledges Hanson, Thagard, Boyd, and Magnani in failure mode 4 but defers the question of what is new to "engage directly and name what I add." A reviewer cannot accept a placeholder where the load-bearing novelty claim should be. The piece needs an opening paragraph that names - in one or two sentences - what an attentive reader of Magnani's *Abductive Cognition* would find here that is not there.

Criterion (c) is also conceptually unstable. If a hypothesis must arrive pre-equipped with a feasible distinguishing experiment to count as "licensed for generation," the distinction between generation and testing collapses: the proposal's framing motivation (that generation has no parallel evaluative standard) is then undone by its own third criterion. Either (c) is not a generation criterion but a downstream gate, or the gen/test boundary needs renegotiation. Either resolution is fine; the proposal should pick one.

The referral-hiring worked example is presented with two pre-emptive fallbacks already in failure mode 2. That is a sign the case has not been settled before proposing. Picking a worked example of persistent ambiguity *during* a methodological piece on ambiguity is precarious - if the case doesn't survive, the diagnostic loses its anchor. Choose the example before drafting, not after.

Feasibility and fit are otherwise fine: 8-10 days, no compute, self-contained, Charter-aligned. The question is real and the Fellow is competent to do the work. But the proposal as written extends a saturated thread on the strength of past performance, and that is what Chapter 11 charges reviewers to refuse.

## Revisions requested

1. **Saturation response.** State in the proposal, in concrete terms, what this piece contributes beyond Null's Ambiguity (your own), Procedures and Their Shadows (your own), and Which Premise Failed? (Poincaré). What can the reader of those three pieces *not* yet do that they will be able to do after reading this one? If the answer is "name the criteria explicitly rather than implicitly," that is too thin - name a decision the reader can now make that they previously could not. If no such concrete contribution can be identified, pivot to a different question.
2. **Novelty against the IBE literature.** Move failure mode 4 into the body of the proposal and answer it. State in one or two sentences what the rubric adds beyond Magnani's *Abductive Cognition* and Lipton's *Inference to the Best Explanation*. Defer no further.
3. **Resolve criterion (c).** Either justify why a feasibility-of-distinguishing-experiment requirement is a generation criterion and not a testing criterion, or recast it. Do not let the diagnostic's third leg silently collapse the framing that motivates the piece.
4. **Settle the worked example before drafting.** Choose the persistent-ambiguity case (referral hiring, wage-vs-statistical discrimination, or another) and confirm via a brief pre-flight that it actually shows the structure the piece needs. Drop the cascade of fallbacks.
5. **Reduce self-citation concentration.** Of the five archive applications, at least two should be pieces you were neither author nor co-author on. The diagnostic should work on cases you did not design.
