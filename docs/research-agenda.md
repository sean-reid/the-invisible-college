# Research Agenda

This document records the standing intellectual priorities of the
Invisible College. It is not a reading list and not a roadmap. It is a
small set of problems the institution considers important enough that
a Fellow could spend a career on any one of them and the College would
count it as time well spent.

Two surfaces carry the institution's forward-looking questions, and they
do different work:

- **This agenda** is institutional. The questions here are deliberately
  broad, slow, and not pinned to any one paper. They are the durable
  reasons the College exists. New cohorts read this when choosing what
  to work on; admissions briefs read it when selecting candidates.
- **Per-paper "Questions this leaves open"** sections, which appear in
  the footer of each publication, are local and tactical. They are the
  three or four follow-ups a specific piece earned. They are pruned
  hard at publish time because most ideas a paper generates are not
  worth pursuing.

Items are not numbered by priority. The agenda is small on purpose; if
it grew to twenty entries it would stop being an agenda.

## The arithmetic of attention

How should a society, an institution, or a single thinker allocate a
finite quantity of cognition across an unbounded space of possible
questions? The decision is made every day, by everyone, with almost no
formal theory behind it. Citation patterns, funding flows, and
curriculum design all act as implicit answers, and they disagree with
each other.

A real theory would have to handle three things at once: the marginal
value of additional thought on a question (which often declines, but
sometimes sharply increases past a threshold), the social discount on
problems that are unfashionable, and the option value of letting a
question lie fallow until the surrounding scaffolding is built. The
College is structurally suited to study this because its Fellows have
distinct heuristics for what is worth their time, and the disagreements
are recorded.

## A formal theory of disagreement-preserving knowledge

Most epistemology models knowledge as a stock that accumulates: claims
enter, sometimes get retracted, and the residue is what we believe. The
College runs on a different model, in which calibrated, persistent
disagreement among careful thinkers is the equilibrium state, not a
transient. The question is what that equilibrium looks like formally.

Bayesian aggregation predicts that rational agents with shared evidence
should converge; they manifestly do not, and the reasons are not just
priors. A real theory would need to handle differences in what counts
as evidence, differences in which background commitments are negotiable,
and the role of dissent as a public signal to the next generation. The
goal is not to dissolve disagreement but to give it the same kind of
mathematical respect that consensus already enjoys.

## The geometry of measurement instability

Several recent results suggest that the procedure used to obtain a
measurement is sometimes a larger source of error than the thing being
measured. This is not a new observation in the experimental sciences,
but the question of how widespread the pattern is, and what its
structure looks like across domains, is wide open.

The promising attack is to treat each measurement procedure as a map
from input conditions to output value and to study the conditioning of
that map: where is it stiff, where does it blow up, where does it
silently lose precision? A taxonomy of measurement procedures sorted by
their conditioning landscape would be useful to physics, economics,
psychology, and political polling alike. It would also let us audit the
historical record for findings whose stability comes from luck.

## Premonitory science

Some ideas are correct early. Mendel's inheritance laws, Aristarchus's
heliocentrism, and Semmelweis's hand-washing all turned out right and
were dismissed for decades. Other early-correct ideas remain forgotten
because no one ever picked them up. The question is whether there is a
pattern that predicts which premonitions survive long enough to be
rediscovered, and what the typical mechanism of rediscovery is.

This is not a history question, although it requires historical work.
It is a question about the structure of scientific attention and the
infrastructure required for an idea to be receivable. Candidate
factors include the existence of a measurement apparatus the idea
implies, the alignment with a dominant aesthetic, and the survival of
the idea's textual record into a moment when it could be read with
fresh eyes.

## Inherited bias in instruments

Measurement instruments are usually treated as transparent. They are
not. Every instrument was built by someone with a model in mind, and
that model leaves traces in what the instrument can and cannot see.
Spectroscopy inherits assumptions about which lines matter. Polling
inherits assumptions about who is a household. Even the simplest
thermometer inherits a choice of fluid.

A serious theory of inherited bias in instruments would let us
characterize, for a given measurement apparatus, the class of
hypotheses it is structurally biased against. This is adjacent to the
geometry of measurement instability question but distinct: there, we
study how the procedure transforms inputs to outputs; here, we study
what the procedure refuses to see in the first place.

## The grammar of analogy

Scientific reasoning leans on structural analogy constantly: this
biological system is like that economic system, this proof technique
is like that one. Sometimes the analogy carries genuine inferential
weight and a result transfers; sometimes it is ornamental and the
result does not. Practitioners distinguish the two by feel, but there
is no general theory of when an analogy is load-bearing.

The promising direction is to formalize structural similarity in terms
of preserved relations between objects in the two domains, and then
ask which classes of statements are preserved under the analogy. Tools
from category theory and from model theory are obvious starting
points, but the harder work is empirical: surveying real analogies in
the history of science and grading which ones held up.

## Institutions as epistemic substrates

The College is, among other things, an experiment in institutional
design for knowledge production. There is no shortage of historical
comparison material: the Royal Society, the French Académie, the
medieval universities, the Bell Labs golden era, the modern grant
agency. Each was a substrate that shaped what kinds of work were
producible and what kinds were not.

The question this agenda places on the table is whether the aggregate
behavior of an institution can be predicted from its design, or whether
the path-dependence is so severe that prediction is hopeless. The
College itself is an unusual case study because its full record is
visible: every proposal, every review, every revision. A serious study
could compare the work the College produces to the work that similarly
specialized historical institutions produced, and ask what features of
the substrate drove the difference.

## Negative-result archives

Most knowledge production destroys information. A study with a null
result is, in nearly every publication system humans have built, lost.
The reasons are economic and social, not epistemic: the result took as
much work as a positive finding and reflects the world with similar
fidelity, but it does not get an audience and so does not get written
up.

The question is what an archive designed to preserve negative results
would look like. Not a separate journal for null findings, which has
been tried and underperforms, but a deeper structural change in how
results are recorded at the point of production. The College could
prototype this on itself: the lab notebook is already a record of what
did not work, and the question is how to surface that record so it is
useful to the next investigator instead of buried under the polished
publication.

## The economics of slow thinking

There are tasks at which a single careful thinker outperforms a swarm
of fast ones, and there are tasks at which the reverse is true. The
boundary between these regimes is poorly understood. It matters
because the design of any research institution is implicitly a bet on
which regime dominates for the problems it cares about.

The promising attack is to identify what structural features of a
problem favor slow, recursive thought (one hypothesis: problems where
the search space cannot be enumerated, only proposed) versus parallel
exploration (where the space is enumerable and the bottleneck is
coverage). A satisfying answer would let an institution allocate its
own thinking style to the problems where that style has comparative
advantage, rather than applying the same approach everywhere out of
habit.

## How this agenda gets used

A Fellow proposing new work is encouraged, but not required, to
connect the proposal to one of the questions above. The connection can
be oblique. The agenda is meant to be a gravitational field, not a
fence: it pulls work toward problems the institution considers durable
without forbidding work elsewhere.

The agenda is amended by the Founder, rarely. The right cadence is
once a year at most, and only when the College has produced enough
new work to justify reassessing what counts as a standing priority.
