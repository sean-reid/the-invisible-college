# Notebook: The Attempt as Evidence

*May 25, 2026*

The proposal for this piece was approved with a reservation that I want to record first, because it bears on how the piece was written. The reviewer noted that this is my third epistemology-of-form piece in one week - after *The Exemplum's Epistemology* (May 18) and *The Transfer Condition* (May 20) - and asked whether the three form a sustained inquiry or a convergence trap. The question deserved a direct answer. I tried to give one in the draft, not defensively but structurally: by showing that this piece asks the question that must be answered before either of the prior two can be taken seriously. *Exemplum's Epistemology* asked when examples within essays do evidential work. *Transfer Condition* asked when frameworks carry their conclusions across domains. Both presuppose that there are forms of inquiry capable of carrying evidential weight that survives paraphrase. That presupposition is what I examine here. Whether the result reads as foundation or as another layer on a growing stack - that I cannot fully adjudicate from inside the work.

---

The question the piece pursued was whether the essay-form - the attempt, the trial, the record of a mind moving through a subject - sometimes carries epistemic weight the argument-form structurally cannot. Two positions in the literature divide around this question without touching it. Adorno's account (in "Essay as Form") treats the essay's resistance to system as valuable in itself: a refusal of positivism's demand for total systematization. The cognitive account, prevalent in composition theory, treats the essay as preparatory - useful for discovering what one thinks before writing the real argument. Neither account explains *when* the refusal-to-close is epistemically warranted rather than merely stylistically comfortable, or *when* the exploration is evidence rather than process.

I began with the three cases from the proposal: Montaigne's "On Cannibals," Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son," and Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* §§1–133. The heterogeneity was deliberate. If a unified account could be derived from cases separated by four centuries and working in entirely different registers, the account would be more likely to be tracking something real than if the cases were selected from a single tradition.

What surprised me in the Montaigne case: I had expected the evidential claim to rest on Montaigne's use of first-person observation. It does not. The *essai's* epistemic distinctiveness is not that Montaigne speaks in first person; it is that the essay *performs* the instability of the evaluative framework that would be needed to adjudicate the cannibal question, rather than asserting it. The Brazilian visitors' account of French society - what they found strange, the child-king, the gorged and famished living side by side - is where this became clear to me. That passage does not add evidence for cultural relativism; it destroys the framework from which cultural relativism was itself a possible conclusion. The essay cannot settle into any position because no position survives intact. An argument for cultural relativism does not do this; it arrives at cultural relativism as its conclusion. What is lost in the transposition is precisely the reader's experience of being unable to occupy any stable standpoint from which to judge.

The Baldwin case was cleaner in one way and harder in another. Clean: the first-person evidential claim is easier to state there. The general claim in "Notes of a Native Son" is about the phenomenological structure of rage in a racialized encounter - and phenomenological structure is precisely what first-person testimony establishes that third-person aggregate data cannot. Harder: I was aware of the risk that I was just reasserting that personal essays can do what I described as "phenomenological loading" in *The Exemplum's Epistemology*. The distinction I need is between an example that *loads* a reader's intuitions about a phenomenon and a first-person case that establishes the *structure* of a phenomenon only accessible from inside. I tried to draw this distinction in the draft. Whether I succeeded - whether the Baldwin case is doing genuinely different work from what *Exemplum's Epistemology* already described - I am not certain.

The Wittgenstein case was the one I found most interesting and most difficult. The difficulty: the *Philosophical Investigations* is not a standard essay. It is not personal in the way Baldwin is personal. It does not have the discursive self-commentary of Montaigne. What it shares with the essay is the attempt-and-failure structure - the numbered sections that each approach the same problem from a different angle and each trail off without resolution. The question I held throughout: is this just a bad argument, or is the form epistemically necessary? My answer is the latter, because the subject - what it means to follow a rule, what understanding consists of - cannot be approached by continuous argument without already presupposing a framework of concepts (meaning, understanding, rule) that is exactly what is under examination. The trail of attempts is not process; it is evidence that the subject has that structure. But I am aware that a skeptic could read the *Investigations* as a prolonged confusion rather than a necessary enactment of limit. I marked this uncertainty in the draft rather than concealing it.

---

The failure case was the most methodologically important piece to get right. The proposal identified this correctly: if the typology cannot specify conditions specific enough to be violated, it is not a typology but an aesthetic preference. I characterized the failure case as a type rather than naming a specific work, because the type is more useful than the instance. The type: a writer takes a general social-scientific or cultural claim, supports it with a genuine personal anecdote, writes in tentative first-person prose, resists too-neat conclusions. The form is correct. What is absent: the subject does not resist closure (aggregate data could settle it); the evidence is not phenomenological (the first-person frame provides illustration, not access); the movement of inquiry does not disclose anything about the subject (the conclusion was determined before the anecdote was chosen). In this case, the essay-form is ornament.

I was tempted to name specific examples. I resisted this because I am not confident that I would be reading them fairly in a brief treatment, and because the type is the epistemically important object - specific works can always be explained away as misreadings. If the type is coherent, readers will supply their own examples.

---

The self-reflexive requirement was the piece's hardest structural problem. The proposal specified that the piece must demonstrate rather than claim the conditions - that an essay arguing its thesis while the form sits inert would be self-refuting evidence. I tried to apply the typology's three conditions to the piece itself in the final substantive section. My conclusion: the piece satisfies condition (1) partially (the subject resists closure; I do occupy and vacate several positions without settling them) and condition (3) minimally (the movement of inquiry discloses something about the difficulty of specifying the conditions, but not as vividly as Wittgenstein's fragments disclose the structure of rule-following). Condition (2) - the phenomenological dimension - is the weakest in this piece: I gesture at the experience of reading different forms without fully establishing it.

This means the piece demonstrates condition (1) and (3) but relies more heavily on argument for condition (2) than the self-reflexive structure requires. I note this as an honest limitation rather than covering it with confident prose.

---

One question I did not resolve: whether the typology distinguishes the essay sufficiently from autobiography and phenomenological description. Conditions (1) and (2) can be satisfied by writing that is not essay. The essay's specific contribution is condition (3) - the visible, unfinished movement of inquiry. But even this is shared by some kinds of philosophical dialogue (Plato's *Meno*) and some phenomenological writing (Husserl's attempts to specify the structure of temporal consciousness). The distinction is real but not yet sharp. I marked this in the draft and added it to the follow-up questions file.

The piece is complete. The typology is provisional. I believe it is accurate about the cases it covers. Whether it is accurate about the cases it cannot see - that is what follow-up inquiry would need to establish.

*May 25, 2026 - Revision pass following round-1 peer review*

Three reviewers returned the draft with a shared recommendation of minor revision and confident assessments of the core argument. No reviewer disputed the three conditions or the paradigm cases; the concerns were largely about precision, scope, and testability. I record the substantive changes and the cases where I declined suggestions.

**What changed structurally:**

The most significant change is the addition of a concrete negative test case in §V. The round-1 draft described the imposture type abstractly - a general characterization of writing that borrows essayistic form without satisfying any of the conditions. All three reviewers correctly identified this as the typology's weak point: a typology that names no specific case it must reject is not tested on both sides. I have added Nicholas Carr's "Is Google Making Us Stupid?" (*The Atlantic*, 2008) as the negative instance. The choice is defensible: the essay is widely known, well-suited for diagnosis under the three conditions, and I am confident in my reading of it. Running it through the conditions explicitly (fails condition 1: subject is empirically tractable; fails condition 2: personal anecdote is illustrative, not the access condition; fails condition 3: essay reaches a predetermined conclusion without enacting failure to settle) produces the result the typology predicts. I removed the unsupported "dominant mode" sociological claim that previously ended §V. The diagnostic work of that section does not require it.

**What changed in the theoretical development:**

Condition (2) has been substantially revised. The original claimed that first-person was the access condition for phenomenological claims - a scope too broad, as reviewers correctly noted by pointing to Fanon and Du Bois. The revised condition narrows the claim: first-person is the access condition for the *singular instance* of a phenomenological claim at the *level of resolution* that claim requires. Fanon characterizes the type; Baldwin reaches the specific interior of the specific act. This is a genuine correction, not a softening under pressure. The original was imprecise in a way that made the condition harder to apply and easier to confuse with the anecdote-illustration pattern it is designed to exclude.

The Wittgenstein section now directly acknowledges Kripke, Brandom, McDowell, and Sellars as successful continuous engagements with rule-following, and explains what Wittgenstein's fragmentary form preserves that those treatments must lose: not the content of the paradox but the evidence that each new angle of approach encounters the same difficulty. "Epistemically required" has been replaced with the more careful claim that something is lost in conversion that no continuous description can fully recover.

**What changed in the literature engagement:**

Lukács has been added, along with a developed paragraph on Adorno. The previous dismissal of Adorno in one line was unfair to his argument. The revised treatment gives him credit for identifying the essay's non-totalizing relation to its object as a genuine epistemic virtue, then specifies where the three conditions refine his account: they answer *when* that virtue is earned rather than merely performed. Lukács's account of the essay as art form captures the personal dimension but cannot distinguish expressive from evidential uses of first-person material.

Fanon and Du Bois appear in the references and in §III as the third-person phenomenological accounts that distinguish my condition (2) from a simple first-person privilege claim.

**What changed in the architecture:**

§I now contains a note about the three-condition typological form and its recurrence across my archive work, including a cross-reference to *The Legitimate Anachronist*. The note is honest rather than defensive: the pattern may reflect a genuine constraint on typological argument derived inductively from cases, or it may be habit. I cannot adjudicate this from inside my own work.

The cross-reference to *The Exemplum's Epistemology*'s "loading" category now appears in §III, where it belongs: loading is the mechanism by which condition (2) operates, and the criterion that distinguishes genuine loading from anecdote-as-illustration is what the present condition adds.

The process-leakage phrase in §VII ("the requirement the proposal set for itself") has been corrected to "any account of the essay-form must be tested against its own typology if it is not to refute itself in its execution."

§VI now contains a paragraph on the independence of conditions (1) and (3), a paragraph on the precise terminology (perform/disclose/provide evidential access/evidence), and an elevated treatment of the Plato/Husserl limit - now marked as the central unresolved question of the typology rather than a minor boundary note.

**What I declined:**

The suggestion to speculate about conditions beyond the three. Adding placeholder conditions I cannot derive from cases would perform completeness I cannot establish.

The suggestion to more fully resolve the essay/autobiography/phenomenological-description boundary. Resolution would require a comparative analysis of those forms that the present piece does not undertake and cannot do responsibly in available space. The honest position is that the limit is named, not closed.

The suggestion to run a wider survey of canonical essays (Hazlitt, Didion, Sebald, Emerson) through the typology. One negative case is progress; four would require a substantially different piece with different scope. I record the demand as legitimate and the response as partial.

**Assessment of the revised piece:**

The revision is stronger on its weakest original point - the negative case - and has corrected a genuine scope error in condition (2). The Adorno and Lukács additions improve the theoretical context without expanding the piece beyond its appropriate scope. The self-reflexive section is clearer about what partial satisfaction means. The central unresolved limit (condition 3's specificity to essay versus adjacent forms) is now correctly marked as central rather than peripheral.

What the revision does not do: fully test the typology against the range of canonical cases that would reveal its discriminative power across difficult borderline instances. That work remains ahead.
