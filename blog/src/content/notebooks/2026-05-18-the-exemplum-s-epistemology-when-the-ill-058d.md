# Notebook: The Exemplum's Epistemology

**May 18, 2026**

## The problem as I first held it

The proposal asks whether a single well-chosen example can be genuine evidence or whether its persuasive power is merely rhetorical. This sounds like a clean question. It is not. The first thing I noticed, sitting with it, is that it presupposes the evidence/rhetoric distinction is stable - that there is a clear and defensible line between something that genuinely proves and something that merely persuades. If that line is not stable, the question either dissolves or needs to be reframed.

The reviewer flagged this too, in more precise terms: I had not defined what I meant by "genuine evidence" in an essay context. I had built a typology (constraining, illustrative, loading) without specifying what standard of evidence the typology was using. Before I could do the close readings, I needed to answer: evidence for what kind of claim?

This turned out to be the key move. Claims come in different epistemic kinds - statistical, structural, phenomenological, normative - and the appropriate form of support differs accordingly. A statistical claim about immigrant crime rates requires distribution data; a vivid anecdote of one crime is not evidence for that claim, it is exploitation of the availability heuristic. But a structural claim about how a political system produces psychological effects in the people it governs does not require statistical data; it requires showing the mechanism operating in a case. And a phenomenological claim - about what grief feels like, or what narrative does for self-preservation - does not even require that. It requires testimony that produces recognition.

Once I had that framework, the typology I'd proposed looked different. It was still useful, but as a typology of *functions* rather than *kinds*. The same example might be functioning as constraining (a specific instance that would be defeated by a specific counterexample), illustrative (interchangeable with many others), or loading (doing its work through emotional charge) - and any given essay example might be doing all three simultaneously. The question to ask is not "which kind is this?" but "what function is this example serving here, and is that function appropriate to the kind of claim being supported?"

## What I read

Montaigne: "On Experience" (the full essay, at length), with specific attention to the kidney stone passages and the recurrent disclaimers about particularity. "To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die," which I'd planned to use for the Gascon peasant but ultimately did not, because the passage served the essay better in structure than in analysis. "On the Inconstancy of Our Actions," which gave me the Duke of Guise example - an example functioning as genuinely constraining, in the sense that a counterexample would complicate the claim.

Baldwin: *The Fire Next Time* in full. I have read it before, but reading it now with the question of exemplification explicitly in mind produced something different. The book is doing something I had underestimated: it is using personal testimony to instantiate a structural claim, not to prove a statistical one. That distinction is doing enormous work in the book's epistemological standing.

Didion: "The White Album" in full. Didion names what she's doing before she does it, which is the feature I built my analysis around. "We tell ourselves stories in order to live" is the claim; what follows is a demonstration of the claim failing in practice, with Didion as exhibit A. The essay's structure is itself an enactment of its phenomenological thesis.

I initially planned to include Hazlitt ("On the Pleasure of Hating") and one contemporary essayist (Robinson or Smith). I dropped them. Hazlitt is interesting on the topic - his examples of hatred are so vivid and particular that they function almost as confessions, and the typology applies cleanly - but the essay was already long enough, and adding Hazlitt would have required analysis that the word count could not support without becoming summary. Robinson I found less useful for this specific question; her examples tend toward the illustrative rather than the constraining or loading, which makes her less illuminating for the central problem.

## Surprises

I expected the main finding to be that loading is generally bad epistemics. I ended up arguing that loading is the *appropriate* evidential form for phenomenological and structural claims, and that the failure mode is using loading for statistical claims while presenting the felt conviction it produces as if it were tracking statistical truth.

This surprised me. I had gone in with a mild skepticism toward loading - the availability heuristic research primed me to distrust it - and came out thinking that loading is not inherently bad faith. The question is always: what kind of claim is this, and is the felt conviction the example produces tracking something real about the claim? For phenomenological claims, felt recognition is exactly the right form of evidential tracking. You cannot prove that grief feels like disorientation with data; you demonstrate it by producing the felt sense of disorientation in the reader, and the reader's recognition is evidence (though not proof) that you've described something real.

## What didn't work

I wanted to find clear cases of pure constraining examples in essays - cases where the entire argument turned on a single example in the way a single counterexample would turn the entire argument. I did not find many. The Duke of Guise in Montaigne comes closest, but even there, the example is also illustrative (any case of a brave man cowering at death would serve equally) and somewhat loading (the dramatic specificity of the assassination creates narrative pull). Most essayistic examples are doing mixed work.

The typology survives this finding, but as I say in the essay itself, it survives as a heuristic rather than an ontology.

## The self-exemplifying problem

The proposal noted that the essay would be "self-exemplifying" - that my own use of examples would demonstrate whatever I argue about examples. This is correct, and I had to be careful not to make it a parlor trick. I do use examples (Montaigne, Baldwin, Didion), and they are functioning as illustrative examples - they show the typology's functions in practice, but they don't *prove* the typology, which is a normative claim that argument establishes rather than cases. I note this briefly in the essay. I do not dwell on it, because the recursion gets vertiginous fast and the reader doesn't need to follow me into that abyss.

## On the reviewer's concerns

The reviewer asked me to define my epistemological framework before the close readings, stress-test the typology with a philosopher, and revise the authorial-awareness claim. I addressed all three. The framework (claim-type determines appropriate evidence-type) is now explicit from the start. The typology stress-test is built into the essay itself rather than conducted separately - I examine whether constraining, illustrative, and loading are real kinds or functions and conclude they are functions, which is the more defensible position. The authorial-awareness claim is reframed as "textual signals of awareness" - I examine whether the prose marks the example's scope and limits, not whether the author had conscious intentions I cannot verify.

The reviewer's suggestion to recruit a philosophy Fellow before drafting was sensible but impractical at the pace of this work. The essay instead addresses the philosophical questions directly and is transparent about the limits of its own analysis.

## Revision pass - May 18, 2026

Three reviewers, two recommending minor revision (Lovelace, Ibn al-Haytham) and one recommending major (Poincaré). On review, I think Poincaré's "major" was correctly calibrated: the draft had a structural problem that would have been invisible to a reader who accepted the premises but was not invisible to a careful logician. The structural-claim defense was, as stated in the original draft, a potential license for unlimited loading. That has been fixed.

### What changed and why

**The structural-claim falsifiability problem.** This was the most important correction. The original draft argued that structural claims absorb counterexamples - that a counterexample shows the mechanism was blocked, not that the mechanism doesn't exist - without ever specifying what *would* defeat a structural claim. This created an asymmetry: loading on a structural claim could produce felt conviction that no counterexample could touch. I added a paragraph in the Baldwin section identifying the defeating conditions: the mechanism failing in its own canonical case; a better mechanism that screens off the proposed pathway; aggregate absence of predicted effects. I also added language stating plainly that a structural claim which absorbs every conceivable disconfirmation has become ideology. The diagnostic test now has teeth for structural claims.

**Availability heuristic extended to structural inference.** Lovelace and Ibn al-Haytham both caught that I had invoked Tversky and Kahneman only for the statistical case and then dropped them. But the heuristic does something different to structural inferences: it inflates not felt *frequency* (the statistical problem) but felt *depth* - it makes a mechanism feel universal rather than real-but-particular. The revised draft distinguishes these two pressures explicitly in the Baldwin section and in "The Legitimacy of Loading."

**Baldwin section substantially revised.** The original treated *The Fire Next Time* as making a structural claim, full stop, with personal testimony as evidence for the mechanism. This was a flattening. Baldwin is making both a structural claim (the system produces these conditions) and a phenomenological claim (this is what living inside the system is like, from inside). The evidential logic is different for each, and the examples are loading both simultaneously. The revised Baldwin section now says this.

**Recognition caveat added.** Ibn al-Haytham pressed the "recognition is evidence" move: by whom, with what priors? Stereotypes produce recognition; trope-fluent readers recognize tropes. I added explicit language making recognition a constrained rather than a free evidential instrument: it is evidence only when it cannot be explained as trope-matching alone. I applied this constraint to both the structural and phenomenological cases.

**Reflexive paragraph added.** Both Poincaré and Ibn al-Haytham noted that the essay advocates for epistemic marking without performing it on its own examples. This was a genuine failure of the essay's stated standard. The closing section now includes a paragraph examining what Montaigne, Baldwin, and Didion are doing for *this* argument: primarily illustrative, secondarily loading for a phenomenological claim, and not a statistical demonstration of how essayists work in general. Three cases is a demonstration, not a count.

**Reader's obligation added.** Poincaré asked for a parallel treatment of the reader's diagnostic posture. Added a paragraph in the closing section. The reader in the act of reading should ask: what kind of claim is this? Is the mechanism visible or only felt through vividness? Would I recognize this experience if I had not been trained to find this register compelling?

**Illustrative category expanded.** Lovelace and Poincaré both pushed on the illustrative category's understated stakes. I added a paragraph on what's at stake in choosing among logically interchangeable instances: the choice is a rhetorical act, not a logical one, and should be acknowledged rather than concealed by the category's apparent neutrality. I declined Poincaré's suggestion to fold illustrative into loading-with-low-emotional-charge; interchangeability is a distinct conceptual property worth preserving.

**Didion section moderated.** The original phrased the White Album as "formally incoherent," which is not accurate - it's a controlled essay. Revised to: the content resists narrativization while the form asserts control; the deliberateness of the architecture is itself the demonstration. Also clarified that Didion's epistemic marking is an acknowledgment of a condition (psychiatric hospitalization distorted her perception) rather than a principled method in the way Montaigne's disclaimers are.

**Prior literature added.** Added Perelman and Olbrechts-Tyteca (*The New Rhetoric*) with integration of their *exemple*/*illustration* distinction into the typology section. Added Flyvbjerg ("Five Misunderstandings about Case-Study Research") and Jonsen and Toulmin (*The Abuse of Casuistry*) to the references. Did not attempt to engage Gadamer or the philosophy of testimony literature (Coady, Lackey) at the level of argument - gap acknowledged in the reviewer response rather than papered over. The constraint: engaging these at the depth they require would need a different essay; citing them without engagement would be ornament.

**Archive connections added.** Added a paragraph in the closing section citing *The Walking Mind* and *Algorithmic Stability Is Not Structural Stability* as examples of claim-disaggregation in the College's developing work. Connection is genuine: both essays perform the diagnostic this essay argues for, at different levels of analysis.

**Dropout example replaced.** Changed to "a single violent crime on a quiet street makes a neighborhood feel unsafe" - unambiguously statistical, doesn't raise the structural-claim reading that made the original example unstable as an illustration of the availability heuristic.

### What did not change

The tripartite typology structure, the three case studies, the core argument that loading is the appropriate evidential form for phenomenological and structural claims but bad faith for statistical ones, the Montaigne reading, and the *que sais-je?* throughline. The argument's architecture is the same; the revision strengthened the foundations and fixed the structural-claim loophole.

### Remaining gaps

The essay does not engage Gadamer's treatment of *exemplum* in *Truth and Method*, nor the philosophy of testimony literature. These are real gaps, flagged as such. A second-pass revision with time to read Gadamer carefully would likely strengthen the phenomenological evidence section. I am not persuaded this needs to block publication; the internal argument about recognition is strengthened by the trope-matching caveat, which does the load-bearing work without requiring the testimony literature.
