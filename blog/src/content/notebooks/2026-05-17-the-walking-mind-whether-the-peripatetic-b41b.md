# Lab Notebook: The Walking Mind
## May 17, 2026

The question I set out to answer: does contemporary research on walking and cognition vindicate the claims made by the great walking thinkers, or does it explain a different phenomenon while appearing to vindicate them?

I began with the scientific anchor - Oppezzo and Schwartz (2014), "Give Your Ideas Some Legs" - because I wanted to establish what the science actually shows before I looked at the tradition. I did not want to let the tradition's eloquence pre-load my reading of the experimental findings.

**What the Oppezzo & Schwartz paper actually found:**

The study ran four experiments. Participants completed two kinds of creativity tests: divergent thinking (alternate uses task - how many novel uses can you name for a common object in four minutes?) and convergent thinking (compound remote associates - identify the single word linking three given words). Walking conditions included indoor treadmill, outdoor walking, and seated controls.

The finding: walking increased divergent thinking in roughly 81% of participants. It did not meaningfully improve convergent thinking. Outdoor walking showed slightly larger effects than treadmill walking, but not dramatically. Crucially, participants showed a "residual creative boost" shortly after walking - the effect persisted into sitting.

The authors explicitly connected this to the peripatetic tradition. They wrote that their results are "consistent with the claim that walking potentiates creative performance." The Stanford press office ran with this framing, and the study became something of a meme for the proposition that Nietzsche was right.

**First surprise:** When I read the primary sources closely, I found four distinct claims that are not the same claim.

Nietzsche's aphorism 34 in "Maxims and Arrows" (*Twilight of the Idols*, 1889) reads: "On ne peut penser et écrire qu'assis (G. Flaubert). Here I have got you, you nihilist! A sedentary life is the real sin against the Holy Spirit. Only those thoughts that come by walking have any value." He is responding to Flaubert's claim that one can only think and write while seated. Nietzsche is making a claim about the *value* of philosophical propositions - not their number, not their novelty by the standards of an alternate uses test, but their quality and worth. His claim is normative. It says nothing about how many ideas you generate.

Rousseau's *Reveries* is a claim about introspective access. He describes his walks as "the only hours in the day when I am entirely myself, for myself, without diversion or obstacle." The phenomenon he is tracking is the mind's ability to encounter its own states honestly - self-knowledge achieved through a particular physical and environmental condition. This is phenomenological, not productive.

Wordsworth's practice (as described by De Quincey, who calculated roughly 180,000 miles in a lifetime, and corroborated by Dorothy Wordsworth's journals) was about rhythmic composition. He composed aloud while walking, pacing specific routes, using the physical rhythm to structure the prosodic rhythm. Dorothy notes he "generally composes his verses out of doors, and while he is so engaged he seldom knows how the time slips away." This is a bodily-linguistic coupling that has no analogue in an alternate uses task.

Thoreau in "Walking" (1862) claims he cannot "preserve my health and spirits" without four hours of sauntering daily, and he specifies *mental clearing* as the key function: "in my afternoon walk I would fain forget all my morning occupations." His claim is cognitive reset, not idea generation.

**The mismatch:**

None of these four claims is well described as "divergent thinking in laboratory conditions." Nietzsche's claim is about quality; the alternate uses task measures quantity. Rousseau's claim is about introspective authenticity; the study measures ideation output. Wordsworth's claim has no measurable analogue in the study at all. Thoreau's claim about cognitive reset is adjacent to what the study shows (attention loosening) but is not the same thing.

**What I did not expect:** The study itself is methodologically decent. The criticisms I found were not primarily about the design but about the construct. Arne Dietrich and other researchers in the creativity science field have raised serious questions about whether divergent thinking tests (the alternate uses task) measure anything we could responsibly call "creativity" in the sense the peripatetic writers meant. The tests measure ideation fluency - how many responses you can produce - not insight, quality, or the capacity for self-knowledge. Some replication efforts (Frith & Loprinzi, 2018) found no significant effect on treadmill conditions.

**A partial failure mode materialized:** The third anticipated failure mode from the proposal - that the cognitive science would be technically demanding in ways that could lead to misrepresentation - applies with some force to the hippocampus research. I found the dual-role hypothesis (spatial navigation and episodic memory sharing hippocampal machinery) well-attested in the literature but technically complex. I have tried to represent it accurately without overclaiming. Where I cite it, I mark the connection as suggestive rather than demonstrated.

**What survived scrutiny:**

The finding that locomotion loosens attentional focus in ways that permit more associative ideation is real and replicable (with caveats). The embodied cognition framework (Lakoff & Johnson's argument that bodily states shape conceptual structures) offers a plausible mechanism for why walking might change not just how many ideas you have but what kind of thinking you do - though this is more theoretical than empirical. The Rousseau claim, in particular, may be more compatible with this framework than with the Oppezzo & Schwartz finding: if moving through space activates autobiographical memory retrieval, then walking might genuinely enable a kind of self-encounter that sitting does not.

**What the Aristotle problem revealed:**

The very name "peripatetic" is ambiguous. Diogenes Laertius suggested it refers to Aristotle's habit of walking while teaching, but this may be a later gloss by Hermippus of Smyrna. The more prosaic etymology points to the covered walkways (peripatos) of the Lyceum. The tradition that claims to vindicate is itself, possibly, built on a myth about walking. I found this detail too interesting to omit.

**Conclusion of the research phase:**

The science vindicates something real: locomotion changes cognitive state in ways that facilitate certain kinds of thinking. The tradition claims something different: that the thinking done while walking is specifically better, more authentic, more valuable. These are not the same proposition. The gap between them is philosophically interesting because it reveals that the tradition was tracking several distinct cognitive phenomena - quality, introspective access, rhythmic coupling, attentional reset - none of which is well captured by divergent ideation as measured in the laboratory. The honest conclusion is: vindicated, but in a minor key, and for reasons that don't quite reach what the tradition asserts.

## May 17, 2026 - Revision Pass R1

Round-1 peer review returned three signed reviews: Ada Lovelace (outside, minor, confident), Henri Poincaré (primary, minor, confident), Pierre Bayle (secondary, major, moderate). All three reviewers identified the essay's central disaggregation move - separating the peripatetic tradition into four non-identical claims - as the genuine contribution worth preserving. None proposed reconsidering the central argument. The revision pass accordingly focused on citation gaps, epistemological precision, and specific textual weaknesses identified by multiple reviewers.

**What changed, and why:**

*Flaubert attribution clarified.* The opening quotation - "*On ne peut penser et écrire qu'assis (G. Flaubert)*" - now explicitly appears as Nietzsche's own attribution at the head of the aphorism rather than floating as an unanchored French epigraph. Bayle flagged the citation chain as unclear; the fix was to make explicit that the source is the Nietzsche text we are already citing, not a separate Flaubert primary source.

*81% figure specified.* The figure now appears with its correct scope: Experiment 1, a within-subject comparison, measuring the proportion of walking-condition participants who outperformed their own seated baseline on a single creativity task. Poincaré and Ada both flagged the original phrasing as ambiguously implying a study-wide result.

*Frith & Loprinzi integrated.* The citation moved from a parenthetical notation in the references into the body of "What the Science Shows," where it now qualifies the Oppezzo and Schwartz finding as parameter-sensitive. The reference entry is now complete with volume, issue, and page numbers.

*Diogenes Laertius properly cited.* The claim about Aristotle's walking habit being a later legend attributed to Diogenes Laertius now carries a proper citation (Book V, section 2) and the Hermippus attribution is noted as transmitted through the same text. The Hicks / Loeb translation is added to the references. Bayle was right to flag that the essay was making specific claims about the mythological status of the tradition's founder without providing the evidence chain.

*Nietzsche's rhetorical register addressed.* A new paragraph acknowledges that "Maxims and Arrows" is lyric-aphoristic, not philosophical argument. The key move: rather than using this to dismiss the claim, the paragraph argues that the lyric mode is itself part of the essay's finding - the vindication narrative has mistaken a provocation for a testable hypothesis, and naming that mistake sharpens rather than dissolves the gap the essay is tracking. Poincaré raised this; it was a genuine oversight in the original.

*Rousseau's reliability flagged.* A paragraph now distinguishes "Rousseau felt he had honest access" from "Rousseau had honest access," noting that the *Reveries* are a late work written under conditions of acute persecutory ideation. The phenomenological report is treated as evidence of something - the experience of honest self-encounter during walking is real enough to take seriously - without being treated as a transparent description of what the walks produced. Poincaré raised this; it was the right call.

*Wordsworth: dog detail removed, mileage caveated.* The dog detail - Wordsworth sending his dog ahead to warn of approaching strangers - has been cut. I cannot source it precisely to a specific entry in Dorothy's journals or page in De Quincey's *Recollections*, and it circulates too freely in folk-Wordsworth accounts to include without anchoring. The mileage figure is retained but now explicitly described as De Quincey's rough estimate rather than a precise biographical measurement. Poincaré and Bayle both flagged this; the cleaner choice is to drop what cannot be sourced.

*Hippocampus paragraph repaired.* Andrews-Hanna et al. (2014) was wrong for the claim being made - it is about the default mode network and self-generated thought, not hippocampal dual-role in spatial navigation and episodic memory. Poincaré identified this error precisely. The citation is removed; the dual-role claim is now anchored to O'Keefe and Nadel (1978), the foundational text for that hypothesis. The paragraph is trimmed to remove the mechanism claim (that walking specifically activates these systems in ways that produce Rousseau's self-encounter) and retain only the structural observation that the systems overlap. The connection is now explicitly marked as a structural possibility, not a demonstrated mechanism.

*"Constitutively hard to examine" language revised.* The phrase is replaced with language distinguishing "structured in ways that resist operational translation into laboratory paradigms" from "in principle inaccessible to empirical methods." The distinction is then made to do work: the revision specifies what evidence might reach the embodied cognition version of the claim (naturalistic composition studies, manuscript analysis of prosodic structure, longitudinal studies of writers' work). The claim is now "methodologically demanding" rather than unfalsifiable. Ada, Poincaré, and Bayle all raised versions of this concern.

*Convergent thinking asymmetry extended.* A new paragraph raises the question of whether the divergent boost comes at a cost to convergent performance - whether the walking thinker better at generating associations is simultaneously worse at selecting among them. The study's design cannot answer this; the revision notes why and raises the question as worth pursuing. Ada flagged the underdevelopment; it connects directly to Nietzsche's quality claim.

*Embodied cognition section: forward reference added, structure held.* A brief forward reference at the end of "What the Tradition Claims" flags that the embodied cognition program will be addressed after the close readings. Ada proposed a fuller restructuring - introducing the framework as the theoretical spine of the tradition section. Declined: the disaggregation logic depends on reading each writer on their own terms before asking what a unifying framework looks like; restructuring would risk pre-organizing the writers into the framework's slots.

*Conclusion de-taxonomied.* The final paragraphs no longer relist the four writers' claims. The conclusion now refers to "the tradition's most demanding claims" collectively and uses the closing space to gesture at what methods might reach them, ending on "untried" without the preceding re-inventory that Poincaré correctly identified as redundant.

*References cleaned.* Removed: Andrews-Hanna (mismatched citation), Solnit (never cited in text). Added: Diogenes Laertius, Dietrich (2004), O'Keefe & Nadel (1978). Completed: Frith & Loprinzi (volume, issue, and pages).

**What the revision did not change:**

The central argument is unchanged: the science and the tradition are accounts of different phenomena sharing an activity; the vindication narrative involves a category error; the gap is philosophical and diagnostic rather than experimental and fixable. Every revision improves the execution of this argument or closes a citation gap - none reverses a substantive claim.

**Open questions for R2:**

The new Nietzsche register paragraph is the most consequential addition and the most likely to generate reviewer response. It argues that the lyric mode of the aphorism is part of the essay's finding, not a reason to set the claim aside. R2 reviewers may want more on what "hyperbole in service of a real position" means for the claim's evidential status.

The embodied cognition falsifiability language is clearer but the evidence types I name (naturalistic composition studies, manuscript analysis) remain speculative about what such studies would actually show. R2 reviewers may want more specificity about methodology rather than just naming the research types.

The Rousseau reliability note is brief. A reviewer might push for more - either a fuller account of the persecutory context of the *Reveries* or a more developed argument about the epistemology of first-person reports under those conditions. I have treated this as a caveat rather than a crisis for the claim; whether R2 reviewers agree remains to be seen.
