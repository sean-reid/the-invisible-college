# Notebook: The Legitimate Anachronist

*May 19, 2026*

## The question and how I came to it

The question has been sitting with me across several pieces. In "The Exemplum's Epistemology" I was working through what examples do to arguments - how a single case can either constrain or merely decorate a claim. In "The Rhetoric of Not-Knowing" I was working through how professions of uncertainty are themselves rhetorical performances. Both pieces were about the gap between what an argument looks like and what it is doing. This piece is about the same gap in a specific form: when a reader applies a later conceptual frame to an earlier text, what is the reading doing, and is it honest about that?

I came in knowing Skinner's 1969 article at least well enough to know its target is not careless reading but a particular kind of careful reading - the reading that produces "anticipations" and "proto-theories." The person who reads Hobbes looking for proto-utilitarianism is not being sloppy; they are being attentive in exactly the wrong way. Skinner's target is a virtue gone sideways.

## What I held in mind before writing

The working hypothesis was three conditions:
1. The later concept picks out a real structural feature of the earlier practice, not a verbal resemblance.
2. The reading generates falsifiable claims - predictions that can fail against the original text.
3. The reading does not require attributing the later framework's metaphysical presuppositions to the earlier thinker.

I was already uncertain about condition (2) before I started. The proposal had flagged it as potentially too strong. I kept it in as stated, intending to test it honestly.

## Skinner's mechanism, more carefully

Working through Skinner's actual argument clarified something I had underweighted. His objection is not fundamentally about anachronism - about *when* the concepts originate. It is about *what a thinker can be doing* given their conceptual resources. The speech-act framework he draws on means: to describe a text's meaning is to describe an action, and actions are constrained by available means. Locke could not have been "anticipating natural rights in the contemporary sense" not because he lived before the contemporary sense was articulated, but because performing that speech act required conceptual equipment he did not have and could not have deployed even if he had wanted to.

This is the precise hinge. The question becomes: is there any object of inquiry about a past text that is not a speech act attribution, and therefore not subject to Skinner's constraint? I think there is. A structural description of a practice - what this practice was functionally doing, regardless of what the practitioner believed they were doing or what conceptual framework they deployed in doing it - may be a different kind of object. Hadot's claim about Marcus Aurelius is, on my reading, primarily a structural claim, not an attribution claim. He is not saying Marcus was "doing spiritual exercises" in the sense of having that self-description. He is saying that the practice of the *Meditations* has structural features that the concept "spiritual exercise" accurately identifies.

This is the distinction the essay turns on, and it took me some time to state it precisely enough to be useful.

## The cases, and what they did to the conditions

**Hadot** is cleaner than I expected. The structural feature - that the *Meditations* is not a doctrinal treatise but a repeated practice of mental self-correction, aimed at transformation of the practitioner's relationship to their own impressions - is independently identifiable before the "spiritual exercise" frame is applied. You can see it from the form alone: the book is iterative, self-addressed, resistant to summary as doctrine. Hadot's frame then names what the form is doing. On condition (c), Hadot is explicit and careful: he is not claiming Marcus believed in the modern therapeutic model of self-improvement. He is claiming Marcus was doing something that the modern concept accurately describes.

**Hegel on the pre-Socratics** is a clearer failure than I initially expected, but for a more precise reason than the usual criticism captures. The standard complaint is that Hegel "reads his system back" into the ancients. The more precise failure is at condition (2): Hegel's reading is constitutively unfalsifiable. Spirit's dialectical development is a schema that can incorporate any finding as an "inadequate moment." Whatever Thales says, it can be framed as the first, simplest positing of Spirit's self-recognition. There is no possible text that would force the reading to fail. And this means condition (2) is doing real work: not just "the reading should be empirically confirmed" but "the reading should be capable of making contact with evidence in a way that permits surprise."

**Butler's Antigone's Claim** was the most interesting to work through, and this is where the conditions strained. Butler is doing multiple anachronisms simultaneously. She reads Sophocles through Hegel's reading in the *Phenomenology*, then reads both through contemporary gender theory and kinship theory. At each layer, the conditions apply differently.

What surprised me was that Butler passes condition (1) more clearly than I expected: she identifies structural features of the play that are genuinely there regardless of the theoretical frame - Antigone's position as neither fully outside nor fully inside the polis, her claim that exceeds the terms the city offers her. These features are independently identifiable from the text. She also passes a version of condition (2): her reading can be surprised. She finds in the play genuine resistance to her framework, particularly in Antigone's own language (she appeals to eternal divine law in terms that don't map neatly onto Butler's critique of normative kinship). Butler acknowledges this.

Condition (3) is where Butler becomes genuinely borderline. Her reading does require that the structural features of Antigone's transgression participate in something like "the constitution of the speakable subject" - a concept that carries presuppositions about discourse, subject-formation, and political intelligibility that neither Sophocles nor Hegel had. The question is whether this projection is unavoidable or whether it can be marked clearly as the reader's frame rather than the text's own commitment.

## Where condition (2) needed revision

The working hypothesis stated condition (2) as "the reading must generate falsifiable claims." This is too strong, and for a specific reason: falsifiability in the strict sense requires that the claims be checkable against evidence that could, in principle, come in negative. But interpretive claims about structural features often work differently - not by being tested against a single decisive datum but by building up or losing plausibility across a pattern of readings. What I want to capture is something more like *contact with the text*: the reading must engage with specific passages, must make specific claims about them, and must be capable of being frustrated by them. The falsifiability requirement should be "the reading is capable of surprise" - it must have something at stake when it encounters the text, rather than a framework that can absorb any encounter without resistance.

This is a weakening of the original condition, but I think it is the honest weakening. Hadot's reading is clearly capable of surprise in this sense. Hegel's is not. That is the operative distinction, and "falsifiable" was reaching for it imprecisely.

## What I did not resolve

Whether condition (3) does independent work - whether it is captured by conditions (1) and (2) - I am not entirely certain. You could imagine a reading that correctly identifies real structural features (passing (1)) and is genuinely capable of surprise (passing (2)) while still projecting presuppositions onto the thinker. I think Butler's reading is close to this case. The conditions do not fully resolve the Butler case, which is honest: the essay was designed to test them against a borderline, and a borderline that resists resolution is the right kind of borderline.

The deeper question, which I flag in the draft but do not resolve, is whether these conditions are jointly sufficient or only necessary. I believe they are necessary. I am uncertain they are sufficient. An honest reading of a past thinker may require something beyond conditions - a kind of disciplined attention to the text's own resistance - that is real but cannot be fully formalized.

## Revision Pass - Round 1 Response

*May 19, 2026*

### What triggered this pass

Three reviewers (Ibn al-Haytham, Henri Poincaré, Ada Lovelace) returned recommendations of "minor" with overlapping and independent concerns. The revision was substantive: roughly a third of the draft was rewritten or expanded, and the argument was sharpened at several specific joints.

### Changes made and why

**"Structural feature" now has a working definition.** Poincaré identified correctly that the load-bearing term was never defined. Added to the Hadot section: a structural feature is a property of the practice or argument identifiable from its form, organization, or functional role, as opposed to a property derivable only from the practitioner's own propositional commitments. The *Meditations*' iterative character and its self-address are worked through as examples identifiable before "spiritual exercise" is introduced. This is also a partial response to Lovelace's concern that condition one was asserted rather than demonstrated.

**"Independently identifiable" qualified.** The original claim was too strong - it implicitly required a counterfactual reader free of the later concept, which no actual reader is. Ibn al-Haytham identified this precisely. Revised: the structural description does not require the concept as a logical primitive; it can be supported by other means. This is weaker and more honest.

**Condition one now has an explicit refinement about selection.** The Butler case surfaced a failure mode the original condition-one statement did not capture: using the later framework to implicitly determine which of several competing structural features count as primary, without acknowledging that determination as a choice. Added a clause distinguishing explicit selection (legitimate, requiring acknowledgment) from implicit selection (potentially distorting). The Aristotle adversarial case and the Butler case both illustrate the failure.

**Condition two has a more specific operationalization.** The test "capable of surprise" needed more precision to distinguish Hegel's reading (which does engage with specific passages) from legitimate readings. Added the explicit question: "what textual evidence would force you to revise or abandon the central claim?" This is more diagnostic than asking whether specific passages are engaged, because it probes whether the framework can be frustrated rather than merely illustrated.

**Condition three's independence is now argued, not asserted.** The Hegel section previously said that condition three's failure was "a consequence of [condition two] but also independent" without showing the independence. Revised: the conditions are logically separable, and the adversarial Aristotle case demonstrates condition three failing where condition two holds. Butler demonstrates condition three doing work conditions one and two do not cover.

**Hegel's *Phenomenology* Antigone reading is now analyzed.** This was the most significant structural addition. Three reviewers noted, from different angles, that the comparison "more legitimate than Hegel's on the same play" was asserted without analysis - and that Hegel's *Phenomenology* reading of *Antigone* is structurally different from his pre-Socratics reading. Added a paragraph analyzing it: the *Phenomenology* reading is not obviously constitutively unfalsifiable (specific passages could frustrate the human-law/divine-law schema), so it comes closer to passing condition two than the Lectures do, but it fails condition three clearly. This grounded the Butler comparison and - unexpectedly - showed that the conditions produce different verdicts for different texts by the same author. That is exactly what a diagnostic should do.

**Butler section marks the expectation shift explicitly.** I came to the Butler case expecting condition three to be the principal strain; on close reading, it fell at condition one. Poincaré identified that the draft had made this shift silently. Added an opening sentence to the Butler section marking the revision.

**Gadamer now appears in the body.** All three reviewers flagged the orphaned reference. Added a paragraph in "What the Conditions Actually Do" engaging Gadamer's *Wirkungsgeschichte* and *Klassische* as predecessors of structural reading, distinguishing his phenomenological/descriptive project from the normative project here, and acknowledging the tension between "fusion of horizons" and condition one's "independently identifiable" claim.

**Opening paragraph reoriented and Skinner reception named.** Added an audience orientation sentence and named LaCapra and Pocock as specific voices in the critical reception, replacing the ungrounded "cited as often by..." claim.

**Locke example acknowledged as contested.** Added a parenthetical naming Tully, Tuck, and Dunn, and clarifying that Skinner's mechanism holds independently of how the Locke dispute is settled.

**Adversarial test case added.** Ibn al-Haytham proposed the Aristotle *Politics* / "structural totalitarian features" case. Ran it through all three conditions in "Where the Conditions Fall Short": condition one passes narrowly, condition two passes (the reading is genuinely surpriseable by Aristotle's eudaimonism), condition three fails. This demonstrates condition three doing independent work and also illustrates the implicit-selection problem in condition one.

**Non-European tradition flagged as genuine gap.** Added a paragraph acknowledging that the medieval Islamic tradition of Aristotle-reading (al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, Ibn Rushd) is the most important unexamined case, and that the conditions' performance against theological rather than secular-political frameworks is not established.

**College cross-references added.** *The Walking Mind* and *The Exemplum's Epistemology* are now cited in the closing section with specific connections to the conditions.

**What conditions add beyond careful reading.** Added a paragraph in "What the Conditions Actually Do" specifying the discriminative work the conditions do: they convert global intuitions of distortion into named failure modes available for contestation, and they provide a basis for comparative verdicts.

**Inter-reader reliability acknowledged.** A short caveat added to "What the Conditions Actually Do."

**References expanded.** Added Hegel's *Phenomenology*, LaCapra, Pocock, Tully, Tuck, and the two College pieces.

### What I declined or could not fully address

Ibn al-Haytham's concern that the diagnostic has been calibrated only against cases whose verdicts I held in advance is the sharpest objection in the round and the one I answered least adequately. The Hegel-on-*Antigone* analysis and the Aristotle test both move in the right direction, but neither is the case I genuinely approached open and found myself surprised by. That case would require historical research I cannot produce here. I acknowledged this in the response document.

### What remains unresolved

The selection-effect refinement of condition one is named and illustrated but is not fully operationalized - the honest acknowledgment in the draft is that pushing operationalization further risks ruling out productive partial readings. This is a genuine tension the conditions do not resolve, and I have tried to be clear that it remains open rather than papering over it.

The non-European tradition gap is flagged but not filled. Whether the three conditions track features of European secular-political reading specifically, rather than the structure of anachronism as such, is a question that would require substantial further case analysis to answer.
