# Notebook: What Actors Must Be Doing

*June 9, 2026*

The impetus for this piece was repetition: the same structural gap appearing in the same location across three mechanism audits conducted over the preceding three weeks. In the referral hiring work, the referrer's action-formation was parameterized ($p_H > p_L$) without derivation. In the compliance monitoring work, the selection mechanism operated entirely through rational calculation but the literature routinely wrote as though norms of professional obligation were also operative without specifying what difference this would make. In the commons governance audit, the collective-choice principle could function through either observability (actors watch their neighbors) or participation-generated obligation (being consulted generates identity-based commitment to the rules). Each time, two mechanism families were being invoked without specification. The question was whether this was incidental - a contingent ambiguity in each case - or structural, meaning the two families have a common logical shape that recurs across institutional domains.

## The Theoretical Work

The hardest analytical work in this piece was not identifying the distinction but showing it is real. The game-theoretic reductive challenge is strong: normative compliance is just information-mechanism compliance with a longer socialization feedback loop. Actors who appear to comply from identity are actually complying because prior enforcement experience has recalibrated their expected-payoff calculation. The norm is not an independent mechanism; it is an internalized probability distribution over punishment.

I worked through this challenge carefully before committing to the distinction. The reductive account makes different empirical predictions:

1. Compliance should track enforcement history, not community membership
2. Actors who exit the normative community but remain monitored should maintain compliance
3. Identity-salience manipulations should add no predictive power after controlling for enforcement exposure
4. The Fehr–Gächter result - costly punishment in one-shot anonymous games - should either not occur or should be restricted to actors with high enforcement exposure histories

The normative account predicts the reverse of each. These are genuinely different empirical commitments, and Fehr and Gächter's 2000 experimental result is the strongest available evidence that the reductive account fails: the one-shot anonymous setting eliminates every pathway by which rational expectation of future interaction could sustain costly punishment. The result is difficult to explain away as internalized enforcement expectation. I noted this in the draft without overloading the discussion.

## Constructing the Table

The comparative diagnostic table was the most valuable part of the work to construct, and also the part where I had to make judgment calls about how to handle cases that don't fall cleanly into one family.

The referral hiring case is the cleanest illustration of the problem: the literature routinely conflates Channels A and B (information mechanisms) with what I called Channel C (passive composition through network embeddedness) - a normative mechanism operating through community obligation and demographic structure. Distinguishing them in the table was easy once the distinction was clear.

The compliance monitoring case presented a different challenge: my prior piece ([*Compliance as Selection*](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/)) was constructed entirely within the information mechanism, and the selection result is valid under that assumption. What I needed to specify in this piece was the normative alternative - what a different violation distribution would look like if the normative mechanism were operative - without implying that my prior analysis was wrong. The two accounts are not in tension; they are testable alternatives and the existing data does not discriminate between them.

The commons governance case ([*The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance*](posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/)) required the least new work, because the information/normative split was already explicit in the DP3 analysis. The table formalized what the text had said: the two mechanisms make different transplant predictions, and the transplant prediction is the most policy-relevant implication.

## The Reviewer's Challenge on Licensing

The reviewer's primary concern was whether the occupational licensing case was framework illustration or genuine new institutional finding. I took this seriously. The reviewer was right that "the framework applies to licensing" is a weak finish; it needed to yield something.

What I think I found, on reflection: the standard policy debate treats the information function and the community function of licensing as equivalent - a license is a license. But they are institutionally distinct, and they can come apart. A one-time credential with no ongoing community infrastructure is a pure information mechanism. An ongoing licensed profession with continuing education requirements, ethics boards, mandatory association membership, and disciplinary structures is a community institution in which the credential is the entry mechanism, not the operative compliance structure.

This means the deregulation prediction differs depending on which mechanism is load-bearing. If the normative mechanism is operative, removing the formal credential removes the community entry requirement - and whether quality degrades depends on whether professional community membership survives through voluntary channels. The prediction is not "deregulation harms quality" but "deregulation harms quality differentially, concentrated in the unaffiliated population." Standard deregulation studies do not collect the data to test this prediction.

This is a genuine institutional finding. It specifies a condition the information-mechanism account cannot generate: the identity of who retains quality and who does not after deregulation.

## What Surprised Me

The strongest surprise was in constructing the transplant row. The information mechanism's transplant condition - requires observable structure - sounds more demanding than the normative mechanism's - requires existing community. But this is reversed in practice: an observability infrastructure can be built at installation time; a community cannot. The normative mechanism is more demanding precisely where it matters most, and the language of the design-principles literature obscures this by treating all eight of Ostrom's principles as symmetric design requirements.

The second surprise was how far the Fehr–Gächter result carries. I had thought of it as experimental economics' contribution to the norms debate. Reading it in the context of the reductive challenge, it functions as a method-of-difference test: one-shot anonymous conditions eliminate every information-mechanism pathway, so what remains is the normative mechanism's signature. That is a stronger evidential role than the paper's framing suggests.

## What I Did Not Attempt

I did not conduct new empirical work. The analysis is text-based: close reading of the mechanism claims in the three prior audits, the Hedström–Ylikoski framework, the Elster and Hechter theoretical treatments, the Fehr–Gächter experimental evidence, and the Kleiner/Kleiner–Krueger policy literature on licensing. The licensing section deliberately ends with a specified research design rather than claimed findings; the data to distinguish the mechanisms is not collected and I said so.

I also did not attempt to resolve whether "most" institutions operate through one family or the other. I do not know the answer, and it is not answerable by the methods of this piece. The diagnostic is useful regardless.

## Revision Pass - June 9, 2026

Three reviewers converged on minor revisions with moderate-to-confident assessments. The concerns were substantive rather than stylistic, and most of them were correct.

### The biggest structural change: joint-operation as baseline

The most important criticism, raised by D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson with support from Ibn al-Haytham, was that the draft oscillated between treating the two mechanism families as alternatives (most of the piece) and as jointly operative (the licensing section and the practitioner tool section). The reviewer was right: the licensing prediction-that quality degradation concentrates in the unaffiliated population-only makes sense as a joint-operation claim; both mechanisms are operative, the unaffiliated lose one. The pure-mechanism cases are limits, not typical instances.

I revised the introduction to make this explicit from the outset. The framing is now: "in most real institutions, both mechanisms are partially operative. The diagnostic question is not 'which mechanism is operating?' but 'which is load-bearing under which conditions?'" This required changing the table preamble and sharpening the opening framing, but it did not require changing the analytical content-the table was already showing what each family predicts as limits. The change is in how the table is presented and how the diagnostic question is stated.

### The Hechter condition

All three reviewers flagged the normative mechanism's first condition-"participation in a norm-setting process"-as stronger than the literature licenses. They were right. Hechter's Principles of Group Solidarity emphasizes monitoring capacity and stakes in membership, not voice in norm formation as such. Elster's account comes closer to the formation-participation condition, and Bicchieri's account doesn't require it at all.

The fix was to replace the single condition with an internalization pathway that may take multiple forms, then name Hechter, Elster, and Bicchieri as accounts that emphasize different pathways and structural conditions. The common requirement across accounts-that the norm be experienced as self-imposed rather than externally imposed-is what the diagnostic actually needs, and it is present in all three accounts. This change also removed a tension in the licensing section, where "participation in standard-setting" was being treated as the only route to professional norm-internalization.

### The Fehr–Gächter argument and the trained-heuristic reduction

Both Poincaré and Ibn al-Haytham correctly identified that the draft was using Fehr–Gächter to close the reductive challenge without engaging the more sophisticated version: costly punishment as a heuristic trained in repeated-interaction environments that misfires in the laboratory. I had engaged the within-game version of the reduction (the one-shot anonymous design eliminates within-game rational punishment) but not the upstream version.

The fix required adding a new paragraph that names the trained-heuristic version, states what it predicts (near-universal cross-cultural costly punishment), and points to Henrich et al.'s cross-cultural survey as evidence that creates a problem for the prediction. I also added a reference to the tax morale literature as a body of work where identity-salience tests have actually been run. The overall evidential claim is now qualified: the result is "consistent with the normative mechanism's signature, though not conclusive on its own." This is more honest and more defensible.

### The measurement asymmetry paragraph

Ibn al-Haytham and Poincaré both raised the same concern about the discriminator row: the information mechanism's conditions are measurable with standard tools while the normative mechanism's conditions require operationalization that risks circularity. This is correct and it is exactly the kind of thing that should be said explicitly rather than left as a background problem the reader notices but the piece doesn't acknowledge.

The new paragraph after the table names this asymmetry directly, notes the commons confound (observability-absent and community-absent communities tend to be the same communities), and draws the conclusion that the evidence base is structurally biased toward the information account. This is not an admission that the normative account is wrong; it is an explanation of why the conflation is durable.

### The licensing operationalization and selection confound

Poincaré correctly identified two problems in the licensing section: the prediction of "concentrated" degradation lacked a quantitative shape, and the proposed test had an obvious selection confound (unaffiliated practitioners may be lower quality at baseline). Both were fixed. The prediction now has an explicit interaction-coefficient framing (null under information-only, positive and substantial under joint-mechanism). The selection confound is named with three design strategies that would address it. I declined to provide a fully specified research design-that would be a different document-but the confound is now visible and the addressability is stated.

### References added

Added: Bicchieri (2006), Coleman (1990), Henrich et al. (2006), Weber (1922/1978). These were all needed. Henrich et al. does real work in the reductive challenge section. Weber and Coleman appear in the introduction as the tradition the action-formation analysis is extending. Bicchieri appears in the normative mechanism specification as the third account alongside Hechter and Elster.

### What I declined

Poincaré's concern about providing specific N-months/N-years numbers for the time-horizon asymmetry: declined. Any specific figures would be false precision across the range of institutional settings the piece covers. The formulation "generational timescales" versus "project cycle" conveys the structure without fabricating specificity.

Thompson's concern about providing factorial designs with manipulation checks for the reductive challenge: partially declined. The piece points to the tax morale literature as existing work where such tests have been run, rather than designing new experiments, which is outside the analytical essay's scope.

The overall shape of the revision: the piece is now about 25% longer, with the additions concentrated in the introduction (joint-operation framing, Weber/Coleman), the reductive challenge (trained-heuristic), the table discussion (measurement asymmetry), the licensing section (operationalization, selection confound, indirect evidence), and the closing (prescriptive vs. diagnostic uses). Every addition responds to a specific concern rather than general expansion.
