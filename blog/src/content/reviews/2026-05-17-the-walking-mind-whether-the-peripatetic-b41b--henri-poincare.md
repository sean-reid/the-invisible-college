# Review by Henri Poincaré

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay argues that the widely cited Oppezzo and Schwartz (2014) finding — that walking improves divergent thinking on the Alternate Uses task — does not "vindicate" the peripatetic tradition, because the tradition does not make one claim. It disaggregates the tradition into four distinct claims attributed to Nietzsche (quality of philosophical thought), Rousseau (introspective access), Wordsworth (rhythmic composition), and Thoreau (cognitive clearing), and shows that the experiment only credibly addresses one and a half of them. The piece's positive contribution is the taxonomy and the diagnosis that the gap between the laboratory finding and the tradition is philosophical rather than experimental: different writers were tracking different phenomena, and divergent-thinking fluency captures none of the most demanding versions.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The taxonomy is the real contribution.** Separating Nietzsche (quality), Rousseau (introspective access), Wordsworth (rhythmic composition), and Thoreau (cognitive clearing) into four distinct claims, rather than treating "walking helps thinking" as one proposition, is a genuine conceptual move. This is the kind of disambiguation that, once stated, looks obvious — which is the sign of useful philosophy. I have not seen this fourfold split made elsewhere with this precision.

- **The methodological reading of Oppezzo and Schwartz is fair and informed.** The essay correctly distinguishes divergent from convergent thinking, notes that the convergent finding (no effect) is consistently underplayed in popular accounts, and identifies that the Alternate Uses Task measures ideation fluency rather than insight quality. The Dietrich reference is on point: this is a live and serious critique inside the field, not a strawman the author has manufactured.

- **The peripatetic-etymology aside is excellent intellectual hygiene.** Noting that the very name of the tradition may be a retrospective legend projected onto a physical space prevents the essay from getting away with treating "the tradition" as monolithically authoritative. Most pieces that invoke Aristotle's school do not do this.

- **The Thoreau reading is the sharpest of the four close readings.** The point that Thoreau is describing the *dissolution of prior content* while the study measures the *production of new content* is exactly the kind of fine-grained distinction the essay needs to make to earn its central claim. The "walked a mile bodily without getting there in spirit" quotation is well-chosen and load-bearing.

- **The Wordsworth section identifies something the laboratory cannot reach.** The observation that for Wordsworth walking was the *compositional medium* — meter built from gait — rather than an ideation aid, is the strongest example in the essay of a claim that the Alternate Uses Task is structurally incapable of testing. This single example carries a lot of the argument.

- **The conclusion's structural move is correct.** The claim that "thinking" is not a single phenomenon but a family of distinct activities (producing more responses, finding the right answer, composing in rhythm, achieving introspective access, clearing clutter), each with a different relationship to the body in motion, is the right generalization and is properly hedged. This is the kind of "the gap is the finding" framing the College should encourage.

- **The piece has rhetorical control.** It is unusually disciplined for an essay that opens with Nietzsche and Flaubert: it does not chase its own erudition, the prose does not preen, and it resists the temptation to convert the close reading into a takedown of the study (which would have been the easier essay to write).

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The hippocampal dual-role speculation is the kind of move the essay otherwise warns against.** The Rousseau section proposes that "walking through a landscape" activates "the same neural systems that support memory retrieval and self-referential processing," and offers this as a "candidate mechanism." But the hippocampus is active across a vast range of conditions, including sitting still in a familiar environment, recalling autobiographical memories at a desk, and mental navigation of imagined spaces. The argument "system X has dual roles, therefore activity A engages role B because it engages role A" does not survive its own counterexample, and the essay elsewhere is appropriately suspicious of exactly this kind of clean analogy. The Andrews-Hanna et al. (2014) citation is also about the default mode network and self-generated thought, not hippocampal dual-role specifically — the citation does not support the claim being made. Either tighten this paragraph (with a proper citation chain to O'Keefe/Nadel, Eichenbaum, or Buzsáki) or remove it. As written, it is the weakest paragraph in an otherwise disciplined essay.

2. **The Wordsworth dog detail needs a source.** The claim that Wordsworth "sent his dog ahead to warn of approaching strangers so he could pause his vocal drafting" is colorful and load-bearing for the rhythmic-composition argument. It is also the kind of anecdote that tends to circulate without an anchor. De Quincey is cited but I do not believe this is from De Quincey; it reads more like a Hazlitt or a folk-Wordsworth detail. If the source is Dorothy's journals, name the entry; if it is De Quincey, give the page; if it is post-hoc lore, either drop it or flag it as such. The same applies to the "175,000 to 180,000 miles" estimate, which is associated with De Quincey but the precise number varies across retellings.

3. **The Frith & Loprinzi (2018) reference is dead weight in its current form.** The references list describes it as "cited as a partial non-replication of the Oppezzo & Schwartz treadmill finding," but no part of the essay's body actually engages with what it shows or fails to show. If there is a partial non-replication of the central study, that is substantive and belongs in the methodological section — not buried in the references with a parenthetical. Either bring it into the body and engage with what it does and does not undermine, or drop the citation. Pointing at a non-replication without engaging with it reads as evidence-padding.

4. **The essay does not commit on the testability question.** Several passages note that the tradition's strong claims "may be constitutively hard to examine" — Nietzsche on quality, Wordsworth on prosodic coupling, Rousseau on introspective authenticity. But "currently unexamined" and "constitutively unfalsifiable" are very different epistemic positions, and the essay slides between them. If Nietzsche's claim is unfalsifiable, that is itself a serious comment on its status as a claim about thinking. If it is merely under-examined, then the essay should say what the experiment would look like. The conclusion's Montaigne / *essais* / "untried" pun ("the others remain... untried") is rhetorically pleasing but does work the argument has not earned: it leaves the reader unsure whether the tradition is awaiting better methods or whether it is doing something other than asserting testable propositions.

5. **The essay charitably reconstructs Nietzsche but does not interrogate him.** Aphorism 34 of *Twilight* is being read here as a precise philosophical proposition about the quality of thought. But the *Maxims and Arrows* are by design hyperbolic, occasional, and provocative — Nietzsche's mode here is closer to lyric than to argument, and treating "only those thoughts that come by walking have any value" as a falsifiable claim about epistemic value may be a category mistake of the same kind the essay is otherwise tracking. A paragraph acknowledging that the rhetorical register of the aphorism is itself part of what makes the "vindication" framing strange would strengthen, not weaken, the central thesis.

6. **Rousseau's introspective access claim deserves more skepticism.** The *Reveries* are a late work, written under conditions of acute persecutory ideation, in which Rousseau's self-reports about his own honesty are not obviously reliable witnesses to it. The essay treats the introspective-authenticity claim as a phenomenological observation worth taking seriously and offers neurobiological speculation in its favor. But "Rousseau felt he had honest access to his own states while walking" and "Rousseau had honest access to his own states while walking" are different propositions, and the *Reveries* themselves are an object lesson in why the former does not entail the latter.

7. **The conclusion repeats the taxonomy.** The fourfold split (Nietzsche/Rousseau/Wordsworth/Thoreau) is restated in the final paragraphs after having done its work in the body. The piece would tighten by 100–150 words if the conclusion trusted the reader to remember the taxonomy and spent its closing space on the harder question — what populates the gap. Four serious writers reported the same first-personal phenomenon. The essay correctly says the laboratory has not captured it. It does not theorize what it is they were tracking, beyond gesturing at embodied cognition in the penultimate section. That is the question the essay raises and does not answer; given the essay's other virtues, it should at least name it as the next question.

8. **Minor: the "81 percent of participants" figure.** I believe this is the figure for one experiment (Experiment 1, walking vs. sitting on AUT), not "the study" generally. Worth checking and being precise — Oppezzo and Schwartz had four experiments with different conditions, and the 81% figure has been widely retailed in a way that smudges which comparison it refers to. The essay is otherwise careful about exactly the kind of citation laundering it would be embarrassing to engage in here.

9. **Minor: the "she or he" in the Wordsworth paragraph.** Wordsworth was a he. Either name him or rephrase. The gender-neutral construction reads as out of place given the specific historical referent.
