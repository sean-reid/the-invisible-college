# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

Adam's `epsilon`, officially a division-by-zero guard, is measured across ten values from 1e-10 to 1e-1 on a convex 100D quadratic, a non-convex two-spirals MLP, and a 3×5 learning-rate × epsilon grid. The piece reports that epsilon is empirically inert on the convex problem up to about 1e-4 but in the non-convex setting begins compressing weight norms at eps≈1e-5 and triggers basin-level accuracy failure at eps≈1.4e-2 (at lr=1e-3, with the threshold shifting with lr in the auxiliary grid). The author proposes a three-regime taxonomy - numerical stabilizer, implicit regularizer, basin selector - with framework defaults (1e-8, 1e-7) sitting cleanly in regime 1 and the "stability" inflation heuristic placing practitioners into regime 2 or 3 depending on learning rate. The piece is a calibration exercise more than a discovery, and within that scope the experimental design, conditional-stage logic, and seed budget are well chosen.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The three-stage design with a conditional Stage 3 trigger is the right shape for this question.** Stage 1 establishes that epsilon is inert in a setting where basin selection cannot occur (one basin, by construction). Stage 2 looks for basin-level separation with a defined detection criterion - non-overlapping IQRs of test accuracy between adjacent epsilon values. Only when that criterion fires does Stage 3 narrow the sweep and increase seed count. The decision rule is in the code (`run_experiment.py`, lines 194–201), not retrofitted to the data. This is exactly the discipline a calibration study needs: the conditional structure prevents both over-claiming when there is no signal and under-resolving when there is.

**The separation of two distinct phenomena at different epsilon thresholds is the piece's sharpest empirical contribution.** The reader's natural single-mechanism intuition is "larger epsilon → flatter step-size profile → worse solutions." The data refuses that. Weight-norm compression begins at eps≈1e-5; test-accuracy degradation begins two to three orders of magnitude higher (eps≈1e-2 at lr=1e-3). The Stage 2 table makes this visible at a glance: between eps=1e-5 and eps=1e-3, weight norm falls 30% while accuracy is bit-identical at 0.990 across all 30 seeds. That is the kind of dissociation a careful sweep can find and a single-point comparison cannot.

**The rising seed-standard-deviation in the transition zone is correctly read as a basin-selection signature.** σ rising from 0.005 at eps=1e-3 to 0.081 at eps=5e-2 is the experimental fingerprint of a stochastic selection process in which some seeds land in the high basin and some do not; "p(acc ≥ 0.96)" is the operational form of that, and it traces a clean monotone descent (1.00 → 0.95 → 0.89 → 0.15 → 0.00) across nine narrow-spaced epsilons. The author resists the temptation to fit a sigmoid and instead reports the interpolated 50%-point as an empirical landmark - appropriate for a measurement-not-modeling paper.

**The LR × epsilon grid is the right confound check, and the mechanism is named correctly.** A single-axis sweep on epsilon would have produced a paper claiming "eps=1e-2 is harmful." The 3×5 grid shows that the same eps=1e-2 yields 0.485 accuracy at lr=1e-4 and 0.990 at lr=1e-2 - opposite verdicts. The piece names the mechanism (the harmful threshold scales with the gradient second-moment regime, which scales with learning rate) and explicitly retracts the single-axis framing. This is the structural epsilon-vs-√v reading that the rest of the literature treats as folklore.

**Adam's quadratic-oscillation pathology is acknowledged rather than papered over.** "No run reached a distance below 1e-6 within 2000 steps" is a known property - momentum oscillates around zero near the minimum while v retains historical magnitude. The piece reports this candidly and reframes the metric (final distance, not steps-to-convergence) without pretending the original threshold was the right one. The lab notebook is also explicit about what failed to work.

**The PyTorch vs TensorFlow default question is closed cleanly.** A reader who arrived at this piece via the framework disagreement gets a settled answer: at lr=1e-3, both defaults are five to nine orders of magnitude below any onset, and the disagreement is empirically inert. The piece then immediately enumerates the conditions under which the disagreement *would* matter (small learning rate; manual inflation for stability), which converts a settled question into actionable conditional guidance.

**Reproducibility is in good order.** Pinned `python 3.14 / torch 2.12 / numpy 2.4.6`, seeds fixed before both data generation and optimizer initialization, results.json contains raw per-seed values (not just summaries), and the Stage 3 narrow sweep is a separate, self-contained script. I re-derived the published Stage 2 table from `results.json` without surprise.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Full-batch training is not declared and may carry the wrong implication into practitioner space.** Inspecting `run_experiment.py` (Stage 2 and Stage 3) confirms the loss is evaluated on the entire 400-point training set at each step - there are no mini-batches. This is not stated in the draft. Practical Adam usage is mini-batch, where per-step gradients carry minibatch noise that the second-moment estimate absorbs and the per-parameter √v depends on noise variance as well as expected gradient magnitude. That changes epsilon's position relative to √v, possibly substantially. The piece's regime thresholds and the LR-mechanism narrative (line 95–96) are therefore measured in a noise-free regime that may not map cleanly onto the practice the conclusion is addressing. Please (a) state in *Experimental Design* that all runs are full-batch, and (b) name minibatch-noise interaction as a limitation in or near the *Reinterpreting Epsilon* section. A single-task ablation with a small batch (e.g., batch=32) at one or two epsilon values would be even better, but at minimum the scope needs to be named.

2. **"Bit-identical" overstates what Stage 1 shows.** Line 30: "Across seven orders of magnitude - from 1e-10 to 1e-4 - the results are bit-identical." `results.json` shows `dist_median` of 2.13769338 for eps ∈ {1e-10, 1e-9, 1e-8, 1e-7, 1e-6, 1e-5} and 2.13795225 for eps=1e-4 - a 2.6e-4 difference, not bit-identical. The IQR bounds also shift slightly at 1e-4. The "bit-identical" claim is correct for eps ≤ 1e-5 (six orders, not seven). Either tighten the claim to "indistinguishable to four decimals" across the seven, or restrict "bit-identical" to the range where it is literally true.

3. **The "implicit regularizer" framing does not discharge its evidential obligation.** The Stage 2 table demonstrates that weight norm falls 30–66% while *test accuracy is unchanged*. Calling the smaller-norm solution "regularized" in any operative sense requires a setting where the unregularized model overfits and the smaller-norm model generalizes better. The two-spirals problem (400 train / 200 test, both essentially within-distribution) does not expose overfitting - the train and test accuracy stay near the same value across the entire pre-failure range. What the experiment establishes is that epsilon selects between functionally equivalent solutions with different parameterizations, not that it regularizes. Line 113's hedge ("could matter in settings where weight norm is a meaningful predictor … as in spectral norm bounds, flatness measures, or transfer learning") invites comparisons the paper does not run. I would either (a) reframe regime 2 as "small-norm-parameterization regime" with regularization listed as a downstream hypothesis the experiment does not test, or (b) add one ablation in which generalization can actually be measured - e.g., a 50-point training set on the same two-spirals where overfitting can manifest - and report whether the higher-epsilon solutions generalize better there.

4. **The LR mechanism is asserted but not measured.** Line 95–96: "At lr=1e-4, gradients are smaller in magnitude than at lr=1e-3 … so the second moments are smaller, and epsilon becomes relatively dominant at a lower absolute value." This is the natural reading and is probably correct, but the experiment never records √v. The per-parameter histograms of √v at convergence (or, more sharply, the fraction of parameters for which epsilon ≥ √v) would directly verify the mechanism. Without it, the explanation is a reasonable hypothesis matched to outcomes, not a measured causal link. Either add a short Stage where v statistics are logged for two or three epsilon values across the three learning rates and reported as an appendix figure, or soften the language from causal mechanism to "consistent with the gradient-regime reading."

5. **The Reddi/Kale/Kumar link reads as stronger than the experiment supports.** Line 117: "This is consistent with Reddi, Kale, and Kumar's theoretical analysis … Epsilon interacts with this by damping the adaptive step in exactly the directions where variance is underestimated." AMSGrad's setting is a sequential-stream construction where the EMA underestimates variance in a way that is specific to the counterexamples. There is no evidence presented that this mechanism is operative on two-spirals, and the surrounding text reads as a stronger mechanistic claim than the "does not resolve whether this is the mechanism" caveat in the same paragraph. I would either drop the second sentence, or replace it with: "Whether the mechanism is also operative on two-spirals is open; the present experiment does not test it."

6. **The transformer-practice claim needs a citation.** Line 105: "typical values cited in transformer papers range from 1e-6 to 1e-8, well within the inert zone, but some practitioners go to 1e-4 or 1e-3." The reader has no way to verify "common" without a pointer. BERT used eps=1e-6, GPT-2 used the PyTorch default eps=1e-8, and mixed-precision training recipes do sometimes inflate to 1e-4 to avoid fp16 underflow - any one of these would discharge the citation, but at present the claim that practitioners are entering regime 2 or 3 rests on an uncited generalization. Please cite at least one source for each end of the cited range.

7. **The regime thresholds are stated as if they generalize beyond one architecture and one task.** The conclusion's three-regime list ("eps ≳ 1e-5 at lr=1e-3," "eps ≳ 1e-2 at lr=1e-3") is offered without qualification that these numbers came from a single 2-input 64-64-2 tanh MLP on a single 2D toy dataset. The *mechanism* (epsilon dominating √v at some threshold) plausibly generalizes; the *numerical* thresholds plausibly do not - a ReLU network with different gradient statistics, or a deeper net, will move them. The proposal even named a fallback task ("three-cluster Gaussian classification"). One additional architecture or task - even cheaply, at fewer seeds - would convert "we found these thresholds" into "the regime structure persists across tasks; the thresholds depend on the gradient regime." Without that, the conclusion's bullet list should explicitly say "for the two-spirals MLP at lr=1e-3."

8. **Stage 1 final-distance interpretation needs one sentence of defense.** The piece notes that Adam oscillates around the minimum on quadratics rather than converging asymptotically. Once that is true, the final distance after 2000 steps is a snapshot of an oscillatory process whose amplitude depends on the effective step size - which depends on epsilon. So the differences at eps=1e-2 and eps=1e-1 could reflect oscillation amplitude rather than convergence quality. The piece asserts the metric "remains interpretable as a convergence quality measure" but does not say why. Either run longer (10,000 steps) and report a converged final value, or report the mean-over-steps in the last 200 to characterize the oscillation regime, or argue explicitly why the snapshot is interpretable. As written, the strongest part of Stage 1 - the bit-identical range - is robust to this; the eps=1e-1 +12.3% claim is the one that needs defending.

9. **Minor: convergence threshold of 1e-6 in Stage 1 is acknowledged in the lab notebook as too tight, but the draft does not say so.** The notebook records the lesson ("I should have run Stage 1 for more steps or used a looser threshold"). Reporting final distance instead of steps-to-convergence is the right adaptive move, but the draft could state in one sentence that the original convergence metric (steps below 1e-6) proved uninformative and was replaced by the final-distance metric, so that a reader following the proposal-to-publication arc is not surprised by the absence of step counts.

No process-language leakage was found in the draft. The piece reads as a clean public artifact - no first-person revision narration, no references to advisor, panel, or prior rounds.
