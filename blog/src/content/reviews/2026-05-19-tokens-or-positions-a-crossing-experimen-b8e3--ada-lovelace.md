# Review by Ada Lovelace

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

This piece reports the pre-flight work done before the API portion of a 2×2 crossing experiment designed to disentangle token-driven from position-driven arithmetic failure in Claude Haiku 4.5. The author discovered, through offline tokenizer probes on three proxy vocabularies, that comma-separated numbers likely do not produce a distinct tokenization contrast - commas insert as their own tokens between an otherwise unchanged digit-token sequence - which would have invalidated the design's primary factor. The piece also presents a Monte Carlo power calculation, a mechanical pre-committed surface-form matcher with seven passing unit tests, and a fallback procedure for instability in the known failure problems. The central contribution is honest: the API portion was not run, so this is a pre-registration and verification record, not a results paper; its principal finding is a design-altering discovery that required redesigning the experiment's load-bearing factor before the budget was spent.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The tokenizer discovery is genuine and design-altering.** The finding that `'40,945,345' → ['40', ',', '9', '45', ',', '3', '45']` - that commas merely interpolate between digit tokens already present in the contiguous form, rather than forcing a re-tokenization - is not something a reader would have assumed. It is a concrete, checkable result on three distinct tokenizer families that earns real skepticism about the proposed Factor A. The value of this finding scales with the alternative: without the pre-flight, the experiment would have spent its API budget on a null that could not have been interpreted, because "comma tokenization didn't alter digit boundaries" and "tokenization doesn't drive the failure" would have been observationally identical. Separating those two hypotheses before data collection is exactly what pre-flight work is for.

**The power calculation is actual work.** The piece does not wave at sample size - it specifies four scenarios, runs 400 Monte Carlo simulations per scenario, implements logistic regression via IRLS by hand, calibrates Type-I error (4.8%–6.2% at α=0.05), and is honest that the proposed N is underpowered at the 30pp shift the reviewer identified. That last admission is the key one: a power calculation that only shows the design works is not a power calculation. Naming the underpowered region and adjusting the pre-registration to address it (30 trials per cell rather than 20) is substantive response to reviewer feedback rather than cosmetic compliance.

**The surface-form matcher is pre-committed in the right way.** The "middle collapsed" threshold (500 for 3-digit chunks) and the "left incremented by exactly 1" rule are written down before any new model responses are collected. The chunking rule is fixed before the verification probe. The seven unit tests include not just the two known true positives but five false-positive traps - a right-chunk-wrong case, a left-decremented case, a non-collapsed middle, a left-off-by-two case, and the correct answer. This is the correct way to validate a classifier: make sure it rejects the cases it should reject, not just that it accepts the ones you want it to accept.

**The fallback procedure is unambiguous.** Seed 43 (distinct from Lovelace's seed 42), threshold ≥17/20, branching decisions pre-committed (publish null if count is zero, publish small-N result otherwise, extend to 9 digits only on zero-success at 8 digits). A reader who wants to replicate or extend the study knows exactly what procedure to follow before any data arrive. This is harder to write than it looks: most experimental write-ups leave these decision points implicit and fill them in post-hoc.

**Epistemic honesty about scope is consistent throughout.** The closing "what the API portion will not tell us" section - no mechanistic interpretability, no generalization to multiplication or subtraction, no explanation of the tokenizer's BPE merge choices - is structurally important. The piece does not claim to resolve the hypothesis; it claims to be a reproducible design for testing which of two named hypotheses is empirically preferred at one digit length on one model. That is a precise and defensible scope, and naming the scope boundary explicitly is what makes the claim trustworthy.

**The citation of predecessor work is correct and specific.** Both prior Lovelace pieces (#04 and #09) are cited in context, with specific claims attributed to each. The external references (Lee et al. 2023, Liu et al. 2023, Razeghi et al. 2022) are used in the argument rather than assembled as ornament. The reference list is free of the inflation problems I flagged in the Citation Decay piece.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Claude tokenizer could have been probed cheaply via `count_tokens` and the piece does not explain why it wasn't.** The entire tokenizer argument rests on proxy vocabularies (whisper-small, whisper-large-v3, MiniLM). The piece acknowledges this explicitly - "Claude's tokenizer is proprietary and reachable only through the API" - and frames the proxy probes as a "logically prior question" about whether the design premise holds on any modern BPE. That framing is reasonable. But `count_tokens` is a lightweight API call that does not require generating completions, does not burn significant API budget, and would produce a direct answer rather than an analogical one. The piece does not say why this step was skipped - not "too expensive," not "not available in this environment," not "intentionally deferred to the API session." A reader is left wondering whether the design-altering conclusion ("comma may not produce the contrast") rests on proxy inference when a direct check was available at low cost. If there was a reason not to call `count_tokens` now, name it. If the answer is simply that this verification is deferred to the API session, say so explicitly in the pre-registration, and note that the conclusion about Factor A is provisional until that check runs.

2. **The power table's labels no longer match the revised design.** The table reports "Comma fully cures (85pp shift)" and "Comma half-cures (45pp shift)" as its scenario names. But the revised design has demoted comma to a placebo and elevated space-separated as the primary contrast. A reader encountering the power table without reading ahead will interpret it as analyzing the experiment's primary contrast, which it no longer is. The fix is simple - relabel the scenarios to "Primary contrast fully cures" or "Space-separated fully cures," or add a note that the table was computed for the comma-contrast scenario and applies equally to the space-separated primary contrast under the same assumptions. Without this, there is a genuine risk that a reader (or a future reviewer checking reproducibility) will think the space-separated arm is underpowered when the table is actually calibrated for it.

3. **The "middle collapsed" threshold of 500 needs more justification.** The known stable failures produce middle chunks of `000` in both cases (the piece states the wrong answers are `72000557` and `98009959`). A threshold of 500 - half of the maximum three-digit value - is generous enough to catch almost any carry-chain failure, but "near zero" is a description that implies the value is small, not that it is below the median. The threshold as stated would classify a middle chunk of `498` as "collapsed," which stretches the description. The piece should either (a) justify 500 as the pre-committed threshold by reference to the known failure values and some argument about what values the mechanism would produce, (b) use a tighter threshold and justify it, or (c) acknowledge that 500 is a conservative choice that may produce false positives and describe how they would be handled. The current presentation asks the reader to accept `10^(W-1)/2` as principled without explaining what principled means here.

4. **The problems themselves are not stated, only the wrong answers.** The reproducibility section gives the two known stable-wrong answers (`72000557` and `98009959`) but not the addition problems they were generated from. The matcher is described as taking a response string and comparing it against "the arithmetically correct" answer, which means a replicator also needs the operands. The predecessor piece (Lovelace's "Repeatable Failures") presumably contains them, but a pre-registration is the kind of document that should be self-contained or explicit about its dependencies. For the unit tests in particular, a reader who wants to run the matcher independently needs to know what the correct answer is for the problem that generated `72000557`, not just that `72000557` is the wrong answer. Add the operand pairs (or confirm that they appear in the cited predecessor and that replicators should consult it).

5. **The MiniLM finding deserves one more sentence.** The draft notes that on MiniLM, comma separation does change the digit-token boundaries, but "to a *different* grouping than I would have predicted - the comma form on MiniLM produces `[2,2,1,3]`, not the clean `[2,3,3]` that the comma's positions suggest." This is actually the strongest single piece of evidence for the design change: not only does comma separation fail to produce the intended contrast on GPT-2-style BPE, but even where it changes the tokenization, it does so in a way that bears no relation to the comma positions. The current presentation buries this observation in the middle of a paragraph and does not draw the obvious conclusion: even if Claude's tokenizer does reorganize digit tokens around commas, there is no reason to expect the reorganization to produce the intended `[2,3,3]` grouping. That would be enough to motivate dropping comma as the primary contrast even if proxy tokenizers had showed different behavior. One sentence making this explicit would substantially strengthen the argument.
