# Review by Charles Sanders Peirce

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The draft proposes three diagnostic conditions for distinguishing productive cross-domain argumentative borrowing from terminological transfer: the mechanism (not merely vocabulary) must transfer; evidential obligations must travel with the mechanism; and the framework must generate non-trivial constraints the target domain could not have framed independently. Testing this framework against Darwin (borrowing from Malthus), Freud (borrowing from Helmholtz), Rawls (borrowing from rational choice theory), and Foucault (borrowing from Nietzsche), the author argues that what matters is not structural correspondence alone but whether the source domain's accountability to evidence survives the crossing. The three conditions together provide a tool for evaluating when borrowed frameworks genuinely extend analytical reach versus when they supply only vocabulary that carries the form but not the substance of the source.

## Strengths

1. **The core contribution is genuinely novel and addresses a real gap.** Existing frameworks (Hesse, Gentner, Bartha) ask whether structural correspondence is preserved. This draft adds a substantive requirement: do the *evidential obligations* that make the source framework accountable travel with the mechanism? The Freud case-perfect relational alignment but zero evidential inheritance-demonstrates why this addition is necessary.

2. **The three conditions emerge from careful cases rather than abstract theorizing.** Each condition is motivated by what Darwin does that Freud doesn't, or what Rawls does differently from both. The author resists the temptation to derive principles from first principles and instead extracts them from the cases themselves.

3. **The prose is exceptionally disciplined.** Complex inferential moves are rendered with structural clarity. The author can explain why Darwin's evidential obligations travel (tracking variation, measuring differential survival, examining fossil records) while Freud's do not (no way to measure cathexis or confirm conservation law) without rhetorical hand-waving.

4. **The engagement with prior literature is sophisticated and honest.** Rather than claiming prior frameworks are wrong, the author shows they are necessary but not sufficient. The relationship to Hesse, Gentner, and Bartha is clearly stated: they establish structural conditions the present framework builds upon.

5. **The Foucault case strengthens the framework by showing it accommodates productive *transformation*.** The argument that transformation can preserve or even strengthen evidential commitment (Foucault grounding genealogy in archives more rigorously than Nietzsche) is a real insight. It shows the framework is sensitive to mechanism-preserving changes that don't simply fail condition 2.

6. **The Rawls borderline case is handled with intellectual honesty.** Rather than forcing it into the three-condition frame, the author names it as a distinct region-"structured question-setting"-where the borrowed apparatus generates a precise question but doesn't carry evidential obligations through to the conclusion. This transparency about the framework's limits is a strength, not a weakness.

7. **Self-citation is handled transparently.** The author directly acknowledges prior work on exempla and anachronism, explains why the present essay is not reducible to those pieces, and describes how transfer conditions must be derived from the cases themselves. This preempts confusion without defensive elaboration.

## Concerns

1. **The claim that psychic energy "has no physical correlate" and cannot be measured needs more rigorous support.** The draft asserts this as fact, but demonstration would strengthen the argument. Did Freud himself acknowledge this unmeasurability? Can the author specify what a measurement procedure would require and show why it is impossible in principle (not merely undeveloped)? The contrast with Darwin only works if we have confidence that the Freudian barrier is categoric, not practical.

2. **Condition 2 needs clearer operationalization of what counts as "motivated by the mechanism."** The draft states the test as "observations exist that would confirm or disconfirm the mechanism, and those observations are motivated by the mechanism rather than available independently of it." But naturalists were studying variation and fossils before Darwin. What does it mean for observation to be "motivated by" the mechanism versus merely "reinterpreted through" it? The boundary between genuine motivation and post-hoc narrative rationalization could be sharper.

3. **The Rawls case suggests the three conditions may not be complete.** The draft introduces "structured question-setting" as a category the conditions handle imperfectly. But this raises the question: are there principles that would handle Rawls cleanly, or is the incompleteness a sign the framework needs modification? The author acknowledges this (line 107: "whether there is a fourth condition...that handles question-setting uses more precisely-that is work this essay opens rather than closes"), but readers should know this is an open question, not a resolved one.

4. **The Foucault case asserts that archival evidence is "more accountable" to evidence than Nietzsche's philosophical genealogy, but this comparison needs more support.** Nietzsche's genealogy of morality in *On the Genealogy of Morality* is also grounded in evidence-historical interpretation, psychological analysis, cultural observation. The draft claims transformation strengthens rather than weakens accountability, but doesn't explain in detail why archives are more evidentially constraining than interpretive historical analysis. What specific evidentiary pressure do archives impose that Nietzsche lacked?

5. **The selection problem is significant and not resolved by adding one more case.** The author acknowledges (line 105) that cases are "canonical and selection is not random," derived from four figures whose borrowings are exceptionally well-documented. Darwin, Freud, Rawls, and Foucault are exactly the borrowings historians and philosophers have written about extensively. Would these conditions work on lesser-known or failed borrowings? On terminological transfer that never caught on and left no trace? The Foucault check is helpful, but five canonical cases still leave open the question of representativeness.

6. **Condition 3 (non-trivial constraint) would benefit from clarification of the baseline.** The draft says the borrowed framework must "rule out possibilities or generate questions that the target domain's existing apparatus could not have framed without it." But what counts as the target domain's "existing apparatus"? Pre-Darwin naturalists could study variation and observe differential survival-Darwin's innovation is connecting them mechanistically. Does this satisfy condition 3 or is it too modest to count as "non-trivial"? The bar for what constitutes a genuine new constraint could be more explicit.

7. **The framework assumes one-directional transfer; it may not handle reverse, iterative, or mutually transformative borrowing.** All four cases flow one way: Darwin from Malthus, Freud from Helmholtz, Rawls from decision theory, Foucault from Nietzsche. What about cases where transfer is bidirectional, or where a mechanism returns to its source domain transformed? The framework might need extension to handle borrowings that are not simple cross-domain transplants.
