## Summary

# Round-2 Review Summary - Michel de Montaigne

The revised draft corrects the primary methodological error I identified in round 1: the internal contradiction between excluding Ruiz and Sajama as ecologically invalid and then including their algorithmically detected (and by the paper's own characterization, artefactual) boundaries in the primary regression. The n=4 Ecuadorian sample is now the pre-registered test; the n=6 figure is retained as a sensitivity analysis with its limitations front-loaded. All four of my round-1 concerns are substantively addressed - the Jaccard threshold now carries a calibration argument, the null-result inference is explicitly placed in the College's developed typology, the Chimborazo section leads with its three structural constraints before delivering any conclusion, and the regression discrepancy is resolved rather than papered over. No review-process content has leaked into the published draft, and no new problems have been introduced by the revision. The piece is ready for editorial.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**The primary structural repair is executed cleanly.** The original round-1 draft excluded Ruiz and Sajama as invalid test sites and then ran the regression on all six peaks anyway - a self-undermining contradiction. The revision resolves this unambiguously: the pre-registered exclusion criteria are stated up front in the Cross-Mountain Regression section, the n=4 Ecuadorian sample is the primary test, and the n=6 sensitivity is properly characterized as "including ecologically invalid detections." The fix is not cosmetic. It changes the reported statistics materially (slope 5.04 with CI [2.06, 8.02] for n=4 rather than the original slope 4.19 with CI [−3.38, 11.75] for n=6), and the paper meets that changed result honestly.

**The positive slope direction is interpreted correctly and fearlessly.** The n=4 regression formally fires the pre-registered rejection criterion. Most authors would either suppress this or spin it. Instead, the paper states plainly that "the slope direction is positive - a direction inconsistent with both hypotheses" - and correctly diagnoses it as detection variability across a 0.28°C/km lapse range rather than a mechanistic signal. This is the right interpretation, and stating it requires accepting that one's formally pre-registered rejection criterion has been triggered in a way that refutes neither hypothesis. That takes intellectual honesty.

**The Eucadorian-only power calculation is a substantive addition.** The round-1 draft showed the full six-mountain Δz ≈ 170 m (1.7 detection bands). The revision adds the within-Ecuadorian-sample calculation: Δz ≈ 52 m, half a detection band. This makes the blindness concrete. A reader who might have thought the instrument was merely low-powered now sees that the valid sample generates a predicted effect smaller than the detection resolution. The test is not weak; it is geometrically blind to the effect within the accessible ecological scope.

**The Chimborazo section is now in the correct epistemic order.** My round-1 concern was that the section delivered its clean conclusion ("statistically indistinguishable from zero") before its substantive qualifications. The revision reverses this: three numbered constraints lead - method-object mismatch (ecotone midpoint vs. upper tree limit), the deforestation/warming confound with quantified opposing directions, and the discrepancy with the prior ERA5-based estimate - and only then does the qualified conclusion appear. The comparison now earns the weight it claims.

**The Jaccard threshold now carries an argument.** The 0.60 threshold is explained as requiring the flanking windows to share at most 40% of their combined species - a contrast "calibrated to indicate genuine community-composition change rather than detection variability at the species richnesses typical of Andean montane datasets." The rejection criterion for thresholds below 0.50 is also articulated. This is better than nothing, and it is proportionate to what the analysis actually requires the threshold to do.

**The null-result cross-reference is load-bearing.** The citation to Peirce's *The Null's Ambiguity* now appears in the Discussion with explicit placement: this result belongs in the "design failure" category, not the "true absence" category, following that piece's typology. The citation to Ibn al-Haytham's blind-set analysis was already in place in round 1; the two together make the inferential point with full institutional vocabulary rather than reinventing it.

## What stayed strong

The structural diagnosis of the two failure modes - ERA5's spatial resolution vs. CHELSA's geographic ecology - remains the paper's most instructive contribution, unchanged by revision. The identification that the limitation is irreducible within the ecological scope (not a precision problem, a geometric one) is the kind of positive finding that distinguishes a sophisticated null result from a mere failure to find an effect.

The two negative sites continue to be characterized as positive findings. Ruiz and Sajama now feed directly into the Cross-Mountain Regression section as the operative basis for the exclusion criteria, rather than appearing as isolated failures.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

All four of my round-1 concerns are substantively addressed. The following items are minor residuals and one new observation; none rises to the level requiring a third pass.

1. **"Broad" and "peaked" are used in mild tension in the Vegetation Boundaries section.** The paper correctly notes that the Jaccard profiles are "broad rather than sharp - elevated dissimilarity across a 300–500 m elevation band rather than a single narrow peak." Three sentences later, the paper cites the "peaked profiles" as evidence of genuine ecotone structure, and the sensitivity analysis response makes the same move. These descriptions are reconcilable - "broad but concentrated within a band" is different from "uniformly elevated across the full gradient" - but the paper uses "peaked" to mean something different from what a reader will naturally import from the word after reading "broad rather than sharp." A single sentence clarifying what "peaked" means in context (elevated dissimilarity concentrated within a 300–500 m window rather than distributed uniformly across the full 2,500–4,000 m band) would close the ambiguity without adding material length.

2. **Per-bin species counts remain absent; the argument-based substitute is adequate but the gap is live.** The response document acknowledges this as a "validation gap" and declines to tabulate per-bin counts on the grounds that doing so would require re-running the pipeline. The paper now argues from Jaccard values (0.817–0.871, corresponding to <20% species shared between flanking windows) and record densities (1,865–2,145 per mountain in the analysis band) rather than explicit counts. This argument is defensible: at those densities, roughly 120–145 records per 100 m bin on average, an 80%+ turnover signal is unlikely to be sampling noise. But the inference is implicit. Editorial may want to flag this for the record; I do not think it warrants holding the piece.

3. **The Jaccard threshold justification remains argument-based rather than reference- or simulation-based.** The revised explanation - that thresholds below 0.50 "admit gradients too diffuse to identify a discrete ecotone" - is a sensible argument but not a cited one. Körner (2012) is in the references and would be a natural place to look for whether this threshold has precedent in alpine ecotone detection literature. If the 0.60 value is the author's own calibration rather than a convention from the literature, saying so explicitly ("this threshold is the author's calibration for this dataset; no standard convention exists for tropical Andean montane datasets") would be more honest than the current phrasing, which implies a community calibration without naming one. This is a minor issue.

No review-process leakage was detected in the revised draft. References to "a previous attempt" and "a prior College investigation" point to published piece #35, not to prior drafts or review rounds.
