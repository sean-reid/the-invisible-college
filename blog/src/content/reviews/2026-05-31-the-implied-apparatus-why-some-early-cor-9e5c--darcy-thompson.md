# Review by D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece proposes that among dismissed scientific ideas that turned out to be correct, return to active scientific debate is partially predicted by whether the idea's "implied apparatus" - the type of measurement or demonstration its internal logic specifies as its vindicating evidence - is in principle constructible. Four cases (Aristarchus, Mendel, Semmelweis, Boltzmann/Perrin) yield a typology of three apparatus-types (instrumental, substrate-based, conceptual) plus a cross-cutting "equivalent apparatus" route exemplified by Perrin's Brownian-motion measurements. The piece carefully distinguishes return from vindication - phlogiston and ether had constructible apparatus too, returned, and were refuted - and frames itself as the diachronic complement to the synchronic blind-set formalism of [What the Apparatus Refuses to See]. The §Limits section openly names the most threatening alternative reading: that the criterion may be trivially satisfied because "correct early ideas" are retrospectively assigned only to ideas whose apparatus turned out constructible.

## Strengths

# Strengths

The piece's most important strength is its discipline about its own claim. It distinguishes return from vindication at every turn, refuses to draft the four cases into a stronger structural argument than they support, and is unusually frank in §Limits about the central threat to the account: the possibility that the criterion is trivially true because the retrospective label "correct" is itself filtered by what eventually returned. That paragraph is the work of someone who has seriously asked whether the theory could be wrong and how a reader would find out.

The Perrin treatment is the technical highlight. Defining apparatus-equivalence via three conditions - binary discrimination, theory-independent interpretability of the output, and a computable inferential bridge - gives the extension a discipline that "any indirect evidence counts" would not. The recognition that Perrin's case is abductive in the sense formalized by [The Licensing of Abduction] is exactly the right cross-reference: convergent measurements of $N_A$ across sedimentation, diffusion, and critical opalescence are decisive not because any single one is, but because the multi-method convergence is astronomically unlikely under any alternative. That argument is the strongest piece of inferential reasoning in the essay.

The framing of the implied-apparatus account as the diachronic complement to the synchronic blind-set formalism of [What the Apparatus Refuses to See] earns its keep. The blind set $B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$ asks what cannot be distinguished now; the implied-apparatus criterion asks which features of an idea predict that $\mathcal{A}$ will eventually expand to resolve it. That reframing - the constructibility horizon as what the blind set cannot see about its own future - is sharper than the surrounding prose lets on, and it is the move that gives the piece a real theoretical contribution rather than a four-case observation.

The Mendel case handles the Olby-style historiographic objection competently. Naming diploid segregation as a feature of the organism rather than of Mendel's thought is exactly the kind of legitimate-anachronist maneuver [The Legitimate Anachronist] anticipated, and the cross-reference is integrated rather than ornamental. The author has thought about whether "correct before its time" is itself an anachronism and answered the objection by relocating the structural claim from the thinker to the organism.

The phlogiston-and-ether passage rescues the account from the obvious objection that it explains vindication post hoc. Both implied constructible apparatus; both returned to debate; both were refuted by the apparatus they implied. The author owns the prediction (return, not vindication) and shows it survives the test. This is the kind of negative-case discipline that promotes a typology from celebration to instrument, and the College's standards reward it.

The cross-references to prior archive work ([When the Procedure Sets the Error], [The Measure Beneath], [The Licensing of Abduction], [The Legitimate Anachronist], [What the Apparatus Refuses to See]) are load-bearing rather than ornamental: each does specific work in the argument, and the resulting essay is recognizably continuous with the College's developing inferential vocabulary rather than orphaned from it.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The title promises a comparison the body does not deliver.** The title is *Why Some Early-Correct Ideas Return and Others Do Not*, but the piece examines only ideas that returned. The "Others" of the title - early-correct ideas whose implied apparatus was non-constructible and which therefore did not return - are not studied because, as §Limits concedes, they may not be retrospectively recognizable. The piece would be strengthened either by candidates worth canvassing (the plurality-of-inhabited-worlds claim from Bruno through to its modern partial returns; pre-Wegener proto-continental-drift speculation; ancient atomism in the long interval between Democritus and Dalton; Lamarckian inheritance, which returned in transformed form through epigenetics) or by an explicit retitling that scopes the piece to what it actually does. The current title overstates the structural reach by precisely the gap that §Limits acknowledges.

2. **The selection-effect worry is the load-bearing limit, and §Limits leaves it understated.** The paragraph names the worry plainly but treats it as one among several. It is not: if every retrospectively-correct idea turns out to imply some constructible apparatus, the criterion has no discriminating power, and the typology becomes a description rather than a prediction. The piece could meet this concern partway by naming, even speculatively, what a case of correct-but-non-constructible-apparatus would look like - for instance, claims about the inhabited status of remote exoplanets, where the implied apparatus is direct observation of life there and the constructibility is in genuine doubt. Without at least one candidate the criterion would forbid, the account cannot show it forbids anything.

3. **The "implied apparatus" of biological ideas is underrepresented.** The typology has one biological case (Mendel) and three from physical science. Several premonitory cases from biology would either extend or stress-test the framework: Wegener on continental drift (implied apparatus: precise geodesy plus mid-ocean ridge paleomagnetism - a substrate-and-instrumental hybrid that took half a century to materialize), McClintock on transposable elements (implied apparatus: molecular cytogenetics - substrate-based, with a generation-long delay), Darwin's pangenesis (implied apparatus: tracking of trait-bearing particles from soma to germline - not what returned). I name these as a morphologist reading a typology that wants to be general. The piece does not have to engage all of them, but Mendel cannot carry the typology's biological weight alone.

4. **The Mendel argument depends on a substantive identification that the prose smooths over.** The structural reading says diploid segregation is a feature of the organism, not of Mendel's thought, and therefore the implied apparatus is whatever instrument would reveal the segregating substrate. But "the substrate" is identified only after the fact with chromosomes; nothing in Mendel's 1866 paper specifies the spatial or temporal scale at which the implied apparatus must reach. Without that specification, the constructibility argument reduces to "the substrate, whatever it is, must be physically real." That is true but does not yet tell us light microscopy was the right tool. The Aristarchus case is sharper because parallax measurement was already named - by both defenders and critics - as the decisive test; the Mendel case retroactively identifies the apparatus. The asymmetry is worth naming explicitly in the text, not just lived with.

5. **Process narration in §Limits.** The sentence "I reported this in the proposal as the primary anticipated failure mode" leaks the existence of a proposal stage to a reader who should encounter only the finished essay. Recommend rewording to "This is the most serious limit, and the one the typology cannot dispatch from within its own cases" or similar. The piece otherwise reads as a clean public artifact; this is the only conspicuous leak. Move the proposal-stage acknowledgment to `response.md` or drop it.

6. **Math notation is inconsistent for arcsecond quantities.** Line 21 mixes "approximately 0.77 arcseconds" and "approximately 60 arcseconds" (plain text) with "approximately `$0.314$` arcseconds" (LaTeX) within the same paragraph. The piece uses math mode systematically for `$10^{-10}$`, `$10^{19}$`, `$N_A \approx 6.4 \times 10^{23}$`, and `$B(M; \mathcal{A}; \theta_0)$`; it is worth picking a convention - measured quantities in math, prose numbers in text - and applying it. The current state is a copy-edit oversight rather than a substantive defect, but it reads as carelessness in a piece otherwise careful about notation.

7. **The constructibility horizon is treated as binary; physical-scale constraints make it more interesting.** Bessel succeeded because 61 Cygni's parallax of approximately $0.3$ arcseconds sat at the edge of what 1830s optics could resolve. If it had been $0.003$ arcseconds, the heliometer would not have closed the gap; interferometry or spectroscopic parallax would have been required, in a different physical regime. The diffraction limit, the Heisenberg bound, and the energy required to probe a given length scale are real physical constraints on what apparatus can in principle exist within a given technological era. The Boltzmann case is, as the author notes, "effectively unattainable in the literal form the derivations model" - that "effective" is doing real physical work and deserves explicit treatment. The typology gains by acknowledging that constructibility is not a binary property of an idea but a function of which physical regime the apparatus must reach, and how far that regime sits from the boundary of current technique.

8. **What apparatus would vindicate the implied-apparatus theory itself?** The piece is reflexive in several ways but not in this one. §Limits gestures at a systematic survey, but does not specify what observable would falsify the criterion. A candidate: a class of dismissed-but-correct ideas, drawn under a Charter-compliant operational definition of "correct," whose implied apparatus is non-constructible by some explicit standard, that nonetheless returns to active scientific debate. The author owes the reader something more precise than "case-by-case argument" on this point - even a sketch of what the survey's discriminating output would look like would help the reader judge how much weight the four-case study can bear.
