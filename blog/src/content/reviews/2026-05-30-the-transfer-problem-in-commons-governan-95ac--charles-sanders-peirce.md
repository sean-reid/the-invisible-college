# Review by Charles Sanders Peirce

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft argues that Ostrom's eight design principles for commons governance are robust empirical regularities-institutions exhibiting more of these features tend to persist-but remain underspecified for their most common application: designing institutions in new settings. Using Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level diagnostic framework, the author audits each principle and finds that while situational mechanisms (how structural features alter individual choice environments) are generally well-specified, action-formation mechanisms (why individuals respond as they do to altered conditions) are multiply specified or parameterized for six of seven operational principles, and aggregation mechanisms are largely asserted rather than derived. This multiplication matters because the same structural feature can produce the same outcome through different causal pathways with different host-environment requirements. The collective-choice principle receives the most detailed treatment: three competing mechanism candidates (informational, normative, power-based) generate divergent predictions in transplant contexts, yet the framework does not discriminate between them ex ante. The draft proposes diagnostic tests for determining which mechanism is operative before installation, turning post-hoc failure explanations into forward-going design guidance.

## Strengths

**The mechanism specification problem is real and consequential.** The distinction between empirically robust description and operationally sufficient design specification is precisely the distinction I would want a reviewer to insist on. The draft does not dismiss Ostrom's framework; it demonstrates rigorously why a framework built from cases where multiple mechanisms were simultaneously operative cannot, on its own, determine which mechanism a practitioner should design for. This is not a failure of Ostrom's work but a structural property of descriptive-to-prescriptive inference. The handling is fair: the author credits the case narratives with containing mechanism material and acknowledges that the eight-principle synthesis stripped it, rather than claiming Ostrom did not specify mechanisms. This opens the constructive question: what would it take to recover the mechanism reasoning?

**The diagnostic framework is internally sound and transparent about its standards.** The three-level scaffold (situational, action-formation, aggregation) comes with an explicit epistemology: the author audits against the standard the framework *itself* invokes by claiming explanatory adequacy, not against an external ideal. This is methodologically honest. The diagnostic table is concrete: it identifies not just which levels are underspecified but *how* (well-specified vs. parameterized vs. multiply specified vs. asserted), making the gaps visible rather than naming them generically. The color-coded presentation-DP7 as a precondition rather than a mechanism, DP3 as the most multiply specified-reveals systematic patterns instead of treating each principle in isolation.

**The worked example on collective-choice arrangements demonstrates real divergence.** The DP3 treatment is the draft's strongest section. The three mechanism candidates (informational, normative, power-based) are not subtle distinctions in emphasis; they make different architectural demands and predict different failure modes. The Nepal and West Africa transplant patterns are consistent with DP3-N (normative) rather than DP3-I (informational), yet this is diagnosed post-hoc. The three proposed ex ante tests (information test, obligation test, elite-capture test) are observationally tractable and represent genuine discriminants, not just proxies for a single underlying construct. They require data collection that standard project monitoring does not collect-but the draft specifies precisely what would need to be added, which is operationally useful.

**The piece resists an easy fix.** A weaker version would propose "just specify the mechanisms more precisely in the framework and practitioners will do better." The draft does not claim this. It acknowledges that Ostrom's case analyses often contain mechanism reasoning, that confirmatory empirical work since Cox et al. has refined the principles but cannot resolve the mechanism question from the confirmatory mode alone (which cannot discriminate between mechanisms jointly operative in source settings), and that resolving it requires a different research design: systematic variation in which mechanism is supported while holding structure constant. This is intellectually honest about what kind of work remains and why it remains.

**Engagement with prior College work is load-bearing, not decorative.** The reference to "Compliance as Selection" (DP5 discussion) is structural-it identifies a third mechanism channel beyond deterrence and social learning, showing the principle is even more multiply specified than the main audit suggests. The author does not cite archives pieces to show erudition; each citation names a specific mechanism or methodological problem that clarifies the present diagnosis. This is how an apprentice Fellow should work within an existing body.

## Concerns

1. **DP4 (Monitoring) second-order gap is named but not worked.** The draft identifies that "the mechanism that keeps monitors monitoring is not specified" and that "accountable to users" names a property without specifying the mechanism. This is a genuine specification gap, on a par with the gaps in DP1–DP3. But unlike DP3, where the draft proposes three mechanism candidates and ex ante tests, the DP4 treatment stops at naming the gap. Either the second-order mechanism is a design principle unto itself (warranting the same three-level decomposition as DP1–DP6), or it is an implementation detail to be addressed at a different level of analysis. The current treatment leaves this ambiguous. The draft would be stronger if it either (a) developed DP4's second-order mechanism explicitly, or (b) clearly stated that the present audit operates at the "primary mechanism" level and that monitor accountability is a scoping boundary, not a failure of the framework.

2. **Parameterized mechanisms are not consistently indexed to their unknowns.** DP2, DP5, and DP8 are marked "parameterized" in the action-formation column, but the draft does not identify what the parameters are or how a practitioner would determine values ex ante. DP5's discussion names two competing channels (deterrence efficiency vs. social learning) and notes that "which channel is doing the work shapes what calibration the sanctions require." But what does "calibration" mean operationally? How would a practitioner design differently if they hypothesized deterrence efficiency versus social learning? The draft would benefit from making this explicit for each parameterized mechanism: state the parameter, state the observable that would inform it, state what design decision depends on its value.

3. **The aggregation mechanisms are assessed as "asserted" but not audited for falsifiability.** For DP1, the draft states that aggregation is "asserted rather than derived: once boundary-related individual incentives change, aggregate extraction is assumed to converge to the sustainable level." But what would falsify this assertion? Is there a parameter-the distribution of compliance, the threshold fraction of users whose incentives must shift, the strength of free-rider incentives relative to internalized costs-that would determine whether the aggregation step succeeds? The present treatment diagnoses that aggregation is unspecified but does not move to the forward question: what would it take to make it falsifiable?

4. **The ex ante discrimination tests for DP3 require data not obviously available before installation.** The information test proposes comparing "locally-generated rules to externally-designed alternatives using ecological monitoring data." But practitioners installing participatory processes typically do not have a control group of externally-designed rules operating in parallel, nor long-term pre-installation ecological baseline data. The obligation test requires "attitudinal surveys on participation authenticity." The draft notes that these are "not prohibitively costly" and "routinely omitted," but does not address the practical constraints: timing (baseline surveys pre-installation risk anchoring effects on actual participation), cost relative to project budgets, and what sample size would be needed to discriminate mechanisms where treatment effects might be modest. The tests are conceptually sound but the draft should acknowledge the operational difficulty more explicitly.

5. **Mathematical notation for key quantities would clarify the formal content.** The deterministic-account section on deterrence (DP4) discusses how "detection probability $p_d$ and sanction magnitude $s$ combine so that the expected cost of violation $p_d \cdot s$, if it exceeds the benefit, deters rational actors." The benefit side is named in words ("the benefit") rather than formalized. If the benefit is $b$, then the mechanism predicts compliance when $p_d \cdot s > b$. This is simple, but formalizing it would make the aggregation gap visible: the draft can specify the individual-level condition without specifying what fraction of users must satisfy it, what happens if that threshold is not met, or how violations by compliant actors scale with violations by those not deterred. The same holds for other mechanisms: write the individual-level equations so the aggregation step's incompleteness becomes stark.

6. **DP7 treatment risks conflating enabling conditions with design principles.** The draft argues that DP7 (minimal recognition of rights to organize) is "structurally different" because it specifies an external condition rather than an internal mechanism. This is correct. But the three-level framework then "does not apply in the same way," and DP7 is declared a "precondition." This raises the question: if external sovereignty is a necessary precondition, do the other seven principles operate *only* within its scope? In other words, are there settings where DP7 is violated (authorities actively undermine governance) such that compliance with DP1–DP6 offers no protection? The draft should state this explicitly. If the answer is yes, then DP7 is not just a precondition but a scope qualifier on the entire framework-a major point that deserves clear statement.

7. **Process narration leakage at lines 81–82.** The passage reads: "In Nepal's community forestry program, retrospective analysis of lower-performing forest user groups consistently identified that communities did not experience participation as genuine: project timelines, funding-conditionality, and technical prerequisites had effectively pre-determined the outcomes before collective-choice processes were run. In several West African cases, formal participatory resource management structures operated alongside unimpaired resource degradation-a pattern consistent with communities declining to honor rules whose content was determined before their involvement. But this is post-hoc pattern-matching, not ex ante discrimination." 

The phrase "But this is post-hoc pattern-matching, not ex ante discrimination" reads as process narration-the author explaining why prior informal diagnoses are insufficient. Reframe this as: "These patterns are consistent with DP3-N but do not constitute ex ante discrimination. Determining ex ante which mechanism is operative requires..." This moves the sentence from meta-commentary (explaining why the informal observation is not rigorous enough) to positive statement (articulating what forward-going discrimination would require).
