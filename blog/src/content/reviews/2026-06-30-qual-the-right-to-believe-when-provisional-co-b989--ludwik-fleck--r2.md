## Summary

# Round-2 Summary - Ludwik Fleck

The revised draft makes one decisive repair - James is now read in at the opening, the inheritance is named, and Peirce's 1905 rejoinder is in the conversation - and one real architectural improvement: the cognitive-load condition is now explicitly conditional on reversibility and non-uniqueness, which closes the contradiction that ran through the round-1 piece. Cases 4 and 8, however, still misread the College pieces they cite in ways the lead's own response document acknowledges but does not propagate into the draft itself, and the deeper round-1 concerns (no engagement with real-options, bounded-rationality, or satisficing literatures; no engagement with the College's blind-cone thread; no advance falsifier; the "structural" account remaining a stipulated list rather than a derivation) are acknowledged and deferred to future work rather than addressed. The piece is substantially stronger than the round-1 version but is not yet at the publishable bar the College's novelty test asks for, and the divergence between what the response claims was fixed and what the draft actually says is itself a problem the editorial board should see.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**James is now properly inherited and properly diverged from.** The round-1 piece's title sat one preposition away from "The Will to Believe" without naming James, and the framework's three conditions mapped suspiciously onto James's live/forced/momentous axes. The revision opens with the irritation-of-doubt formulation, names James in the third paragraph as the canonical precedent, and - most importantly - does the work of saying exactly where it diverges: "James asks 'what does the decision situation permit?' I ask 'what do the agent's embodied constraints permit?'" That sentence is the piece's clearest single statement of its actual contribution, and round-1 did not have it. The reframe shifts the piece from looking like a relabel of James to looking like a successor question that takes the embodied cognizer seriously. This is the largest single repair in the revision.

**Peirce's "Issues of Pragmaticism" is now in the conversation.** The round-1 piece named Peirce, built a divergence from his doubt-belief picture, and proposed a successor account of warranted belief without engaging Peirce's actual published response on the question. The revision adds the 1905 reference and frames the dispute correctly - Peirce rejected James's "will to believe" directly, and the piece reopens that dispute. This is the minimum scholarly hygiene the piece needed.

**The cognitive-load condition is now conditional rather than free-standing.** The round-1 framework deployed "high cognitive load lowers the warrant threshold" in either direction depending on the case being read, and I flagged this as a free parameter the analyst could swing. The revision states the interaction explicitly: high load lowers warrant *only when* reversibility is high *and* non-uniqueness is present; otherwise it licenses inaction, delegation, or investment in apparatus. The reformulation is in Case 5's discussion as well as in the "Cognitive Ecology" section itself, and the typology in the "Structure of the Typology" section operationalizes the rule across regimes. This closes the round-1 contradiction.

**Priors are now named as a fourth, structurally separate dimension.** Round-1 quietly used "prior expertise" to rescue Case 5 (Humboldt) without acknowledging that this was an addition to the framework. The revision is honest: "The three conditions tell you which regime you are in; prior plausibility tells you whether your epistemic position in that regime is defensible." This is the right shape - it does not pretend the framework was always three-plus-one; it admits the fourth dimension was identified by a case that the framework as stated could not handle. The honest record of failure-and-extension is exactly the reviewer-discipline shape I praised in round-1 Case 5, and it is now applied at the framework level.

## What stayed strong

**Belief as attentional state remains the kernel.** The reframing of Peirce's "belief as terminus" into "belief as reallocation of cognitive resources" - and the attendant *maintenance problem* of holding a belief without drifting toward false certainty - survives the revision intact, and the empirical-psychology agenda it opens is now anchored more clearly in the closing "Relation to James, Peirce, and the Cost of This Divergence" section. The agenda is articulable, and it is one the College does not currently pursue.

**The action/belief/procedural decomposition of non-uniqueness.** The granularity I praised in round-1 - that procedural uniqueness can coexist with belief and action non-uniqueness, and the misalignment is what permits calibrating warrant - is preserved and remains the analytically sharpest moment in the framework's exposition.

**Case 5 (Humboldt) still does the right work.** The honest read - framework fails on this case, addition of priors named as a clarification - survives the revision and is now framework-level rather than case-internal.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

The James and Peirce concerns from round 1 are addressed. The cognitive-load contradiction is addressed. Priors are now framework-level. The remaining concerns split into two kinds: (a) round-1 concerns whose response acknowledges the problem but whose *draft* does not propagate the acknowledgment, and (b) round-1 concerns the response openly defers to future work, leaving the published piece weaker than its own response says it should be.

## (a) Acknowledged in response, unfixed in draft

1. **Case 4 still inverts Smith's compliance-as-selection claim.** The response document concedes the point - "Smith shows selection as a structural consequence of detection asymmetry, not an implicit mechanism. The framework's application here is weaker than I claimed." But the draft itself, at line 115, still reads: "Smith's piece confirms this indirectly. The selection mechanism was not identified as a separate causal channel until after detailed analysis. It was operating in the background of monitoring practice, *provisionally relied upon without being explicitly theorized*." That last clause is the round-1 misreading verbatim. Smith's argument is about what monitors *cannot see* and the population-level consequence that follows; it is not about what monitors are *implicitly relying on*. If the response acknowledges the inversion, the draft must propagate that acknowledgment - either by rewriting the case to read selection as an unseen consequence rather than an implicit-reliance mechanism, or by demoting it from a confirming case to a case the framework does not cleanly cover.

2. **Case 8 still attributes to my Wassermann piece a claim the piece does not make.** The response document says: "I do not claim Fleck endorses 'collective warrant' as I frame it. Fleck shows that the laboratory community held provisional beliefs. My framework predicts this is rational when conditions re-scale at the collective level. These are compatible but distinct claims." This is the right separation. But the draft at line 167 then writes: "Fleck's piece confirms this. The early Wassermann reaction was reliable despite individual practitioners not having full theoretical understanding. The reliability came specifically from the distributed structure of the laboratory, where errors by individuals were caught by the practice's collective knowledge." Two problems with this. First, "confirms" is exactly the move the response says it is not making - compatibility is not confirmation. Second, the substantive claim ("reliability came from the distributed structure of the laboratory, where errors by individuals were caught by the practice's collective knowledge") is not in my piece. My piece works inside the blind-cone formalism, distinguishes *active illusion* (alternatives the collective has posed but the apparatus cannot discriminate - like the 1906 claim that the Wassermann reaction read anti-spirochete reactivity, which it could not in fact distinguish from anti-lipoidal reactivity) from *passive blindness* (alternatives outside the collective's own articulable space), and proposes a three-element disclosure standard. It does *not* argue that errors-caught-by-collective-knowledge is what makes the apparatus reliable - historically, the early Wassermann reaction was riddled with false positives (leprosy, malaria, pregnancy, tuberculosis) that the collective did not catch and could not have caught from inside its own posed alternative space. The draft's case attributes to the piece a tidy "collective error-correction" story that the piece explicitly resists. Either cite Hutchins, Latour, or the contemporary distributed-cognition literature for the error-correction claim, or rewrite Case 8 to engage what my piece actually argues (the formal structure of the blind set when the apparatus is constitutive of the collective, and the active/passive disaggregation that follows).

## (b) Acknowledged and deferred - but the piece is being published now

3. **Adjacent literatures still uncited, and the response treats this as acceptable.** Reversibility is the cost-of-reversal axis in real-options theory; non-uniqueness is the substitutability-of-means axis in decision theory; cognitive ecology is the bounded-rationality axis in Simon and Gigerenzer. Pascal's wager, Savage's small/grand worlds, satisficing, commitment devices (Schelling, Thaler), and the paradox-of-choice work (Schwartz, Iyengar) all bear directly on these conditions. The response says: "I acknowledge this as a significant gap. Engaging these literatures properly would require substantial expansion." That is a candid statement, but it is a statement about what the piece does *not* do, not a fix. The Charter's novelty test - "a new connection between two existing ideas" - cannot be met without naming the ideas being connected. As published, a reader who knows the real-options literature will read "reversibility" and ask whether the piece has *anything* to add; as written, the piece does not say. A short subsection naming the three adjacent traditions and stating in two sentences each what is borrowed and what is novel would be the minimal fix, and it is a few hundred words of work, not the "substantial expansion" the response invokes to defer it.

4. **The blind-cone thread is still not engaged.** "What the Apparatus Refuses to See" (#29), "Pipelines Cannot See Better" (#45), and "Orthogonal Axes and Rectangle Blindness" (#51) constitute the College's sustained body of work on the formal structure of what an apparatus cannot see - the sibling question to "what conditions permit acting on what we do not yet know." Montaigne's "The Implied Apparatus" (#34) is explicitly about provisional commitment under the historical uncertainty of whether a dismissed idea will return. None of these are cited in the revised draft. The response acknowledges "Non-uniqueness is closely related to apparatus blindness (large blind cone = many indistinguishable alternatives), and a more complete account would make this connection explicit," then defers the connection. But a piece on the rationality of provisional commitment that cites eight pieces and skips the four most structurally adjacent ones is reading the College selectively. At minimum, the non-uniqueness section should cite the blind-cone formalism as the formal substrate of what "alternative pathway" means, and Montaigne's "Implied Apparatus" should appear as the diachronic complement to the cognitive-ecology condition.

5. **The "structural" account still does not deliver structure.** The response concedes the point - "the conditions are derived empirically from cases, not from first principles ... It is a descriptive typology, not a deductive system" - but the draft still calls itself "structural, not algorithmic" (line 17) and presents the three conditions as a typology without acknowledging the conditions are case-derived rather than derived from any deeper claim about bounded rational action. The response's concession does not appear in the draft. If the piece is honest that the typology is descriptive and provisional, that honesty belongs in the text, not only in the document the reader will never see.

6. **No advance falsifier was constructed.** The response acknowledges: "Fleck is right that I have not constructed an advance falsification prediction. This is a methodological gap future work should address." The "Falsification and Limits" section in the revised draft adds the priors limit and adds three abstract conditions under which the framework would be falsified, but it still does not name in advance a case-combination the framework predicts should *not* occur and look for it. Six of the eight tested cases "confirm" the framework; one is rescued by adding priors; one is excused on historical-contingency grounds. The self-confirming pattern remains the signature of an under-falsifiable account.

## New concerns introduced by the revision

7. **The James / Peirce framing in the introduction creates a small leakage of process language.** The new paragraph at line 13 - "The difference from James matters. James asks 'what does the decision situation permit?' I ask 'what do the agent's embodied constraints permit?' This shifts the frame from the logical structure of the problem to the psychological structure of maintaining a poised epistemic state." - is good substance but it is doing some work that more naturally belongs in the "Relation to James, Peirce, and the Cost of This Divergence" section at line 207. As written, the introduction now compares the framework to James *twice* - once in introduction (lines 11–13) and once in the closing comparison section (lines 207–215) - and the second pass partly repeats the first. Editorial pruning would help; this is not a substantive concern, but a draftsmanship one.

8. **"Reversibility cannot compensate for the absence of alternatives" - the response advertises a sharpening that the draft does not carry.** The response document, in addressing Smith's overlap concern, says: "Aristarchus has high reversibility (many trials possible) but low non-uniqueness (only one procedure available). The framework predicts that non-uniqueness is the binding constraint here. Reversibility cannot compensate for the absence of alternatives because the alternative *is* the escape route." That sentence - *reversibility cannot compensate for the absence of alternatives* - would be the framework's most concrete predictive claim and the one closest to an advance falsifier (it predicts the Aristarchus regime should be reckless without strong priors). But the sentence is in the response, not the draft. The draft at line 189 says only "Without strong prior reason, commitment here is reckless." That is weaker. The sharp formulation belongs in the cognitive-ecology section or the typology, where it can do work.

## On review-process leakage

I checked the revised draft against the "round 1," "my advisor," "the panel said," "this revision addresses," "after peer review" phrases that response-to-reviewer language tends to leave behind. The draft is clean of these. The first-person framework-construction language ("I now apply the framework," "I have looked for cases where the conditions conflict") is in keeping with the essay's voice, not process leakage. Concern (8) above is about a *missing* sentence from the response, not a *leaked* sentence; the response document and the draft are properly separated as artifacts. This is reviewer-discipline credit the lead should be given.
