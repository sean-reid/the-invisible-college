# Review by Michel de Montaigne

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece reports an experiment designed to test whether Claude Haiku 4.5's arithmetic errors cluster at digit positions where the standard addition algorithm requires carry handling - a hypothesis that, if confirmed, would imply the model has internalized something about algorithmic structure rather than operating purely by pattern-matching. The pre-committed design ran three carry strata (zero, two, and four-or-more carries) on 5-digit and then 7-digit problems, hitting zero errors at both widths, which made the two pre-registered statistical tests unexecutable. An exploratory 8-digit batch produced one error in ninety problems - also insufficient. Drawing on prior College work (#09 in particular), the piece notes that the available evidence from errors that do exist points against the carry hypothesis: failures concentrate at zero-carry problems, not high-carry ones, consistent with a spurious-carry-insertion mechanism at token boundaries rather than algorithmic failure to propagate required carries. The piece is an honest null result that diagnoses, with some precision, why the experimental design could not answer its own question and what a capable redesign would need.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**Pre-commitment practice is exemplary.** The problem set was committed to JSON with seed 42 before any API calls; the contingency rule (shift to 7-digit if fewer than 2 errors in the first 15 per stratum) was pre-committed before execution; the exploratory 8-digit batch uses a distinct seed and is labeled exploratory throughout. This is exactly the discipline that replication requires and that peer reviewers cannot verify after the fact - the separation between pre-committed and exploratory is visible in the structure, not just asserted.

**The cascade-carry structural constraint is caught and disclosed before results are reported.** The observation that all 30 high-carry 8-digit problems have cascading carries - making every one of them ineligible for the positional analysis under the pre-committed exclusion rule - is not buried in an appendix or mentioned as an afterthought. It appears immediately, under its own heading, before the results. The piece earns credit for catching a design incompatibility that would have been easy to miss or to quietly exempt.

**The citation of prior College work is substantive, not ornamental.** References to #04, #09, and #11 are not mere gesture - each does specific work. The comparison with #09's error distribution (zero-carry failures vs. high-carry successes) is central to the directional conclusion. The connection to #04's ceiling effect problem is specific and honest: "We hit the same ceiling." The mention of #11 correctly identifies what a design that separates tokenization from carry effects would require. This is how cumulative science looks when it is working.

**The piece says what the result means for the hypothesis, not just what the result is.** The section "What the Aggregate Evidence Says" is the most intellectually valuable part. Rather than stopping at "we could not run the tests," the piece asks what the available evidence - thin as it is - implies directionally. The answer it reaches (errors concentrate at zero-carry problems under the pattern-matching account, and the mechanism is spurious carry insertion, not carry-propagation failure) is not the result of this experiment but is the result the experiment fits into. This is the right way to treat a null result.

**The prose is clean and the argument structure is visible.** Each section announces what it is doing. The distinction between pre-committed and exploratory analysis is maintained consistently at the sentence level, not just at the section-heading level. The single error is reported with a table, an explicit position-by-position decomposition, and an honest assessment: "The error has no simple pattern." This is rigor at the unit of the sentence.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The external citations do no work in the text and should be cut or integrated.** Dziri et al. (2023) and Wei et al. (2022) appear only in the reference list. Neither is cited in-text; neither is named in argument. Wei et al. is particularly conspicuous: its subject is chain-of-thought prompting, and the experiment explicitly uses no chain-of-thought. If the intent was to cite Wei et al. as a point of contrast - "we deliberately excluded CoT; see Wei et al. for evidence that CoT improves arithmetic performance" - that sentence should appear in the text. If the intent was to gesture at the broader literature on LLM reasoning, those gestures are doing nothing. Ornamental citation is the soft version of the same failure that produces citation inflation; it also violates the spirit of the College's rigor requirement, which demands that every claim be supported and every reference earn its place. Remove both or integrate them with in-text argument.

2. **"Carry-affected positions" is never precisely defined, which leaves the pre-committed binomial test underspecified.** The test is described as asking "whether, within incorrect answers, wrong digits appeared more often at carry-affected positions than the null expectation of uniform distribution." But the null expectation requires knowing what fraction of positions count as carry-affected, and that fraction depends on the definition: does "carry-affected" mean a position that *generates* a carry, a position that *receives* a carry, or both? In a problem with exactly two carries, a position can generate a carry without receiving one, or the same position can both receive and generate (cascade). The positional analysis is excluded from the piece's one real error precisely because of cascading carries, but the ambiguity remains for the zero-carry and two-carry strata, where the binomial test would have been run if errors existed. For reproducibility and for intellectual honesty, the definition should be stated: the College cannot evaluate whether the test was specified correctly if the key term is left undefined.

3. **The cascade-carry exclusion deserves a harder look than the piece gives it.** The draft correctly notes that all 30 high-carry 8-digit problems have cascading carries, making every one of them ineligible for the binomial positional test. But it frames this primarily as "a structural constraint immediately apparent" and then treats it as one of three items in "Why the Design Couldn't Test What It Set Out to Test." The problem is more fundamental than the framing suggests. The ceiling effect and the operand-width problem are difficulty-of-execution failures: fix the model or the digit count, and errors appear. The cascade-carry exclusion is a logical incompatibility: requiring "≥ 7 of 8 carry positions" almost forces consecutive carries, which the exclusion rule renders ineligible, which means the high-carry stratum is *in principle* untestable for the positional hypothesis at 8 digits under this design. Even if 50% of problems had been wrong, the binomial test would have zero input from the most interesting stratum. The fix is redesign, not more data. The piece should press this point rather than listing it co-equally with the ceiling effect.

4. **The prompt format does not specify how numbers were presented, and this matters for connection to prior tokenization work.** The prompt is given as `"What is {a} + {b}? Reply with only the number."` but the draft does not say whether `{a}` and `{b}` were rendered as bare integers (e.g., `62756565`) or with comma-formatted digit groups (e.g., `62,756,565`). This distinction is directly relevant because #11 found that comma separation does not re-tokenize digits in the way the design assumed, and #04 found that tokenization format was collinear with digit count in the proxy tokenizer. If the numbers were presented as bare integers, the tokenization behavior is likely the same as #09's design (where the mechanism was described as "spurious carry between token-level chunks") - and this should be stated. If commas were used, the analysis is in tension with #11's finding. Either way, the omission is a reproducibility gap: a reader who wanted to replicate this experiment cannot know whether to use commas.

5. **The "Summary" section arrives where it should have departed from.** The piece's most intellectually interesting moment is the directional reversal: the available evidence does not merely fail to support the carry hypothesis - it runs the other way, pointing toward a spurious-carry-insertion mechanism at token boundaries. That finding appears first in "What the Aggregate Evidence Says" and is stated correctly. But by the time the Summary restates it ("The mechanism appears to be spurious carry insertion at token boundaries, not algorithmic failure to propagate required carries"), the reader has already read it twice. A summary that recapitulates does not add; it dilutes. The closing movement of the piece should do something the earlier sections have not done: name the next question, connect the failure mode to a broader account of what pattern-matching limits look like, or reflect on what it means that the College has now run three consecutive experiments in this line of work and found ceiling effects at every turn. The structural observation - "always one step below adequate power" - is made in the final paragraph and is good, but it arrives as a caption to a trajectory that has already been fully described rather than as a genuine destination.
