## Summary

# Summary (Round 2)

The revised draft is substantially stronger than the version I reviewed in round one. All five concerns I raised have been addressed in substance, not merely acknowledged: the Past Hypothesis now has a dedicated section that does real philosophical work; the Bayesian parallel is developed through a Jaynes engagement and a concrete worked example; the Simányi citations are precise with their genericity hedges explained; the title now says "relocated" rather than "renamed," which accurately represents the essay's calibrated position; and Brush is cited inline where it belongs. The essay's central thesis — that modern dynamics has moved the philosophical burden rather than discharged it, and that the burden now has three dimensions (measure, partition, cosmological initial condition) rather than one — is now stated with the argumentative support it requires. The additions do not pad the essay; they convert what were asserted positions into defended ones.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**What got better.**

The Past Hypothesis section is the most important addition, and it works. The essay now correctly identifies time-reversal symmetry as the mechanism by which the typicality argument, stated without PH, predicts entropy increase in *both* temporal directions — a genuine problem, not a rhetorical flourish. Framing PH as a second substantive non-mechanical commitment (alongside the measure choice) is exactly the right move; it strengthens the essay's central thesis rather than complicating it. The section considers and declines the consolidation move — treating PH and the measure as one commitment — and does so with two independent reasons, both defensible. The Wallace/Earman contrast is used efficiently: Wallace's concessive defense is endorsed not because it is comfortable but because it is honest, and the essay says so plainly.

The Bayesian parallel is now a defended position rather than a hinge. The Jaynes engagement — the essay grants that symmetry-invariance arguments are genuinely powerful, then gives a two-part response about the limits of the symmetry group and the separate problem of partition underdetermination — converts what was an asserted analogy into an argued one. The worked example (a measure ν = f(q,p)·μ on the energy shell, with discussion of which classes of f the dynamics admits) gives the reader something concrete to evaluate. The Lazarovici-Reichert engagement provides the empirical-content analysis the first draft lacked.

The "Where Empirical Constraints Bite" section is a genuine addition to the essay's intellectual content. Equilibrium statistical mechanics, fluctuation theorems, and large-deviation rate functions are named and explained as three distinct sources of constraint, each narrowing the admissible alternatives without uniquely fixing the measure. The honest summary that follows — "the remaining underdetermination is not 'any measure works' but 'among measures very close to Liouville, the dynamics and data do not yet pick out a unique one'" — is exactly the right formulation. It prevents the essay's philosophical argument from appearing to impugn the empirical standing of the second law, which it should not.

The KAM section now explicitly distinguishes the calibrated version of the typicality claim from the universal foundationalist version, and names the distinction. The phrase "mechanics-plus-Liouville-typicality-plus-the-empirical-fact-that-the-systems-we-study-have-the-right-chaotic-to-KAM ratio" accurately states what the three-conjunct claim amounts to, and the observation that the third conjunct "is not derivable from the first two" is the sharpening the first draft needed.

**What stayed strong.**

The observation that both Zermelo and Boltzmann were conducting their 1896 exchange against an implicit Liouville measure — that their opposed conclusions are compatible statements of the form "almost every trajectory, in Liouville measure, has property P" — remains the essay's most original contribution. It reframes the historical exchange in a way that makes the subsequent century legible as explicitation rather than correction.

The prose is disciplined throughout. Each section does what it announces. The conclusion collects the three dimensions of relocation (measure, partition, cosmological initial condition) without merely repeating the body; the revised closing sentence ("It has not discharged it") matches the essay's calibration rather than retreating to the sharper claim the first draft's closing sentence was guilty of. The cross-reference to the Poincaré stability essay remains precise and earns its place.

## Concerns

# Concerns

All five concerns from my round-1 review have been addressed substantively. The following are minor residual points; none blocks publication.

1. **The essay sides with Wallace over Earman without fully arguing for the choice.** The Past Hypothesis section presents Earman's position (PH is "not even false," lacks the content to be a candidate for derivation from mechanics or anything else) and Wallace's position (PH is the best available structural posit, its lack of mechanical derivability is not a special embarrassment), then adopts Wallace's. The grounds given — that Wallace "concedes the substantive character of the commitment rather than trying to dissolve it" — is a characterization of Wallace's intellectual honesty, not a reason to prefer his position to Earman's. A reader sympathetic to Earman's more deflationary reading will want a sentence that engages the epistemic problem rather than the rhetorical virtue. This does not require a new argument; the essay already has the resources: the point about "physics regularly makes boundary-condition posits whose origin is outside the system being modeled" is the relevant one. Bringing it forward as the grounds for preferring Wallace, rather than leaving it as an observation, would close the gap. This is a single-sentence repair, not a structural problem.

2. **The partition-underdetermination argument is stated correctly but exits quickly.** The essay credits Lazarovici and Reichert (2015) for pressing the partition question hardest, distinguishes measure-empirical from partition-empirical content, and says the author finds them "somewhat optimistic about how much empirical content survives the underdetermination." That last judgment is stated without the support that distinguishes it from a preference. A reader who wants to evaluate the disagreement will not have enough to go on. Since the essay is already long, I do not suggest expanding this — but the author should either offer one reason for the "somewhat optimistic" verdict or soften the formulation to something like "I am less confident than they are that the measure-empirical content fully survives the partition underdetermination," which is a claim about relative confidence rather than a contested verdict delivered without defense. The current formulation borrows the authority of a named judgment without supplying the argument.
