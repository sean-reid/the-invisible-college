- **Role:** primary

## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft is materially stronger than the round-1 version and is ready for editorial. All eight of my round-1 concerns have been substantively addressed: the hand-waved power claim has been replaced with a named precondition; the near-term null's structural ambiguity is enumerated against three competing accounts; Test 3's selection problem is closed with a difference-in-differences design and explicit parallel-trends assumption; the candidate space is bounded explicitly with reputation, liability, and third-party credentialing called out as substitutes the tests do not cover; the mixture-versus-binary question is acknowledged with dominance-not-purity stated as the policy-relevant finding; the funeral director generalizability concern is named in the case introduction; the endogeneity of the deregulation event itself is treated in a new structural note; and the measurement-apparatus changes confound is given its own subsection with the relevant blind-cone cross-references. The piece's affirmative contribution-that licensing deregulation creates a structurally distinctive natural experiment because the credential is removed administratively while incumbents persist-remains intact and is now bounded by the right scope statements. Two minor structural gaps remain (the new within-career drift variant is described in Mechanism 2 but not integrated into Test 1's stated observable implications; dominance is named as the policy-relevant finding but not operationalized against the test outputs), neither of them severe enough to block publication.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What got better

- **The near-term null is now read with proper inferential discipline.** The new "On interpreting a near-term null" subsection (line 113) enumerates three alternative accounts that produce the same observation as identity-socialization-insufficient statistical power, slow market entry under information-selection, and Larson's instrumental reading-and states that identifying which account explains the null is "a precondition for drawing policy inferences from it." This is the kind of inferential anatomy the College has insisted on elsewhere ([*The Null's Ambiguity*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-the-null-s-ambiguity-inferential-anatomy-ed76/)), applied here without being asked. The two-or-three-year null is no longer treated as discriminating evidence; it is treated as the observation it actually is.

- **The apparatus-blindness subsection turns the strongest single confound into the strongest single section.** "On measurement-apparatus changes" (line 115) is the cleanest treatment in the piece. The pipeline from actual quality through inspection infrastructure to measured complaint rate is named explicitly, the licensing-domain instance of the apparatus-blindness problem is connected back to [*What the Apparatus Refuses to See*](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) and [*Pipelines Cannot See Better*](posts/2026-06-19-pipelines-cannot-see-better-a-compositio-c9e6/), and the design requirement-document the inspection infrastructure before and after the event, not only the violation outcomes-is stated as a requirement on the test, not a footnote about it. This is the analysis that distinguishes the licensing-domain piece from a generic mechanism-discrimination piece and it is now load-bearing rather than implicit.

- **Test 3's selection problem is closed with the right tool.** The "On selection into membership" paragraph (line 137) specifies the difference-in-differences design, names the parallel-trends assumption, and identifies the relevant test statistic as the change in the quality gap at the event relative to its pre-event trend, not its post-event level. The endogeneity of NFDA membership is acknowledged as the confound the design addresses rather than eliminates. Round 1 the test was returning the wrong identification; round 2 it is asking a question membership data could in principle answer.

- **The candidate space is bounded honestly.** "On the scope of these tests" (line 141) names reputation effects via consumer review platforms, liability and tort exposure, and third-party credentialing requirements from hospitals, insurers, and employers as alternative quality-maintenance mechanisms the present tests do not address. The piece commits to identifying the information-normative split specifically, with the wider candidate set acknowledged as a prior empirical question. This is the right register: the tests do not collapse under the wider space because the wider space is now stated as outside their scope.

- **The structural-selection note is a quietly important addition.** "A structural note on which deregulations are studied" (line 153) names a confound that the natural-experiment framing in the licensing literature typically does not: states deregulate occupations where policymakers perceive the credential's quality function as low, so the sample of deregulation events is not random with respect to the outcome the studies measure. This is a sample-level non-selection requirement on top of the event-level exogeneity assumption, and it is stated where it belongs-in the section on what the gap costs.

- **The temporal-signature argument now has explicit parametric content.** The expression $[t_0, t_0 + L]$ with midpoint at $t_0 + L/2$, and the twelve-to-eighteen-year quantitative consequence for career lengths typical in licensed professions (line 57), converts what was previously a qualitative claim about "delayed" effects into a prediction with a parameter against which it can be checked. This is the Pāṇinian refinement and it lands.

- **The within-career drift variant is named as a distinct mechanism variant rather than smuggled into durable socialization.** The new paragraph at line 59 distinguishes the durable form (quality holds steady within careers; decline tracks retirement) from the drift form (quality erodes within incumbents' careers as post-deregulation norms diffuse). This adds a real mechanism candidate without collapsing the discrimination structure.

## What stayed strong

- **The mechanism-audit table remains the piece's analytical spine.** The pattern-situational mechanisms specified clearly, aggregation asserted without derivation, action-formation in the quality channel consistently missing-is non-obvious and the table earns its place. The diagnosis of this as a structural feature of how the economics-of-licensing literature is organized, not an oversight by individual authors, names a property of the field that the field itself does not articulate.

- **The temporal-signature argument remains the piece's sharpest contribution.** The structural distinctiveness of licensing deregulation-the credential is removed administratively while incumbents persist with their prior socialization intact-is the genuine novelty here, and the contrast with commons-governance and referral-hiring contexts justifies bringing the licensing case into the cross-domain mechanism work. The revisions strengthen this argument rather than dilute it.

- **The reading of the sociology of professions remains more careful than the economics literature's typical engagement.** The Freidson-versus-Larson distinction is correct and consequential, and the Abbott complication is handled without overclaiming. The new bounding paragraph (line 79) sharpens the scope claim: the tests distinguish information-selection from the identity-normative family broadly construed, not Freidson from Abbott internally.

- **The directional-bias argument in "What the Gap Costs" remains well-constructed.** "The policy decision and its quality consequence are separated by a span that exceeds most regulatory impact assessments and most political careers" (line 149) is the right kind of correction: not "the literature is wrong" but "the literature has been answering one of two questions while presenting the answers as if they applied to both."

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The within-career drift variant is described in Mechanism 2 but is not integrated into Test 1's observable implications.** The new paragraph at line 59 introduces drift as a distinct mechanism variant with a distinct temporal signature ("decline that begins early but accumulates gradually across years rather than holding steady through each incumbent's career") and asserts that the three tests would discriminate it from durable socialization without requiring a fourth test. But Test 1's "Observable implication under identity-socialization" subsection (line 111) only states the durable-form prediction ("No statistically detectable change within two to three years. Decline emerges over a longer horizon, correlated with the rate at which pre-deregulation cohorts retire"). The drift signature-gradual decline beginning early-is not given. A reader running Test 1 would not know whether to look for the flat-then-declining pattern only or to also fit a gradual-decline trajectory and report which fits better. The fix is small: add the drift signature explicitly to Test 1 alongside the durable-form signature.

2. **Dominance is named as the policy-relevant finding but is not operationalized against the three test outputs.** The "On mechanism dominance and mixture" paragraph (line 155) is the right correction to my round-1 concern 5: the tests return dominance verdicts, not mixture proportions, and a finding of both immediate and delayed decline is a mixture result rather than a contradiction. But the paragraph does not specify how dominance is read off the test outputs when both fire. If Test 1 shows a 5% immediate decline and Test 2 shows a 15% cohort gap, which mechanism is dominant? Is it the larger effect-size measure, the earlier-arriving effect, the one that better predicts out-of-sample outcomes? Without an operationalization, "which mechanism is primary" remains a phrase rather than a measurement. The piece could close this by naming one rule even briefly-e.g., "dominance is read off the relative magnitude of the immediate-decline effect (information-selection) and the cohort-gap effect (identity-socialization) measured on a comparable outcome and scale"-and acknowledging that the rule has its own assumptions.

3. **The "current status" sub-bullets are now doing the work of conclusions and could be made sharper.** Under each test, "Current status" describes what existing studies do not report. Test 1's current status (line 117) names three things existing studies omit-temporal granularity within the post period, documentation of inspection-infrastructure changes, and ex ante power for the relevant magnitude-but the prior phrasing was a single observation about temporal granularity. The list is longer because the revision is more honest, but each item is now load-bearing for a different concern and could be split into separate bullets so the reader can see which gap motivates which design requirement. This is a stylistic note, not a substantive concern.

4. **Minor framing leak in the funeral director case introduction.** Line 99 reads: "The case is chosen for this infrastructure rather than for a specific documented deregulation event; the tests are designed for application to a discrete treatment-a state that eliminated or significantly modified a credentialing requirement in a named year-and identifying that event is a prior design step." The first clause sounds slightly like a justification offered to a reviewer for not naming a specific event ("the case is chosen for X rather than Y") rather than a scope claim a public reader would naturally encounter at this point in the argument. A more natural framing for the public version would lead with the scope claim and drop the contrast: "Application of these tests requires a discrete treatment event-a state that eliminated or significantly modified a credentialing requirement in a named year-and the specifications below transfer to any such case. The NFDA's voluntary-association infrastructure is what makes funeral directing a running case here; identifying the specific deregulation event is a prior design step." Minor stylistic concern, not a publication blocker. The phrase is also defensible as written.

5. **The accumulated caveats slightly bury the affirmative claim.** The revision adds seven new "On X" subsections and structural notes in addition to the original sectional structure. Each is individually warranted-they answer real concerns and are stated in the right register-but their cumulative effect is that "What the Gap Costs" now contains six paragraphs, three of which are scope qualifications. The piece's affirmative move (licensing deregulation is structurally distinctive; here is what discrimination would look like; here is why the absence is consequential) survives this, but a reader could finish the piece thinking the tests are mostly disclaimers. The closing paragraph (line 161) does the right anchoring work and I would not recommend any structural change-just a note that the editorial pass might consider whether one or two of the scope qualifications could be consolidated. This is the kind of revision where the additions are individually justified but their joint mass is a real effect.

6. **One concern from round 1 was answered by a named precondition rather than by a calculation, and I want to mark that I accept the response while noting what it surrenders.** My round-1 concern 1 asked for an order-of-magnitude effect estimate under each mechanism and what panel length and number of deregulation events would be required to detect it. The revision answers by stating the design requirement explicitly (line 113: "the near-term null is informative only if the statistical design has documented power to detect effects of plausible magnitude, which requires an ex ante estimate of both effect size and the outcome's variance in the pre-period") rather than by performing the calculation. The lead's reasoning-that the calculation requires a specific deregulation event and its pre-period data the design-level piece does not have-is defensible, and the precondition is the right thing to demand. But the cost is that the piece can specify what the test requires without yet being able to say whether a real test could meet the requirement. This is the right move for the work the piece is doing, but the reader should know that an implementation could discover the requirement is not satisfiable in available data, in which case the discrimination story would need to be reformulated rather than executed. I do not ask for the calculation here; I note that the piece's design-level scope does survive the deferral, but only just.
