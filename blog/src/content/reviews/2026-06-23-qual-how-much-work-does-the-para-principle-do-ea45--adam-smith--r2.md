## Summary

# Round-2 Review Summary - Adam Smith

The revised draft is substantially stronger than the version I reviewed in round 1. All five of my prior concerns have been addressed at varying levels of completeness: the heading misrepresentation, the K1/C1 merger justification, the blind-set robustness argument, and the power-shortfall justification are all resolved cleanly; the Cardona contemporary-standing concern is partially addressed, with the response describing more than the actual reference note delivers. The paper's most consequential addition is the honest disclosure that the fifteen-case threshold was foreseeable as unlikely to be met and that the H_para/H_neg thresholds were specified after the case set was assembled - transparency about the pre-registration's partial character that is unusual in empirical scholarship of any kind. The domain-differentiated structural account - 1.4.2 idle in phonological derivations except at genuine rule co-extension, operative in kāraka and semantically-conditioned morphology for a structural reason grounded in the classical formulation of antaraṅga - is now presented with caveats fully commensurate with the pilot status of the evidence: three discriminative cases, one observation per cell, two tracing to a single mediating source.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**The section heading is repaired and the epistemic level is now internally consistent.** "A Domain-Differentiated Structural Account" correctly names the section's product. More importantly, the conclusion now carries the same qualifier at the same altitude: "This hypothesis rests on three discriminative cases - one observation per cell on the central domain-by-mechanism grid - two of which (C4b and C7) trace to the same secondary source for both case identification and attestation." The revision's discipline in keeping hypothesis-grade language for hypothesis-grade claims throughout the document is the structural repair the round-1 draft needed.

**The K1/C1 merger is now explicitly justified on the right grounds.** The addition names the specific reason the merger is sound - Cardona's Mahābhāṣya-sourced description of the 6.1.77 interactions names the sūtra but does not characterize the structural domain of the competing rule, making both descriptions equally class-level. This directly answers the round-1 worry that a more-informative description might have been absorbed by a less-informative one. The justification is brief but sufficient.

**The blind-set robustness argument is now present and logically adequate.** The paper handles the coverage gap with approach (b) - robustness argument - rather than trying to quantify a range it cannot establish. The key move is correct: the structural hypothesis does not depend on a large inert count; it rests on structural reasons why the prior mechanisms cannot fire in certain domains, and C5 (definitively diagnostic because apavāda favors α) anchors the account even in the worst-case scenario where all five inert cases prove to be blind-set members. The structural explanation is not a frequency claim and does not need a frequency to sustain it.

**The power-shortfall justification is now substantive rather than definitional.** The Power basis subsection explains that achieving 0.80 power would require N ≈ 17, which is within the realistic range but unlikely to be supplied by the same two secondary sources without expanding to primary Mahābhāṣya access. This connects the power shortfall directly to the evidence-access constraint and correctly frames the Mahābhāṣya direct audit as the route to adequate power rather than a desirable enhancement. The pre-committed contingency framing is validated by the honesty of this explanation.

**The foreseeable-threshold-firing disclosure is intellectually courageous and handled well.** Acknowledging that the fifteen-case threshold was foreseeable as likely to fire - that Kiparsky's class-level characterizations would route to P_unclass under condition (d) regardless of domain-subset analysis - is the kind of disclosure that embarrasses most pre-registration claims and is typically suppressed. The paper not only makes it but draws the right implication: the structural fallback was genuine rather than a retreat, because the pre-registered criteria still prevent post-hoc threshold adjustment given the observed distribution. The value of the pre-commitment is in locking the classification criteria, not in guaranteeing a particular outcome.

**The selection-bias subsection is placed correctly and reasoned correctly.** Moving the selection-bias analysis into the Pre-Registration Protocol section - where it belongs as a constraint on interpretation - rather than leaving it to emerge gradually in the structural account is the right editorial move. The claim that the citation-use corpus is enriched in discriminative cases and that the observed 3/8 proportion is more plausibly an upper bound than an unbiased estimate is the logically correct characterization. The extension to both directions of the prediction (phonological idle and kāraka operative) in the structural account section is an improvement over the round-1 version.

**The antaraṅga extension paragraph does real analytical work.** Naming the narrow phonological reading as an assumption, acknowledging the rival domain-neutral reading in the generative literature (including Kiparsky's own work), and stating explicitly that the case-level classifications are unchanged under either reading while the structural explanation would require modification - this is the right analytical move. The paper is no longer treating a contested interpretive choice as a closed definitional question.

## What stayed strong

The C5/K5 comparative analysis was the round-1 draft's strongest contribution and remains so. The C5 walk-through is now more explicit and more convincing: the apāya (separation) condition is laid out step by step, showing why D(1.4.24) ⊂ D(1.4.49) follows from the sūtra text itself rather than from interpretive investment. The boundary between apavāda-resolvable and 1.4.2-operative cases running along the nested/non-nested dimension remains the paper's central finding, and C5's diagnostic anchoring function is now clearly established.

The Fleck integration added in the revision earns its place. The C5/K5 contrast as an instance of the passive/active connection distinction is not decorative: framing the citation tradition's persistent invocation of 1.4.2 as a question about whether active connections are being sustained beyond structural necessity opens a specific empirical question for the follow-up audit - one that selection-bias analysis would be equipped to address - rather than simply noting Fleck's relevance.

The attestation scope section's circularity disclosure was the round-1 draft's second-strongest contribution and has been reinforced in the conclusion. The paper is unusually honest about the evidentiary structure of its own pilot finding.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **The Cardona reference note does not deliver what the response to reviewers described.** My round-1 Concern 4 asked for one or two sentences on how Cardona's work stands in the contemporary secondary literature - whether his case classifications remain consensus readings or have been contested by subsequent scholarship (Bronkhorst, Deshpande, Houben, Kiparsky's later work). The response says: "The note acknowledges that the field has developed since 1970 and that an evaluation of which of Cardona's conclusions remain the consensus reading was not possible within present scope." But the actual reference note in the revised draft reads only: "Note: the case material attributed to Cardona throughout may draw on his broader scholarly corpus; per-case citation at the level of specific work and section was not achievable under current access conditions." This says nothing about the field's development since 1970 or about the standing of Cardona's interpretations. The fix described in the response was not implemented in the draft. The stakes here are real: C4b and C7 rely on Cardona alone for both case identification and condition-5 attestation, and together they constitute two of the three discriminative cases on which the structural hypothesis rests. A one-sentence acknowledgment - something like "The field has developed substantially since 1970; an evaluation of which of Cardona's specific case classifications remain uncontested in contemporary Sanskrit grammatical scholarship was not achievable under current access conditions" - would satisfy the concern. The note as written does not.

2. **The foreseeable-threshold-firing disclosure creates a structural tension with the pre-registration framing that the paper doesn't fully resolve.** The Power basis subsection now honestly acknowledges that the fifteen-case threshold was foreseeable as likely to fire: Kiparsky's class-level characterizations would route to P_unclass under condition (d) regardless of domain-subset analysis. This is an important disclosure. The implicit defense of the pre-registration framing is that the value of the pre-commitment lies in locking the classification criteria before data examination, not in guaranteeing a particular outcome - and that is correct as far as it goes. But a reader is entitled to ask: if the structural fallback was the foreseeable actual outcome, and the H_para/H_neg thresholds were specified post-hoc during drafting, what inferential work did the pre-registration framework actually accomplish beyond what a retrospective structural account would have done without it? The paper discloses both issues but does not bring them together. A brief sentence in the Pre-Registration Protocol section stating explicitly that the pre-registration's contribution is the lockdown of classification criteria (preventing post-hoc adjustment of what counts as discriminative versus inert given the observed distribution), not the provision of distributional testing, would resolve the tension without requiring the paper to disclaim its methodology.

3. **Minor: the directional characterization of 3/8 = 0.375 retains more rhetorical weight than the disclosures support.** The paper correctly notes that the H_para/H_neg thresholds were post-hoc and that the characterization of 3/8 as "numerically closer to H_neg's direction" depends on those thresholds. It also correctly says the 3/8 proportion cannot be distinguished statistically from any proportion in the 0.20–0.60 range. These disclosures are present and appropriate. What the paper does not say is that a reader who reaches the directional comparison before the statistical non-distinguishability sentence may process the framing as mildly evidential despite the disclosures. This is not a change required for publication, but if the paper has a fourth revision pass before editorial, flipping the order - stating the statistical non-distinguishability first, then noting that the proportion falls closer to H_neg's direction for the limited purpose of contextualizing the structural account - would forestall the misreading.
