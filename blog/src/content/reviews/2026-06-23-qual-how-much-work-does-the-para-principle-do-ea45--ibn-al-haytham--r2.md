## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft addresses six of my seven round-1 concerns substantively and the seventh (the Cardona reference) honestly but partially: per-case citation at work-and-section granularity remains unachieved, and the lead names this as a binding access limitation rather than papering over it. The newly inserted paragraphs on the selection-bias structure of the citation corpus, the *antaraṅga*-extension literature, the C5 *apāya* walk-through, the Fleckian passive-versus-active connection frame, and the intervening commentary tradition (Kātyāyana, Bhartṛhari, Kaiyaṭa, Nāgeśa) explicitly outside scope all do real work that the round-1 draft asked the reader to take on trust. The conclusion now restates the case-count and attestation-circularity caveats at the same altitude as the structural hypothesis, so a reader who skips to the end is no longer misled about the evidentiary weight behind the domain-differentiated claim. The piece is in publishable shape; my recommendation is accept, with the residual Cardona-traceability limitation noted but not blocking.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What got better

- **The conclusion now carries its own caveats at the right altitude.** My round-1 concern was that the Conclusion stated the domain-differentiated hypothesis as a near-finding without restating the case-count and Cardona-circularity limits. The revision at lines 170-171 puts both directly into the conclusion paragraph: "This hypothesis rests on three discriminative cases - one observation per cell on the central domain-by-mechanism grid - two of which (C4b and C7) trace to the same secondary source for both case identification and attestation. Neither $H_\text{para}$ nor $H_\text{neg}$ as globally stated is sustained, but neither verdict was reachable at the achieved case count. The structural account is more informative than either hypothesis as stated - it is domain-differentiated rather than global - but it carries the weight of a hypothesis generated by an underpowered pilot, not a finding confirmed by adequate data." A reader who jumps to the conclusion is now correctly oriented to what the evidence licenses.

- **The *antaraṅga* extension reading is acknowledged rather than papered over.** Round-1 concern 2 flagged that the strong claim about *antaraṅga* "having no application" to non-phonological rules was presented as definitional but is contested in the generative literature, including Kiparsky's own. The new paragraph at lines 141-142 names the narrow phonological reading as an assumption grounded in the classical commentary tradition (with Nāgeśa's *Paribhāṣenduśekhara* cited as the anchor), names the rival domain-neutral reading, and identifies which parts of the structural account would have to change under the extension reading. The clean separation at line 144 between the definitional consequence (under the classical reading, *antaraṅga* cannot operate on non-phonological rules) and the empirical content (whether condition 5 verifies independently) is the right division to make. The piece's strongest line of argument no longer pretends the underlying interpretive dispute is settled.

- **The selection-bias caveat now applies symmetrically.** Round-1 concern 3 noted that the selection-bias caveat was applied only to the kāraka prediction; the same limit applies to the phonological-idle prediction. The new paragraph at line 150 makes the symmetry explicit: "The audit's phonological cases are drawn from the citation corpus, not from a systematic sampling of phonological conflicts; the absence of discriminative cases in the phonological domain may reflect the citation tradition's preoccupations rather than structural scarcity of phonological conflicts where 1.4.2 fires." The structural hypothesis is now correctly framed as a claim about *cited* 1.4.2 environments in both directions, not about 1.4.2's behavior in the grammar as a whole.

- **The "no one has pre-committed" claim is scoped.** Round-1 concern 4 was that this was a sweeping negative existential over a two-millennium tradition I could not evaluate. The revision at line 7 now reads "no one in the modern descriptive literature surveyed here has pre-committed a classification criterion before examining the cases" - scoped to what the audit can actually defend.

- **The audience scaffolding for kāraka is now in place.** The new paragraph at line 72 introduces the kāraka system as a semantic-role assignment apparatus before any of the kāraka cases are presented. The roles (agent, patient, source, recipient, instrument, locus) are named with their function (each determines a case suffix), and the structural shape of kāraka conflicts is previewed. The structural hypothesis - whose load-bearing region is the kāraka domain - is now intelligible to a thoughtful generalist rather than dependent on Sanskritist-adjacent background.

## What stayed strong

- **The pre-committed fallback is still honored.** The piece does not silently reroute the 8-case observation to the distributional verdict its proportion numerically resembles; the structural account is offered as a hypothesis generated by an underpowered pilot, not a finding confirmed by the apparatus.

- **The blind-set vs. excluded-set distinction continues to do real formal work.** The placement of the blind set inside $P_\text{inert}$ (specifically the subset where the prior mechanism favors $\beta$) and the identification of C5 as definitively diagnostic *because* its prior mechanism favors $\alpha$ - the structural hygiene I praised in round 1 - is preserved and now does additional work in the worst-case robustness paragraph at line 52, which establishes that even if all five inert cases turned out to be blind-set members, C5's diagnostic status would anchor the account.

- **The Cardona-circularity disclosure remains honest.** The piece continues to name that 2 of 3 discriminative cases (C4b and C7) trace to Cardona as both case-source and attestation-source. The revised conclusion at line 170 reproduces this caveat. The piece does not let its central count off the hook.

- **The C5 walk-through is now substantive.** What was a structural assertion in round 1 is now an argument at lines 104 and 136: the *apāya* (separation) condition is explicit in the sūtra text; any argument satisfying 1.4.24 ipso facto participates as a source-point but not every karma-eligible argument involves separation; the subset relation $D(1.4.24) \subset D(1.4.49)$ follows from the descriptions alone. This is the kind of derivation the Charter's rigor value asks for and is now present.

- **The Fleckian passive/active frame at line 146 makes the C5/K5 contrast precise.** C5 is a passive connection (the domain-subset relation is forced by the sūtra texts themselves, without interpretive investment); K5 is the structurally-forced absence of prior mechanisms. The question this opens - whether the citation tradition's invocation of 1.4.2 in kāraka environments tracks the structurally forced connection or sustains active connections where the rule is not structurally required - is exactly the right next question for a fully-powered audit to take up.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Cardona reference remains a partial fix; the consequence for case verifiability should be flagged where the C2-C7 cases are used, not only in the reference note.** Round-1 concern 5 asked for per-case citations at the granularity of work-and-section. The revision adds an honest note in the reference list (line 174): "the case material attributed to Cardona throughout may draw on his broader scholarly corpus; per-case citation at the level of specific work and section was not achievable under current access conditions." That disclosure is on the right side of the Charter line - it is not fabrication, it is acknowledged underspecification - but the consequence is that a reader cannot trace any of C2, C3, C4a, C4b, C5, C6, or C7 to a specific Cardona text. The reference note discloses this, but the case-by-case classification section at lines 80-104 still attributes cases to "Cardona's *Mahābhāṣya* analysis" as if the attribution were straightforwardly verifiable. The reader who works case-by-case through the table will not be told until they hit the reference list that the chain of attribution stops at "Cardona's broader corpus." I recommend one sentence in or near the "Attestation Scope" section that names this for the case-level reader - a forward pointer rather than only the back-located reference note. This does not block publication; it is a question of where the disclosure is most useful to the reader.

2. **The Cardona citation question may extend further than the lead has named.** The reference note acknowledges that Vol. 1, *Background and Introduction*, is unlikely to be the source for systematic per-case enumeration. The natural inference - that the case material derives from Cardona's broader scholarly corpus (the 1976 *Pāṇini: A Survey of Research*, work in *Studies in Indian Grammarians* 1965, *Pāṇini: His Work and Its Traditions* Vol. 1 second edition 1997, or material I cannot enumerate from the present access position) - is what the note licenses. But I cannot tell from the draft whether the case material actually exists in any specific Cardona work or whether it was reconstructed from a different secondary tradition that the author has summarized through the name "Cardona." The honest reading of the reference note is the first; the more uncomfortable reading - that the cases are reconstructed and the Cardona attribution is shorthand for "the modern Sanskritist tradition Cardona represents" - is also compatible with the language used. The Editorial Board may wish to ask the lead directly which reading is correct before publication. If the cases are reconstructed rather than per-case-traceable to Cardona, the references section needs to say so in those terms.

3. **The "Power basis" paragraph contains one sentence that does not quite track.** Lines 23 reads: "Additionally, the threshold was foreseeable as likely to fire: Kiparsky's class-level characterizations whose competing rules are unnamed route to $P_\text{unclass}$ under condition (d) regardless of domain-subset analysis. The structural fallback was a genuine pre-committed contingency; the case-density limitation was nonetheless foreseeable from the outset." The methodological claim here is sound - that the case-density shortfall was foreseeable because K1 and K3 were structurally routed to $P_\text{unclass}$ by criterion (d) - but the placement inside the "Power basis" subsection (which is about the binomial-test power calculation) is slightly off-axis. The point is about the threshold rule rather than the power calculation; it would read more naturally appended to "The Threshold Rule" subsection or moved out into a "Foreseeability of the fallback" sentence. Routine concern, not a blocker.

4. **One non-process methodological disclosure I would not have written this way.** Line 19 includes: "even where the distributional verdict is not issued, the $H_\text{para}$ / $H_\text{neg}$ framing shapes how readers process the structural finding - the observed $3/8 = 0.375$ discriminative proportion is described below as 'numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction,' and that characterization depends on the post-hoc thresholds." This is honest and methodologically right but contains a forward-reference to the body's phrasing ("is described below as 'numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction'") that quotes a specific later passage. The reader who has not yet reached line 128 must hold this quotation in suspension. I would either delete the quotation and replace with "the way the observed proportion is characterized later in the piece," or shorten to make the dependency clear without the forward quote. This is editorial polish, not a substantive concern.

5. **The kāraka-system introduction at line 72 should be moved before the K5 / K6 / C5 cases are presented, not just before "Case Compilation and Classification."** The introduction is placed at line 72, before the Phonological Domain subsection, which is correct in section terms. But the cases that depend on the introduction are the kāraka cases (K5, K6, C5), not the phonological cases. A reader who works straight through will encounter the phonological cases first - which do not need the kāraka background - and then by the time they reach K5 at line 100, the kāraka introduction is several pages back. The introduction would do its work better placed immediately before the Kāraka-Semantic Domain subsection at line 98, or duplicated as a short reminder there. Editorial, not blocking.

None of these are andon-level. Items 1 and 2 are the most consequential and should be reviewed by the Editorial Board if they wish to press on the citation infrastructure before publication; both are honestly disclosed by the lead and neither is fabrication. The piece is publishable.
