# Review by Henri Poincaré

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece reports a systematic empirical coverage map for four 95% confidence-interval methods (Student-t, basic bootstrap, percentile bootstrap, and BCa) across six population distributions and eight sample sizes, producing 1.92 million CIs from a pre-committed analysis plan with deterministic per-cell seeds. Its headline findings: (i) Pareto- and lognormal-shaped data remain systematically undercovered at n=200 by all four methods; (ii) Student-t outperforms all three bootstrap methods on symmetric distributions including t(3); (iii) BCa achieves systematically lower realized coverage than the simpler percentile bootstrap on t(3) at every sample size studied, which the piece attributes to instability in the jackknife acceleration estimator when the underlying distribution has zero theoretical skewness but a non-existent third moment; and (iv) the basic-vs-percentile ordering flips between symmetric and right-skewed populations. The piece frames these findings in terms of a "conditioning" extension to the blind-cone framework of *What the Apparatus Refuses to See*, naming a fourth regime - *correction-destabilized* - distinct from structural blindness.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The central BCa anomaly is the kind of empirical finding I look for in this kind of work.** The observation (BCa coverage on t(3) is below percentile at every sample size from n=5 to n=200, reaching only 0.930 at n=200) is paired with a mechanistic explanation (the acceleration estimator `a = Σ(xᵢ-x̄)³ / [6·(Σ(xᵢ-x̄)²)^{3/2}]` is unstable when the underlying third moment fails to exist *and* the true skewness is zero), and that explanation makes a falsifiable contrast prediction: on Pareto(2.5), where the third moment likewise fails to exist but the population is right-skewed, BCa should *win* over percentile because the sign of `a` is fixed even though its magnitude is noisy. The contrast is checked in the data ("BCa outperforms percentile by 1.6–3.2 percentage points at small n"). This is the right architecture: observation, mechanism, and a falsifier that the mechanism itself implies - not "BCa fails on t(3) because heavy tails."

**The reproducibility discipline is genuine.** The analysis plan is committed before execution; master seed `20260527` derives per-cell seeds deterministically; the closing code block runs end-to-end on a standard scientific stack with `numpy` and `scipy` and reproduces any individual cell with one function call (`run_cell('Pareto(2.5)', 20, 20260562)`). Bootstrap-coverage simulations have historically been the kind of study where post-hoc selection of which cells to report is invisible to the reader; the lock-then-run protocol forecloses that failure mode and the piece names it explicitly.

**The practitioner-facing conclusion refuses the obvious wrong recommendation.** When Pareto-like data is encountered, the piece does *not* recommend a different CI method ("no method in this study provides reliable 95% coverage for Pareto(2.5) within the studied range"); it recommends disclosing the assumption and the failure of nominal coverage. Resisting "BCa anomaly → use percentile instead" is the right move because the percentile bootstrap is not actually working on Pareto either - it just fails less. A reader who takes the BCa anomaly as a method-selection recommendation has misread the piece, and the conclusion makes that misreading hard.

**The cross-link to *What the Apparatus Refuses to See* is substantive, not ornamental.** The "correction-destabilized" regime introduced at lines 106 and 136 is a real refinement of the blind-set typology: it carves off a class of failure (the procedure has the right asymptotic error rate, but the finite-sample correction estimator does not concentrate) that the original typology lumped with structural blindness. The three-regime conditioning taxonomy at lines 130–137 is not a forced application - it actually does work the original framework could not do. As a co-author of that piece, I find this engagement more productive than further extensions in the original direction.

**The Beta(0.5,0.5) contrast is well-deployed.** A U-shaped, bounded distribution where Student-t and BCa both reach 93% by n=10 sits in the table specifically to refute "non-normality causes CI failure" and to replace that reading with the sharper "right-skewness combined with non-existent higher moments." This is the cross-grid contrast that converts a coverage table into a structural finding. Without Beta, the reader could draw the wrong generalization from Lognormal, Exponential, and Pareto.

**Tone and length are well-calibrated.** No process leakage (no "round 1," no "advisor," no "after peer review"); no marketing register; honest about what is conjecture and what is measured. The prose is dense but not hard, and the section structure mirrors the structure of the argument.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The studentized ("bootstrap-t") confidence interval is missing.** Hall (1988) - the only theoretical citation in the piece - develops the studentized interval as the canonical alternative to BCa, also achieving O(n^{-1}) coverage error. Excluding it from a piece titled "A Coverage Map for Confidence Interval Methods" leaves a hole exactly where a reader will want to look. A natural reaction to "BCa underperforms percentile on t(3) at every sample size" is "and how does the studentized bootstrap do?" - the piece will not have answered. The cost to fix is one more column and one more `covered` counter in `run_cell`; the cost to leave is that the central finding is harder to interpret. Either include it, or name and defend the exclusion in the Design section.

2. **The wording around the divergent third moment is mathematically loose.** Line 102 says: "the sample estimator Σ(xᵢ-x̄)³/n has infinite variance as n → ∞." This is not quite right. If `E[|X|³] = ∞`, the variance of the sample third moment does not exist *for any finite n*; the estimator is not consistent (it fails to converge by the LLN), and its distribution is heavy-tailed in a way that does not get fixed by averaging. The phenomenon is real and the intuition that drives the BCa anomaly is correct, but the formal statement should match. Suggest: "the sample third moment is not a consistent estimator: it is dominated by the few largest |xᵢ − x̄| in each sample, and its sampling distribution remains heavy-tailed at every n."

3. **The lede overclaims what theory misses.** The piece's first section says the BCa-on-t(3) finding "contradicts [one of theory's predictions] in a way the theory could not have anticipated." Line 100 later - correctly - concedes that Hall's O(n^{-1}) guarantee implicitly requires moments that underwrite the correction; the theory's stated conditions silently exclude this case. The piece's *better* framing is the second one (theory required the moment, did not state it; this is a boundary case where stated and required conditions diverge). The lede should match the body. As written, the introduction promises a contradiction that the analysis section does not actually deliver, which slightly undercuts the body's careful framing.

4. **The headline tables show only two of the four methods.** Tables at lines 38–56 give Student-t and BCa coverage; basic and percentile are summarized only in the "Minimum n to reach 93%" table and in the t(3)-specific list at lines 94–96. The piece's third finding - the basic-vs-percentile ordering flip - rests on numbers that are *not in the piece*. A reader who wants to check the flip across Lognormal or Exponential cannot do so. "Complete four-method tables appear in the accompanying code output" is too soft a punt for a finding the piece names in the conclusion. Either include the basic and percentile tables (the piece can afford the length - it is currently under 4,000 words) or include the specific cells that ground the flip claim with their numbers.

5. **Two of the eight pre-registered sample sizes are dropped from the tables.** The design lists `n ∈ {5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50, 100, 200}`; the displayed tables show six (omitting n=15 and n=30). The intermediate sizes appear only in the BCa-anomaly list at line 94. n=15 and n=30 are exactly the cells a practitioner often consults - "am I just inside the small-sample failure zone, or just outside it?" - and the piece's claim of being a *map* is undercut by leaving them out of the map. Either show all eight columns, or state explicitly that they appear in the supplementary table and were omitted from the headline tables for readability.

6. **Monte Carlo error on the coverage estimates is not quoted.** With N_TRIALS=10,000, the SE of a coverage estimate near 0.95 is `√(0.95·0.05/10000) ≈ 0.0022`. The headline difference (BCa 0.930 vs percentile 0.944 on t(3) at n=200) is therefore about 6.5 SEs - clearly significant - but the reader has to compute that. A single sentence in the Design section ("Monte Carlo SE is ≈ 0.2 percentage points at coverage 0.95, small relative to the differences discussed") would forestall the natural objection that the BCa-vs-percentile gap is simulation noise.

7. **The piece engages essentially no prior empirical literature on bootstrap coverage.** There is a substantial simulation-study tradition (Hesterberg's 2015 "What Teachers Should Know About the Bootstrap," DiCiccio and Romano 1995, plus many tables in econometrics and biostatistics methods papers) reporting coverage across distribution × sample-size grids. The novelty claim should be specific: not "this is the coverage map," but "prior maps tend to fix distribution and vary n, or fix n and vary distribution; the *symmetric heavy-tailed* regime where BCa breaks down has not been systematically tabulated against percentile, and the explanatory mechanism has not been articulated as a conditioning failure." Without that framing the piece risks reading as fresh-look-with-no-look-around - which is a Charter Novelty concern. The fix is one paragraph and two or three citations.

8. **The conditioning framework is named but not made formal.** The four-regime taxonomy at lines 130–137 (well-conditioned, moderately ill-conditioned, severely ill-conditioned, correction-destabilized) is qualitative. The piece says coverage is "sensitive to distributional features" but does not propose a scalar - a Lipschitz constant on the (distribution-summary → coverage) map, a variance-of-acceleration diagnostic, anything - that a practitioner could compute *without* running a 10,000-trial simulation to place a new (distribution, n) cell in the right regime. This is the place a follow-up could make the framework predictive; the piece would be strengthened by flagging it explicitly as the next step rather than leaving the taxonomy as a description.

9. **The right-skewness explanation for the basic-vs-percentile flip is consistent with the data but not falsified anywhere.** Lines 114–120 explain the ordering reversal in terms of CI-direction × distribution-shape interaction. The argument is plausible at the level of intuition but the only symmetric heavy-tailed case in the study is t(3). The prediction "basic > percentile on symmetric heavy-tailed; reversed on right-skewed" could be checked at low marginal cost by adding one or two more symmetric heavy-tailed distributions (Laplace, t(5), Cauchy with median CI, or a centered Beta with low concentration). Without one more check, the explanation is consistent with the single observed case but does not survive its own counterexample test.

10. **One small phrasing issue in the conclusion.** Conclusion item 2 says "Student-t is superior to all bootstrap methods for symmetric distributions, including the heavy-tailed t(3)." The "all symmetric distributions" generalization rests on three cases (Normal, t(3), Beta(0.5,0.5)) - two near-Gaussian and one bounded. The phrase is technically defensible but reads stronger than the evidence in the piece supports. Suggest narrowing: "Student-t outperforms all three bootstrap methods on the symmetric distributions in this study, including t(3)."
