## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft retains its central disaggregation — four distinct claims by Nietzsche, Rousseau, Wordsworth, and Thoreau, only one and a half of which the Oppezzo and Schwartz finding reaches — and tightens the apparatus around it. The hippocampal speculation, the dangling Frith and Loprinzi reference, the unsourced Wordsworth anecdote, the 81 percent figure, the slide between "currently unexamined" and "in principle unfalsifiable," the under-interrogated Nietzsche, and the under-skeptical Rousseau have all been addressed substantively rather than papered over. The conclusion no longer repeats the taxonomy and instead names the methods that might reach the tradition's harder claims. The piece is now doing the work it always promised to do, with the gaps in citation and epistemic register closed.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What got better

- **The Nietzsche paragraph is now the strongest revision.** The new paragraph at the head of the Nietzsche section — which acknowledges that "Maxims and Arrows" is "closer to lyric provocation than to philosophical demonstration" and then argues that this register is itself part of the finding — does something I did not expect. Rather than weakening the essay's central claim, the move sharpens it: the vindication narrative has mistaken a provocation for a testable hypothesis, and the lyric register *reveals* the category error rather than dissolving it. This is the right answer to my concern 5, and it is more philosophically interesting than the straightforward "Nietzsche is hyperbolic, take him less seriously" move would have been.

- **The Rousseau skepticism is now properly distributed across the argument.** The added paragraph distinguishing "Rousseau felt he had honest access" from "Rousseau had honest access" places the skepticism exactly where it needs to be — inside the close reading, not appended afterward — and the phrase "the *Reveries* themselves are an object lesson in why the former does not entail the latter" is the kind of compression the essay otherwise rewards.

- **The hippocampal paragraph has been rebuilt rather than patched.** The Andrews-Hanna citation is gone, the speculative mechanism claim is gone, and what remains — the dual-role observation, anchored to O'Keefe and Nadel, offered explicitly as a structural possibility rather than a demonstrated mechanism — is intellectually honest. The author's acknowledgment in the response that they "cannot support" the original mechanism claim is the kind of self-correction the College should reward.

- **Frith and Loprinzi is now load-bearing rather than decorative.** Moving the parameter-sensitivity finding into the body, with the gloss that the claim is more precisely "locomotion of a particular kind, at a moderate pace, for a sufficient duration, increases divergent ideation," does productive work that a parenthetical citation cannot.

- **The new convergent-thinking paragraph is a genuine addition, not a concession.** The question of whether the divergent boost comes at a cost to convergent performance — and the observation that this matters because the tradition's most ambitious claims are about quality of judgment, not quantity of output — was not in the round-1 draft. It is the kind of question my role is supposed to surface, and the essay surfaces it well: as a hypothesis the data cannot adjudicate, raised because the tradition's framing makes it salient.

- **The testability question now has a clear answer.** "Methodologically demanding in ways the current experimental paradigm was not built to meet" is the right formulation, and the worked examples of what evidence might reach the embodied cognition claim — naturalistic composition studies, manuscript analysis of prosodic shifts, longitudinal study of writers with ambulatory practices — give the position concrete content. The author no longer slides between epistemic positions; the position is held.

- **The 81 percent figure is now properly anchored.** Specifying that it is Experiment 1, within-subject, walking versus seated baseline, was a small but important correction. The essay now models the citation hygiene it asks of others.

- **The Wordsworth section is more disciplined.** The dog detail is gone, the mileage figure is properly framed as De Quincey's rough estimate with subsequent biographers' skepticism noted, and what remains — composing aloud while walking, gait structuring meter, walking as compositional medium — is now anchored to defensible attestation rather than folk lore.

- **The conclusion is forward-looking rather than recapitulatory.** Removing the second listing of the four writers, and using the closing space to name what the current paradigm has not tried, makes the ending do the work my round-1 concern 7 asked for. The "untried" pun is preserved but now lands without the redundancy.

## What stayed strong

- The taxonomy of four distinct claims remains the essay's real contribution. No revision diluted it.
- The peripatetic-etymology aside — the tradition that claims to vindicate may itself be built on a myth about walking — is intact.
- The Thoreau reading, which I called the sharpest of the four, is unchanged and still load-bearing.
- The Wordsworth observation that walking was the *compositional medium* rather than an ideation aid still carries the structural argument that the alternate uses task cannot, in principle, reach this claim.
- The rhetorical control of the original — the discipline not to convert the close reading into a takedown of the study — is preserved.
- The Dietrich citation is now complete and the methodological-criticism section sits where it should.

## Concerns

# Concerns

All nine of my round-1 concerns were engaged substantively. What follows is a small set of residual notes, none of which I consider blocking. I record them so that the editorial decision can weigh them against the strengths above; I do not stake a dissent on any of them.

1. **The hippocampal paragraph is now appropriately hedged but borderline on whether it earns its space.** The paragraph admits that the gap between "these systems share anatomical substrate" and "ambulatory activity produces honest introspective access" is large and that the author is not aware of evidence closing it. This is honest, but it raises the question of why the paragraph appears at all — a structural possibility offered without supporting evidence is close to gesturing at a mechanism the author has just disclaimed. I would not remove it; the dual-role observation is genuinely interesting and the hedging is exactly right. But the author might consider tightening the paragraph by one sentence so the rhetorical weight matches the epistemic load.

2. **The "what populates the gap" question is named but not theorized.** My round-1 concern 7 asked the essay to do more with the question of what the four writers were collectively tracking. The revision names the embodied cognition program as the candidate unifying frame and acknowledges this as the strongest version of the tradition's claim. This is an improvement, but the essay still treats the embodied cognition account as the *last* word rather than as a research program with its own commitments and difficulties. A genuinely Poincaréan move here would be to identify the next question — what would it take to test the strong embodied-cognition reading of the tradition against the weak ideation-fluency reading? — and the essay points at this without quite formulating it. I flag this as a missed opportunity, not a defect. The author has reasonable grounds for resisting reorganization around embodied cognition, as explained in the response to Ada Lovelace.

3. **The "175,000 to 180,000 miles" range is now properly hedged but still does some unrhetorical work.** The figure is presented as De Quincey's approximate estimate with subsequent biographers' skepticism noted, which is the right move. The number nonetheless functions as a concrete-seeming anchor for a claim that does not actually depend on a specific count — Wordsworth was a sustained, habitual walker, and the essay would lose nothing by saying so without the numerical envelope. Minor; the hedging makes it acceptable.

4. **The convergent-thinking paragraph raises a question the essay then drops.** The new paragraph is a real addition (see strengths.md), but it surfaces a hypothesis — that the divergent boost might come at a cost to convergent performance — and then moves on. The hypothesis is worth raising precisely because it is testable and would matter. The essay does not need to test it, but a sentence locating who might do so, or what study design would address it, would convert the observation from a rhetorical move into a research question. This is the kind of thing my role is supposed to do, so I am flagging it from that angle rather than treating it as a defect of the essay.

5. **The Diogenes Laertius / Hermippus passage is now properly cited but its work in the argument is unchanged from round 1.** I noted nothing about this in round 1, so this is a new note, not an unaddressed concern. The aside is good intellectual hygiene, but a careful reader might want one additional sentence on what the etymological point licenses — does the possibility that "peripatetic" originally referred to a place rather than a practice strengthen or weaken the essay's reading of the tradition? The essay implies "weakens" (the tradition that claims to vindicate may itself be built on a myth about walking) but does not commit. This is minor and I would not block on it.

None of the above rises to the level of "remaining problem from round 1 inadequately addressed." The author addressed each round-1 concern in good faith and the revisions are real improvements. The residual notes are forward-pointing — directions a thoughtful editor might suggest for a final polish, not corrections.
