# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece argues that at Hedström and Ylikoski's action-formation level, two distinct mechanism families - information/calculation and identity/obligation - are routinely conflated in institutional accounts, and that the conflation generates systematic policy error because the two families predict different failure modes, different interventions, and different transplant behavior. The author specifies three operative conditions for each mechanism, confronts the game-theoretic reductive challenge (norms as internalized enforcement priors) by recasting it as an empirical commitment with diverging predictions, and applies the diagnostic to three prior College audits (referral hiring, compliance monitoring, commons governance) before deploying it prospectively on occupational licensing. The prospective claim is that deregulation literature, by treating licensing as an information mechanism only, predicts a uniform quality degradation across deregulated cohorts when the normative mechanism, if also operative, predicts degradation concentrated in the post-deregulation unaffiliated population - a falsifiable claim the existing data infrastructure does not currently support. The headline contribution is the asymmetry the author names at the close: the information mechanism is designable at installation, the normative is not, which makes the distinction operationally consequential precisely in transplant settings.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The mechanisms are specified, not merely labeled.** Each family is given three operative conditions - for the information mechanism: observability structure, informational relevance, individually rational cost structure; for the normative: participation in norm-setting, identity-constitutive membership, detection-independent emotional response. This is the distinguishing move that prevents the diagnostic from being a vocabulary repackaging. Specifically, the third normative condition ("would comply when unmonitored and feel ashamed in the absence of any audience") names the empirical signature without which the family collapses into its reductive alternative. That is the right operationalization to anchor on.

- **The reductive challenge is confronted on its own terms rather than waved away.** The section "The Reductive Challenge" is the piece's most disciplined move. Rather than asserting that the normative mechanism is irreducible, the author names what the reductive account *commits to empirically* - that compliance should track enforcement history rather than community membership; that exit-from-community-but-continued-monitoring should preserve compliance; that identity-salience manipulations should add no predictive power after controlling for enforcement exposure. This converts a metaphysical-sounding dispute into a set of predictions whose divergence is empirically decidable. This is exactly the right diagnostic posture: the question is not whether the categories *feel* distinct but where they *predict* differently.

- **The transplant asymmetry is the operationally consequential finding.** The closing claim - that the information mechanism is designable at installation time while the normative is not, because community pre-existence cannot be created on a designer's timeline - is the piece's sharpest contribution. The conventional ordering of "technical demanding" versus "sociologically demanding" gets correctly inverted: the technical infrastructure can be built; the social precondition cannot. This reframes how design and transplant decisions ought to be sequenced and is more actionable than the diagnostic's other implications.

- **The licensing prospective application earns its place.** Most diagnostic frameworks demonstrate generality by re-describing cases already in their training set. The licensing section instead makes a falsifiable forward prediction - that post-deregulation quality degradation will concentrate among practitioners who exit professional community structures, not distribute uniformly across the deregulated cohort - and identifies precisely what existing studies fail to collect (individual-level affiliation tracking before and after the policy change). The architectural features of licensed professions named (continuing education, ethics codes, disciplinary boards, formal markers) make the normative mechanism's *operative conditions* present in a real institutional setting in a way the reader can verify against their own knowledge. This is the right shape for a prospective application: a prediction the field could in principle run, and a named reason the data do not yet exist.

- **The Fehr–Gächter anchor is doing real evidential work, not decorative work.** The argument that one-shot anonymous costly punishment is strictly irrational under the information mechanism and consistent with the normative account if subjects have internalized a compliance identity is the kind of decisive-case appeal that the diagnostic needs to ground its empirical bite. The phrasing "What remains is the normative mechanism's distinctive mark" is appropriately careful - it does not claim the result *proves* the normative mechanism, only that it eliminates the alternative pathway the information mechanism would have to use.

- **The retrospective applications are calibrated to the cases.** The three prior pieces are not treated symmetrically - the referral hiring case is criticized for *implicit* defaulting to information, the compliance case for an *undischarged* alternative, and the commons case as the one where the split was already named but not made precise. This graded treatment is more honest than a uniform "all three illustrate the diagnostic" framing would have been.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The observable discriminators in the last row of the table are presented as symmetric in their measurability, and they are not.** The referral-hiring discriminator - "Does the referral quality premium persist when employers have independent quality assessment?" - measures presence/absence of a price premium against an arguably observable construct. The compliance-monitoring discriminator - "Do violations cluster in the hard-to-detect region, or in the population outside the normative community?" - requires partitioning violators by community-membership status that no enforcement dataset currently records, and that the piece itself names as missing infrastructure. The commons discriminator - "Do transplant failures correlate with absent observability infrastructure, or with absent community preconditions?" - requires retroactively coding "community preconditions" across transplant cases in a way that is itself contested. The diagnostic loses some of its purchase if its discriminators are not equally feasible to run. I recommend a short paragraph after the table acknowledging this asymmetry: the information-mechanism conditions are generally easier to measure than the normative-mechanism conditions, which biases the available evidence base in favor of the information account even before any interpretive choice is made. Naming this measurement asymmetry strengthens rather than weakens the central argument - it explains *why* the conflation is durable.

2. **The Fehr–Gächter argument does not engage the reductive response on the very ground the reductive response would occupy.** The piece says "the one-shot anonymous setting eliminates every pathway by which rational expectation of future interaction could sustain costly punishment." But the reductive account named two paragraphs earlier predicts exactly this: subjects bring an internalized probability distribution over punishment *into* the experimental setting from prior socialization, and that distribution can sustain costly punishment in one-shot anonymous play because it was not calibrated for the experimental boundary. The piece needs to say what the reductive account *predicts* for Fehr–Gächter and *why that prediction fails* - for example, the cross-cultural variation in costly punishment that Henrich and colleagues documented after Fehr–Gächter, which is hard for a single internalized-prior story to explain. As stands, the piece's Fehr–Gächter paragraph and its reductive-challenge paragraph are talking past each other; the reader has been given the reductive account and then handed an experimental result whose treatment does not refer back to it.

3. **The Hechter "participation in a norm-setting process" condition is stronger than the normative-mechanism literature generally supports, and the piece uses it as if it were uncontested.** "Hechter makes this condition explicit: it is not sufficient to know a rule exists; one must have been part of the community whose authority it expresses." This is a defensible reading of Hechter on solidary groups, but it is doing more work in this draft than the rest of the normative literature licenses. Bicchieri's account of social norms, for example, requires a normative expectation about others' compliance plus a preference to conform conditional on that expectation - no voice-in-formation condition. Elster's account (cited in the bibliography) is closer to the piece's reading but also does not insist on voice-in-formation as a *necessary* condition. The piece needs either to (a) defend the strong "participation" condition explicitly as the operationalization most likely to be empirically discriminating, acknowledging that it narrows the normative mechanism's scope; or (b) loosen the condition to one that the broader literature would recognize. Currently the condition is load-bearing for the licensing application - "participation in standard-setting" is what the piece offers as the licensed-profession analogue of community voice - but it also implicitly excludes many cases (norms internalized through childhood, professional norms acquired post-hoc without standard-setting voice) that the normative-mechanism tradition would want to include.

4. **The licensing prediction needs operationalization for "concentrated."** "Quality degradation under deregulation will be concentrated in the unaffiliated population, not distributed uniformly across the deregulated cohort." This is a falsifiable claim but the prediction needs a threshold to be testable. What ratio of variance-explained between affiliated and unaffiliated subgroups would count as "concentrated"? What is the null distribution under the information-only account - does it predict *strict* uniformity, or merely no systematic difference by affiliation after controlling for prior credential? Without this operationalization, the prediction is vulnerable to the same problem the piece criticizes elsewhere: it sounds discriminating but cannot be brought into contact with data. I would add one sentence specifying a quantitative shape - for example, that the normative account predicts a between-subgroup variance ratio at least as large as some named multiple of the within-subgroup variance, or that it predicts a quality-by-affiliation interaction term that the information-only account predicts to be null.

5. **The three reductive predictions (compliance tracks enforcement history; exit-but-monitored preserves compliance; identity-salience adds no power after controlling for enforcement exposure) are stated as if they are clean tests, but the piece does not say whether any have been run.** This is a place where the piece could either lift its evidential weight substantially (by pointing to published tests of identity-salience manipulations against controlled enforcement-exposure histories - there is such a literature in regulatory compliance and tax morale) or honestly note that the tests are sketched rather than performed. As stated, a careful reader will wonder whether the diagnostic's empirical bite has been bracketed for "future work" while being presented as already decisive. One paragraph naming the closest extant tests (Frey on tax morale; the German waste-charge studies; the Italian audit-natural-experiment literature) would either land the punch or, if the evidence is mixed, qualify the claim appropriately.

6. **The action-formation locus claim is asserted, not defended.** The opening paragraph says "the action-formation level... is where structural ambiguity most reliably enters" across institutional domains. This is a substantive structural claim that the rest of the piece relies on but does not justify. Why does the ambiguity not enter at the aggregation step? Or at the situational step where "observability structure" is defined? In the three retrospective cases the piece audits, is the ambiguity *always* at action formation, or sometimes elsewhere? A short paragraph would discharge this: name what makes action formation the load-bearing step (e.g., it is the only step where both mechanisms have to specify what an actor does in response to the same situational input, so any specification gap at this step forces an implicit choice between mechanisms). Without this, the framing inherits its scope from the Hedström-Ylikoski apparatus without independent justification.

7. **The transplant asymmetry claim mildly overstates Ostrom's symmetric presentation.** "The symmetric presentation of all eight of Ostrom's design principles as equivalent design requirements obscures this asymmetry." Ostrom herself, particularly in the post-1990 work and in *Understanding Institutional Diversity* (2005), is explicit that the design principles describe what long-enduring institutions exhibit rather than what designers can install, and she repeatedly returns to the difficulty of transplant. The point the piece wants to make is sound - the information/normative asymmetry has not been made precise in the principles' formal presentation - but a phrase like "Ostrom's design principles, when read as equivalent design requirements rather than as the empirical regularities they were drawn from, obscure this asymmetry" would be both more accurate and more defensible.

8. **The closing paragraph reuses the word "diagnostic" without naming what it discriminates.** "Specifying which mechanism is being invoked, before rather than after the intervention is implemented, is the minimum discipline this distinction requires." This is the right conclusion, but the diagnostic has been doing two things in the piece: (a) discriminating which mechanism is operative in an existing institution, and (b) prescribing which mechanism design tools to deploy in a new one. These are different uses with different evidential requirements, and at the close the piece could distinguish them explicitly - the prescriptive use (the practitioner tool) is more secure because the transplant asymmetry forces it, while the diagnostic use (deciding which mechanism is operative in a functioning institution) inherits all the measurement asymmetries named in concern (1). A one-sentence acknowledgment that the practitioner tool is robust where the discriminator is fragile would strengthen the close.

No process-language leakage was detected; the piece reads as a self-contained public artifact. No math-notation issues; the piece is essentially prose with no quasi-mathematical symbols. The citation list is appropriate to the argument and I see no fabricated references.
