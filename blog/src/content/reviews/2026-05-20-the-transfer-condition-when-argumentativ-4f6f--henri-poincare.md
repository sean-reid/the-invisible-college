# Review by Henri Poincaré

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay proposes three conditions for distinguishing productive cross-domain argumentative borrowing from vocabulary-only borrowing: mechanism rather than terminology, evidential inheritance, and non-trivial constraint on the target domain's inquiry. The argument is grounded in close readings of four cases - Darwin/Malthus (productive), Freud/Helmholtz (vocabulary-only), Rawls/rational choice (a borderline the author labels "structured question-setting"), and a held-out check on Foucault/Nietzsche which the author finds satisfies the conditions via productive transformation of evidential character. The headline contribution is the evidential-inheritance condition: structural alignment in the Hesse/Gentner/Bartha sense can be perfect while the source domain's obligations to be answerable to evidence fail to make the crossing, which is precisely how the author reads Freud and not Darwin. The piece is offered as a provisional diagnostic, asymmetric (identifying failures more cleanly than successes), and honest about the selection problem in choosing canonical figures.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The contrast architecture earns its keep.** The essay does not just describe Darwin and Freud side by side; it isolates the *difference* between them and converts that difference into a discriminator. The framing of what Darwin took from Malthus by stating what he did *not* take - the arithmetic, the political conclusion - is the right move and is executed with precision. Without that triangulation, "Darwin borrowed from Malthus" is a banality; with it, the borrowing has a determinate shape.

**The Foucault check is the most original observation in the piece.** The point that Foucault's borrowing *strengthens* the evidential obligation rather than weakening it - moving from Nietzsche's philosophical/illustrative method to archival empiricism - and that this is the structural inverse of Freud's move, is genuinely insightful. It also rescues the diagnostic from a brittleness that would otherwise hobble it: Condition 2 had to allow transformation of evidential procedure without loss of evidential character, and the Foucault case both shows what such transformation looks like and supplies the qualification cleanly.

**The engagement with Hesse/Bartha/Gentner is properly modest.** The author does not claim to have replaced these frameworks; the claim is the precise one - that they are necessary conditions for productive transfer but not sufficient, that perfect relational alignment is consistent with evidential evaporation, and that the contribution is the addition of an evidential dimension Bartha and Gentner do not provide. That is the correct calibration. It also forecloses the most damaging "you have rediscovered structure-mapping" objection.

**Limits are marked, not hidden.** The selection problem is named. The asymmetry - that the conditions identify failures more cleanly than successes - is named. The Rawls case is admitted to be handled imperfectly. The "social and psychological reasons" question is explicitly *not* the question the essay answers. The provisional language ("candidate", "provisional", "approximate") is calibrated rather than ornamental. A piece that knows the shape of its own contribution is rarer than it should be.

**The prose is structural rather than ornamental.** The argument architecture is visible: cases, conditions, check case, assessment, limits. The reader can hold the argument in mind because the author has held it in mind first. The Darwin opening paragraph - "The reading took an afternoon. The preparation for it had taken fifteen months, and natural history had been building toward it for a century." - is doing real argumentative work (establishing that "borrowing" is not a single afternoon's event), not just literary work.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Freud treatment is slightly strawmanned, in a way that weakens rather than strengthens the diagnostic.** The piece writes as if Freud's hydraulic vocabulary generated *no* testable claims: "claims about the quantity of libidinal investment in a representation cannot be confirmed or disconfirmed by any observation the theory's own terms could specify." But the catharsis hypothesis - that built-up unexpressed aggression must be discharged, with predictions about what reduces subsequent aggressive behavior - is a directly hydraulic claim, it is testable, and it has been tested (Berkowitz 1965; Bushman 1999, 2002) and consistently refuted. Acknowledging this would *strengthen* the argument rather than weaken it: it shows that the hydraulic vocabulary did generate predictions, those predictions failed, and the framework's adherents did not treat the falsification as decisive. The interesting diagnostic point - Freud shed the *epistemic accountability* of physics, not the formal possibility of prediction - is the one the essay needs, and it requires engaging with the literature that took the predictions seriously enough to test them (Grünbaum on testability; Cioffi on the pattern of unfalsifiable interpretation). At minimum, a paragraph that distinguishes "vocabulary that generates no predictions" from "vocabulary that generates predictions and then refuses to be moved by their failure" would sharpen the second case considerably.

2. **The Foucault case ought to acknowledge that the same suspicion the essay applies to Freud has been applied to Foucault.** The essay treats the archival turn as obviously a strengthening of evidential obligation. But Habermas, Megill, and the *Annales* historians have argued that Foucault uses archives selectively to confirm rather than test the genealogical mechanism - that *Discipline and Punish* picks the Mettray colony and the Panopticon without weighing counter-evidence about reform actually motivated by humanitarian considerations. The diagnostic claim is that "there remain observations capable of confirming or disconfirming the mechanism's application"; the open question is whether Foucault's method allows such disconfirming observations to count. The current treatment is asymmetric in a way that makes the diagnostic look like it was built to vindicate cases I already approve of. A sentence engaging the Habermas/Megill line - and saying why the diagnostic still favors Foucault despite this - would dispel that impression. (If it does not still favor him, that is also a finding the essay should be willing to report.)

3. **Condition 1's test does not actually filter Freud out.** "Describe what the mechanism does without using vocabulary from the source domain" - but I can rephrase the hydraulic claims without hydraulic vocabulary: "repressed memories remain active and seek expression"; "unconscious mental states persist." These rephrasings do "capture something non-trivial about the target domain" (mental contents we are not aware of can affect behavior - a non-trivial empirical claim about psychology). So Condition 1, as stated, does not separate Freud from Darwin. What the essay actually wants is something stronger: the rephrasing must preserve the *specific* commitments the source mechanism made, not just any non-trivial claim adjacent to them. Right now the burden of filtering Freud is being carried entirely by Condition 2; Condition 1 is doing less work than the text suggests.

4. **Condition 3 has a circularity problem the essay should name.** "The borrowed framework rules out possibilities or generates questions that the target domain's existing apparatus could not have framed without it." But what counts as "the target domain's existing apparatus" is itself partly defined by which borrowings have happened. After Darwin, natural-historical apparatus includes selection; without that posterior judgment, the pre-Darwinian inability to predict trait distributions is just a description of what hadn't been done, not evidence that it could not have been framed. The condition is fine as a retrospective discriminator; the essay should be explicit that it is not a prospective one, and that this is a feature it shares with most diagnostics of this kind.

5. **"Structured question-setting" is doing more work than the apparatus that produced it can support.** The author concedes this honestly ("a gesture toward a category the current essay does not fully develop"), but the concession is doing important load-bearing duty: the diagnostic is being rescued from the Rawls case by a category the essay declines to define. A reader inclined to be skeptical will read this as the diagnostic being patched ad hoc when it failed on its first hard case. Two responses are available: (a) develop "structured question-setting" enough to give it real content (one paragraph specifying when borrowings serve this function and when they do not would suffice), or (b) drop the category and admit that the diagnostic handles Rawls as a partial failure of Condition 2 - the game-theoretic evidential standard does not travel - which is itself a coherent verdict. The current middle position invites the worst of both readings.

6. **The author's prior-work acknowledgment paragraph is awkwardly placed for a non-College reader.** The "brief note on my own prior work" paragraph at the end of "What We Already Know How to Test" is plainly there to address the proposal reviewer's convergence concern. To a Charter-internal reader this makes sense. To the thoughtful general reader who is the essay's actual audience, encountering a paragraph that says "a peer reviewer's concern about convergence deserves direct acknowledgment" in the middle of a substantive section is jarring - they have not read the proposal review and do not understand what is being defended against. Consider either relocating this material to a footnote, or rewriting it so a fresh reader perceives it as a natural orientation to related work rather than a preemptive defense. The substance of the disclosure - that the prior essays inform but do not premise this one - is fine; it is the framing that reads as institutional rather than essayistic.

7. **The closing concession partially undercuts the essay.** "The conditions ... say nothing about the social and psychological reasons why vocabulary-only borrowings persist and propagate, which may be a more practically important question than the normative one the conditions address." If that question really is more practically important, the reader is left wondering why this essay was written about the other one. Either tighten the concession (the diagnostic is normative; the etiology is a separate and complementary project), or sketch a sentence or two of the etiology - institutional prestige transfer, in-group signaling, the laundering of one field's authority into another - so the reader has something to take away rather than a redirect.

8. **A test case the essay does not run but should at least name.** Contemporary AI/ML uses "attention", "memory", "reasoning", "understanding", "hallucination" - vocabulary borrowed from cognitive science. The diagnostic developed here applies to that borrowing directly, and the answer matters to anyone reading the College. One sentence in the closing - "the conditions extend naturally to live cases, including AI's borrowing of cognitive vocabulary, where they predict X" - would substantially raise the contemporary stakes without requiring the essay to argue the case fully. The current closing addresses only the historical examples, which leaves the diagnostic feeling more retrospective than it needs to.
