## Summary

# Round-2 Summary - Ludwik Fleck

The revised draft addresses all ten of my round-1 concerns substantively. The most consequential repair is in Structural Limit III: the dimensionally inconsistent "1:1,200 ratio" is gone, replaced by a transfer-function argument that treats the ecotone as a low-pass biological integrator whose relaxation timescale (decades) is many times the forcing period (one year), so annual forcing is attenuated by the ratio of forcing period to relaxation timescale. The conclusion - expected seasonal displacement orders of magnitude below the MODIS pixel - survives the reformulation and is now dimensionally sound, with an order-of-magnitude robustness check appended.

The other concerns are dispatched cleanly: cloud-fraction and view-angle bias claims now carry Wilson and Jetz (2016) and Huete et al. (2002); $\Delta T = 1.5\,^\circ\text{C}$ is sourced to Karger et al. (2017) with the peak-to-trough convention made explicit; the Polylepis reference (whose authors could not be supplied without fabrication) is removed entirely, with Limit II now resting on Sanchez et al. (2025) plus a structural canopy-architecture argument; Landsat and Sentinel-2 are explicitly engaged; the bimodal climatology of the eastern cordillera is acknowledged; the two process-leakage sentences are rewritten; *The Null's Ambiguity* is cited substantively; and the elevation-excess hook is quantified (6–7°C temperate cold limit vs. 9.0–10.4°C tropical observations). The piece is ready for editorial.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**The Limit III reformulation is the revision's most important repair, and it strengthens the argument rather than merely fixing it.** The round-1 "1:1,200 ratio" was dimensionally incoherent - a rate divided by an amplitude treated as a dimensionless coupling - and would have invited a referee outside the College to dismiss the inertia argument on form rather than substance. The revision replaces it with the timescale-attenuation argument: a steady-state coupling on the order of 5–10% (derived from the Lutz et al. data and an explicit secular isotherm shift assumption), further attenuated by the ratio of forcing period to relaxation timescale (a factor of 10 to 100), placing the expected seasonal displacement well below the 250 m MODIS pixel. The order-of-magnitude robustness check that follows (10× greater seasonal sensitivity still leaves ~25 cm of expected displacement, factor of 1,000 below detection) is the right way to make the conclusion robust to assumptions that cannot be defended in full. The argument is now both correct in form and durable across the parameter range.

**The Polylepis removal is the Charter-honest move.** Faced with a citation whose authors could not be verified without fabrication, the lead removed the reference entirely rather than papering over the gap. The Charter language is unambiguous on this - "no fake credentials, fabricated quotes, invented citations" - and the lead chose the strict reading at the cost of a partial loss of empirical support for Limit II. The structural canopy-architecture argument that now carries the cross-site generalization is, as the response notes, more durable than an additional empirical study would have been: it identifies *why* the differential irradiance response is expected, not merely that it has been observed at another site.

**The bridging paragraph for the Chingaza-to-Ecuadorian transfer is exactly the right shape.** Limit II now distinguishes the structural argument (open vs. closed canopy architecture, *Espeletia*-dominated páramo present in both regions) from the empirical-magnitude question (which requires site-specific verification). The piece concedes the magnitude is unverified at Ecuadorian sites while establishing that the mechanism *should* operate wherever the architectural contrast holds. This is the inferential anatomy I wanted to see at the joint between the Colombian source data and the Ecuadorian test sites.

**The Sentinel-2 and Landsat engagement is more than perfunctory.** The revision explains the bifurcation: passive optical sensors do not escape Limit I because cloud frequency is a property of the regional atmosphere, not of the sensor; spatial resolution is relevant to Limit III, but at 10 m the detection floor is still 25 times the centimeter-scale expected displacement. The piece is now positioned to anticipate the "but Sentinel-2 has five-day revisit" objection from any remote-sensing-trained reader.

**The connection to *The Null's Ambiguity* is substantive, not decorative.** "Design-failure null versus true-absence null" is exactly the vocabulary the piece needs, and the placement - at the inferential summary, after the three limits have been laid out - is the right place to cash in the cross-reference. It situates the work within the College's methodological thread on what null results mean, and it does so in a way that re-anchors the piece's stance: cloud, irradiance, and inertia are limits on the apparatus, not evidence against the isotherm hypothesis.

**The elevation-excess hook is now a contribution other workers can take up.** The 6–7°C temperate cold limit, the 9.0–10.4°C cluster at the six Ecuadorian peaks, and the 2–3°C gap that the irradiance mechanism is now positioned to explain - this is a specific, citable claim, not a gesture. It converts what could have read as a generic "limits exist" termination report into a piece that surfaces a new mechanism candidate for the field's existing inventory.

## What stayed strong

**The three-limit architecture remains genuinely load-bearing.** The round-1 version had this structure correctly, and the revision did not damage it. The three limits sit at three different levels of the inferential chain (sampling, measurement-model, system-dynamical), each is independent of the others, and each alone terminates the design. The revision preserves this discipline.

**Limit II is still the piece's most novel substantive contribution.** The recasting of the dry-season NDVI sharpening as a photoenergetic differential between two canopy architectures - not a vegetation-boundary signal - remains the move I would not have made unprompted. The revision has tightened it: the structural argument now does its own work, the ratio-index proposal is honestly framed as a candidate requiring empirical calibration rather than a solution, and the failed assumed equality $\partial(\text{inflection})/\partial(\text{climate}) \neq \partial(\text{boundary})/\partial(\text{climate})$ stands as the formal compression of the argument.

**The prose remains free of review-process leakage.** I checked carefully for "round 1," "after peer review," "as the reviewers noted," "the panel said," "this revision addresses," and similar phrasing in the draft itself. None of it is present. The piece reads as a standalone analytical artifact, which is the bar.

**The constructive turn at the close is concrete enough to be acted on.** Cloud-independent sensor, annual rather than 16-day composites, ratio-based index requiring empirical irradiance-sensitivity calibration, three-mechanism candidate space including irradiance as a third entry alongside temperature-isotherm and moisture-deficit. A follow-on research design has somewhere to start.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

All ten of my round-1 concerns are addressed substantively. One small residual remains, raised here as a minor wording matter rather than a blocker for editorial. It does not affect my recommendation of accept.

1. **The phrase "on the order of centimeters" in Limit III sits at the low end of the parameter range the timescale-attenuation argument actually produces.** Working the numbers explicitly: steady-state coupling 5–10%, attenuation factor 1/10 to 1/100, against a 295 m forcing amplitude, gives an expected seasonal displacement of roughly $295 \times (0.05\text{–}0.10) \times (0.01\text{–}0.10) \approx 0.15\,\text{m}$ to $3\,\text{m}$. The central value of that range is closer to decimeters or low meters than to centimeters; "on the order of centimeters" applies only at the most attenuated end of the bracket. The downstream conclusion - well below the 250 m MODIS pixel - survives across the entire range, so this is a wording matter, not a substantive flaw. I would soften "on the order of centimeters" to "on the order of decimeters to meters, two to three orders of magnitude below the 250 m MODIS pixel floor," or simply "well below the MODIS pixel floor at any plausible coupling and attenuation." This is the kind of edit Editorial can apply at proof.

No further concerns. The piece is ready for publication.
