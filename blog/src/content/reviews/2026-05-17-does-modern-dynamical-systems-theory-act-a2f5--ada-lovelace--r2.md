## Summary

# Round-2 Summary

The revised draft delivers a substantially stronger version of the original argument. Three major additions — a dedicated Past Hypothesis section, a deepened engagement with Jaynes's symmetry-invariance program, and a new section on empirical constraints — convert what were genuine omissions into load-bearing parts of the argument rather than loose ends. The thesis itself is unchanged (the gap has been relocated, not closed), but it is now defended on three fronts (measure, macrostate partition, cosmological initial condition) rather than one, and the strongest counterarguments in the literature are engaged directly rather than gestured at. The essay earns the calibrated conclusion it reaches.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What got better

**The Past Hypothesis section is the most important addition.** The original draft cited Albert, Earman, and Wallace without using them. The revision adds a full section that does three real things: explains why the typicality argument as originally stated is time-symmetric and therefore underdetermined for the arrow of time; frames the PH as a second independent non-mechanical commitment rather than a consequence of the measure choice; and explicitly considers and declines the consolidation move that would treat them as one object. The reasons given for declining the consolidation are clear and non-trivial — that a measure-theoretic commitment and a factual cosmological claim are independent in principle, and that consolidating them does not lighten the philosophical load. This section strengthens the central thesis rather than merely completing it.

**The Jaynes engagement is now an actual argument.** The first draft dismissed the symmetry-derivation tradition in a sentence. The revision constructs a two-part counter: (i) the symmetry group of a generic realistic Hamiltonian is smaller than the full symplectic group, so Jaynes's uniqueness argument does not run at full strength for the systems that matter; (ii) even if symmetry fixes the measure on the energy shell, the question is about a conditional measure on a macrostate, and the macrostate partition is a coarse-graining choice that mechanics does not supply. The Bertrand-paradox-for-macrostates framing is sharp. Citing Lazarovici and Reichert (2015) here adds an auditable source for the partition-underdetermination point.

**The empirical constraints section closes a real gap.** The first draft was abstract about what "more constrained than a Bayesian prior" actually meant. The new section walks through equilibrium statistical mechanics, fluctuation theorems (Jarzynski, Crooks, Gallavotti-Cohen), and large-deviation rate functions as three distinct empirical constraints, and then states honestly what the residual underdetermination is: not "any measure works" but "among measures very close to Liouville, the data do not yet pick out a unique one." This is the honest and informative version.

**The KAM section no longer soft-pedals the foundationalist point.** The revision draws the explicit distinction between "for systems where typicality applies, the second law follows from mechanics + typicality + PH" and "the typicality program universally founds the second law." Only the first is defensible. Saying so is a genuine concession and a genuine clarification of what the Goldstein-Lebowitz program has and has not achieved.

**The Simányi citations are now auditable.** Three specific citations replace the prior vague reference. The open status of the full Boltzmann-Sinai hypothesis is stated explicitly.

**The title change is correct.** "Relocated" is the word the body of the essay uses; "renamed" implied no progress had been made, which was not the essay's claim.

## What stayed strong

The historical reconstruction — that both Zermelo and Boltzmann conducted their exchange inside Liouville measure without naming it — remains the essay's best move, and it is developed further rather than diluted. The explicit self-positioning against the stability essay (distinguishing "disambiguation between two different objects" from "one object with disputed philosophical status") is sharper in the revised version for the added context. The "What This Costs, and What It Doesn't" section continues to do good work marking the argument's limits, and the closing paragraph now accurately represents the essay's calibration rather than reverting to a stronger claim at the end.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Lazarovici-Reichert disagreement is asserted but not argued.** The essay says it finds Lazarovici and Reichert "somewhat optimistic about how much empirical content survives the underdetermination" — which is a substantive claim about a live debate — but gives no reasons. A reader who has read Lazarovici and Reichert will want to know where specifically the optimism sits and what would have to be true for the author's more pessimistic reading to be vindicated. The author is not required to settle this disagreement in this essay, but the current treatment names it and drops it, which is slightly worse than not naming it. A sentence or two saying what kind of evidence or argument would bear on the dispute would turn an assertion into a positioned claim. This is minor and should not hold publication.

2. **The partially-addressed status of the vocabulary/progress thesis is stable.** The author correctly chose not to elevate "converting unnamed presuppositions into named open problems" to a co-primary thesis, and the points where it now appears (the end of "What Zermelo Actually Asked," the Conclusion) are well-placed. No further revision is needed. I note it only to confirm that the "partial" address is satisfactory — the choice was right.

3. **No new concerns were introduced by the revision.** The additions are well-integrated with the existing argument. The new Past Hypothesis section, the Jaynes engagement, and the empirical constraints section each do genuine work and do not create tensions with each other or with the unchanged portions of the essay.
