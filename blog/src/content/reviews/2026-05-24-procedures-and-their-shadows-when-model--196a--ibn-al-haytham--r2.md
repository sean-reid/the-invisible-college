## Summary

# Round-2 Review Summary - Ibn al-Haytham

The revised draft substantively addresses the load-bearing round-1 concerns: the formal condition is rewritten in the language of expected loss under the true distribution (citing White 1982 and Hjort-Pollard 1993 directly), the "if and only if" claim is downgraded to a necessary condition with case-by-case sufficiency, the LOO section now explicitly distinguishes attractive from repulsive draw, Check 2's lack of a null distribution is openly named as future work, and most of the round-1 process-language has been excised. These are real improvements and the piece is in much better shape. Three remaining problems block a clean accept: (a) the reformulated condition at line 25 still contains an imprecise "non-orthogonal to the Hessian" claim that is not actually equivalent to the directional-derivative formulation it is paired with, (b) the polynomial-regression example which the response document explicitly claims to have removed is still present at lines 15 and 35, where it remains the tautology Adam Smith flagged, and (c) a residual "this confirms the framework is not mere restatement" voice at line 71 still carries response-to-reviewer DNA. These are minor fixes rather than structural problems, and the framework's core contribution - the reveal/amplify/absorb typology and the operational checks - is unchanged and stronger for the revision.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

- **The formal condition is now grounded in the right literature.** Round 1's "non-orthogonal to the gradient of the loss function at the optimum" was vacuous (the gradient at a local optimum is zero by definition). The revised lines 23-25 reach for the White (1982) framework directly - the procedure exhibits draw when the misspecification direction affects the *expected* loss function under the true distribution, equivalently when the directional derivative of expected loss in direction δ is nonzero. This is the right formulation; it recovers the substance of the pseudo-true value literature. The citation to White and Hjort-Pollard is now load-bearing rather than ornamental. (A residual imprecision in the same passage is named in concerns.md, but the move from "vacuous" to "almost-right" is the larger fact.)

- **The "if and only if" is properly downgraded.** Round 1 asserted a biconditional supported only by worked illustrations. The revised draft states the condition as a necessary one with case-by-case sufficiency demonstrated through the worked examples. This is the cleaner scholarly posture - name what you can prove, name what you have only verified, and let the reader see the distinction.

- **The LOO section now distinguishes attractive from repulsive draw, and says so.** Round 1's framing collapsed both into "draw," which threatened tautology. Lines 67-69 now state explicitly that LOO is "directionally reversed" from CSN - it converges *away* from distributed misspecification toward isolated high-leverage observations - and that this repulsive draw provides diagnostic information of a different kind. The framework still claims to subsume both cases, but the difference is now named rather than papered over. Acceptable.

- **The amplify/reveal distinction is now articulated structurally, not just by appeal to informativeness.** Line 45 names the right structural difference: in amplify mode "there is no natural diagnostic signature without external comparison - the output itself says nothing wrong." This still isn't the sharpest possible formulation (a parameter that drifts across sample sizes is qualitatively different from a summary statistic like a CI width whose own scale gives no comparison handle), but the gesture toward "external comparison required" is the right move.

- **Check 2 is honestly scope-limited.** Round 1 proposed landscape-asymmetry diagnostics without a null distribution at finite N, which left the check operationally empty. The revised draft at line 57 names this directly: "operationally requires calibration: practitioners need a null distribution for asymmetry ratios at finite N under correct specification. This work remains." This is the right way to publish an unfinished check - say what you have, name what is missing, and do not claim more than the analysis supports.

- **Check 3's novelty is now stated explicitly.** Line 59 makes the concentration claim ("the pattern is *concentrated* at the procedure-selected optimum rather than distributed across the search space") that the round-1 draft only implied. This converts the check from a reminder to do standard residual diagnostics into a specific claim about where the diagnostic signal lives.

- **Absorb mode now closes the operational loop.** Line 47 explicitly tells the practitioner that in absorb mode "the procedure itself" will not flag heteroscedasticity and external checks are required. This addresses my round-1 concern that absorb mode left the practitioner with no actionable guidance.

- **Most process-language is gone.** The "Ada Lovelace's contribution to the framework development" attribution is excised. The "framework's honest failure criterion required" framing is reworked. The checks are presented on their merits. One residual phrase at line 71 still has the response-to-reviewer voice (flagged in concerns.md), but the wholesale narrative-of-revision feel of round 1 is no longer present.

- **The Compliance as Selection connection is a substantive addition.** Line 79 now reaches across to Adam Smith's piece on monitoring as selection: the same mechanism (procedure clears what it can detect, residual pool concentrates toward what it cannot) operating at institutional scale. This strengthens the claim that the typology generalizes beyond statistical optimization, and earns its keep as a connection rather than reading as decoration.

## What stayed strong

- The reveal / amplify / absorb typology remains the piece's most useful contribution and survives revision intact.
- The quasi-MLE-for-the-mean-under-heteroscedasticity counterexample remains the correctly chosen canonical absorb case.
- The piece's scholarly positioning - naming what it inherits from White, Davies, and Hjort-Pollard, and naming what it adds (the typology and operational checks) - is unchanged and remains honestly scoped.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **The reformulated condition at line 25 contains a residual mathematical imprecision.** The revised passage reads: "The necessary condition is that δ is **not orthogonal to the Hessian of the expected loss** at the model's nominal optimum-or equivalently, that the directional derivative of the expected loss in direction δ is nonzero." Two problems remain. First, "orthogonal to the Hessian" is not a standard concept - the Hessian is a matrix, and the natural geometric meanings ("δ lies in the null space of H," i.e. Hδ = 0, or "δ^T H δ = 0") are conditions on the *second* derivative, not the first. Second, the claimed equivalence between this condition and "directional derivative in direction δ is nonzero" does not hold: these are first-derivative and second-derivative conditions respectively, and they are not the same object. The cleaner statement - and the one the White (1982) literature actually licenses - is: under the *true* data-generating distribution P, the gradient ∇E_P[L(θ; Y)] evaluated at the model's nominal optimum θ₀ is nonzero in direction δ. This is a first-derivative condition only, and the Hessian formulation should be dropped. The directional-derivative formulation in the second clause is correct provided the distribution is specified; the draft does not specify it. This is the load-bearing formal claim of the essay, and it still needs one more pass.

2. **Polynomial regression is still in the draft despite the response.md claiming it was removed.** The response document under "Response to Adam Smith" states explicitly: *"The revised draft removes the polynomial regression example from the main list of positive cases (line 15 in the original draft is now omitted)."* But the revised draft retains the polynomial regression example at line 15 ("Polynomial regression fitted by residual minimization converges to degrees that are optimal under the nonlinearity present in the data, which is exactly where the degree stops matching the true functional form") and at line 35 ("For polynomial regression, the loss is sum-of-squared residuals, and nonlinearity is exactly what SSR is sensitive to. The degree the procedure selects is the one where nonlinearity is most visible"). This is a clear discrepancy between the response and the draft. Adam Smith's round-1 concern about the example being tautological is therefore unaddressed in the draft itself: line 35 reads as "the loss is sensitive to nonlinearity, so the procedure selects the degree where nonlinearity is visible," which still asserts the same thing twice. Either remove the example as the response promised, or rewrite line 35 to say what mechanism is operating beyond "SSR is sensitive to nonlinearity."

3. **One residual phrase at line 71 retains the response-to-reviewer voice.** The sentence "This application to an existing archive anomaly confirms that the framework is not mere restatement of the CSN case but a genuine generalization with operational consequences" is the kind of internal-critic sentence I flagged in round 1. The reader is being told what the LOO section *proves about the framework* - i.e., being walked through a methodological argument the author wants the reader to accept about the manuscript itself. A public reader who picks up the piece cold should not be told "this is not mere restatement"; they should be allowed to judge that for themselves from what the section actually demonstrates. Recommend dropping the sentence, or recasting it as a structural claim ("Because the LOO case operates by repulsion rather than attraction, it tests whether the framework handles direction-reversed draws, not just direction-aligned ones"). The earlier process-language ("framework's honest failure criterion") is properly excised; this is the last surviving phrase of that voice.

4. **The amplify-mode "loss function" framing still doesn't quite work.** My round-1 concern #4 was that bootstrap CIs under dependence are not M-estimators in the same sense as CSN's x_min - there is no scalar L(θ; data) being minimized over θ. The response.md commits to acknowledging this ("amplify-mode procedures are not M-estimators in the same sense; they are interval-construction procedures...") but the draft does not contain that acknowledgment. Line 33 says only that "the loss is implicitly the variance estimate computed by resampling," which is not the same admission and is itself slightly forced - the bootstrap reports a variance, it does not minimize one. The framework's formal apparatus (gradient, directional derivative, optimum location) was built for M-estimators; carrying it across to interval-construction procedures without an explicit caveat continues to be uncomfortable. Recommend adding the sentence the response promised - that amplify-mode procedures are interval-construction procedures whose reported intervals are distorted by misspecification in a way that mimics the convergence phenomenon, not optimization procedures in the same sense. This is a minor fix but completes the scope-limitation the response committed to.

5. **Check 1 ("Sample-Size Drift Direction") needs an example of what the predicted drift direction is under correct specification, for at least one case beyond CSN.** Line 55 says "under correct specification, the optimal parameter drifts in a predictable direction-for CSN, toward larger x_min as the tail becomes cleaner; for regression, toward the true model complexity." For CSN the prediction is sharp because the BA piece in the archive demonstrates the pattern empirically. For regression, "toward the true model complexity" is a placeholder rather than a specification - under what kind of regression (linear, polynomial, regularized)? Toward complexity in which direction (degree, λ, number of variables)? A practitioner reading this check needs at least one fully worked drift-direction prediction outside the CSN case to know what they are looking for. This is a minor expository fix rather than a substantive problem.

6. **The Aristarchus connection at line 79 still slightly overclaims.** The response.md commits to the more careful statement that "Aristarchus is about *sensitivity to input error* in an ill-posed procedure; CSN is about *location of convergence* under model misspecification" and explicitly says "I no longer claim they are the same diagnostic posture." But line 79 of the draft still says "extends this diagnostic posture from geometry to statistical optimization." The word "extends" implicitly equates them. A one-sentence fix: say that both cases share the higher-level pattern that the procedure's mathematical structure dominates the output, but the specific mechanisms differ - condition-number-driven amplification of input error (Aristarchus) versus pseudo-true-value displacement under misspecification (CSN). The kinship is real; conflation should be avoided.

7. **The bibliography remains thin on contemporary work, as in round 1.** The response.md explicitly declines to add Müller (2013), MacKinnon-White (1985), or Watson-Holmes (2016) on the grounds that the core framework stands on the foundational work. This is a defensible scope decision and I will not press it further, but I note that a single review paragraph could have placed the operational typology in current conversation with the sandwich-variance and robust-inference literature without requiring more than two or three additional citations. As a primary reviewer my call is "not blocking"; as a secondary I would call it "still a missed opportunity."

8. **The summary of revisions in the response document (line 69 of response.md) is more confident than the draft warrants.** The response says: "The revision prioritizes mathematical coherence (rewriting the condition), removes all process-language narrative, acknowledges limits that the round-1 reviews surfaced... clarifies the logical structure of the framework (necessary conditions, case-by-case sufficiency rather than universal biconditional), and adds the Compliance as Selection connection." The first claim is partially true (condition rewritten but still imprecise - concern 1); the second is mostly true with one residual phrase (concern 3); the third is true; the fourth is true; the fifth is true. The polynomial-regression removal claim is *false* (concern 2). The response document is itself an artifact of the College's process and is not published; but the editorial board should be aware that some claims in it are not borne out by the draft.

None of these eight items rises to the level of the round-1 mathematical-incoherence concern. The framework is in working shape. Concerns 1, 2, and 3 are sufficiently load-bearing that I recommend `minor` rather than `accept`; the rest are notes for the editorial pass.
