# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**The C1/C3 distinction is now made positively, not flagged-and-deferred.** This was the load-bearing concern in round 1, and the revision does the structural work the original deferred. The new "Three Mechanisms, Two Modes" section reorganizes the taxonomy around the environmental/reader-state axis and engages the "isn't it all just 'already knowing the answer'?" collapse explicitly: it names the two competing simplifications, argues for the second (environmental as primary distinction, condition 2 and 3 as reader-state sub-types), and grounds the choice on the right criterion - *recoverability*. The historian-of-ideas counterargument - couldn't a Skinnerian reconstruction restore the framework? - is met head on with the framework-as-object vs. framework-as-live-environment distinction, which is the exact structural fact that makes environmental decay distinct from reader-state override. The argument is now made on the page rather than gestured at.

**The reader-state family is given internal structure.** The new sub-distinction within reader-state - content-template (bracketable within a reading) vs. method-knowledge (requires prior un-exposure) - is the kind of clean structural cut the piece is best at. Lines 71 spell out the operative difference: "what attention can achieve within a reading and what requires a different preparation before it." This is the right level of resolution.

**Condition 3 is now properly defended.** The strongest objection from round 1 - that the *Investigations* was always written for prepared readers, never for naive ones, so the "decay from naïveté" frame misdescribes the situation - is now accepted and used as a revision rather than a refutation. The reformulated claim ("decay from *designed-for* preparation to *wrong* preparation") is more defensible and more precise. The further sub-decomposition (knowing-the-fragmented-form-is-intentional vs. knowing-the-specific-arguments) is a useful additional cut that makes the strategic-naïveté remedy honest about its limits.

**The Baldwin section now has its composite case.** The paragraph at line 49 acknowledges that "Notes of a Native Son" plausibly did condition 1 work in 1955 (destabilizing the framework that Black American experience was comprehensible from outside) and that the condition 1 component has decayed via consensus formation while the condition 2 component remains. This is exactly the right move: the typology operating at sub-essay granularity, with the same essay carrying both a degraded and a still-functional formal operation. This is also the cleanest version of the piece's own thesis, and it lands.

**The Lestringant paragraph now does the work it should.** The 1580s context is no longer historical garnish: the St. Bartholomew's Day massacre, the wars-of-religion polemics, Catholic/Protestant uses of cannibal imagery, and Montaigne's specific intervention (people who eat the already-dead vs. people who burn enemies alive) are all named and tied to the structural claim that the framework was under genuine pressure at composition. This models the "epistemic archaeology" the essay later advocates, and it does so without breaking essayistic tone.

**The reflexive note on condition 2 is intellectually honest.** Lines 45–47: the author explicitly concedes that her own reading of Baldwin cannot verify, from the inside, that it was reception rather than recognition; and that if template-replacement is sufficiently pervasive, the condition 2 robustness is "largely theoretical." The piece then defends the structural-formal claim as structural-formal and does not pretend to empirical purchase it does not have. This is the right kind of self-criticism - naming what the argument does and does not deliver.

**Skinner is now appropriately qualified.** Lines 12–13 acknowledge that illocutionary reconstruction itself degrades at long range, and that the epistemic-archaeology remedy inherits this limitation. This was a small thing in round 1 but it had a structural consequence (decay on two levels); the qualification is well-placed and one sentence is enough.

**Jauss is integrated without distorting the prior distinction.** The horizon-of-expectations framework is engaged at the right depth: the piece extracts what Jauss does deliver (an explicitly diachronic account) and specifies what Jauss leaves open (the distinction between meaning-production and original-evidential-function), and the integration tightens the hermeneutic argument rather than crowding it. The reference list is updated accordingly.

**The cross-archive engagement with *The Implied Apparatus* (#34) is tight.** Line 87 draws the complementary relationship in two sentences: that piece on what positions an idea for return; this piece on what determines whether a formal strategy remains available. No more than is needed.

**Prose discipline holds.** No process-language leakage. No "round 1," "the panel," "this revision," "after peer review." The qualifications and competing-account passages are all framed as within-text intellectual moves - "A qualification on that 'recoverable' is owed," "A more pointed challenge to the singularity claim must be addressed," "A competing account deserves acknowledgment." These are essayistic moves, not revision-process narration. Good discipline.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **One residual "rates" usage at line 49, plus a softer one at line 89.** The response document states that "All 'rates' language has been replaced," which is *almost* true. Line 49 reads: "the essay's different formal operations decay at different rates within the same text." The framing in the surrounding paragraph is already "decay at different mechanisms" and "principles generalize"; the lone "rates" wording is a leftover that says more than the analysis defends. A trivial fix - "decay through different mechanisms within the same text" - would close it. (Line 89 uses "different rates" implicitly inside "the recovery profiles are as different as the analysis claims," which is acceptable as ordinal recovery-profile language.) Minor.

2. **The environmental/reader-state distinction would benefit from one more sentence locating where the boundary actually sits.** The argument is now made positively, and I accept it as substantively responsive to round 1. But the line between "no individual reader can recover the framework from inside a reading" (environmental) and "some readers may carry the prior configuration" (reader-state) is drawn empirically - it depends on the actual distribution of reader configurations in the present culture. A reader raised in an unusual sub-community could in principle still hold the 1580 framework; whether that is so rare that environmental decay is *effectively* irrecoverable, vs. so possible that the environmental/reader-state distinction blurs at the edges, is not addressed. The piece could acknowledge that the distinction is sharpest at the asymptotes - universal-consensus vs. wide-distribution-of-configurations - and softens in middle cases. One sentence; not blocking.

3. **The condition 2 reflexive note is honest but it makes the structural defense load-bearing in a way the piece could acknowledge more directly.** The reflexive concession (lines 45 and 47) effectively says: I cannot verify from the inside that condition 2 robustness obtains for me; if template-replacement is universal, the claim is structural-only. Taken together with the response-document statement that "the structural claim is defended as structural," the piece's condition 2 claim has now narrowed to: *if the bracketing remedy can be exercised by any reader, then condition 2 is reader-state rather than environmental.* That conditional is correct, and it is a meaningful structural claim. But the way the conditional sits in the text leaves the reader uncertain whether "robustness" is being claimed in the conditional form ("structurally possible") or the categorical form ("more robust than C1/C3 in actual reading practice"). One clarifying sentence - "What follows is the structural claim that the bracketing remedy is available in principle; whether contemporary readers actually exercise it is a question this analysis does not settle" - would make the load-bearing role of the structural defense explicit and forestall a reader reading the section as making the stronger empirical claim. Minor.

4. **One residual passage that looks faintly like response-to-reviewers thinking, framed as within-text.** The paragraph beginning "Two competing simplifications of this taxonomy are worth addressing" (line 69) is the structural backbone of the revision and is doing real argumentative work - but a careful reader can detect the topography of a referee response underneath ("two simplifications" reads as "two reviewer proposals"). The content is essayistic and the framing is internal, so this does not rise to a process-leakage concern; I flag it only because the wording could be slightly tightened. "Two ways one might simplify this taxonomy are worth considering" or simply leading with the structural claim and then naming the alternative collapses would feel less audit-driven. Optional.

5. **The empirical-test paragraph at line 81 is honest but very thin.** "The challenge is that both the decay and its recovery are difficult to assess from outside the reading encounter. But the structure of what would need to be shown is clear, even if the method for showing it is not yet in hand." This is the right honest stopping point and I do not want it removed. But it would be sharper if it named even one candidate operationalization that could in principle be attempted - pre-and-post intervention with primed vs. unprimed readers, comparison of close-reading protocols at varying historical distances, something. The absence of any concrete proposal makes the "differential recovery pattern" test feel more like a slogan than a research program. Not blocking; would strengthen.

No process-language leakage of the kind the reviewer-instructions flag ("round 1," "this revision," "my advisor," "the panel"). No factual claims I can identify as wrong. No charter concerns. No notation issues. The piece is ready to publish.
