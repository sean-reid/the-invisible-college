## What got better

- **The DP1 channel distinction is now sharp where it was previously blurred.** The revised passage at line 27 separates the property-rights channel from the information channel by the design conditions that activate each: candidate (a) requires that defined shares carry sufficient value to motivate protection; candidate (c) requires that monitoring is active and attribution is reliable. The two examples - high resource value without monitoring activates (a) without (c); effective monitoring with low individual stakes activates (c) without (a) - convert what was a vague co-mention into a discriminant a practitioner could in principle run. The identity channel (b) is preserved as structurally distinct and is now clearly differentiated by predicting a different primary lever (group cohesion, identity salience). The table row also carries "(2 potentially joint)" so the reader is not deceived about co-occurrence.

- **The DP4 second-order gap is now visible in both prose and table.** The table cell (line 52) reads "well-specified ($p_d \cdot s > b$); second-order gap" rather than the prior "well-specified" verdict that buried the gap in a footnote. A new paragraph in the prose names three candidate routes by which monitor accountability could be produced - mutual observation among monitors, reputational stakes that make detection of monitoring failure personally costly, hierarchical audit - and cites Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom (2010) as containing the case material that has not been assembled into the principle. This is the right level of development: enough to show the diagnostic generalizes to the second-order problem without converting the piece into a standalone monitoring-of-monitors analysis. It also strengthens the broader thesis: even the best-specified principle in the framework has a residual mechanism gap that the design-principle abstraction strips.

- **The asserted-versus-parameterized distinction now has positive content.** The new paragraph after the table (line 58) defines three rungs: fully specified (mechanism derived from individual parameters and resource characteristics), parameterized (structure named without quantities fixed), and asserted (aggregation question simply not posed). DP4 is used as the benchmark - showing what a fully specified aggregation derivation would require - and the five asserted cases are correctly described as falling short of even the parameterized level. The table's most-populated column verdict no longer floats free of testable content. The paragraph also names that the gap is in the eight-principle summary, not necessarily in Ostrom's case literature, which is the right historiographical qualification.

- **The DP5 cross-reference now delivers what it promised.** The full paragraph at line 35 traces the connection: fixed monitoring concentrates residual violations in harder-to-detect forms; graduated sanctions interrupt that selection effect by retaining soft violators in the detectable range. This is correctly identified as a deterrence-efficiency explanation. The two channels' divergent optimal contexts are then stated cleanly - the selection-driven deterrence-efficiency channel is most beneficial where monitoring has been intensive and the residual pool has concentrated in low-detectability forms; the social learning channel is most beneficial where community cohesion is high enough to make the social cost of early sanctions meaningful. This is the proportioning the cross-reference deserved.

- **The Nepal and West Africa citations land where they need to, and the West African handling has been honestly amended.** Nepal is anchored to Agrawal (2001) and Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom (2010); West Africa to Ribot (2004). More importantly, the revision acknowledges that Ribot's evidence is consistent with normative mechanism failure, capture-based mechanism failure, or both - which is the historiographically correct read of that case material. The added disclaimer ("These patterns are consistent with DP3-N but do not discriminate it from competing explanations: lower-performing groups may also have had thinner ecological knowledge, weaker monitoring, or less graduated sanction structures") is exactly the discipline the methodological commitments demand. The piece now reads as having interrogated its own empirical anchor rather than leaning on it.

- **The ex ante framing is now honestly scoped.** Line 98 redefines "ex ante" as "before full-scale installation and lock-in," and explicitly names that the information test requires locally-generated rules to exist for comparison and the obligation test requires contrasting institutional forms in parallel or sequence. The tests are correctly characterized as pilot-phase diagnostic instruments. This closes the gap between what the framing promised and what the tests deliver without retracting the diagnostic claim.

- **"Compliance burden" is now defined where it does its argumentative work.** The half-clause at line 29 - "understood here as the combined opportunity, cognitive, and legitimacy costs of following the rule" - resolves the undefined construct ahead of the three candidates that implicitly disambiguate it. The fix is minimally invasive and exactly the right size.

## Concerns

# Concerns

The revision substantively addresses all seven of my round-1 concerns. The items below are minor polish-level remarks that the editorial board can take or leave; none rise to the level that should hold the piece.

1. **The property-rights channel's logic could be made one degree more explicit.** Line 27 describes candidate (a) as operating through "a share of the resource whose value falls if over-extracted, thereby internalizing the cost of over-extraction to identifiable individuals." Read carefully, the cost-internalization here can run by two distinct sub-mechanisms: (i) the user's *own* extraction depletes a stock that constitutes their own future-stream, so they internalize their own behavior's cost privately and without monitoring; or (ii) the user shares in the consequences of *others'* over-extraction proportionate to their stake, which generates an interest in collective restraint that depends on some attribution-and-enforcement capacity to act on. (i) is closer to a private-ownership logic and works even with no monitoring; (ii) starts to look like a coordination problem that re-imports something close to (c). The current wording is consistent with either. A single clause specifying which sub-mechanism is the channel's load-bearing one would tighten the (a)/(c) distinction further. This is at the level of polish, not a substantive concern - the cases at the end of the paragraph (high value without monitoring activating (a); monitoring without stakes activating (c)) implicitly favor reading (i), but a reader has to do that reconstruction.

2. **Process-language check: clean.** I looked specifically for revision-process residue, given the prompt's warning. I found none. The first-person constructions present - "The standard against which I audit Ostrom's framework..." (line 21), "I propose a three-level diagnostic" attributed to Hedström-Ylikoski - are the essayistic stance, not session metadata. There is no "this revision addresses," "the prior draft," "after peer review," or comparable phrasing. The piece reads as a freestanding artifact.

3. **Math notation check: clean.** The LaTeX usages are appropriate ($p_d$, $s$, $b$, $p_d \cdot s > b$). The benefit variable $b$ is now correctly introduced before being deployed in the inequality. No prose-spelled Greek letters, no inline operator-words where symbols carry more weight.

4. **Citation completeness check: clean.** All in-text citations (Agrawal 2001; Baland and Platteau 1996; Ribot 2004; Cox, Arnold, and Villamayor Tomás 2010; Hedström and Ylikoski 2010; Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom 2010; Ostrom 1990) appear in the References section. Conversely, all entries in References are cited in the text. No orphan references, no orphan citations.

5. **Minor: "ex ante" is being used in a slightly non-standard sense.** In policy and program-evaluation usage, "ex ante" often means "before deployment in any form." The revision redefines it - explicitly and with signposting - as "before full-scale installation and lock-in." The signposting is the right move and the redefinition is honest. A reader steeped in the standard usage might still pause; if the piece wanted to be maximally cooperative it could use "pre-commitment" or "pilot-phase" as the operative phrase and reserve "ex ante" for the practitioner's formal need. But this is a stylistic choice, not an analytical defect, and the present handling is defensible.
