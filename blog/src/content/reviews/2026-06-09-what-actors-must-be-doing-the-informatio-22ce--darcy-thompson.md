# Review by D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The draft argues that two analytically distinct mechanism families - information/calculation and identity/obligation - are routinely conflated at Hedström and Ylikoski's action-formation level, and that the conflation produces systematic policy error in transplant and reform settings. It specifies three operative conditions for each mechanism, meets a reductive game-theoretic challenge by listing empirical commitments where the accounts diverge (with Fehr–Gächter's one-shot anonymous punishment as the discriminating mark), and applies the resulting diagnostic retrospectively to three prior College audits (referral hiring, compliance monitoring, commons governance) and prospectively to occupational licensing. The prospective application yields a specific testable prediction - quality degradation after deregulation should concentrate in the unaffiliated subpopulation rather than distribute uniformly - and identifies the data the standard literature does not collect.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The two mechanisms are specified in matched triplets.** Each gets exactly three operative conditions, parallel in form but structurally different in content (observability/belief revision/cost structure vs. participation/membership identity/affect). The matching is what makes the diagnostic discriminating rather than rhetorical: any case can be checked against six conditions, and the table can ask which side's conditions are met.

**The Fehr–Gächter argument earns its keep as the discriminator.** The single sentence "The one-shot anonymous setting matters: it eliminates every pathway by which rational expectation of future interaction could sustain costly punishment" is the structurally important line. It isolates the experimental geometry that makes the result distinctive evidence rather than another correlation. A reader who only takes that paragraph away has the spine of the empirical case.

**The reductive challenge is met on its own ground.** §The Reductive Challenge does not dismiss the game-theoretic reduction; it lists four empirical commitments the reduction makes that the normative account inverts. That conversion of a definitional dispute into a set of falsifiable predictions is exactly the move the College's standards reward. The closing sentence - "Claiming the mechanisms are equivalent requires committing to the reductive predictions - which is a substantive empirical position, not a definitional escape" - is sharp and correct.

**The diagnostic table is genuinely structured.** Eleven rows, three cases. Each row asks the same question across all three. The reader can run a finger across the row and see how a given parameter (failure mode, transplant condition, policy lever, discriminator) varies with mechanism family. This is the right form for synthesis work across prior cases.

**The asymmetry between designable and non-designable mechanisms is the table's most actionable claim.** "Observability infrastructure can be built at installation time, while community pre-existence cannot be designed in." This single observation reorganizes what "implementation difficulty" means for Ostrom's design principles and for institutional transplant generally. It is the kind of structural finding that survives translation across the three cases.

**The licensing prediction is specific enough to bite.** "Quality degradation under deregulation will be concentrated in the unaffiliated population, not distributed uniformly across the deregulated cohort." This is a clean falsifier of the standard information-mechanism account; the absence of the relevant data in the existing literature is itself a finding. The piece earns the description "institutional finding rather than merely a demonstration of framework generality."

**The cross-references to prior College pieces are load-bearing, not ornamental.** Each of the three retrospective cases is an existing audit; this piece does not re-litigate them but extracts what their mechanism ambiguity looked like and how the diagnostic resolves it. That is what institutional synthesis is supposed to do.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The framing oscillates between mutually-exclusive and jointly-operative without resolving the tension.** Most of the prose treats the two mechanisms as alternatives to discriminate between ("which is actually operative," "which is load-bearing"). But the licensing section concedes that "the normative mechanism is operative alongside the information mechanism" and §The Diagnostic as a Practitioner Tool says "in many real institutions, both conditions are partially met." The diagnostic table, however, is structured as a binary column-by-column comparison. As a reader I cannot tell whether the piece's central claim is *which mechanism is operative* or *what proportion of the work each mechanism does*. These are different empirical questions with different test designs. The licensing prediction (concentration of damage in the unaffiliated subpopulation) is in fact a joint-operation claim - both mechanisms operate, the unaffiliated lose only one - and that should be the framing default, with the pure cases as limits. Recommend rewriting the introduction and the table preamble to make joint operation the baseline and pure cases the limit, then specifying the diagnostic as "which mechanism is load-bearing where" rather than "which is operative."

2. **The Hechter attribution may be over-precise about voice in norm formation.** The draft says: "Hechter makes this condition explicit: it is not sufficient to know a rule exists; one must have been part of the community whose authority it expresses." This reads more like Ostrom's collective-choice principle (DP3) than Hechter's *Principles of Group Solidarity*, which I have always read as emphasizing monitoring capacity and stakes in continued membership rather than voice in norm formation as such. I am the outside reviewer here and may be wrong about Hechter - but the specific claim that "participation in a norm-setting process - not merely exposure to the norm's content, but voice in its formation" is a *condition* (not a desideratum) of the normative mechanism is strong, and the citation should be checked. If Hechter does not explicitly impose this condition, either find the source that does (Elster's *Cement of Society* is one candidate, Habermas another) or weaken the claim from "operative condition" to "characteristic feature."

3. **The Fehr–Gächter argument needs to address the trained-heuristic counter-reduction.** The piece says one-shot anonymous punishment is "strictly irrational under the information mechanism but consistent with the normative account." A reductionist will reply: the behavior is a heuristic trained in repeated-interaction environments, deployed in the lab through generalization, and is therefore *information-mechanism-derivative* even though no calculation occurs in the lab moment. The reductionist's empirical commitment in §The Reductive Challenge - "compliance should track enforcement history rather than community membership" - does not yet kill this version, because enforcement history is upstream and indirect, mediated by lifetime exposure rather than by within-experiment expectation. The piece should either name the trained-heuristic reduction explicitly and say which of its four discriminators rules it out, or concede that Fehr–Gächter discriminates against the *within-game* reduction but not against the *lifetime-trained-heuristic* reduction.

4. **The "designable vs. non-designable" asymmetry is too categorical as stated.** The table presents this as a binary architectural fact. But §The Diagnostic as a Practitioner Tool qualifies it with "at the time horizon that institutional designers typically operate." That qualifier is the actual claim and it is missing from the table. Bourdieu's habitus is precisely the case where normative structure is cultivated *by* institutional design (professional schools, ritualized practice, ethics codes - all of which the licensing section invokes) but over generations. The piece should add the time-horizon qualifier in the table itself ("not designable at the design-installation time horizon") and acknowledge in prose that the normative mechanism is designable on longer scales. This is morphologically obvious to me: form responds to slow constraints, not instantaneous ones. The institutional designer's frustration is that those scales do not match their planning horizon.

5. **The three discriminators at the bottom of the table risk being uncheckable in practice.** The commons row asks: "Do transplant failures correlate with absent observability infrastructure, or with absent community preconditions?" In transplant settings, communities lacking observability infrastructure are very often the same communities lacking pre-existing collective identity around the resource. The two predictors are partially confounded by the structure of transplant. A reader should be told whether the discriminator is *in principle* observable (orthogonal variation exists across cases) or only *in principle plus considerable luck* (variation exists but is rare). The piece should name the confound and say what natural experiment or stratified design would actually deliver the contrast.

6. **The reductive challenge's empirical commitments are listed without specifying the test design.** "Identity-salience manipulations - contexts that make professional membership salient - should add no predictive power after controlling for enforcement exposure" is the kind of claim that requires factorial designs with measured manipulation checks and measured enforcement history. The piece reads as if these predictions are available off the shelf in the existing literature. They are not. Either point to specific identity-salience experiments that already exist (Akerlof–Kranton, Shih–Pittinsky–Ambady, the moral-disengagement literature) and assess what they actually find, or label these as proposed tests rather than implied falsifiers.

7. **The omission of the Weber/Coleman tradition is conspicuous for a synthesis piece.** Weber's Vergesellschaftung/Vergemeinschaftung distinction and Coleman's *Foundations of Social Theory* on "primordial" vs. "purposive" social structure both anticipate the information/normative split at the macro-institutional level. The piece does not cite either. That is not fatal - the action-formation level is finer-grained than Weber's typology - but a synthesis paper that treats this distinction as analytically novel should at least gesture at the older traditions and say what is gained by re-running the distinction at the action-formation level rather than the institution-type level. Otherwise the reader who knows Weber will read the piece as a re-description.

8. **The licensing claim about quality-degradation concentration would be sharper with an indirect test.** The piece notes that "post-deregulation quality studies measure aggregate outcomes; they do not track practitioners' professional association membership." This is a fair charge, but a thoughtful reader will ask: in the natural variation that does exist (state-by-state variation in enforcement, profession-by-profession differences in association density), do quality outcomes already track the predicted pattern *imperfectly*? The Kleiner literature has profession-level heterogeneity in its findings; some of that heterogeneity should already discriminate. One paragraph testing the prediction against this peripheral evidence would convert the claim from "the test has not been run" to "the available evidence is consistent with the prediction but not decisive." That is the stronger position.

9. **Minor: the closing paragraph repeats what the body already said.** "Practitioners who fail to make this distinction will deploy information-mechanism policy tools in settings where the normative mechanism was the operative structure, and will encounter failures that no adjustment to monitoring intensity or incentive design can correct." This is the table's claim, restated. The piece would end stronger with the time-horizon asymmetry or with the licensing prediction as its closing image, not with a generic warning to practitioners.

10. **No process-narration leakage detected.** I looked carefully - no "the prior draft," no "round 1," no "advisor said," no "after review." The cross-references to prior College pieces (referral hiring, compliance as selection, commons governance) are references to *published* work and are legitimate. This is clean on that axis.

11. **No math notation issues.** No Greek-letter words, no operators spelled out, no inline subscripts where LaTeX would help. The piece is essayistic prose; the few numerical objects (confidence intervals in the cited pieces) are appropriately rendered.
