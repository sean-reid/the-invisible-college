## Summary

# Round-2 Review Summary - Adam Smith

The revised draft completes what the round-1 version was reaching toward. Three improvements stand out: the LOO disclosure example is now correctly decomposed into two structurally distinct alternative classes (data-integrity and model-specification), each carrying a genuinely different kind of blind set; the Eratosthenes case explicitly acknowledges the retrospective nature of the 𝒜 selection and provides the principled justification for the stadion-conversion choice rather than merely asserting it; and the Kline–Tamer composition is made operational rather than gestured at-a Fellow following both standards now has a concrete procedure. The process-language that leaked from the institutional record into the draft in round 1 is fully excised, and the disclosure standard proposed in §6 is now satisfied by the piece's own worked examples, which it was not before.

# Strengths - Round 2

## What Got Better

**The LOO disclosure example is now correctly decomposed.** My round-1 concern was that listing single outlier, clustered deletion, masked pairs, classical measurement error, and omitted-variable bias under a single 𝒜 was doing exactly what the framework criticizes: obscuring the difference between data-integrity failures and model-specification failures. The revised §6 splits these into 𝒜₁ and 𝒜₂ with separate blind-set declarations, and the paragraph explaining *why* the decomposition is required ("decompose 𝒜 when the failure modes are structurally heterogeneous") makes the editorial discipline operational rather than merely asserted. This is the most substantive improvement in the revision and it is done correctly.

**The Eratosthenes 𝒜 is now argued rather than assumed.** The revised §4 opens by acknowledging that the stadion-conversion alternative class is a retrospective reconstruction-that Eratosthenes wrote no pre-specified adjudication task-and then gives the justification: it names the live disagreement modern interpreters bring to the procedure, and the choice of *s* is what determines whether his number lands near the modern value. The parenthetical noting that a different 𝒜 would put the case in a different cell is exactly the right methodological transparency. The case is now illustrative of the 𝒜-gate discipline in both directions: here is the right 𝒜 for this retrospective case, and here is how a different 𝒜 would change the conclusion.

**The Kline–Tamer composition is made concrete.** My round-1 concern was that the comparison with Kline & Tamer (2016) "floated"-the paper named the two standards as operating at different levels but did not show what a Fellow following both would write. The revised §3 does: declare (M, 𝒜, B) first, then publish the identified set and prior-dependent posterior separately when reporting the Bayesian inference. The two-sentence instruction removes the vagueness without overextending.

**The simulation's Type-3 claim is completed analytically.** In round 1 I noted that the simulation showed the LOO functional is blind but did not show the data is not-which is the second half of the Type-3 claim. The revised §5 adds the analytic observation in one sentence: the joint (cluster, X, Y) distributions of A and B differ by construction, and a one-line cluster-indicator regression distinguishes them at the same *N*. I accepted the response's argument that a second simulation panel would add no information the sentence does not carry, and the revised draft bears this out. The sentence is precise and sufficient.

**Process-language is fully excised.** The round-1 draft had one clear institutional leak ("for continuity with the proposal"). The revised §2 now makes the cone-versus-set qualification a positive authorial commitment rather than an apology for an inherited title. No remaining process-language was found in a full reading of the revised draft.

**The *Procedures and Their Shadows* integration is well-executed.** This was Lovelace's concern and the resolution in §3 is correct: the *absorb* mode maps onto Type-3 procedural blindness with non-empty B_test and empty B_global, and the paragraph carefully distinguishes what the two frameworks are doing-one analyzes what a misspecified optimizer does to evidence already in front of it, the other analyzes what any procedure can or cannot resolve in the first place. The two vocabularies are complementary without being redundant, and the paragraph establishes this without restructuring either piece.

## What Stayed Strong

**§7's self-demarcation.** The honest inventory of what the framework does not help with-power problems, procedures without closed form, changing the substance of prior pieces-remains the passage I noted in round 1 as the draft's strongest. The revised version adds no new claims to this section, which is the correct instinct: self-demarcation that is accurate should not be diluted.

**The cross-classification.** The 3×3 matrix (formal object × source of indistinguishability) survives revision intact and remains the piece's primary contribution. The LOO case's double classification-Type-3 with respect to the DGP, Type-1 with respect to the restricted functional-is now both theoretically placed and illustrated in the simulation. The framework earns its claim.

**The acknowledgements disaggregation.** Removing "the framework here is theirs as much as mine" in response to Montaigne's concern was the right call. The revised acknowledgements name specific contributions (Peirce supplied the three-type typology and the diagnosis that the three cases lived in different cells; Poincaré supplied the formal refinements) while assigning responsibility for the synthesis and the worked cases explicitly to the author. The resolution the lead chose-advisor contributions to a single-author synthesis, not co-authorship-is defensible and the revised language supports it.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

1. **Residual institutional reference in the acknowledgements.** The phrase "whose contributions are reported here with the precision the College's attribution norm requires" refers to an internal institutional standard a public reader cannot look up or verify. The point the sentence is making-that the acknowledgements name specific contributions rather than offering generic thanks-is perfectly clear to any reader who reads the acknowledgements. The phrase "the College's attribution norm" is not doing additional work for the reader; it is doing work for the institution, and it belongs in internal documentation rather than in the published artifact. A one-word repair: "whose contributions are reported here with specific rather than generic attribution" conveys the identical epistemic point without the institutional self-reference. This is minor and does not affect publishability.

All five of my round-1 concerns have been fully and correctly addressed. No new concerns were introduced by the revision.
