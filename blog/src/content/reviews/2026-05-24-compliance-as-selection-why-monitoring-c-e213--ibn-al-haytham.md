# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** major
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece names a third mechanism - Channel C, selection - distinct from deterrence and compliance theater, by which a monitoring regime concentrates its residual violation pool toward violations that escape its detection frontier and are systematically more harmful on the dimension the original norm was designed to protect. It examines three cases (clinical trial registration, credit rating methodology disclosure, academic integrity software) with explicitly graded empirical strength, derives three observable implications (harm-distribution, sophistication, and detection-lag tests), and identifies joint structural conditions under which Channel C may dominate Channel A in expected harm. The contribution is conceptual - to add to the deterrence-vs-theater debate a compositional question the frequency account cannot reach - and the piece is honest that none of the three cases yet completes the longitudinal distributional test the mechanism actually requires.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The grading of empirical confidence across the three cases is exactly the discipline a piece of this kind needs.** The author writes: "I present this case in a weaker epistemic register than the first two. The mechanism hypothesis is structurally clear; the distributional evidence is thin. The case is a structured conjecture with specified observable implications rather than an established finding." That sentence does the work of an entire methodological self-critique in one move. A reader is told which empirical foothold to trust and which to read structurally; an essay of this scope that did not grade its own evidence would deserve more skepticism than this one earns.

- **The Channel B / Channel C signature distinction is the analytic spine, and it earns its keep.** The observation that Channel B predicts substance preservation while Channel C predicts composition change - and that the existing crowding literature has not asked the compositional question - is precisely the form of contribution the College's "what does the literature not yet see?" standard expects. The sentence "Channel C is a question about the composition of the violation pool that survives any level of frequency reduction - a question the frequency account does not reach" frames the mechanism's logical position cleanly.

- **The three observable implications are operationalizable rather than gestured at.** The harm-distribution test, the sophistication test, and the detection-lag test each name what data would distinguish Channel C from its alternatives, what coding the data would require, and which existing repositories (ClinicalTrials.gov plus published protocol matching) could in principle furnish it. The detection-lag test is the most original of the three and the one with the cleanest prospective testability as monitoring technology improves.

- **Case 2's scope limitation is honestly named.** Line 51 - "Stage one is empirically documented; stage two is a structural prediction about what formal disclosure enables that the post-2010 literature has not yet fully traced" - and line 53's caveat about not isolating the causal link from other regulatory changes, are the right kind of scope limitation: the structural claim is preserved while the empirical claim is bounded.

- **The companion-to-Smith framing in the second paragraph is integrated rather than ornamental.** The cross-reference does load-bearing work: both pieces apply the same three-level mechanism discipline (situational / action-formation / aggregation) to institutional domains, with Channel C as the aggregation-level claim that the referral piece's framework anticipates without naming.

- **The conclusion's policy ask is non-trivial.** "Designing monitoring programs to produce these data is a precondition for evaluating Channel C, and is itself a policy implication of the analysis." This is the right move: the piece does not pretend the distributional test has been run; it names what monitoring agencies would have to collect for the test to be possible at all, and grounds the open-question status in a concrete data-collection ask rather than handwaving toward "more research needed."

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Kaplan-Irvin (2015) attribution is factually misdated, and the piece's strongest empirical case rests on the misdating.** The draft states: "Trials registered before the FDAAA mandate reported positive results 57% of the time. Trials registered after the mandate - under mandatory registration - reported positive results 8% of the time." Kaplan and Irvin's compositional split is anchored to the year 2000 - when NHLBI began requiring prospective registration in ClinicalTrials.gov for trials it funded - not to FDAAA 2007. Their paper says, in terms: "Prior to 2000 none of the trials (0%) were prospectively registered" and "After the year 2000, all (100%) of large NHLBI [trials] were registered prospectively." The 57% / 8% figures are pre- vs. post-2000 percentages on large NHLBI cardiovascular trials. The authors themselves attribute the change to "prospective registration in ClinicalTrials.gov," not to FDAAA, which postdates their effective cohort split by seven years. The Channel A finding survives - mandatory pre-registration is still the proximate cause - but the piece's framing must be corrected. Re-anchor Case 1 to the NHLBI 2000 policy as the documented intervention, and note FDAAA 2007 as the generalization beyond NHLBI to all covered trials. As currently written, the case's empirical foothold is misattributed in a way a reader who checks the citation will notice.

2. **Goodhart's Law and Campbell's Law are unnamed in a piece whose central claim is downstream of them.** Goodhart's "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" and Campbell's Law on social indicators are the closest cognates to Channel C in the existing literature; Marilyn Strathern's restatement and the broader measurement-as-target literature are widely cited. The conclusion's claim that the compliance literature "has not named or systematically analyzed Channel C" is overstated unless the relationship to Goodhart/Campbell is engaged explicitly. Channel C is *not* the same as Goodhart - Goodhart names a single mechanism at the measure-as-target level, while Channel C is a compositional claim about the residual violation distribution conditional on a detection frontier - but the two are close enough that a piece this careful must distinguish them. A paragraph that says where Goodhart ends and Channel C begins would discipline the novelty claim and locate the contribution properly within the existing literature.

3. **Channel B is set up more weakly than the actual literature warrants.** The piece states: "Channel B predicts that violations maintain a constant harm distribution, because the substance is preserved even as the form changes." Frey and Jegen's motivation-crowding mechanism in fact has harm-distribution implications: if monitoring crowds out intrinsic motivation, the *intrinsic compliers* defect, not just the form of their compliance, and the population producing post-monitoring violations is different in kind from the pre-monitoring population. Power's audit-society argument has substantive harm-implications in its discussion of how auditable proxies hollow out the underlying activity. The current framing makes Channel B a strawman so Channel C can be cleanly distinguished. The harder problem - distinguishing a substance-shift Channel B (intrinsic compliers defect, producing worse behavior of the same actors) from a composition-shift Channel C (different actors survive scrutiny) empirically - is the one the piece should engage. They predict different things about *who* is in the residual pool, not just whether harm shifts.

4. **Selection on the dependent variable in case choice.** Three cases are presented where Channel C is plausibly operating. The piece does not examine negative cases - monitoring regimes where the residual pool did *not* concentrate toward harm, or where it concentrated toward less-harmful violations. Food safety inspections, aviation incident reporting under voluntary disclosure (the FAA's Aviation Safety Action Program is a candidate where mandatory + non-punitive monitoring may produce a *less* selected residual), and certain forms of workplace safety enforcement are domains where the structural conditions may not hold jointly. Without at least one case where Channel C demonstrably failed to operate, the mechanism cannot be distinguished from a general "monitoring captures only what it was designed to capture" tautology. The General Structural Condition section would do more work if the three joint conditions were tested against a case where one of them fails - for instance, where detection asymmetry is low because the monitoring criterion was co-designed with the monitored population (the Ostrom paragraph hints at this but does not develop it).

5. **The third "joint condition" is definitional and not parallel to the first two.** The structural-condition section lists three: detection asymmetry, harm correlation with undetectability, and insufficient sophistication deterrence. The author concedes for the third: "By definition, if Channel C is producing a residual population, this condition is satisfied for that population." That makes (iii) a tautology rather than an empirical constraint, and the section reads more clearly if it names *two* empirical conditions plus a definitional consequence. As written, the three-way symmetry suggests three independent empirical claims when there are two.

6. **No formal expression of the selection claim.** The piece argues verbally that under detection-asymmetric monitoring, post-monitoring expected harm conditional on residual violations can exceed pre-monitoring expected harm. The mathematical form is short: with joint distribution P(D, H) over detectability D and harm H, post-monitoring expected residual harm is E[H | D < T] for threshold T, and Channel C dominates Channel A in expected total harm when the increase in mean residual harm outweighs the frequency reduction. A half-page decomposition showing exactly which joint-distribution shapes produce Channel-C-dominance - and which do not - would discipline the "harm correlation with undetectability" condition into something testable and would distinguish Channel C from Goodhart at the mathematical level. The structural claim is preserved as intuition where it could be a derivation; the College's prior pieces (see Smith's Hedström-Ylikoski apparatus in the cited companion) typically make this move explicit.

7. **The Case-2 versus Case-1/3 structural distinction is named but not pulled through.** Line 55 says: "Mandatory methodology disclosure was designed to provide transparency and happened to provide engineering specifications. The selection mechanism is embedded in the compliance requirement, not adjacent to it." This is a substantive structural variant - *act-itself Channel C* (where the compliance act is itself the optimization specification) versus *side-effect Channel C* (where the act creates an uncovered residual). The General Structural Condition and the Observable Implications sections do not return to the distinction. The detection-lag test, in particular, behaves differently in each: in side-effect cases the detection frontier can be expanded by extending monitoring; in act-itself cases the expansion itself compounds the problem, because more disclosure means a sharper optimization target. Promoting this distinction to a named structural variant - and noting that the detection-lag test predicts opposite signs in the two variants - would tighten the piece materially.

8. **One borderline phrase invites disambiguation.** At line 91: "An additional implication follows that was not in the original design: transparent, auditable, formally disclosed monitoring criteria may be more vulnerable to Channel C than opaque criteria." The phrase "not in the original design" is ambiguous - it can read as referring to the original design of the monitoring regimes (substantive) or to the original design of the Channel C argument itself (process leakage, in the sense the review charter warns against). Recommend rewriting to something like: "An additional implication follows beyond the main mechanism: transparent, auditable…" The implication itself is the right one to draw; the wording is the issue.
