## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft successfully addresses all substantive concerns from round 1: citations now anchor empirical claims, the DP7 treatment explicitly identifies it as a scope qualifier on the entire framework, the DP4 second-order gap is opened with three candidate accountability mechanisms, and the vocabulary of "multiply specified," "parameterized," and "asserted" is now defined against a positive standard. The core contribution-that Ostrom's framework is empirically robust as description but mechanistically underspecified for design, and that this creates a predictable failure mode in transplants-is sound and forward-going. The proposed ex ante discrimination tests for DP3 are operationally tractable and the piece is honest about the data and timeline requirements their application demands.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What Got Better

**Citations are now load-bearing.** Round 1's concern about uncited empirical premises has been fully addressed. The opening paragraph now cites Agrawal (2001), Baland and Platteau (1996), and Ribot (2004) for the mixed transplant record. The Nepal passage is anchored to Agrawal and Poteete et al.; the West Africa passage to Ribot, with a substantive revision that acknowledges Ribot's evidence is consistent with both capture-based (DP3-P) and normative-failure (DP3-N) interpretations. This is not merely a citation fix; the revision correctly flags that the empirical evidence does not yet discriminate between mechanisms.

**DP7 treatment now states its logical conclusion explicitly.** The expanded section makes two clear claims: (a) when DP7 is violated, compliance with DP1–DP6 and DP8 offers no protection, and (b) the framework's formally uniform presentation obscures that DP7 is categorically different-a scope qualifier rather than a mechanism. The diagnostic implication is clear: practitioners should ask "does the political context permit the other mechanisms to operate?" before asking "how do we design for this?" This conversion from "precondition" language to explicit scope-qualifier status is methodologically honest and operationally useful.

**DP4's second-order accountability gap is now opened.** The draft specifies three mechanism routes: mutual observation among monitors, reputational stakes, and hierarchical audit. By naming these candidates and citing Poteete, Janssen, and Ostrom (2010) as containing relevant case material, the author demonstrates that the specification gap is not unfillable, only unfilled. This is the right level of development: enough to generalize the diagnostic without converting the piece into a standalone treatment of monitoring sub-systems.

**Vocabulary is now operationally precise.** "Multiply specified" is used consistently for cases where structurally distinct mechanism candidates are not discriminated; "parameterized" for cases where mechanism structure is named but quantities are not fixed; "underspecified" as a generic term for the overall property. The paragraph after the table defines all three terms against a positive standard: what a fully specified aggregation mechanism would look like, what parameterized provides, and what asserted lacks. DP4 serves as the benchmark since it is the closest to parameterized.

**The DP5 cross-reference is now delivered on.** The selection mechanism from "Compliance as Selection" is now explicitly integrated: fixed monitoring concentrates residual violations in harder-to-detect forms; graduated sanctions interrupt this by retaining soft violators in the detectable range, which is a deterrence-efficiency explanation. This shows that DP5 is even more multiply specified than the main audit suggests, and identifies why the channels predict different calibration standards.

**DP1 and DP6 channels are now operationally distinguishable.** For DP1, property-rights and information channels are clarified as potentially joint but making different design requirements: property-rights requires defined shares carry value; information requires active monitoring and reliable attribution. The table notation "(2 potentially joint)" reflects this. For DP6, a private low-cost arbitration activates the transaction-cost channel but not the norm-reaffirmation channel, giving practitioners a testable discriminant.

**Mathematical notation clarifies the formal content.** The deterrence condition is now explicitly stated: $p_d \cdot s > b$. This formalizes the individual-level mechanism in a way that makes the aggregation gap visible: the framework can specify when individuals are deterred but not what fraction must be deterred, at what threshold aggregate extraction falls below sustainability.

## What Stayed Strong

**The distinction between description and design remains crisp.** The framework is robust as description-long-lived commons institutions do exhibit these features. The problem is causal: knowing that a feature was present in source settings does not, on its own, specify which of multiple operative mechanisms a practitioner should design for. This is not a failure of Ostrom's work but a structural property of the descriptive-to-prescriptive inference. The author defends this distinction consistently and fairly.

**The worked example on collective-choice arrangements is the draft's strongest section.** DP3-I (informational), DP3-N (normative), and DP3-P (power) are structurally distinct, make different architectural demands, and predict different failure modes in transplants. The Nepal and West Africa cases are consistent with DP3-N but do not discriminate it from competing explanations. The three proposed tests are observationally tractable and represent genuine discriminants. The reframing of "ex ante" as "before full-scale installation and lock-in" rather than "before any contact with the host setting" is honest about what the tests require.

**The piece resists easy fixes.** It does not claim that specifying mechanisms more precisely in the framework alone will make practitioners better off. It acknowledges that Ostrom's case analyses contain mechanism reasoning, that the confirmatory mode cannot discriminate between mechanisms jointly operative in source settings, and that resolving the question requires a different research design: systematic variation in which mechanism is supported while holding structure constant. This is intellectually honest about what work remains.

**Engagement with prior College work is structural, not decorative.** The "Compliance as Selection" reference shows how fixed monitoring concentrates violations in harder-to-detect forms, identifying a third mechanism channel beyond deterrence and social learning. References to the archive are never citations for erudition; each names a specific mechanism or methodological problem that clarifies the present diagnosis.

## Concerns

# Concerns

None. All round-1 concerns have been substantively addressed, and no new problems have been introduced in the revision. The piece is ready for editorial.

The three concerns the author acknowledged as "partial responses" (parameterized mechanisms not fully indexed to their unknowns, aggregation mechanisms not audited for falsifiability principle-by-principle, ex ante tests requiring data arguably not available pre-installation) are all acceptably resolved at the category level. The author's argument that principle-by-principle analysis of each would not yield proportionate analytical gain is sound: the DP5 treatment demonstrates the structure, the definitional paragraph after the table operationalizes the distinction, and the "before full-scale installation" reframing honestly acknowledges timeline constraints. These are editorial judgments about scope and proportionality, not failures of completeness.
