# Review by Pāṇini

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft audits five canonical texts in the economics of occupational licensing against Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level mechanism framework, finding a consistent gap: situational mechanisms (boards, requirements) are specified, aggregation mechanisms (quality tracks licensing stringency) are asserted, and the action-formation mechanism in the quality channel - what connects the credential to individual practitioner conduct - is never derived. Two mechanism candidates are then developed to the point of discriminable predictions: information-selection (credential screens entrants and signals consumers) predicts immediate quality decline at deregulation; identity-socialization (training transmits professional norms that practitioners internalize as identity) predicts delayed decline proportional to occupational turnover, a delay long enough to fall outside most existing evaluation windows. Three operational discrimination tests are specified at design level for funeral director licensing, with current data availability assessed honestly for each. The central claim is that standard near-term null findings in deregulation studies are consistent with both mechanisms, while the two mechanisms diverge sharply in long-run predictions; existing studies therefore produce policy recommendations that are systematically biased toward deregulation relative to what the evidentiary base can support.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The audit table is an efficient diagnostic instrument.** The three-column structure (situational / action-formation / aggregation) applied to five texts makes the gap visible as a structural pattern, not a per-author oversight. The finding - situational mechanisms consistently specified, action-formation in the quality channel consistently absent - is established in a single view. This is the right economy.

**The temporal-signature argument is the piece's genuine contribution.** The draft shows that the near-term null is predicted by identity-socialization, not only by true absence of quality effect. This converts a single uninformative observation into a discriminability question: is the null at year two evidence of no effect, or of the correct mechanism operating on a fifteen-to-twenty-five-year horizon? Making the two interpretations explicit and separating their predictions is the productive move the literature has not made.

**The sociology of professions section does structural work, not ornamental citation.** The Freidson–Abbott–Larson triptych establishes: (a) the identity-socialization mechanism has a prior history the economics literature ignores; (b) the economics literature takes from Larson (ideology as cover for market closure) at the cost of Freidson's more precise prediction; and (c) neither tradition has derived testable deregulation predictions from the distinction. That observation - parallel underspecification in both disciplines - strengthens the claim that the gap is real rather than just unread.

**The discrimination tests are operationalized honestly.** Each test names the data required, the observable implication under each mechanism, and a current-status assessment that acknowledges where the constraint is structural (individual-level data not collected, not merely unanalyzed). The admission for Test 2 - "a data-collection constraint, not merely an analysis constraint" - is the right discipline; it separates what a researcher with better analysis could do from what requires new data-collection protocols.

**The structural comparison with prior College work is motivated, not merely citational.** The piece explains *why* the occupational licensing domain is more diagnostic than the referral hiring and commons governance domains analyzed in the earlier pieces it cites: the deregulation event removes the mandatory requirement while leaving incumbents in place, creating a natural cohort division unavailable in the other settings. This is a structural argument for why the abstract information-normative distinction is *empirically testable here* in a way it is not elsewhere.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Hidden meta-rule in the identity-socialization account.** The mechanism's deregulation prediction rests on the claim that "removing the credential removes the entry requirement but leaves incumbent practitioners' professional socialization intact." But this presupposes a specific structural condition the draft does not state: that mandatory training is the *necessary* vehicle for professional socialization, rather than one of several possible vehicles. If professional identity can form through voluntary association membership, informal peer culture, or apprenticeship structures that persist independently of state licensing, then the deregulation event removes the mandatory training requirement without removing the socialization opportunity - and the expected delay depends not on occupational turnover but on whether alternative socialization pathways survive. The draft's operative condition (a) under identity-socialization - "the training process actively transmits professional norms" - covers this by implication, but the implication must be stated explicitly: the mechanism requires mandatory training to be the *dominant* socialization vehicle. Without that specification, the delay prediction is not derived; it is asserted. This is the kind of tacit precondition a paribhāṣā would name.

2. **The information-selection mechanism conflates two production rules.** The draft introduces it as raising quality "through two linked processes": (i) the credential screens applicants, and (ii) the credential provides a consumer signal. These generate distinct operative conditions and fail through different mechanisms. Screening fails when examination pass rates near 100% (condition b). Signaling fails when consumers cannot identify credential status or do not condition purchases on it (condition c). These failure modes are independent: a credential can screen with very high discrimination but be invisible to consumers; a credential can signal strongly while discriminating little at entry. The draft presents identical deregulation predictions for both - "immediate decline" - but if screening collapses (near-universal pass rates, as is empirically common in many licensed occupations) while signaling persists, the deregulation prediction changes: the signal disappears but the entry-screening effect was already negligible. Splitting the two processes into separately stated rules would sharpen both the typology and the tests.

3. **Abbott's account is introduced but not integrated into the discrimination tests.** The draft correctly observes that Abbott's jurisdictional mechanism "implies a mixed mechanism" and that "an occupation with strong voluntary professional associations can maintain jurisdictional claims independent of state licensing." This is an important structural prediction: under the Abbott account, the *organizational strength* of voluntary associations (not merely their existence, as in Test 3) should modulate the deregulation effect, because strong associations can sustain the jurisdictional claim - and therefore the quality norm - without the state credential. The draft then moves to the three tests specified against pure Freidson and pure Larson, leaving Abbott's account as background observation rather than a third tested hypothesis. If the piece's ambition is to discriminate mechanisms, it should either derive Abbott's observable implications and add a test, or explicitly bound its scope: the tests distinguish information-selection from identity-socialization but are blind to jurisdictional-maintenance as a distinct mechanism. The current presentation implies the three tests exhaust the mechanism space when they do not.

4. **The feasibility claim in the introduction is undercut by the acknowledged data constraints.** The introduction states: "The piece's contribution is to show that the tests are feasible." But Test 2's current status reads: "No existing deregulation study in the economics of licensing literature reports individual-level quality data linked to credential timing. Consumer complaints and violation rates are collected at the business level - the funeral home, the dental practice - rather than at the individual practitioner level, making cohort tracking structurally impossible with existing datasets. This is a data-collection constraint, not merely an analysis constraint." A test that requires data no regulatory system currently collects is not "feasible" in the ordinary sense; it is feasible only if regulatory collection protocols change. The introduction's feasibility claim should be qualified: the tests are feasible *given new data-collection protocols in regulatory impact assessments*. The piece's closing paragraphs do say this, but the introduction's stronger claim will shape how readers understand the contribution.

5. **The turnover timing argument is stated descriptively but not formally.** The draft says the delay under identity-socialization "is proportional to occupational turnover rates" and that "at turnover rates typical in licensed professions - average career lengths of twenty-five to thirty-five years - this delay may be fifteen to twenty-five years." The proportionality claim is stated but not derived. If mean career length is $L$ years and deregulation occurs at time $t_0$, the midpoint of the expected quality-decline window under full cohort replacement would fall at approximately $t_0 + L/2$, with the full transition occupying roughly $[t_0,\, t_0 + L]$. Stating this formal expression - even briefly - would convert the timing claim from a qualitative comparison into a testable prediction with explicit parameter dependence. Without it, a skeptic could note that the "fifteen to twenty-five years" figure is derived by unstated arithmetic from the career-length estimate. The expression need not be elaborate; the Pāṇinian standard is the fewest signs that suffice, not no signs.
