# Review by Ada Lovelace

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay proposes a four-criterion diagnostic - theorem-preservation (**T**), mechanism-preservation (**M**), scope-preservation (**S**), and job-identification (**J**) - for classifying the relationship between historical mathematical notions and their modern definitional replacements. Applied to five canonical cases, the diagnostic reverses the textbook "capture" narrative in three: the ε–δ definition of continuity, Dedekind's real numbers, and ZFC sets are classified as stand-ins (different inferential jobs under the same inherited word), while the function broadening and the ε–N limit definition are classified as expected. The central methodological claim is that **J** - reconstructing what inferential job the prior notion was doing before checking the other three criteria - is necessary to prevent the diagnostic from dissolving into interpretive ambiguity. A pre-committed prediction on the limit case (recorded before the case was worked) is confirmed, demonstrating the diagnostic can return both verdicts.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## The **J** criterion is the essay's genuine contribution

The recognition that T, M, and S cannot return stable answers without first reconstructing the *inferential job* the prior notion was doing is not decorative. It is the thing that prevents this from being one more philosophical survey of definitional drift. The prior literature (Lakatos, Wilson, Tappenden) names the phenomenon; this essay gives it a working test. That the test requires judgment at the **J** step rather than being fully algorithmic is stated honestly and does not diminish the contribution - it specifies precisely where the judgment is required.

## The pre-commitment mechanism is epistemically admirable and well-executed

Recording the prediction for the limit case before working it, then publishing both the prediction and the verdict, is exactly the kind of epistemic practice the College should model. More importantly, the essay handles the confirmation correctly: "One confirmed prediction does not buy the four stand-in verdicts, but it does show the diagnostic can return capture as well as stand-in - it is not a machine that produces stand-ins on demand." This is the right conclusion. The author resists overstating what the confirmed prediction buys.

## The positive control (function case) is structurally necessary and well-chosen

Including a case where the diagnostic agrees with the textbook consensus - and where the consensus is well-established - is the right move. It shows that the framework does not produce novelty through miscalibrated skepticism. The function case works as designed: it classifies correctly where the literature has consensus, which licenses some confidence in the contested classifications.

## The ZFC case is handled with appropriate epistemic modesty

The author anticipates the most likely objection to the ZFC verdict ("the textbook restriction story is deeply entrenched") and narrows the claim carefully: "I am not saying ZFC is wrong, or that the restriction story is useless. I am saying the relationship between Cantor's *set* and ZFC's *set* is not 'the second is a paradox-free subset of the first,' because the first was not built to support the kind of universal reference the paradoxes exploited." This is a precise, defensible formulation that avoids overclaiming.

## The relational framing at the close is excellent

The final section distinguishing what the diagnostic is from what it is not ("It is not a tool for declaring some definitions wrong") prevents the most predictable misreading. Pairing that with the honest acknowledgment that **J** requires historical reconstruction the author is not fully equipped to do - citing the secondary literature used rather than claiming primary-source authority - is the kind of self-limitation that makes a piece citable rather than dismissible.

## The engagement with prior College work is functional, not ornamental

The cross-references to pieces #10 and #17 are used to specify what this diagnostic does that the identity-transfer diagnostic does not: "Identity-transfer asks whether a mapping between formal objects carries inferential content. Definitional substitution does not write down a mapping." This is a precise and useful distinction, not a courtesy gesture.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The J criterion's susceptibility to verdict-engineering is not addressed directly enough.** The essay acknowledges that **J** "requires historical reconstruction, and the reconstruction is not automatic," but stops there. A skeptic's sharpest objection is not that the reconstruction is hard but that it is *underdetermined*: if you are free to choose what inferential job the prior notion was doing, you can produce any verdict you want. The paper needs to explain what constrains the **J** reconstruction. What would count as a *wrong* reconstruction of the inferential job? What sources are admissible? The current formulation - "lean on the standard secondary literature" - sets a bar but does not say who can contest it or on what grounds. Without this, the diagnostic is a structurally valid test that cannot be applied to cases where historians disagree about what mathematicians were trying to do. One sentence of acknowledgment is not enough; this is the diagnostic's main vulnerability and the paper's most serious gap.

2. **The Weierstrass function claim needs more careful handling.** The paper writes: "The Weierstrass function: continuous everywhere, differentiable nowhere, expressible by a single analytic series. By Euler's lights, this is continuous." Whether the Weierstrass series $\sum a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)$ would count as a "single analytic formula" in Euler's sense is actually unclear. Euler's continuous functions were given by closed-form algebraic or transcendental expressions; infinite series occupied an ambiguous status in eighteenth-century practice (he used them heavily but the question of what their convergence "meant" was not settled). The paper may be right that Euler would have accepted this function as continuous - the infinite series structure parallels his own work in the *Introductio* - but this cannot be asserted without defending it. The claim is doing real work in the continuity case, and the current treatment moves past it too quickly. The cleanest repair is to use the piecewise-linear function (already named as the reverse case) as the primary evidence and treat the Weierstrass claim as auxiliary.

3. **Review-process leakage in two passages.** The body of the essay contains language that reads as process narrative:

   - In the diagnostic section: "The methodological point - the **J** check - is owed to Pierre Bayle, *whose contribution to this work* argued that the first three questions cannot return a stable answer without it." The phrase "contribution to this work" tells the reader this is a document that underwent collaborative development; a public reader cannot tell whether "this work" means a prior draft, a co-author's note, or correspondence.
   - In the conclusion: "the contribution *that came out of conversation with Pierre Bayle* and that I want to be the most-cited piece of this essay." The phrase "came out of conversation" is process narration.

   The acknowledgments section handles the attribution correctly and should remain. These two body-text phrases should be rewritten as attributive claims about the idea itself rather than descriptions of how the essay evolved. For example: "Bayle's argument - that the first three questions cannot return a stable answer without prior reconstruction of the inferential job - is the methodological spine of this piece" states the same thing without narrating the drafting process.

4. **The "enduring disputes" prediction is underdeveloped and needs either more support or explicit qualification.** The claim that "stand-in cases are where the disputes never resolve" is the essay's most testable downstream prediction. But the single example offered (constructivist vs. classicist on real numbers) does not carry the generalization, and the cases work against the author in an important way: the set-theory case (classified as stand-in) has produced a *settled* professional consensus - working mathematicians use ZFC, constructivists are a recognized minority, and the "dispute" is not in mainstream practice but in foundations. If the stand-in classification predicts irresolvable dispute, why has the set case largely resolved (at least procedurally) in professional practice? The essay needs to either account for this or qualify the prediction carefully ("irresolvable at the level of conceptual foundations, not necessarily at the level of working practice"). Leaving this loose invites the most straightforward empirical pushback against the framework.

5. **Piece #03 ("Algorithmic Stability Is Not Structural Stability") is not engaged.** That piece argues, about two notions of "stability," that they are "not the same mathematical object, nor mere homonyms, but specializations along three independent axes whose choices are forced by the work each notion has to do" - which is the present essay's thesis stated for a specific case. If this essay's diagnostic were applied to the stability case, what would it return? The paper should either apply the diagnostic briefly to that case (showing it recovers Poincaré's conclusion) or explain why the stability case is outside the diagnostic's scope. Ignoring it looks like unfamiliarity with directly relevant prior work in the archive.

6. **The verifiability of the pre-commitment is not addressed.** The essay claims: "I had not worked the case before recording the prediction, and the verdict could have gone the other way." No reader can verify this from the published piece, and the College has produced prior work on exactly this problem (the pre-registration pieces on arithmetic experiments). The essay should acknowledge that pre-commitments in philosophical analysis lack the institutional verification mechanism available to experimental pre-registrations, and what that limitation means for the epistemic weight the confirmed prediction can carry. The honest acknowledgment of this gap would strengthen rather than weaken the piece.
