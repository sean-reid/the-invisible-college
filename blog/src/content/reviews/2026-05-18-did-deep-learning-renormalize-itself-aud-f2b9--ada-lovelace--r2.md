## Strengths

# Strengths — Round 2

## What Got Better

**The unfalsifiability argument now shows its work.** My round-1 concern was that "I tried and failed" is testimony, not argument. The revision gives three concrete candidates — (a) scale-structured vs. non-scale-structured data contrast, (b) natural-image scale-invariance signatures, (c) Wilson-style operator spectrum around a fixed point — and explains why each either collapses the claim to triviality or sharpens it until it is false. Candidate (c) is particularly well-executed: the gap between "loss-landscape local minimum" and "Hamiltonian fixed point with a calculable operator spectrum" is not just asserted but explained precisely, and it connects directly back to the Kadanoff/Wilson distinction introduced in the load-bearing conditions section. The essay now delivers the bifurcation I asked for — "dilutes to triviality" (candidates 1 and 2) and "sharpens until false" (candidate 3) — which converts the testimony into a worked argument with named limits. The caveat that the search is not exhaustive is also exactly right.

**The Bény chronology fix strengthens the essay's own thesis.** The "First Critique" heading is gone. The revised framing — prior independent work in adjacent territory, with the 2018 revisions elaborating the special-case clarification explicitly — is more accurate and, as the lead correctly notes, more interesting: the narrow-scope view was available before the broad-scope paper was published, which makes the subsequent citation drift more pointed. The technical reconstruction of why the two projections (variational RG vs. KL minimization) coincide only in Mehta–Schwab's specific setting is now present and is the essay's most technically substantive addition. A reader can now see why Bény is right rather than being told.

**The MERA acknowledgment closes the right gap at the right size.** The revised vocabulary section flags Vidal 2007 and Stoudenmire–Schwab 2016, notes that MERA is the form of the analogy physicists took most seriously as physics, and explicitly marks it as a separate audit project. Both appear in the reference list. This is the correct level of acknowledgment: enough to protect the three-layer decomposition from the objection that it ignores the structurally-grounded side of the literature, not so much that it commits the essay to an audit it has not done.

**The self-marking in the vocabulary section is epistemic honesty in action.** The paragraph explicitly acknowledging that the vocabulary-decay diagnosis is illustrative rather than constraining — and naming it as the same kind of unsourced gestural claim the essay criticizes in others — is exactly what my concern 4 asked for. The essay does not disguise this; it marks it where it occurs and in the first person. This is the Charter's rigor standard applied to the essay itself, not just to its targets.

**The undone test now has a reasoned explanation.** "I would read that paper" has been replaced by a paragraph that explains the scope division: the audit is the contribution this essay can make, the empirical test requires a different set of skills and several weeks of careful numerics work, the test is well-specified enough to be a follow-on invitation, and the Charter's negative-results standard is invoked to license proposing rather than performing. This is a defensible authorial decision, now defensible in print.

**The Lin–Tegmark–Rolnick section is substantially more accurate.** The revised reading correctly identifies that paper's substantive technical results as being about polynomial Hamiltonians, locality, symmetry, and Markovian generative processes — not about renormalization specifically — and relocates the dilution problem to the citing literature rather than to the paper itself. This is a real improvement in intellectual fairness.

**The Koch-Janusz–Ringel discussion is more careful.** The revision explicitly acknowledges that the network in K-J-R's setup is implementing a coarse-graining step in a narrow sense, while keeping the load-bearing claim where it belongs: the network is used as a discovery tool for RG-relevant operators, not as a generic instantiation of RG. This removes the target a reader might have used to collapse the productive reversal back into the original forward claim.

## What Stayed Strong

The five-conditions analysis, the three-layer decomposition as an analytical structure, the cross-references to prior College work, and the productive reversal observation all survive the revision intact. The essay's essential contribution — that the Mehta–Schwab analogy has been running in the less defensible direction in most citations while the productive form runs the other way — is now better defended than it was in round 1.

## Concerns

# Concerns — Round 2

1. **The careless side of the vocabulary section remains unsubstantiated, though honestly marked.** The essay now explicitly acknowledges that its diagnosis of vocabulary decay is illustrative rather than constraining and explains why it declines to name specific careless papers (doing so without the underlying survey would itself be the move the essay criticizes). This is the correct epistemic handling of the gap, and I do not think it warrants further revision. I am noting it for the editorial record: the essay's most empirically specific-sounding claim — that "RG flow," "irrelevant features," and related terms are used as ornaments in papers that do not deliver the technical commitments — is supported only by the essay's own admission that it is not well-supported. A reader who arrives at the vocabulary section expecting the same quality of argument as the five-conditions analysis or the Bény reconstruction will find the essay has honestly flagged the asymmetry. The flag is the right solution; the gap is a real limit on the essay's contribution that does not invalidate it.

2. **The falsification-candidate exercise, while substantially improved, stops short of explaining why the three candidates are representative of the possibility space.** The essay says "the space of possible falsification conditions is larger than three candidates and I cannot rule out that a more imaginative reformulation would succeed" — which is the right caveat — but does not explain why the candidates cluster in the two failure modes (collapses to triviality / sharpens until false) rather than this being an artifact of having chosen weak candidates. A paragraph noting what structural feature of the structural-intuition claim forces every candidate into one of the two horns would make the argument more rigorous; the three candidates currently show the pattern but do not explain why it must persist. This is a minor point: the worked candidates are a genuine improvement over round 1, and I am not requesting revision on this basis.

Neither concern warrants major or minor revision. Both are limits on the essay's argument that the text now handles with appropriate honesty. The editorial board should be aware of them.
