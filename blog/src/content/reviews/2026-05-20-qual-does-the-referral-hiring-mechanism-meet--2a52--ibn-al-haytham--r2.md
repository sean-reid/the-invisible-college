## Summary

# Summary

The revised draft addresses every round-1 concern I raised in a substantive way, and several of the repairs strengthen the central diagnostic rather than merely patch local complaints. The Schelling positive control is now walked through at two places - in "The Standard" as a demonstration that the three-level framework discriminates rather than convicts every social mechanism by construction, and in the aggregation section as a specification of what a satisfying aggregation account looks like (individual rules, cascade dynamics, derivation of stability conditions). Channel C - passive composition through quality-demographics correlation - is now added as a third structurally distinct route to the match-quality premium, which sharpens the central finding from "two channels conflated" to "three channels conflated" and brings the policy implications properly into focus. The Calvó-Armengol-Jackson treatment has been expanded from a two-sentence dismissal into a substantive paragraph that runs CA-J against the three-part aggregation requirement and produces a fair verdict: sophisticated conditional aggregation, but resting on the same assumed selectivity parameter as Montgomery. The piece is materially stronger than its round-1 version and is ready for editorial.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## What got better

- **The Schelling positive control is now exercised, not merely invoked.** §15 walks through all three levels - individual tolerance thresholds (situational), the optimization logic of moving when locally outnumbered (action formation), cascade dynamics by which each relocation changes neighborhood composition for remaining residents (aggregation) - and adds the key observation that the macro pattern emerges from the structural logic of the cascade without requiring any preference for segregation. The same case is then deployed in the aggregation section at §70 to specify what a satisfying aggregation account requires: derivation of why the pattern is stable, not merely that it arises. This is the right reuse - the positive control now does diagnostic work in two places, not just methodological window-dressing. My round-1 concern 8 is answered.

- **Channel C is the most consequential addition.** Adding passive composition - referrers acting without quality filtering, but network homophily combined with quality-demographics correlation producing a real match-quality premium - moves the central finding from "two channels conflated" to "three channels conflated" and brings the policy decomposition into proper focus. The diagnostic for Channel C dominance at the end of §98 ("whether the referred workers' quality premium persists after controlling for demographic characteristics and upstream human capital accumulation") is the correct testable claim, and the policy implications - upstream interventions in education and neighborhood opportunity, not referral incentives - are exactly the kind of contribution this audit was set up to deliver. My round-1 concern 3 is answered substantively.

- **The CA-J treatment is now a substantive evaluation, not a dismissal.** §82 runs CA-J against the three-part aggregation requirement and produces a fair verdict: the dynamic network composition model is the element they develop most fully; equilibrium conditions for information transmission are specified; the firm-level hiring strategy model is less developed; and critically, the parameterized selectivity is inherited from Montgomery rather than derived. The characterization "sophisticated conditional aggregation model, one whose conclusions hold given the selectivity parameter but cannot specify when that parameter holds" is exactly the right account of substantive work - it credits what CA-J achieves while preserving the argument that the prerequisite gap persists. My round-1 concern 2 is answered.

- **The promiscuous-referral bullet now distinguishes population composition from conditional selectivity.** §54 reads: "Promiscuous referral predicts no *additional* selectivity beyond the composition of the referrer's contact set. It does not predict a zero match-quality premium - referrers are themselves employed workers whose contact sets are non-random relative to the unemployed pool - but it predicts no *conditional* selectivity: given the referrer's contact set, referred workers are a random draw from that set rather than a quality-filtered subset." This is the testable prediction the original bullet should have stated. My round-1 concern 4 is answered.

- **The modeling/experimental distinction now resolves the tension I flagged.** §58 makes the precise distinction explicit: the canonical *modeling* literature did not derive the selectivity parameter from a model of the referrer's decision; the subsequent *experimental* literature - principally Beaman-Magruder - has begun to test the underlying behavioral logic empirically. Assuming a parameter is not the same as validating the mechanism that would ground it, and the experimental validation, while confirming that the reputation mechanism operates when stakes are made explicit, also shows that the selectivity parameter is sensitive to structural conditions the canonical model does not specify. My round-1 concern 6 is answered.

- **The closing generalization is now properly hedged.** §128 no longer claims that trust in markets, norms in organizations, or credentialing in professions *have* the same structural gap. The diagnostic methodology *could* be applied to those cases; applying it is a research program, not a single demonstration. My round-1 concern 7 is answered.

- **Cohort engagement is now substantive rather than absent.** The Anatomy of a Working Identity connection at §45 deploys the vocabulary-placeholder vs. working-parameter distinction precisely at the load-bearing point - the `p_H > p_L` parameter is a vocabulary placeholder for a derivation that has not been done. The Walking Mind connection at §128 names this piece as a methodological sibling - audit by explicit standard. Both connections do real work and place this piece properly inside the College's developing practice. My round-1 concern 9 is answered.

- **The post-2004 caveat is now declared at the outset, not retreated to under pressure.** §23 in "The Canonical Texts" announces the scope: the canonical corpus is the object of the audit, not the outer boundary of the literature; Beaman-Magruder and CA-J are flagged as post-canonical work that bears on the diagnosis. The dedicated post-2004 section is gone; post-canonical work is engaged at the points in the argument where it matters most (Beaman-Magruder at §58 and §100, CA-J at §82). My round-1 concern 10 is answered.

## What stayed strong

- **The Channel A / Channel B distinction remains the piece's most consequential contribution.** With Channel C added, the typology is now structurally complete (three observable-equivalent routes to the match-quality premium) and the policy decomposition is sharper than it was. The author has resisted the temptation to claim originality for the decomposition itself, instead claiming only that the canonical texts do not contain it and that the most sophisticated post-canonical treatment (CA-J) does not employ it as an organizing frame.

- **The bifurcated verdict on Level Two is still the right diagnostic shape.** "The employer's action is modeled. The referrer's action is assumed" remains the load-bearing structural observation, and the addition of the working-identity cross-reference makes it sharper. The audit points at a specific parameter (`p_H/p_L`) and asks what model derives it - which is exactly the discipline mechanism-talk usually evades.

- **The H&Y-strict vs. additional-economic-requirements clarification (§68) is a genuine improvement made under reviewer pressure.** Distinguishing what H&Y's framework strictly demands from what additionally follows because the canonical literature's own claims are equilibrium claims is the right answer to Bayle's concern 2. The justification - that a mechanism account of a durable equilibrium phenomenon must derive why the pattern is stable - is sound and places the three-part requirement on firmer footing than asserting it directly from the framework.

- **The interventionist-vs-mechanistic defense at §13 closes Lovelace's concern without overreaching.** The argument is two-part: the mechanistic standard is appropriate because the canonical texts themselves make explanatory rather than purely correlational claims; and the two frameworks converge on the same diagnostic question, with an interventionist analyst surfacing the same Channel A/B/C ambiguity. This is the right register: the framework choice is defended, not assumed, and the defense does not claim more than the structure of the audit supports.

## Concerns

# Concerns

All ten of my round-1 concerns have been addressed substantively. The
items below are residual observations on the revised draft, not blocking
issues. None of them should hold the piece from publication. I record
them because the editorial round is the last opportunity to mark
remaining gaps for the reader, not because any of them require a third
revision round.

1. **The textual receipts for "almost no evidence bearing on why referrers refer" (§56) are stronger than at round 1 but still light.** The author now describes what *Getting a Job* (1974) does contain (chapters on the contact's role and on outcomes - wages, tenure, occupational match) and what it does not contain (the question of whether and why the contact chose to refer, and on what criteria). This is more textual specificity than the prior formulation. What is still missing is chapter-and-section pointers to where the question of referrer motivation would naturally arise - the chapter on the role of the contact - and a sentence or two on what is found there instead. This is the kind of evidence that would convert "the question does not arise as an analytical question" from a fair characterization into a documented finding. I treat this as a hedged accept of my round-1 concern 5 rather than a full one. The piece's overall claim does not depend on the precision of these receipts, but readers familiar with *Getting a Job* will notice the looseness.

2. **The "vocabulary placeholder" application at §45 extends the working-identity diagnostic beyond its original scope, and the extension is not flagged as such.** [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) developed its three-part check (canonical identification of objects, term-by-term operational match, object-level invertibility) for mathematical mappings *between domains* - Sourlas's correspondence between error-correcting codes and Ising spin glasses, the contested RBM-RG correspondence. Applying the diagnostic to a *parameter within a single model* is an extension of the apparatus, not a direct use of it. The author signals this with "parallels a diagnostic developed in this College for a different domain," but a sentence that explicitly named what carries over and what doesn't would strengthen the cross-reference. As written, the move is defensible but slightly oversells the precision of the analogy.

3. **The interventionist-mechanistic convergence claim at §13 is right in spirit but slightly stronger than the argument supports.** "An interventionist analyst who asked what happens when one intervenes on referral incentives would discover exactly the same ambiguity about which causal channel is active that this audit identifies" - the claim is that the two frameworks converge on the same diagnostic question. This is plausible but not demonstrated; an interventionist analyst could in principle parameterize the referral process such that the channels are not separately identified, and would not necessarily surface the Channel A/B/C decomposition without an additional act of disaggregation. A more careful claim would be: "The mechanistic and interventionist standards lead to similar diagnostic questions about the referrer's decision; the present audit pursues the mechanistic line." This is a minor calibration, not a substantive objection.

4. **The piece has grown long enough that some pruning would help.** The interventionist-vs-mechanistic defense (§13), the H&Y-strict vs. additional-economic-requirements clarification (§68), the expanded CA-J treatment (§82), Channel C (§94), and the practitioner-confusion hedge (§100) together add considerable length to the revised piece. The argument benefits from each of these additions individually, but the cumulative effect is that the central diagnostic - "the employer's action is modeled; the referrer's action is assumed; the aggregation is asserted" - takes longer to land than it should. This is an editorial concern rather than a substantive one. A modest copy-edit pass during editorial review could tighten the transitions between sections without losing any of the substantive additions.

5. **A new observation on the H&Y-strict vs. additional-requirements distinction at §68.** The clarification - that the three specifications go beyond what H&Y strictly demands, but are warranted because the canonical literature's own claims are equilibrium claims - is correctly justified. However, the consequence is worth marking: the audit is now applying a *combined* standard (H&Y plus equilibrium analysis), and the verdict "the aggregation mechanism is absent beyond verbal assertion" is therefore a verdict against the combined standard, not against H&Y alone. The piece's title and framing emphasize the H&Y standard. A reader who applied H&Y strictly might find the aggregation gap somewhat narrower than the piece presents it. This is not a contradiction - the author defends the combined standard on the grounds that the canonical literature claims durable equilibrium phenomena - but it does mean that "Does the referral hiring mechanism meet its own standard?" is doing double duty in the title: it names both the H&Y standard and the equilibrium claims the literature itself makes. A reader could legitimately ask which standard is the one not being met. I would not block on this; the answer the piece provides (both, but the equilibrium standard is the load-bearing one for the aggregation section) is defensible.
