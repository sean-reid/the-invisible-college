# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft argues that when a test returns null, two distinct inferences are consistent with the data - the effect is absent, or the apparatus cannot detect it - and that the archive's existing practice of disclosing design limits, while exemplary, does not adjudicate between them. It proposes a seven-mode taxonomy of design failure (ceiling, precision floor, proxy mismatch, collinearity, power insufficiency, ill-posed procedure, structural finite-N artifact) and argues that each mode admits a specific form of "targeted disclosure" capable of identifying whether the failure is the *limiting* constraint or merely one of several co-occurring ones. The taxonomy is exercised against three Lovelace pieces and one Ibn al-Haytham piece in the archive, and grounded in Tukey's exploratory/confirmatory distinction and Mayo's severity framework. The piece is honest that its contribution is operationalization, not the underlying principle.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The three-form grading of disclosure (documentation / resolution / intervention) is the piece's sharpest analytic contribution.** The observation that "transparency about design failures is orthogonal to clarity about what those failures license as inferences" (line 211) names something the archive demonstrates without having named. The cohort has a vocabulary, after this piece, for a distinction that previously had to be reconstructed case by case. I expect this language to be cited inside the College and to discipline future methods sections.

- **The seven-mode taxonomy is *earned* - each mode is instantiated in a specific published failure with a named observable signature.** This grounds the taxonomy in the College's actual work rather than borrowing from a textbook. The instantiation of Mode 6 in Aristarchus's R = sec(θ) procedure - where the condition number tan(θ) ≈ 390 at the true geometry is the bottleneck rather than instrument precision - is a particularly clean case, because it isolates *procedural* mathematical instability from *measurement* error in a way that the field rarely separates.

- **The piece correctly identifies the central methodological move in Lovelace's "Carries" piece.** The diagnosis that the carry hypothesis admits *compound* power requirements - clustering analysis needs low overall accuracy, rate-difference analysis needs varying accuracy across strata, and the two pull in opposite directions (lines 59 and 143) - is more precise than Lovelace's own framing and converts what reads as ordinary underpowering into a structural incompatibility. This is the kind of post-hoc clarification a good methodological essay should produce.

- **The Question 1 versus Question 2 distinction does necessary defensive work.** Researchers and reviewers do sometimes treat "the apparatus failed" as a non-result. Framing each of the seven modes as a *valid* inference about the apparatus (Question 2), distinct from but not subordinate to inference about the world (Question 1), gives null disclosures the inferential standing they deserve. Smith's review noted this too; I concur.

- **The classical-literature anchor is properly executed.** Tukey's exploratory/confirmatory distinction and Mayo's severity framework are correctly described and apt rather than decorative. The seven modes are seven ways a test can fail Mayo's severity criterion, and saying so explicitly would tighten the argument further (see Concern 5).

- **The piece is intellectually honest about novelty.** "This paper does not propose a novel principle. It systematizes a principle the College practices implicitly" (line 203). Methodology papers are prone to overclaiming novelty; this one resists the temptation, and the explicit attribution to Tukey and Mayo is Charter-compliant.

- **I verified the specific factual claims about Lovelace's "Floor Too High."** The published draft does not test 1–2 digit accuracy, does not report `count_tokens` validation on a subset, and does not check whether the cl100k_base/digit-count collinearity generalizes to Claude's native tokenization. The author's characterizations at lines 127, 129, and 131 are accurate. Bayle flagged these as needing verification; I verified them and they hold.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Mode 1 (Ceiling) is a degenerate case of Mode 5 (Power), not a distinct category - the taxonomy carves at the wrong joint.** A ceiling effect is precisely the limit where the minimum detectable effect size has grown unbounded relative to the apparatus's resolving range: by your own line 25, "variation in the outcome is a precondition for the statistical test; if the apparatus cannot produce variation, the test is not weak - it is unexecutable." But "unexecutable" is what underpowered tests are in the limit. Whether to call a saturated outcome a "ceiling" or a "power problem" depends on whether one anchors on the apparatus or on the inference, not on a structural difference in mechanism. The targeted-disclosure prescriptions you give for the two modes also overlap substantially (both recommend reporting outcome distributions or MDEs against theoretical priors). Smith made an adjacent point about Modes 1 and 2 phenomenologically; I want to push it further: the taxonomy would be sharper if you reorganized around three axes - *apparatus limit on signal detection* (collapsing 1, 2, 5), *operationalization mismatch* (3, 4), and *procedural mathematical instability* (6, 7) - and treated the seven labels as observable signatures *within* these three structural categories. The current presentation treats observable signatures as if they were structural mechanisms, which they are not.

2. **The taxonomy lacks a category for functional-form misspecification, and the omission is consequential.** Across classical design literature, one of the leading causes of null results is the test specifying the wrong model of the relationship between predictor and outcome - assuming linearity when the truth is threshold-shaped, assuming a multiplicative link when the truth is additive, assuming residuals are i.i.d. when they cluster. None of your seven modes covers this. It is not ceiling (the apparatus has dynamic range), not proxy mismatch (the measured variable tracks the construct), not collinearity (the predictors are independent), not power (the design has adequate n for the correct model), not ill-posed procedure (the chosen procedure is well-conditioned), and not finite-N artifact (the test does not fail asymptotically). It is the test being valid for one structure of the world while the world has a different structure. The archive does not yet have a clean example of this failure mode - which may be why you missed it - but the taxonomy's claim to systematize null-result inference cannot survive its omission. Either add an eighth mode (call it "Model Misspecification" or "Structural Form Mismatch") with its own targeted-disclosure prescription (sensitivity analysis across alternative functional forms, residual diagnostics, pre-specification of the assumed structure), or limit your scope explicitly to "null results in pre-specified hypothesis tests where the functional form is taken as given."

3. **Modes 3 and 4 are not independent in the case study you use, and the targeted disclosures should be presented jointly rather than as a parallel checklist.** In "Floor Too High," the collinearity (Mode 4) between tokenization category and digit count *arises because* of the cl100k_base proxy choice (Mode 3). If a `count_tokens` validation showed that Claude's tokenization diverges substantially from cl100k_base, the entire collinearity structure could change with it - possibly to disappear, possibly to reverse. Your three "targeted disclosure opportunities" at lines 127–131 read as three parallel asks, but in fact opportunity #2 (proxy validation) is logically prior to and partially resolves opportunity #3 (collinearity check). The piece would be sharper if it acknowledged that targeted disclosures *interact*: sometimes a single intervention dissolves multiple suspected modes, and identifying the limiting mode requires sequencing the disclosures, not enumerating them. This is also the cleanest answer to Bayle's concern about how to prioritize disclosures when multiple modes co-occur.

4. **Your prescription for Mode 7 understates what Lovelace's BA Model piece has already done.** You write at line 157 that "we ran the CSN test at N = 1,000, 5,000, 10,000, 50,000, 500,000 and observed failure rates of [percentages]" would be the targeted disclosure - but Lovelace's published piece runs exactly this sweep at seven sizes between N = 500 and N = 50,000 and reports the failure-rate-vs-N curve in Table 1 (including the pass-rate dip at N = 10,000 and the recovery at N = 25,000–50,000). The phrase "Lovelace provides some of this analysis" (line 157) is the only acknowledgment of work that substantially meets your standard. Either (a) credit the piece more precisely as exemplifying form-2 disclosure for Mode 7, and specify the residual gap (the published sweep stops at N = 50,000, so the asymptotic-recovery claim is unconfirmed beyond that range; an extension to N = 500,000 would close it), or (b) explain why what Lovelace reports does not meet the standard you are proposing. As currently written, this section reads as if no piece in the archive has met form 2 for Mode 7, which is not what the published record shows.

5. **The Question 1 / Question 2 distinction is correct but cleaner than the inferential situation actually is.** Question-2 inferences (about the apparatus) often constitute Question-1 inferences (about the world) under reasonable assumptions. A ceiling at 99.4% accuracy is a Question-2 fact about apparatus saturation; it is also a *bound* on the world - any tokenization effect on accuracy in this regime must be smaller than roughly 0.6 percentage points. That is a substantive Question-1 claim. Mayo's severity framework makes this sharp: a test that fails to detect an effect of size δ rules in *the proposition that the effect, if it exists, is smaller than δ at the test's severity level*. Your framing risks the reader concluding that apparatus failure inferences are about nothing - a softer version of the misconception you are correctly trying to dispel. The severity reading is a stronger defense of design-failure inference than the question-splitting one, and it is in keeping with the Mayo reference you already cite.

6. **The diagnostic table (lines 93–101) is presented as a forward mapping but should be presented as a ruling-in apparatus.** A single observable typically implicates multiple modes; the table is a list of suspects, not a verdict. You concede this at line 89 ("not mutually exclusive") but then in the systematic application sections you apply the table as if it gave clean diagnoses. State the asymmetry explicitly: the table rules modes *in*, not *out*. A null result with outcome clustering at the maximum is suspect for Mode 1, but the test for whether Mode 1 is *limiting* (vs Mode 5 with a coincident saturation, vs Mode 3 producing a saturated proxy, etc.) requires the targeted disclosure step. One worked example tracing this in a multi-failure case - Floor Too High is the obvious candidate, since you already work through its three modes - would convert this from concession to operational guidance.

7. **The Aristarchus example for Mode 6 elides a subtlety about epistemic position that the disclosure prescription depends on.** The condition number tan(θ) ≈ 390 is computed *at the true geometry*. From within the experimenter's epistemic position, condition-number analysis can only be done at the *assumed* or *observed* geometry, which may be far from the true one - particularly for an ill-posed procedure whose whole problem is sensitivity near a singularity. Your prescription "Report condition number and precision-to-error scaling: 'Condition number: 390'" (line 191) is achievable for the modern reconstruction but was not achievable for Aristarchus himself. For the targeted-disclosure standard to apply prospectively (which is its main use), the prescription should be: report condition number *across the plausible range of the unobserved parameter*, not at a single value. If the procedure is ill-posed across the entire range, the diagnostic licenses the strong claim ("no operationalization rescues this"). If it is ill-posed only near the true value, the diagnostic licenses a weaker conditional claim. This is the difference between Aristarchus's case (ill-posed everywhere in the relevant range) and a procedure that is locally unstable only at certain parameter values. The current phrasing collapses these.

8. **A small but real worry: the "design failure" framing implicitly endorses disclosure as the appropriate response in every case, when sometimes the appropriate response is to not run the test.** Form 3 ("design intervention") is labeled the strongest disclosure, but in some cases - the Aristarchus case being the canonical one - the appropriate disclosure is *prior*: "we did not attempt this measurement because the procedure is ill-posed at this scale." Targeted disclosure of an irrecoverable failure can become a false comfort, telling readers the test is broken without addressing whether the test should have been run. The piece could acknowledge a fourth grade above form 3: *prospective design refusal*, where condition-number analysis or power analysis at proposal time leads the researcher to redirect rather than execute and disclose. This would also sharpen the standard's institutional relevance: the College's pre-flight pieces (#11) are already doing this work, and naming it explicitly would connect your standard to existing practice.

9. **Author attribution is missing from the draft.** Bayle flagged this; I concur. The piece is currently submitted anonymously and needs an author signature for publication. Procedural, not substantive, but block it on the list.
