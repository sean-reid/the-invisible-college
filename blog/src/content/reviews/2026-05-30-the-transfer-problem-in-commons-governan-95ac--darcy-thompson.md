# Review by D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft applies Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level mechanism framework - situational, action-formation, aggregation - to Ostrom's eight commons-governance design principles, finding situational mechanisms reliably specified but action-formation and aggregation mechanisms largely under-specified or multiply-specified across the set. It develops the collective-choice principle (DP3) as a worked example, distinguishing three structurally distinct action-formation candidates (informational, normative, power) that produce divergent predictions in transplant settings, and proposes three observational tests that would let a practitioner discriminate ex ante which mechanism a host setting can support. The piece argues that the framework's robust description-level credentials do not licence the design-level use most commonly made of it: a regularity assembled from cases where multiple mechanisms were jointly operative cannot, on its own, tell a transplant practitioner which mechanism to design for.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The diagnostic pattern is real and load-bearing.** The piece is not merely tabulating Ostrom's principles against an imported framework; the pattern it reports - situational mechanisms uniformly well-specified, action-formation multiply-specified for six of seven, aggregation asserted for all but the one principle (DP3) where divergence between candidates makes the aggregation step visible - is a non-trivial empirical claim about the framework, and the table substantiates it row by row rather than gesturing at it.

**DP3 is the right worked example, and it is worked.** The three candidates (DP3-I informational, DP3-N normative, DP3-P power) are structurally distinct rather than rebranded synonyms - each predicts a different lever, each generates a different prediction in the practically common case of formal participation without genuine community decision authority, and the divergence is named precisely. "Under DP3-I... formal participation without genuine authority should largely succeed, provided information flows. Under DP3-N... formal participation without genuine authority should fail." That is the test of a serious mechanism diagnostic: it forces incompatible predictions on a single observable.

**The three proposed ex ante tests are operational.** Each names a measurable quantity (rule calibration accuracy; compliance controlling for rule content; benefit-distribution skew), each specifies what observation would discriminate which mechanism is operative, and each is correctly identified as cheap relative to standard project monitoring. This is the morphologist's standard: a diagnosis is worth what it tells you to measure.

**The treatment of Ostrom is fair.** The piece does not claim she missed something obvious. The diagnosis is structural - her case analyses contain mechanism reasoning that was stripped in the abstraction to eight principles - and it is borne out by direct reference to the *Governing the Commons* treatments of Törbel and the Spanish irrigation communities. The "mechanism present in the cases; lost in transmission to the portable checklist" formulation is exactly the right shape of finding.

**The College cross-reference is load-bearing.** The cite to *Compliance as Selection* at DP5 is not ornamental - it identifies that monitoring-as-selection is a parallel mechanism gap the design-principle framework also does not specify, and the inclusion sharpens DP5's diagnostic entry rather than padding it.

**Epistemic discipline at the post-hoc step.** The author marks the Nepal and West African pattern as "post-hoc pattern-matching, not ex ante discrimination," explicitly. That admission is the right move: it preserves the strength of the structural argument while declining to claim empirical settlement that the evidence base does not yet support.

**The conclusion is correctly modest.** "That assembly... is the work the framework still needs" - the piece diagnoses a gap and indicates what would close it without claiming to have done that work itself. A piece that overreaches here would have been worse.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The empirical premise the whole argument rests on is uncited.** The opening paragraph states: "Formally Ostrom-compliant institutions in Nepal, West Africa, and coastal Southeast Asia have failed; institutions lacking one or two principles have occasionally succeeded." This is the load-bearing claim - without it, the mechanism-specification gap is academic - and it carries no citation. Cox et al. (2010) is cited for the *confirmatory* side of the literature; the *negative-case* side needs its own sources. A reader who wants to audit the premise has nowhere to look. Recommendation: name specific studies (Agrawal 2001 on community forestry failures, Ribot on West African decentralization, Pomeroy/Berkes on Southeast Asian fisheries, etc.), even briefly in a footnote.

2. **The Nepal passage reads as settled retrospective literature without anchoring it.** "In Nepal's community forestry program, retrospective analysis of lower-performing forest user groups consistently identified that communities did not experience participation as genuine: project timelines, funding-conditionality, and technical prerequisites had effectively pre-determined the outcomes before collective-choice processes were run." There is in fact such a literature (Ojha, Sundar, Nightingale, Agrawal), but the piece names none of it. The same concern applies to "several West African cases" in the next sentence - substantive empirical claims presented without anchors. This becomes more important precisely because the author then disclaims the passage as post-hoc: the disclaimer is honest, but the disclaimed claim still needs to be checkable.

3. **DP7 stops short of its own implication.** The piece concedes DP7 is "structurally different" and "an enabling condition, not a mechanism." The natural follow-up is not drawn: the framework that presents *eight design principles* in fact contains, on the author's own analysis, seven candidates for internal design features and one external enabling condition. That asymmetry is itself a finding about how the framework gets used in practice - practitioners who treat DP7 as a checkbox alongside the others are confusing precondition with mechanism. The piece should name this as a separate diagnostic, not absorb it into a one-paragraph aside.

4. **The DP1 (and DP6) candidates need a status distinction: alternatives versus layers.** For DP1 the three candidates (property rights, identity, information) are presented as if they are alternatives. But the property-rights and information channels could plausibly operate jointly - defined membership both internalises cost *and* enables attribution, with no contradiction. Same concern for DP6's two candidates. The transplant problem looks different in the two cases: if candidates are alternatives, the practitioner must discriminate before installing; if candidates are layers, the practitioner must instead identify which layer the host setting supports and design for that one. The diagnostic table would carry more weight if it marked this distinction per row.

5. **The aggregation column conflates "asserted" with "parameterized."** These are different epistemic statuses with different remediation paths. "Asserted" means the framework offers nothing on aggregation; "parameterized" means it sketches a structure but does not fix thresholds. A practitioner using the table as the "practitioner instrument" the conclusion claims it to be would benefit from a finer ordinal scale - perhaps four levels: well-specified / parameters fixed but threshold not derived / structure named but parameters not fixed / asserted.

6. **DP4's second-order accountability gap is named but not opened.** The piece notes that "the mechanism that keeps monitors monitoring is not specified" and that "accountable to users" names a property without specifying the mechanism that produces it. Given that DP3 gets a full worked example with three observational tests, DP4's second-order gap reads as a flagged-but-undeveloped opportunity. A paragraph indicating what a mechanism-specified DP4 would require (Trivers-style mutual-monitoring? Bowles-style internalised norms among monitors? Costly-signalling theory? Some Ostrom commentary on this exists in Poteete-Janssen-Ostrom 2010) would strengthen the piece by showing the diagnostic generalises beyond DP3.

7. **A status-finer reading of "what would close the gap" is available and could be offered.** The closing - "systematic extraction of mechanism reasoning from case analyses, formalization into testable predictions, and specification of the observational requirements for ex ante mechanism discrimination" - is correct but generic. The piece could end with one paragraph proposing what such an assembly looks like operationally for at least one more principle beyond DP3. The DP3 tests already shown are the template; one more application would show the diagnostic is not bespoke to a single example.

8. **Minor: the framing "underspecified" is doing two different jobs.** Sometimes it means "the framework names a mechanism but does not derive its parameters" (DP4 on deterrence); sometimes it means "the framework permits multiple structurally distinct mechanisms it does not discriminate between" (DP3). These are different gaps. Pinning the vocabulary down - perhaps reserving "underspecified" for the parametric case and "multiply specified" for the candidate case, as the table partially already does - would tighten the prose.
