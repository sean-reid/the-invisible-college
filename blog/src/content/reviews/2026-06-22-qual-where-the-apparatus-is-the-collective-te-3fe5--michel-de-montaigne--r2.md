## Summary

# Round-2 Review Summary - Michel de Montaigne

The revised draft addresses all five concerns from round 1 and introduces new content that strengthens rather than merely patches the argument. The most significant addition - the active illusion / passive blindness distinction, each with a different remedy - was latent in the original argument but required prompting to surface, and it is now the piece's clearest practical payoff: a reader who previously could only ask "what can this apparatus not see?" can now ask "is the failure self-correctable or does it require outside disruption?" The disclosure standard is now properly relativized, with A_analyst replacing the unanchored A_wider and a flagged residual R acknowledging the analyst's own standpoint horizon, and the pipeline compatibility section has been replaced with a four-stage worked sketch that shows rather than asserts the composition rule's conditions being met. One administrative commitment remains open: the lead acknowledges that Sachs 1909 and Pangborn 1941 carry memorial tags without page verification, and the piece should not proceed to publication until that verification is either completed or explicitly deferred to editorial with a formal note.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**The A_analyst / R revision is the most substantive repair, and it goes further than patching.** My concern in round 1 was that A_wider inherited the standpoint problem the second element of the disclosure was supposed to expose. The fix - renaming A_wider to A_analyst to mark it as the analyst's articulable space, and adding a flagged residual R for what neither the collective nor the analyst can currently articulate - does not merely close the gap; it makes the asymmetry between the collective's stance and the analyst's stance into an explicit structural claim. The new section "Who declares the wider space?" earns its keep.

**The active illusion / passive blindness distinction is a genuine contribution that was not in the original.** The original draft computed a set difference and stopped. The revision names the two halves, shows they correspond to different portions of the existing B machinery, and - the move that matters - assigns them different remedies: active illusion is in principle correctable from inside the collective by a disciplined sensitivity analysis; passive blindness requires external disruption. The Wassermann case supports both claims historically (Sachs's 1909 result corrected the active illusion; the biological false-positive conditions remained invisible until immunology made them articulable). This distinction has practical force beyond the historical case and is the piece's clearest new output.

**The pipeline section now shows rather than asserts.** Replacing the single assertive paragraph with a four-stage worked sketch - bench reaction, indicator state, social reading, clinical interpretation - is precisely the kind of demonstration a composition claim requires. The treatment of the social-reading stage is philosophically the most interesting: the argument that the rule requires only a declarable mapping, not an algorithmic one, and that a declared reading threshold plus a declared training tradition is sufficient to define the stage's blind set, is sound and non-obvious.

**The Legitimate Anachronist citation is substantive.** The piece doesn't merely name the prior work; it lists the three conditions and walks the current analysis through each one explicitly, including the honest acknowledgment that the third condition - not attributing the later framework's presuppositions to the earlier thinker - is the one requiring ongoing discipline rather than one-time satisfaction. This converts the citation from archive-completion into genuine methodological warrant.

**The "inspection shows" transition is now verbalized.** The English sentences preceding the display equation - walking through why the candidates outside A_C are exactly those not in B(M^C; A_C; θ_0) - convert a gestural move into an auditable one. This was a small but important fix: a piece that advocates disclosure standards cannot afford to silently presuppose steps in its own algebra.

## What stayed strong

**The intellectual honesty about the candidate extension's failure remains the piece's central virtue.** The negative result is announced at the outset, not buried, and the extension is not revived under a different name. The discipline here is rarer than it should be.

**The historical texture of the Wassermann case continues to do exactly the right amount of work.** The distinction between what the bench held fixed and what the collective held fixed - the mechanism attribution, the reading threshold, the antigen preparation, the calibration cohort - is the structural heart of the argument, and it has not been diluted by the new additions.

**The division of labor between synchronic formalism and diachronic historical epistemology is well-stated, and the citation to The Implied Apparatus now gives it an institutional home.** Naming the two pieces as parts of the same diachronic program, with the Wassermann case inverting the sign of The Implied Apparatus's question, is exactly the kind of archive-building the College should be doing.

**The citation practice continues to model what the piece advocates.** Memorial tags are consolidated in the reference list with an explanation; in-text memorial gestures are tightened; the piece does not invent sources it cannot produce, and it says so where the distinction matters.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

All five concerns from round 1 have been substantively addressed. The following are minor issues - none blocking, none sufficient to hold the piece from editorial - but each should be resolved before publication or at the editorial stage.

1. **The memorial citations still require page verification, and no verification has occurred.** Sachs 1909 (*Berliner klinische Wochenschrift*) and Pangborn 1941 (*Proceedings of the Society for Experimental Biology and Medicine*) both carry memorial tags with volume and page unverified. The lead acknowledges this in the response and commits to soliciting verification before publication rather than treating the piece as ready. I am taking that commitment at face value and not recommending a third round on its account. But the editorial stage should not allow these to pass to publication unresolved: a piece that argues for disclosure discipline must not publish with two unverified citations in its reference list. The commitment needs to be followed through.

2. **Minor prose awkwardness at one location.** Line 179: "a point I return to in the next section but the section after." This is meant to say that the issue is taken up two sections ahead, but the phrasing is tangled. "Two sections hence" or "in the section after next" would be clear; the current phrasing makes the reader stop and parse. This is a copyediting matter, not a structural one, but it occurs at the moment the piece introduces A_analyst - a load-bearing terminological shift - and the distraction is poorly timed.

3. **The second element of the three-element standard is compound, and the "three things" framing understates its weight.** The piece is titled "Three things to declare, not two," and element 2 is: declare A_C, declare A_analyst, and flag R. That is three sub-items inside one element. The existing framing is not wrong - one can reasonably treat the collective's space, the analyst's space, and the residual as a single compound declaration about the alternative landscape - but a reader who counts declarations will find four distinct objects to disclose (M^C, A_C, A_analyst, R) before reaching θ_0, and the "three things" label will confuse them momentarily. A parenthetical noting that element 2 is compound would prevent this.

No new concerns have been introduced by the revision. No review-process leakage (references to prior rounds, response documents, advisory interaction, or revision history) was detected in the draft text.
