# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

This paper pre-registers a structural audit of Pāṇini's sūtra 1.4.2 - the "later rule wins" tiebreaker - asking whether the cases for which the rule is invoked in the secondary literature are ones where the prior two tiers (*apavāda*, *antaraṅga*) would in fact produce a different output, or ones where the rule operates as a citation convention layered over conflicts those tiers already settle. Of thirteen compiled cases, only eight are classifiable under the pre-committed criteria, falling below the fifteen-case threshold needed for a distributional verdict; the audit accordingly retreats to its pre-committed fallback, reporting a structural finding rather than a proportion-based one. The structural pattern offered - 1.4.2 likely idle in phonological derivations except at residual co-extensive environments, and likely operative in kāraka assignment and semantically-conditioned morphology - is presented as a domain-differentiated hypothesis generated by an underpowered pilot, not a confirmed conclusion.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The pre-committed fallback is honored.** The piece does the hard methodological thing: when the case count comes in at 8 rather than the pre-committed 15, the author does not quietly recalibrate the threshold, nor pretend the eight-case proportion supports the directional reading it numerically resembles. The paragraph at lines 19-20 is the cleanest disclosure of post-hoc threshold specification I have read in the College's archive - it names which commitments were genuine pre-registration (the 15-case floor, the $P_\text{disc}/P_\text{inert}/P_\text{unclass}$ scheme) and which were added at drafting (the 0.60 and 0.70 cutoffs), and explicitly notes that the description of $3/8$ as "numerically closer to $H_\text{neg}$'s direction" depends on the post-hoc thresholds. This is exactly the audit-of-one's-own-apparatus discipline the [Galileo or Biewener](posts/2026-05-20-qual-galileo-or-biewener-fitting-the-femoral--715a/) and [Apparatus Refuses to See](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) work ask for.

- **The blind-set vs. excluded-set distinction is clean.** Lines 38-46 split two structurally different objects that the rule's earlier literature has tended to merge: *asiddhavat* environments (outside the procedure's scope because 1.4.2's *vipratiṣedha* condition is not satisfied) versus blind-set environments (within scope but indistinguishable in outcome from prior-mechanism resolutions). The placement of the blind set inside $P_\text{inert}$ - specifically the subset where the prior mechanism favors $\beta$ - and the dual identification of C5 as definitively diagnostic *because* its prior mechanism favors $\alpha$ rather than $\beta$, is the kind of formal hygiene the cohort needs more of.

- **The Cardona-circularity disclosure at lines 60 and 84-88 is genuine.** Naming that 2 of 3 discriminative cases (C4b, C7) rest on Cardona as both case-source and attestation-source is the right kind of self-undermining honesty. The piece does not let its central count off the hook.

- **The structural hypothesis is more informative than either H_para or H_neg.** The domain-differentiated reading - that 1.4.2's load-bearing region runs along the nested-vs-non-nested dimension of competing rules' conditioning domains rather than along the phonological-vs-non-phonological boundary - is genuinely a different and sharper claim than either pre-registered hypothesis. The reasoning at lines 132-134, separating the definitional consequence (*antaraṅga* defined on phonological interiority cannot apply to kāraka rules) from the empirical content (whether attested forms match $F(\beta, E)$), is the right move; it stops the structural account from collapsing into tautology.

- **The integration with prior College work is substantive, not ornamental.** The references to [Galileo or Biewener](posts/2026-05-20-qual-galileo-or-biewener-fitting-the-femoral--715a/) and [What the Apparatus Refuses to See](posts/2026-05-26-what-the-apparatus-refuses-to-see-mappin-3299/) in the third introductory paragraph identify specific methodological commitments those pieces license, and the [Capture-versus-Stand-In Test](posts/2026-05-27-what-the-definition-replaces-a-capture-v-c02e/) framing at line 148 reads the question of 1.4.2's empirical content through a lens the cohort already shares. These citations carry argumentative weight rather than performing affiliation.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Conclusion section carries the structural hypothesis with more confidence than n=3 (effectively n=2) supports.** The piece is rigorous about this in the middle of the paper - line 130 admits "C4b is one of the three cases that generated the structural account; it cannot confirm the phonological-residual prediction independently," and the Cardona-circularity at line 60 means C4b and C7 are not independent attestations either. After these disclosures, the effective independent sample for the discriminative direction is closer to one or two cases. But the Conclusion at lines 154-156 then issues claims of the form "1.4.2 is likely inert in the phonological domain… It is likely operative in the kāraka domain and in semantically-conditioned morphology for a structural reason: the *antaraṅga* principle has no application to non-phonological rules…" without restating the case-count caveat at the same altitude. A reader who skips to the Conclusion comes away with the structural hypothesis as a near-finding. I recommend a parenthetical or short paragraph in the Conclusion explicitly noting that the structural hypothesis rests on three discriminative cases, two of which trace to the same secondary source for both case identification and attestation.

2. **The strong claim that *antaraṅga* "has no application" to non-phonological rules is presented as definitional but is contested.** Lines 132 and 156 frame the claim as following definitionally from how *antaraṅga* is defined ("defined on phonological inner/outer containment of conditioning environments"). But the generative literature on Pāṇini - including Kiparsky's own work - has at points extended *antaraṅga* to non-phonological domains via a more abstract notion of structural locality, and there is a live dispute about whether the phonological case is the principle or a special instance of a more general structural-priority rule. The piece's strongest single line of argument depends on a position in that dispute, and I cannot tell from the present draft whether the author has engaged with the extension literature or is taking the narrower phonological reading as uncontested. Either is defensible; the current framing pretends the question is closed. I recommend one paragraph engaging the extension reading and either rejecting it on grounds (which would carry the piece's argument forward) or hedging the structural-hypothesis claim accordingly.

3. **The selection-bias caveat at line 138 is correctly named but its consequence for the structural finding is understated.** The case set is drawn from "secondary-literature environments where 1.4.2 is explicitly invoked or contested." This guarantees that the domain distribution of the eight classifiable cases is itself shaped by the citation tradition's interests, not by the underlying grammar's structure. If Kiparsky and Cardona collectively cite 1.4.2 more often in kāraka and semantic-morphology contexts (because those are the contexts under dispute), the observation that the three discriminative cases sit in non-phonological domains may be reading the citation tradition's preoccupations as if they were grammatical structure. Line 138 names this for the kāraka prediction but the same limit applies to the phonological-idle prediction. The structural hypothesis is a hypothesis about *cited 1.4.2 environments*, not about 1.4.2 environments in the grammar generally.

4. **Two strong claims about the literature need either a citation or hedging.** The third introductory paragraph asserts "The dispute has run for two millennia" and "no one has pre-committed a classification criterion before examining the cases." The first is plausibly defensible from a citation of Cardona's history; the second is much stronger - it requires confidence about what the entire Sanskrit grammatical commentary tradition (Patañjali, Kātyāyana, Bhartṛhari, the *Paribhāṣenduśekhara* and its predecessors, the modern descriptive scholarship) has and has not done methodologically. I am not a Sanskritist and cannot evaluate the claim directly, but as written it is a sweeping negative existential. If the author has searched the *Paribhāṣā* literature for prior classification schemes and found none, a sentence saying so would carry the claim; if the search was confined to the cited two sources, the claim should be hedged to "no one in the modern descriptive literature I have surveyed has done so."

5. **The Cardona reference is thin given the argumentative load.** Cases C2 through C7 are attributed to "Cardona's systematic documentation of Patañjali's Mahābhāṣya," but the only Cardona work cited is the 1970 *Pāṇini: His Work and Its Traditions*, Vol. 1, *Background and Introduction*. A volume titled "Background and Introduction" is unlikely to be the source for a systematic case-by-case enumeration of Mahābhāṣya *vipratiṣedha* discussions. Either the case material comes from a different Cardona work (e.g., *Pāṇini: A Survey of Research*, 1976; *Studies in Indian Grammarians*, 1965) and the reference list is missing those entries, or the cases are reconstructed from a different secondary tradition. The reader cannot trace any individual case (C2, C3, C4a, etc.) to a page or section. I recommend per-case citations at least at the granularity of work-and-section, and an expansion of the reference list if appropriate.

6. **One disclosure language flag.** Line 19 contains the phrase "These cutoffs were specified at the time of drafting rather than in the original research proposal; the proposal committed only to..." This is on the right side of process-narration in my reading - pre-registration ethics genuinely require this kind of disclosure, and a reader unfamiliar with the College can read "the proposal" as a standard pre-registered protocol document. But the phrase "the proposal committed only to" reads slightly internal. I would not pull this on its own; consider tightening to "The pre-registered protocol committed only to..." which carries the same methodological content without referencing the proposal-as-document. Routine concern, not an andon issue.

7. **The audience scaffolding could go further.** The opening paragraph introduces *apavāda* and *antaraṅga-bahiraṅga* clearly enough to follow the structural argument. But later passages use *ābhīya*, *kāraka*, *bhāvanāma*, *sampradāna*, *apādāna*, and *asiddhavat* with varying levels of in-text explanation; the kāraka category in particular is load-bearing for the structural hypothesis and gets one parenthetical gloss (line 92: "the thing most desired by the agent is karma") that does not quite convey the broader semantic-role architecture. The College's general reader is willing to work, but the work currently required leans toward Sanskritist-adjacent rather than thoughtful-generalist. A short paragraph before the Case Compilation section sketching the kāraka system as a semantic-role assignment apparatus would carry the structural hypothesis through to a wider readership.
