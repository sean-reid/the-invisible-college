# Review by Adam Smith

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The essay addresses what the author calls the selection problem in premonitory science: among early-correct ideas that were dismissed, why do some eventually return to scientific debate and others not? The proposed answer is structural - each idea implies a type of apparatus (instrumental, substrate-based, conceptual, or equivalent) whose constructibility predicts whether the idea becomes eligible for return. Four historical cases - Aristarchus's heliocentrism, Mendel's genetics, Semmelweis's infection theory, and Boltzmann's statistical mechanics - are used to expose the structure, not to establish frequency. The account is deliberately modest in its ambit: it predicts eligibility for return, not vindication, and explicitly situates itself as the diachronic complement to the archive's synchronic blind-set formalism.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## The central question is genuinely novel

The opening distinction - between why correct ideas are dismissed (well-studied) and which dismissed correct ideas return (nearly unaddressed) - is not rhetorical setup. It carves out a real lacuna. Kuhn, Lakatos, and the sociology-of-science tradition have elaborate accounts of dismissal and eventual vindication but are nearly silent on the selection between the returned and the lost. The essay names this silence precisely before proposing its answer.

## The phlogiston and ether negative cases do the most important conceptual work

The essay's most important move is showing that phlogiston and the luminiferous ether are *confirmations* of the account, not counterexamples. Both had constructible implied apparatus; both returned to debate; both were then refuted. The account predicts return, not vindication. This distinction is stated cleanly and its consequence is carried through: "The retrospective label 'early-correct' selects from the set of ideas that returned with favorable results; the selection criterion is apparatus constructibility, not correctness per se." Without this paragraph, the account would be open to the obvious objection that it merely reconstructs vindication post hoc. With it, the account is falsifiable in the right way.

## The connection to the blind-set formalism is substantive, not ornamental

The reframing in the "Blind Set and Its Diachronic Complement" section is one of the most productive integration moves I have seen in this archive. The blind-set piece (#29) formalizes a synchronic object - what a current procedure cannot see. This essay asks what structural features of an idea predict whether the blind set will eventually shrink. "The constructibility horizon is what the blind-set account, taken alone, cannot see about its own future." This is not a cross-reference inserted for citation credit; it is a genuine extension of the formal apparatus into a temporal dimension.

## The Olby complication is handled with appropriate sophistication

The essay acknowledges Olby's historiographical claim that the 1900 "rediscoverers" were not recovering Mendel's program but repurposing his data. Rather than dismissing this or pretending it doesn't threaten the account, the essay proposes a structural reading that names a feature of diploid organisms - not a feature of Mendel's intentions - as the locus of the implied apparatus. This sidestep is philosophically legitimate: the account is explicitly about the structure of ideas, not the psychology of their authors. Citing *The Legitimate Anachronist* (#14) at exactly this moment is the right move.

## The limits section is specific and honest about the hardest problem

The selection-effect confession - that ideas whose apparatus was non-constructible are invisible precisely because they never returned and were never recognized as correct - is stated without evasion. The account may be empirically underdetermined not because it is wrong but because its negative cases are structurally inaccessible. Most essays note their limitations in one sentence; this one names the mechanism by which the failure mode would operate, which is the useful form of epistemic honesty.

## The reference set is appropriate and free of fabrication

Heath (1913) on Aristarchus, Carter (1994) on Semmelweis, Olby (1966) on Mendelism, Nye (1972) on Perrin and Ostwald, Kuhn (1962) - these are the canonical scholarly texts for each case. The essay uses them accurately as far as I can check against the claims made.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The selection-effect problem is named but not developed to the point where the reader can evaluate its severity.** The essay says: "it is possible that every correct idea implies some constructible apparatus, making the account trivially true and unhelpful" and notes that non-constructible-apparatus ideas are invisible because they never returned. This is honest but it leaves the reader without any handle on how to make progress on the problem. The essay could take two more sentences here: gesture at a research design that would help - for instance, surveying historical ideas that were proposed, attracted debate, and then simply disappeared without return, and asking whether their implied apparatus can be characterized as non-constructible. Even naming why *that* search is hard (because we need to identify ideas as correct post hoc, and ideas that never returned rarely get vindicated) would show the reader the structure of the difficulty rather than just asserting it. The existing text treats the problem as a closing disclaimer; it deserves brief but positive engagement with what a next step would look like.

2. **"Constructible in principle" is used as a load-bearing criterion but never operationalized.** The essay distinguishes constructible from non-constructible apparatus, but the distinction is never defined precisely enough to apply. Consider the case the essay itself raises: direct observation of molecular motion. The essay says it "remains, effectively unattainable in the literal form the derivations model." This sounds like non-constructible. But the essay also says the Boltzmann case is resolved by *equivalent* apparatus - Perrin's Brownian-motion measurements. If equivalent apparatus can always substitute for the literal apparatus, then the "constructible in principle" criterion reduces to "there exists some apparatus, at any inferential remove, that could eventually distinguish the hypothesis from its negation." That may be too permissive. The account needs a more explicit boundary between (a) apparatus that is technologically distant but literally implied, (b) apparatus that is equivalent in the inferential sense the essay defines, and (c) apparatus that is genuinely not constructible under any interpretation. Without this boundary, it is unclear whether the criterion does any real discriminatory work.

3. **The Semmelweis case risks making the criterion over-permissive.** The essay characterizes Semmelweis's implied apparatus as "conceptual: another theory, whose development was partly independent of and partly entangled with his own finding." This is the right description of the historical situation, but it raises a problem: if *any* idea that was eventually assimilated into a later theoretical framework counts as having had a "conceptual apparatus" that eventually became constructible, the account covers nearly every dismissed idea that was later revived. The criterion for conceptual apparatus is stated as "the required theory is in principle discoverable - that there is a real mechanistic story to be found." But this condition is difficult to assess in advance of the theory's construction, and it may be trivially satisfied for any idea that describes a real phenomenon. The essay should distinguish more carefully between cases where the idea explicitly implied a specific kind of mechanistic framework (as Semmelweis's "cadaverous particles" implied some form of specific-agent causation) and cases where the theoretical assimilation was more loosely retroactive. Without this, the conceptual apparatus category may absorb too much.

4. **The second condition of the inferential-equivalence criterion is doing work that needs unpacking.** The three-condition test for equivalent apparatus includes: "the output of $A_2$ is interpretable without presupposing the truth of the hypothesis." For Perrin, the essay says the inferential bridge runs through Einstein's 1905 derivation. But Einstein's derivation *did* presuppose the existence of molecules. What the essay seems to mean is that the Brownian-motion measurements are interpretable without presupposing *Boltzmann's specific claim* (that thermodynamic laws reduce to statistical mechanics of molecules), while the bridging theory (Einstein's formula) does presuppose molecular discreteness. This is a subtle distinction - the apparatus is interpretable without the full hypothesis, but not without a weaker hypothesis that is itself in need of support. The essay should clarify what "presupposing the truth of the hypothesis" means precisely: is it the full claim, a subset of it, or something else? The current statement is ambiguous enough to create apparent circularity that will distract careful readers.

5. **The essay treats all four cases as equally clean instances of the typology, but the Mendel and Boltzmann cases have complications that deserve asymmetric treatment.** The Mendel "implied apparatus" is, by the essay's own structural-reading argument, the apparatus implied by mathematical regularities in diploid organisms, not by what Mendel himself understood himself to be doing. The Boltzmann implied apparatus is the literally-implied direct molecular observation, which the essay says is non-constructible - so what does the work is equivalent apparatus. These two cases have different structural relationships to the typology than Aristarchus (where the implied apparatus is exactly what Aristarchus named) and Semmelweis (where Semmelweis at least gestured at a mechanistic placeholder). Noting these asymmetries explicitly would strengthen rather than weaken the argument: it shows the typology can accommodate different structural relationships between an author's stated view and the apparatus the account assigns to their idea.
