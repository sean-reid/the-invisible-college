## Summary

# Round-2 Review Summary - Ibn al-Haytham

The revised draft substantively addresses all eight of my round-1 concerns and the parallel concerns from D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson and Henri Poincaré. The most important repairs-the joint-operation baseline introduced in the opening, the action-formation locus now explicitly justified, the measurement-asymmetry paragraph added after the table, the trained-heuristic version of the reductive account engaged via Henrich et al.'s cross-cultural data, the normative mechanism's first condition reframed as an "internalization pathway" disjunction with Hechter, Elster, and Bicchieri named, the licensing prediction reshaped as a contrast between null and positive interaction coefficients, and the practitioner tool section distinguishing the prescriptive use (robust) from the diagnostic use (less secure)-land at or above the strength I asked for, and a few are handled more cleanly than I had suggested. Two minor residual concerns remain: an empirical claim about the tax morale literature is asserted without a specific citation in the references, and "substantial" in the licensing interaction prediction remains undefined. Neither rises to the level that should hold the piece. No process-language leakage was introduced. The piece reads as a self-contained public artifact and is ready for publication.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What Got Better

- **The measurement asymmetry paragraph is the revision's single best addition.** The paragraph following the diagnostic table now names what I asked for in round-1 concern 1-that the information mechanism's operative conditions have established measurement traditions while the normative mechanism's conditions require operationalization independent of the behavioral outcomes they are meant to explain-and then does something I did not specifically ask for but which strengthens the piece materially: it draws the consequence ("the available evidence base is structurally biased toward the information account-not because the normative account is wrong, but because the conditions for the normative account are harder to measure independently. This is part of why the conflation is durable"). The piece now explains *why* the conflation persists at the institutional level, which is a deeper finding than the measurement asymmetry alone. The commons confound-that "communities lacking observability infrastructure very often also lack pre-existing collective identity around the resource"-is named explicitly with the correct epistemic register: orthogonal variation exists but requires deliberate case selection.

- **The action-formation locus claim is now defended rather than asserted.** The opening paragraph's added clause-"Action formation is the step where both mechanism families can always, in principle, account for the same observed behavior. The analyst must specify what actors actually do in response to the same situational input, and at that step the mechanism choice is unavoidable"-gives the reader the structural reason the load-bearing step is action formation rather than the other two. The piece no longer inherits its scope from Hedström and Ylikoski without independent justification. This was my round-1 concern 6 and the repair is exactly the shape I asked for.

- **The trained-heuristic version of the reductive account is engaged on its own ground.** The new paragraph after "The Reductive Challenge" first names what the more sophisticated reductive theorist would say (costly punishment is a trained heuristic from repeated-interaction environments, deployed in the laboratory through generalization), then specifies what that account commits to (roughly uniform cross-cultural intensity), then points to Henrich et al.'s 2006 documentation of substantial cross-cultural variation correlating with market integration and world-religious participation as evidence that the universal-heuristic version cannot straightforwardly explain. This is exactly the methodological move I asked for in round-1 concern 2: the prior reductive paragraph and the Fehr–Gächter paragraph no longer talk past each other, and the empirical bite of the diagnostic is now anchored in evidence beyond a single experiment.

- **The internalization pathway disjunction handles the Hechter problem correctly.** The normative mechanism's first condition is now a disjunction-direct voice, extended participation in a practice community, professional enculturation, childhood socialization-rather than the single Hechter-derived voice-in-formation condition. Hechter, Elster, and Bicchieri are each named as emphasizing different pathways, with the common requirement (norm-as-self-imposed, not externally imposed) identified across accounts. This was my round-1 concern 3 and the repair is more careful than I had suggested. It also widens the normative mechanism's scope to cover cases the round-1 condition implicitly excluded (childhood internalization without standard-setting voice; professional norms acquired post-hoc).

- **The licensing prediction now has an explicit operational shape.** Where the round-1 draft said the normative account predicts quality degradation "concentrated in the unaffiliated population," the revision states the contrast as a regression interaction: the quality-by-affiliation coefficient (post-deregulation quality on affiliation status, after controlling for pre-deregulation quality measures) should be null under the information-only account and "positive and substantial" under the joint-mechanism account. This addresses my round-1 concern 4 by giving the prediction a falsifiable form. The piece also now identifies the selection confound-practitioners who remain affiliated likely differ on pre-existing quality correlates-and names three design strategies (matched comparisons across multiple state-level deregulation events with variation in association cost structures, instruments shifting affiliation cost without shifting practitioner quality, within-practitioner panel tracking).

- **The Kleiner–Krueger heterogeneity paragraph is a good indirect-evidence move.** The acknowledgment that profession-level heterogeneity in the licensing wage premium is "consistent with the prediction that professional community embeddedness moderates quality effects, though it falls short of the individual-level tracking the proper test requires" is the right calibration of indirect-evidential force. It accepts the limit ("the test is feasible; it requires design decisions the existing literature has not made") without abandoning the prediction.

- **The practitioner tool now distinguishes prescriptive from diagnostic uses, with calibrated robustness assessments.** The closing section now states that the prescriptive use (which design tools to deploy in a new institutional installation) is "relatively secure" because the transplant asymmetry forces a specific choice, while the diagnostic use (identifying which mechanism is operative in a functioning institution) "is less secure precisely because the normative mechanism's conditions are harder to operationalize independently of the behavioral outcomes they are meant to explain." This was my round-1 concern 8 and the repair lands exactly. The acknowledgment that the diagnostic use should be held with appropriate uncertainty, and that the information account should be treated as the default requiring some evidence to overturn (not as the residual), is honest about the asymmetric epistemic position the piece itself has documented.

- **The Ostrom phrasing is corrected without losing the point.** The change from "the symmetric presentation of all eight of Ostrom's design principles as equivalent design requirements obscures this asymmetry" to "treating Ostrom's design principles as equivalent design requirements, rather than as the empirical regularities they were drawn from, obscures this asymmetry" is faithful both to Ostrom's own post-1990 framing and to the substantive critique the piece wants to make. The round-1 phrasing was the closest the piece came to mischaracterizing a major source; the revision is precise.

- **The designable/non-designable asymmetry now reads as a time-horizon claim, not a categorical one.** The acknowledgment that "the normative mechanism is cultivable over longer scales-professional schools, ethics codes, and ritualized practice can build it over generations-but that is a different problem from what a policy designer can address within a project cycle" closes the small overreach the round-1 draft had. The transplant asymmetry retains its force-designers operate within project cycles, not generations-while no longer denying the cultivability that long-form institutional history obviously does.

- **The Weber–Coleman paragraph in the introduction situates the contribution correctly.** Naming the Vergesellschaftung/Vergemeinschaftung contrast and the purposive/primordial distinction at the level of institution types, then specifying what action-formation analysis adds (finer-grained: which mechanism is load-bearing for the particular behavioral pattern, not which kind of institution), is the right intellectual humility. It also forecloses an obvious reviewer objection from a sociology reader.

## What Stayed Strong

- **The mechanism specifications remain operationally tight.** Each family still gets three operative conditions, and the third normative condition-the actor who would comply when unmonitored and feel ashamed of a violation in the absence of any audience-still names the empirical signature without which the family collapses into its reductive alternative. This was the piece's strongest single move in round 1 and the revision did not blunt it.

- **The retrospective applications retain their graded treatment.** Referral hiring as an implicit defaulting to information, compliance monitoring as an undischarged alternative, commons governance as the case where the split was already partially named-this graded register is preserved and now connects more cleanly to the licensing prospective.

- **The Fehr–Gächter anchor still does real evidential work.** The phrasing "What remains is consistent with the normative mechanism's signature, though not conclusive on its own" is appropriately careful-it does not claim the result proves the normative mechanism, only that it eliminates the within-game pathway by which the information mechanism could account for it. The reductive challenge section then handles the more sophisticated trained-heuristic alternative separately.

- **The headline finding-the transplant asymmetry-remains the piece's sharpest contribution.** The conventional ordering ("technically demanding" versus "sociologically demanding") gets correctly inverted: the technical infrastructure can be built; the social precondition cannot, not on the time horizon designers operate. This was the operationally consequential finding in round 1 and the revision sharpens it by closing the categorical-impossibility overreach.

- **The compliance-monitoring discussion now reads as cleanly complementary to *[Compliance as Selection](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/)*.** The framing that "the two accounts bear on different aspects of the violation distribution: one characterizes where in the detectability dimension violations concentrate, the other characterizes who the violators are" correctly situates the present work as adding a dimension rather than displacing the prior result. This is honest about institutional credit and does not generate internal friction with the prior College piece.

- **The closing crystallization is now substantive, not generic.** Ending with the licensing-specific consequence-"the deregulation policy debate has proceeded as if only one mechanism is available, producing predictions about quality effects that are too uniform and policy prescriptions miscalibrated to the mechanism actually maintaining quality"-is the right way to end. The reader leaves with a concrete institutional finding, not a generic exhortation about specifying mechanisms.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

These are residual concerns. None rises to the level that should hold the piece, and none is a return of a prior concern the response dismissed unconvincingly. I record them so the editorial board has them and so the final form is as clean as the substance warrants.

1. **The tax morale literature claim is asserted without a specific citation.** The reductive-challenge section now says: "the identity-salience test-whether making professional or civic membership salient adds predictive power after controlling for enforcement history-has been investigated in the tax morale literature, where civic identity and group-membership factors predict compliance above and beyond deterrence variables across multiple countries and enforcement regimes." This is a substantive empirical claim about a body of work, and the piece cites no specific paper. The reference list contains no tax morale entry (Frey, Feld, Torgler, Alm, or others). For a piece otherwise rigorous about citing its empirical anchors (Fehr & Gächter, Henrich et al., Kleiner & Krueger), the absence of even one anchoring citation here is a notable gap. The response document explicitly accepts this concern from my round-1 review and points to the tax morale literature; it did not, however, add a specific reference. I would add one-Frey and Torgler's 2007 "Tax morale and conditional cooperation" or Torgler's 2007 book *Tax Compliance and Tax Morale* would serve-and a one-clause inline cite of the form "(Frey and Torgler, 2007; Torgler, 2007)" would discharge this without disturbing the surrounding prose. This is the only concern in this review I would press for resolution before publication.

2. **"Positive and substantial" in the licensing prediction is operationalized in direction but not in magnitude.** The revision now states that under the joint-mechanism account the quality-by-affiliation interaction coefficient "should be positive and substantial." This is a clear directional contrast with the information-only null, which is a real improvement over the round-1 "concentrated" formulation. But what counts as substantial remains undefined. The piece could specify-e.g., a standardized effect size in the d ≥ 0.3 range, or an interaction explaining at least some named fraction of post-deregulation between-practitioner quality variance-or, equivalently, name that the right magnitude threshold is field-specific and would be set as part of the test design rather than the prediction. The current "positive and substantial" leaves a small interpretive door open: a test could return a positive but small interaction and produce ambiguous evidential weight. Because the piece is an analytical essay rather than a research design, I am comfortable letting this stand if the author prefers. But naming this as a design decision the test would need to make would close the door cleanly.

3. **The trained-heuristic counter is rebutted via "universal" framing, and the reductive theorist's natural retreat to a non-universal trained-heuristic is not addressed.** The new paragraph on the trained-heuristic version says: "if costly punishment is a universally-trained heuristic arising from the common human developmental environment, it should appear with roughly similar intensity across human societies." Henrich et al.'s cross-cultural variation then constitutes a problem for this. But the reductive theorist's natural reply is to drop universality: yes, the trained-heuristic varies cross-culturally, because enforcement-history exposure varies cross-culturally. Market integration and world-religious participation could be reframed (by the reductive account) as proxies for the kind of cross-society variation in repeated-interaction structures that the trained-heuristic story would in fact predict. The piece anticipates this implicitly-"the normative account, which makes compliance conditional on community membership and internalization pathway, fits better"-but the argument is that the non-universal trained-heuristic account would collapse into the normative account's structure (community-conditional training is community-conditional internalization under a different name). The piece could state this collapse explicitly: a sufficiently community-conditional trained-heuristic is the normative mechanism in reductive vocabulary, and the choice between them then turns on which conceptual frame predicts the wider set of observable patterns. As written, the rebuttal is correct but its strongest form is left implicit.

4. **The internalization-pathway disjunction is well-handled, but it now leaves the discriminator slightly under-specified.** With the normative mechanism's first condition expanded to include direct voice, extended participation in a practice community, professional enculturation, and childhood socialization, the diagnostic in principle covers a wider population-which is correct as a description of the literature. But the discriminator question ("Is there an internalization pathway and community membership that makes normative compliance identity-constitutive?") becomes harder to falsify negatively: in any institutional setting, *some* candidate internalization pathway can be named. A short sentence acknowledging that disconfirmation of the normative mechanism in a given case requires showing the absence of *all* the named pathways, not just one, would prevent the disjunction from doing inadvertent work as an unfalsifiable refuge. This is a small methodological point about the diagnostic's discriminating power, not a fault in the analysis.

5. **Process-narration leakage: none detected.** I read the revised draft with attention to phrases of the form "the prior draft," "after peer review," "this revision addresses," "the panel said," "my advisor," and similar. None appears in the body of the piece. The opening of "A Prospective Application" uses "yields an institutional finding rather than merely a demonstration of framework generality," which on a generous reading could be heard as responsive to a reviewer who pushed for the framework to earn its prospective application-but in context it reads as a substantive claim about what the application produces, not as process narration. I would not flag this.

6. **One internal cross-reference convention check.** The piece cites three prior College pieces in the retrospective applications-the referral hiring, compliance, and commons pieces-using the in-line slug-link convention. These work and are correctly labelled. No fabricated cross-references.

7. **No fabricated external references detected.** I spot-checked the bibliography against my own working knowledge: Bicchieri 2006 (Cambridge), Coleman 1990 (Harvard), Elster 1989 (Cambridge), Fehr and Gächter 2000 (AER), Hechter 1987 (California), Hedström and Ylikoski 2010 (Annual Review of Sociology), Henrich et al. 2006 (Science), Kleiner 2006 (Upjohn), Kleiner and Krueger 2013 (JHR), Ostrom 1990 (Cambridge), and Weber 1922 (Mohr) / 1978 translation (California). Each is a known and correctly-identified work in the relevant literature. The Henrich et al. 2006 Science paper is in particular the canonical cross-cultural punishment study and supports the cited claim about variation correlating with market integration and world-religious participation.
