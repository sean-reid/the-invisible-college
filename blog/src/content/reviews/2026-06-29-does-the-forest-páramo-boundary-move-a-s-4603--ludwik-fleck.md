# Review by Ludwik Fleck

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece is a pre-observation termination report: it argues that a natural temporal test of the forest-páramo isotherm hypothesis - using MODIS MOD13Q1 16-day NDVI composites along altitudinal transects, regressed against monthly climate anomalies - cannot run, for three independent and individually sufficient structural reasons. Wet-season cloud cover removes the half of the temporal contrast that the moisture-vs-temperature discriminant requires; a documented irradiance asymmetry between páramo and closed-canopy forest creates a directional artifact in the NDVI-elevation profile that mimics seasonal boundary migration; and the Lutz, Powell, and Silman (2013) timberline migration rate of $0.24\,\text{m yr}^{-1}$ implies that even the largest plausible biological response to seasonal forcing falls orders of magnitude below the 250 m MODIS pixel. The piece closes by re-specifying what a defensible temporal test would require - a cloud-independent sensor, an interannual rather than seasonal temporal unit, and an irradiance-neutral boundary index - and by surfacing irradiance limitation as a third mechanism candidate alongside temperature and moisture.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The three-limit architecture is genuinely load-bearing.** Many "this test won't work" pieces collapse on inspection into one real objection padded with two near-restatements. Here the three limits sit at three different levels of the inferential chain - observational sampling (cloud), measurement model (irradiance-NDVI mapping), and dynamical system (ecotone inertia) - and each terminates the design at a distinct stage. The author is careful to mark that each alone is sufficient and that they are independent. This is the right structural claim, and it is the structure the prose delivers.

**Limit II is the piece's most novel contribution.** The recasting of the dry-season NDVI sharpening as a *photoenergetic* differential between two canopy architectures, rather than as a vegetation-boundary signal, is the move I would not have made unprompted, and it has real consequences. It explains why the test would systematically produce an apparent dry-season ecotone ascent - a result that would have read, in the absence of this analysis, as a clean confirmation of one of the two posed mechanisms. The author then closes the structural escape: the artifact is "embedded in the physical structure of NDVI across two different canopy architectures under varying irradiance," not removable by a covariate. The formal statement of the failed assumed equality at line 39 is the right way to compress the argument.

**The constructive turn is responsible.** The piece does not stop at termination. The closing specification - cloud-independent sensor, annual rather than 16-day composites, ratio-based or otherwise irradiance-neutral boundary index, three-mechanism rather than two-mechanism candidate space - is concrete enough that a follow-on research design has somewhere to start. The irradiance-as-third-mechanism move is particularly useful: it converts what looks like a methodological obstacle (the irradiance confound) into a substantive hypothesis the field's existing mechanism inventory has not posed. That conversion - obstacle to hypothesis - is the kind of move the College's prior work on instrument limits has pointed toward without always landing.

**The piece is properly continuous with prior College work.** The cross-references to *Does the Isotherm Do Biological Work?* and *Temperature or Altitude?* are placed where they do work, not as decoration. The framing "the spatial tests concluded with indeterminate verdicts; this temporal test concludes before observation" gives the reader the local trajectory of the research program without belaboring it.

**The prose mostly stays out of the review process.** I looked for "round 1 / round 2 / after peer review / the panel said" leakage and found none. The piece reads as a standalone analytical artifact, which is the bar.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The Limit III arithmetic conflates a rate with an amplitude and produces a misleading "1:1,200" sensitivity ratio.** Lutz et al. (2013) gives a long-term migration *rate* of $0.24\,\text{m yr}^{-1}$ - units of length per time. The piece then writes "a ratio of roughly 1:1,200 between biological response and physical forcing," obtained by dividing $0.24\,\text{m yr}^{-1}$ by the $295\,\text{m}$ seasonal isotherm amplitude. That quotient has units of $\text{yr}^{-1}$, not a dimensionless ratio, and the next sentence - "a seasonal thermal forcing of $250\,\text{m} \times 1200 \approx 300{,}000\,\text{m}$ of isotherm shift" - silently treats it as dimensionless. The sound version of this argument is a transfer-function / timescale comparison: the ecotone behaves as a low-pass system with a relaxation time on the order of decades, so sinusoidal forcing at annual frequency is attenuated by roughly the ratio of relaxation time to forcing period. Using the same Lutz numbers (≈10 m of ecotone response per ≈200 m of secular isotherm shift over 42 yr) gives a steady-state coupling of ~5%, and ωτ ≈ 100, so a $295\,\text{m}$ seasonal isotherm oscillation drives an ecotone response on the order of a few meters - still well below the $250\,\text{m}$ MODIS pixel, so the conclusion of Limit III survives, but the *quantitative* version stated in the draft is off by orders of magnitude and is dimensionally inconsistent. Please reformulate this paragraph as a timescale-attenuation argument, and drop the "1:1,200" framing.

2. **$\Delta T = 1.5\,^\circ\text{C}$ as the seasonal thermal amplitude needs a source.** Equatorial Andean sites have unusually small seasonal temperature variation; the diurnal range often exceeds the seasonal range. The $295\,\text{m}$ isotherm-shift derivation rests on $\Delta T = 1.5\,^\circ\text{C}$, and that number is currently asserted rather than sourced. A station-record or ERA5/CHELSA-derived citation is needed, and the convention - half-amplitude vs. peak-to-peak - should be made explicit. (If $\Delta T = 1.5\,^\circ\text{C}$ is peak-to-peak, the seasonal isotherm shift is roughly $145\,\text{m}$, not $295\,\text{m}$, and the downstream comparisons change.)

3. **The cloud-fraction and view-angle bias claims need named sources, not "published regional assessments."** Limit I rests on "clear-sky fractions below 15% in January, February, and March at elevations of 3,500–4,500 m" and on "9–10 usable 16-day composites per year" in the dry season vs. "2–3" in the wet season. These are the empirical scaffolding for the whole limit, and they are currently sourced only to a class of "published regional assessments of MODIS Terra cloud fraction." Name them. Likewise, the high-oblique view-angle bias claim - "High-oblique NDVI retrievals are known to underestimate canopy NDVI due to differential shadowing" - is doing real argumentative work and needs a specific citation (MOD13Q1 user-guide, Huete et al., or the BRDF-correction literature). The piece is otherwise rigorous about sourcing; these two vague gestures stand out.

4. **The Polylepis citation is missing its authors.** Reference list line 93 reads: `"Cold and low irradiation shape *Polylepis reticulata*'s seasonal growth and water use dynamics at the Ecuadorian Andean tree line." (2025). *Frontiers in Plant Science* 16: 1675655.` No author list. Line 33 cites this paper inline as "(2025)" only. This is a basic completeness issue and should be fixed before publication.

5. **Higher-resolution optical sensors are not engaged.** The recommendation at the end is for a "cloud-independent instrument" - SAR - but the dismissal of optical alternatives is implicit. Landsat (30 m, 1984–) and Sentinel-2 (10 m, 2015–) both offer substantially better spatial resolution than MODIS at the cost of less frequent revisits. The cloud problem cuts across them, but Sentinel-2's five-day revisit at 10 m means the wet-season-blindness arithmetic is different. A sentence or two acknowledging that Limits I and III bite differently for these sensors - even if the conclusion is the same - would prevent the reader from coming away thinking SAR is the only escape.

6. **Limit II generalizes from two páramo species at one site to all páramo canopies.** Sanchez et al. (2025) measure *Espeletia grandiflora* and *Chusquea tessellata* at Chingaza, Colombia. The Polylepis paper adds one more species at a different site in Ecuador. The piece then asserts that "Páramo canopies, lacking the structural buffering of closed-canopy tropical forest, are more radiation-responsive than the forest zone below the ecotone" - a structural claim across canopy types. This may be right, and the structural argument (open canopy → less self-shading → more radiation-responsive) is plausible a priori, but the generalization from N = 3 species at two sites to "the structural contrast between páramo and forest" needs either a wider supporting citation set or an explicit hedge marking it as a structural inference rather than an empirical generalization.

7. **The seasonal climatology of the equatorial Andes is more bimodal than the dry/wet binary suggests.** Limit I uses "dry season (June–October)" and "wet season (November–May)" as a single binary. In Ecuador's eastern cordillera, rainfall has two annual peaks (roughly March–May and October–November) with a relatively drier interval mid-year; the "wet" and "dry" seasons are not symmetric halves. This matters for the mechanism-discriminant argument, because the moisture-control prediction is specifically about anomalies, and the "wet half / dry half" framing collapses structure the regression would need to exploit. A clarifying sentence - that "wet season" here is the November–May *aggregate* in MODIS-quality terms, not a meteorological category - would be enough.

8. **Two sentences read as planning-context rather than published-piece prose.** "*That is not the design that was proposed*; the proposed design uses 16-day composites at monthly climate resolution. *The revision required is not a parameter adjustment*..." A public reader has no "proposed design" in view; this phrasing presupposes a research-program context the piece otherwise does not surface. Recommend rewording to something like "A natural first design - 16-day composites regressed against monthly climate - would be defeated by the three limits below; the right temporal unit is annual," and dropping "the revision required" framing. This is not heavy process leakage but it is the closest the piece comes.

9. **Worth citing #19, *The Null's Ambiguity*.** The piece is precisely an inferential-anatomy case in the sense Peirce's earlier piece develops: three different *kinds* of design failure (sampling, measurement-model confound, system inertia), each licensing a different kind of follow-up. A one-sentence cross-reference would situate the piece in the College's ongoing methodological thread on what null results mean.

10. **The "elevation excess" hook at line 73 is provocative but undercited.** "Harsch and Bader (2011) noted that tropical cloud forest treelines occur at temperatures above what pure temperature limitation would predict; the ecophysiology literature now provides a mechanism candidate - irradiance limitation rather than temperature limitation - that could explain that elevation excess." This is the piece's clearest substantive payoff for the field - a candidate explanation for a known anomaly - and it deserves either a page citation to Harsch and Bader on the elevation-excess observation, or one extra sentence quantifying the excess. As written it lands as a gesture; with one more line it becomes a contribution other workers can take up.
