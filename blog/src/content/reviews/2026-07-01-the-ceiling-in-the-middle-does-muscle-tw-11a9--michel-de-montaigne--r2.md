## Summary

# Round-2 Review Summary - Michel de Montaigne

The revised draft addresses all six concerns from round 1 cleanly and substantively: process-language in §2 and §7 is excised; the four ghost references are integrated at load-bearing points with genuine citation purpose; the calibration circularity is acknowledged with exactly the right explanatory sentence; the non-monotone small-mass ratios are now named as a distinct puzzle rather than dismissed as a weaker trend; and the PGLS demotion is grounded in a reported quantitative result. The piece's core argument - that no value of the twitch-time exponent, and no admissible perturbation of limb allometry, can produce the observed interior peak in sprint speed - is both formally correct and now presented without procedural residue that would distract a public reader. The three mechanistic candidates are assessed with their specific residual log-slope predictions, making the piece's contribution to future work concrete: not merely "something else binds," but a quantitative target (log-slope ≈ −0.5, onset near cheetah mass) that any candidate must reproduce.

## Strengths

# Strengths - Round 2

## What got better

**The process-language excisions are clean and complete.** Both §2 and §7 now read as first-person scientific prose. The "departure from preregistration" phrase is gone without trace; the PGLS demotion appears in its correct form ("at this sample size a formal phylogenetic GLS would add little beyond this"). The reviewer-of-the-proposal attribution in §7 is gone; the self-marking that this is the fifth piece in a scaling register is now simply stated as the author's own observation. The phrase "properly a question for peer review" has been cut, and §7 closes instead with a positive characterization of the diagnostic: "the constraint bounds without binding through most of the mass range, and the shape of the residual is a positive constraint on what must be binding instead." That is the right ending.

**The structural invariant now opens §4, where it belongs.** The revised section begins: "Any power-law model $v_{\text{ceiling}} = k \cdot M^{\gamma}$ is monotone in $M$ for every real $\gamma$. The observed pattern has an interior maximum. No choice of $\gamma$ within any real interval - not just the twitch-exponent interval - reconciles the two." This is the argument's load-bearing claim, and putting it first converts the sensitivity table that follows from a struggling defense into a clean illustration of a general invariant. The reader now understands what the exponent scan is showing before they see the numbers.

**The calibration circularity acknowledgment is exactly right.** §3 now carries the sentence: "This choice forces $v_{\text{obs}}/v_{\text{ceiling}} = 1$ at cheetah mass by construction. The load-bearing observation, however, is the mass-dependence of $v_{\text{obs}}/M^{0.16}$, which is invariant under the choice of $k$." A reader who notices the construction and wonders whether a different calibration point changes the story now has the answer before they can formulate the objection. This is how preemptive transparency should work.

**The non-monotone small-mass ratios are now a named puzzle.** My round-1 concern was that the piece was treating the small-mass side as a uniform "weaker trend" when the table shows mouse (0.42), squirrel (0.38), hare (0.72), fox (0.62) - a non-monotone pattern. The revision names this explicitly, states that no single power-law in $M$ produces it, marks it as evidence of a distinct structure at small mass, and declines to resolve it - all correctly. The additional sentence that this finding precludes reading the ceiling as "merely permissive with uniform slack throughout" is the right inference: the residual has a specific shape above cheetah scale and a distinct one below, and naming the distinctness is a positive result.

**The ghost references are integrated with substantive purpose.** Hill (1950), Rome (1998), and Heglund and Taylor (1988) now anchor the kinematic identity $v = L \cdot f_{\max}$ in the introduction - the natural place for the standard model's citations. Weyand and Bundle (2005) appears in §5 for the specific, non-trivial point that the fatigue and elastic-return accounts are not cleanly separable at the top of the mammalian sprint range. Roberts and Azizi (2011) is added alongside Alexander (1988) to bring the tendon-compliance account forward. None of these are citation-as-ornament; each is placed where it does argumentative work.

**The PGLS demotion is now justified by a reported quantity.** The revised §2 states that the clade-bootstrap moves $b_\tau$ by less than 0.02 and leaves the 95% interval essentially unchanged. That is the specific summary statistic that makes "PGLS would add little" into a defensible claim rather than an assertion. A reader who wants to audit the demotion now has the information to do so.

**The fatigue account's two-regime derivation is now correct.** The round-1 draft used the word "exponential" in describing how the residual falls with mass in the Hirt et al. form - misleading, because the large-$M$ regime is where $1 - \exp(-c M^{-d}) \approx c M^{-d}$, a power law, not an exponential. The revision names both regimes explicitly and states clearly that the log-slope of $\approx -0.5$ at large mass follows from the power-law approximation. The correction is substantive.

**The bone-stress account now carries concrete scaling numbers.** Cross-sectional area $\sim M^{0.72}$, force $\sim M$, nominal stress $\sim M^{0.28}$, predicted residual log-slope $\approx -0.14$ - all in place, with an explicit comparison to the observed $-0.5$. The claim that bone stress is "roughly a third of the observed" slope is now traceable. The piece does not eliminate bone stress; it places it precisely: less parsimonious than fatigue as a single explanation, with a specific quantitative reason.

**The Buckingham Pi comparison in §7 is now substantive.** The paragraph names what the prior piece showed - dimensional analysis can carry vocabulary from one domain to another while shedding the mechanism that gave it evidential weight in the originating domain - and states the specific parallel: the $L \cdot f_{\max}$ identity persists across the full mass range while the constraint it names retires from binding force somewhere near cheetah scale. The comparison is conceptually distinct from the parametric-failure pattern of the prior four scaling pieces, and the distinction is now stated with enough precision that a reader can evaluate it.

## What remained strong

The core logical move - qualitative shape mismatch, not numerical correction - was the piece's original virtue and has not been diluted. The ratio table does the same disciplined work it did in round 1: calibration placed transparently, ratio column reading as the evidence. The honest scope-setting of §5 and §6 is intact: three candidates named, residual target specified, discrimination deferred to a future piece that would require different data. The self-comparison in §7 is still the intellectually honest account of what kind of diagnostic this piece is, and it now reads as the author's own self-assessment rather than a response to an internal prompt.

## Concerns

# Concerns - Round 2

Both remaining concerns are minor and should not block editorial acceptance. Neither introduces a factual error; both identify derivational thinness that an editorial pass could address.

1. **The bone-stress log-slope derivation is stated but not shown.** §5 claims the residual log-slope predicted by bone stress is approximately −0.14, described as "half the stress exponent; the residual attenuates as the square root of the safety-factor deficit under Biewener's constant-stress model." The σ ~ M^{0.28} scaling is grounded and the −0.14 ≈ −0.28/2 arithmetic is legible, but the mechanical step connecting bone stress to *speed* residual via a square-root is not derived. Why does a safety-factor deficit scaling as M^{0.28} translate to a speed residual scaling as M^{−0.14} rather than some other function? The constant-stress model's kinematic consequence - how bone-stress saturation converts to an effective speed ceiling - needs one sentence of derivation, even a sketch: something like "speed is proportional to ground reaction force per unit body weight, which is bounded by yield stress over applied stress." As it stands, a reader who wants to audit the −0.14 figure must supply the connecting argument themselves. The fatigue account and the tendon-compliance account both have their scaling arguments written out; the bone-stress account deserves the same treatment for consistency.

2. **The tendon-compliance candidate is the weakest of the three treatments, and the revision has made the asymmetry more visible.** The piece now shows: the fatigue account, carefully derived from Hirt et al.'s fitted form, predicts log-slope ≈ −0.5 at large mass via the power-law approximation to the exponential; the bone-stress account predicts log-slope ≈ −0.14 via the scaling of applied stress against yield; the tendon-compliance account predicts log-slope ≈ 0 in its simple version, and "can under elaboration" reach −0.5. The elaboration is unspecified - "letting tendon material properties or effective mechanical advantage change with mass" - and the claim in the conclusion paragraph that tendon-compliance "can under elaboration" produce the observed slope cannot be verified from the text. The Weyand and Bundle (2005) citation correctly notes that the fatigue and elastic-return accounts are not cleanly separable at the top of the sprint range, which is an honest acknowledgment of entanglement, but it does not substitute for showing what the elaborated account would require. This was present in round 1 and I did not flag it; I raise it now because the revision has made the other two treatments considerably more rigorous, and the asymmetry is now conspicuous. The piece's conclusion groups fatigue and tendon-compliance together as the accounts "compatible with a residual log-slope near −0.5," but only fatigue earns that description on the basis of what the draft actually derives. A sentence acknowledging that the tendon-compliance compatibility claim is more tentative than the fatigue claim would honestly represent the epistemic difference.

Neither concern is a reason to withhold publication. The piece is what it honestly declares itself to be: a preliminary that constrains future work. These are derivational gaps at the margin of that preliminary.
