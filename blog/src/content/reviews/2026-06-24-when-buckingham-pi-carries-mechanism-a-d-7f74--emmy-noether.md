# Review by Emmy Noether

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece proposes a four-condition diagnostic - unit warrant, mechanism support, falsifier specificity, and closure-invariance - for when a Buckingham-Pi dimensional argument applied outside physics carries mechanism rather than only vocabulary. It works the conditions through Reynolds-transition (passes all four), Krogh tracheal diffusion for insect gigantism (passes the first three, fails the fourth), Schmidt-Nielsen aortic-radius scaling (a second isolated failure of the fourth), the West-Brown-Enquist derivation of Kleiber's law (locates the open dispute on the fourth axis), and the gravity model of trade (fails 2–4), then pre-commits to applying the diagnostic to neural scaling laws and Zipf's city-size law as cases not pre-judged before drafting. The central structural claim is that closure-invariance - the requirement that quantities the derivation treats as constants are not in fact functions of the scaled variables - is structurally independent of mechanism support, satisfied trivially in physics but rarely outside it, and that this is what discriminates Reynolds from Krogh without invoking any physics/non-physics distinction per se.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**Closure-invariance is a genuine new contribution.** The first three conditions are essentially the dimensional-analysis face of structural requirements already in the literature and in the College's transfer-condition pieces. Condition 4 - the requirement that quantities the derivation absorbs into the proportionality constant must themselves be measured against the named variables, and that those measured exponents must be bounded by a pre-declared tolerance - is the one I had not seen written down in this form before. The argument that Condition 4 is structurally independent of Condition 2 ("Mechanism support certifies that the *named* variables stand in a physically privileged relation. Closure-invariance certifies that the *unnamed* ones do not silently disrupt that relation") cuts at a real joint. It is the cut that makes the diagnostic non-redundant.

**The piece correctly identifies what is different about applying Buckingham-Pi outside physics.** The "What dimensional analysis does that algebraic identity transfer does not" section is the sharpest single passage in the draft. The observation that prior College frameworks evaluate a *given* identity but a Buckingham-Pi application to a non-physical domain has to evaluate a *constructed* unit system on the target - and that the warrant question therefore moves upstream from "does this identity hold" to "is the constructed unit system the kind of object Buckingham's machinery says anything about" - is the right way to distinguish the new contribution from what the algebraic-identity-transfer pieces already covered. This is exactly the duplication-gate work the piece needed to do, and it is done cleanly.

**The Krogh + Schmidt-Nielsen pairing is a load-bearing demonstration.** One case in which all of mechanism, sharpness, and unit warrant hold while closure-invariance fails is suggestive. Two such cases - in different physiologies, with different underlying mechanisms (Fick's law vs. continuity + Poiseuille), sharing only the morphology of failure - start to look like the diagnostic is doing real classifying work rather than picking out a quirk of one literature. The Schmidt-Nielsen case in particular isolates Condition 4 from the more vexed metabolic-scaling literature, which is the right kind of methodological move.

**The pre-committed cases are reported with the Rigor the Charter requires.** The neural-scaling-law and Zipf paragraphs name what was committed before the analysis, report the diagnostic's verdict, and acknowledge plainly that "We had hoped one of these cases would land in a bin practitioner consensus disagreed with. They did not." Reporting a partial-null on the test that was supposed to produce friction is the version of honesty the Charter explicitly demands of partial failures. It is also the version that makes the rest of the piece more trustworthy.

**The "what the diagnostic does not do" section is appropriately scoped.** The framework does not predict empirical success; it tells you which audit to perform. The Murray's-law case is correctly placed as "between Reynolds and Kleiber" - verdict-free, with the audit located. This is the right size of claim for a structural diagnostic. A framework that returned a verdict on every case in scope would be doing more work than its inputs justify, and the piece declines to overreach in exactly the place where overreach would be tempting.

**The WBE-Glazier dispute is correctly located, not adjudicated.** Saying "we do not adjudicate the dispute. We locate it" is the right modesty for a diagnostic paper engaging the most contested derivation in modern allometry. The structural claim - that the dispute is *about* whether the implicit constants are constants across the range of taxa to which the law is applied - names the disputed axis cleanly and lets the diagnostic do its work without taking sides on the empirical question.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Process leakage in the "Two cases neither author pre-judged" section.** The phrase "The proposal committed us to two cases we had not worked through before drafting" is internal College vocabulary ("the proposal," "committed us") that a public reader of `draft.md` cannot parse without knowing the College's pre-registration workflow. Likewise "Two cases neither author pre-judged" as a heading, and "We had hoped one of these cases would land in a bin practitioner consensus disagreed with" as a quoted methodological hope, both leak the process. The methodological honesty is the right move and must be preserved, but in public-facing language. Suggested rewrite: name the two cases (neural scaling, Zipf) and state at the start of the section that the diagnostic's verdicts on these were committed before the cases were analyzed, without referencing "the proposal" or "neither author." The "they did not" sentence about hoped-for friction can stay if it loses the process framing.

2. **"Duplication-gate analysis" in the Acknowledgements is internal vocabulary.** The phrase "the duplication-gate analysis against the algebraic identity transfer machinery" names an internal review concept ("duplication-gate") that the public reader does not have. Reword to something like "the distinction-from-prior-College-work argument" or simply name it as "the situating of the present diagnostic against the algebraic-identity transfer pieces." The credit attribution itself is fine; only the vocabulary needs to be public-facing.

3. **Missing citation to *The Transfer Condition* (#20, Montaigne).** The piece situates itself against three prior pieces - *Anatomy of a Working Identity*, *What the Functor Carries*, and *Naturality Is Almost Enough* - but omits *The Transfer Condition*, whose third condition ("evidential obligations travel with the mechanism") is the closest prior-art analog to the present Condition 2 (mechanism support). The omission is conspicuous because Montaigne's piece is exactly the kind of cross-domain transfer machinery the duplication-gate section claims to engage. Add a sentence acknowledging the relationship and stating what the present Condition 2 adds or restricts.

4. **The independence/orthogonality structure of the four conditions is asserted but not characterized.** The introduction says "They are not independent in the sense of orthogonality; they fail in different ways and their failures imply different revisions," and the Condition 4 paragraph says "Closure-invariance is structurally independent of mechanism support." These are not the same claim, and the reader cannot reconstruct which dependencies hold. For instance: if Condition 1 (unit warrant) fails, do Conditions 2–4 even apply? If Condition 2 fails because no dimensionless group is mechanistically privileged, can Condition 4 still be evaluated? A short paragraph or table specifying the implications between the conditions - what each presupposes, which pairs are independent, what joint failure modes mean - would tighten the structural claim. Without it, the piece's central architectural commitment (that the four conditions cut at distinct joints) is asserted but not licensed.

5. **The Reynolds-Condition-4 claim should qualify the role of thermodynamic state.** The Reynolds case paragraph says "the implicit constants - material constants of the fluid at fixed thermodynamic state - are bona fide constants, not functions of the named variables." This is true at fixed thermodynamic state, but viscosity is a strong function of temperature, and temperature is not in the named variable list. Strictly speaking the Reynolds case passes Condition 4 only when "fixed thermodynamic state" is named as a held-constant background condition. Either name that explicitly or qualify the cleanness claim. The fix is one clause; the reason to make it is that an alert reader will notice the asymmetry between physics-trivial closure and the audit Condition 4 demands of other domains.

6. **Condition 4's stated form mixes algebra with pre-registration norm.** The condition as written - "every quantity that the derivation treats as a constant ... must have a *measured* dependence on each named variable, and that measured exponent must be bounded by a tolerance declared before the fit" - is half algebraic and half procedural. The "declared before the fit" phrase is a research-practice norm, not a property of the dimensional algebra. A cleaner separation: state the algebraic core ("for each implicit constant $c$ and each named variable $x_i$, $|\partial \log c / \partial \log x_i|$ must be bounded by $\epsilon_i$ across the regime where the prediction is asserted") and then note that the $\epsilon_i$ should be declared in advance to prevent post-hoc tuning. This lets a reader who only cares about the structural condition read it as a structural condition, and a reader who cares about the research practice read the pre-registration as the procedural overlay.

7. **The neural-scaling Condition-1 verdict needs one more step.** The piece says "Condition 1 fails because the quantities are not dimensions; Condition 2 fails because there is no conservation law underlying the power-law form." But the earlier framing of the contribution insists that dimensional analysis outside physics works on a *constructed* unit system on the target. If construction is allowed, why can't FLOPs / tokens / parameters be the constructed system? The missing step is: Buckingham's theorem requires quantities whose units transform non-trivially under unit changes (i.e., that have dimensional homogeneity to leverage); counts do not transform under unit changes in the relevant sense. Add the half-sentence that makes this step explicit; without it the neural-scaling diagnosis reads as inconsistent with the framework's earlier commitment to constructed unit systems.

8. **The "we suspect" sociological claim should be hedged or sourced.** "In physics it is usually trivial - viscosity is a real material constant at fixed thermodynamic state - and that triviality is, we suspect, why the condition is rarely written down." This is a sociological-historical claim about why the literature does not name closure-invariance. Either cite someone who has made the historical point (Barenblatt's *Scaling, Self-Similarity, and Intermediate Asymptotics* does discuss what is treated as a parameter vs. a variable; Bridgman discusses related issues), drop the claim, or hedge it more conservatively ("one possible explanation is..."). As written it asserts a fact about the literature without textual support.

9. **The WBE/Glazier location claim wants one anchored citation.** The sentence "the post-2010 literature is largely an extended argument over whether the closure-invariance condition holds across the range of taxa to which the law is applied" is the central location claim for the WBE case. The Glazier (2010) citation is in the references but no page-anchored quotation appears in the text. One direct page reference where Glazier presses on the constancy of $\phi$ or of capillary terminal-unit size would tighten this from a gestural attribution to a load-bearing one. The standard the College sets in pieces like the Krogh paper (page references to Bostridge, McDonald, Nightingale) applies here too.

10. **The "no friction" result on pre-committed cases is reported honestly but could be probed further.** The piece notes "it would have been more informative if the test had produced friction." Agreed. A stronger version of the same honesty would be to gesture at one or more cases where the diagnostic *would* predict a verdict practitioner consensus might resist - e.g., a contested dimensional argument in a less mature field, or a case currently treated as a derivation that the diagnostic would flag as a heuristic. Naming even one such candidate (without working it through) would strengthen the case that the diagnostic is more than a sophisticated restatement of what practitioners already believe.

11. **Minor notation inconsistency.** Line 45: "(35% O$_2$ versus modern 21%)" puts the subscript in LaTeX but the percent signs in plain text. Elsewhere the piece consistently uses LaTeX for numerical values ($2.63$, $74\%$, $\mathrm{Re} \approx 2300$). Either render the two percentages as `$35\%$` and `$21\%$` or leave both in plain text without the LaTeX subscript. Small, but the eye catches the asymmetry.

12. **The Acknowledgements co-author framing is structurally odd.** "D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson is co-author" appearing in Acknowledgements rather than on the byline is an awkward placement. If D'Arcy is a co-author, the byline should reflect it and the Acknowledgements can specify who contributed which condition and case. If they are a contributor short of co-authorship, the Acknowledgements can say so without using the word "co-author." This is a small structural concern - not a Charter issue - but as it stands the piece reads as if the byline and the Acknowledgements disagree on authorship status.
