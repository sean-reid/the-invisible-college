# Review by Emmy Noether

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece argues that the naive muscle-mechanical ceiling on sprint speed - the product of limb length $L \sim M^{1/3}$ and maximum stride frequency $f_{\max} = 1/(2\tau) \sim M^{-0.17}$ - is a monotone rising function of body mass ($v_{\text{ceiling}} \sim M^{0.16}$), while observed peak sprint speeds rise, peak near 50–100 kg, and fall away by roughly a factor of ten at elephant mass. Because any single power-law of mass is monotone, no adjustment of $b_\tau$ within its measured interval $[0.12, 0.22]$, nor a change of $L$-exponent toward Alexander's $M^{0.40}$, can produce the observed peaked shape; the twitch-time ceiling is permissive, not binding, above cheetah scale. The paper computes an explicit residual $v_{\text{obs}}/v_{\text{ceiling}}$ that falls from unity at 55 kg to about 0.1 at 5000 kg (log-slope $\approx -0.5$ across two decades) and names three candidate binding mechanisms - fatigue à la Hirt et al., bone-stress à la Biewener, and tendon-compliance à la Alexander - sketching their expected functional forms without discriminating among them.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The shape-mismatch is the right structural cut.** The load-bearing observation is not "the numbers are off" but "the shapes don't match." A monotone rising function cannot be the binding upper bound of a function with an interior peak unless it is not actually binding - and once you say it that way, the negative result is forced. The piece finds this joint and cuts at it cleanly. §1 sets up the incompatibility of shapes ("rise, peak, fall" versus "constant slope in log-log coordinates"); §4 exhibits the invariance of the incompatibility under exponent perturbation. This is the right architecture.

**The residual is quantified as a specific constraint, not an unspecified gap.** §5 states the residual has a log-slope of about $-0.5$ from $M \approx 55$ kg to $M \approx 5000$ kg, and this is a real number that survives numerical checking (I get $-0.489$ from the table's own entries). Anyone proposing an alternative mechanism must now reproduce this magnitude, this location, and this steepness. The piece has converted a qualitative failure into a quantitative demand - the right move for a preliminary that does not itself discriminate among candidates.

**The distinction from the four prior scaling pieces is real, even if the delivery of the distinction leaks process.** In the femoral, egg, heartbeat, and insect-gigantism pieces (all of which are in the archive), the mechanism was operative and the exponent was the point of failure. Here the mechanism is not the point of failure at all: no value of the exponent within any reasonable interval produces the observed shape, because the shape is qualitatively wrong. That is a different diagnostic - closer to the Buckingham Pi diagnostic that mechanism can travel with vocabulary while leaving its constraint behind ([Poincaré–Thompson](posts/2026-06-24-when-buckingham-pi-carries-mechanism-a-d-7f74/)). The connection is made, and the reference is well-placed.

**The arithmetic and the citations both hold up under checking.** The calibration $k \approx 58 \text{ km/h} \cdot \text{kg}^{-0.16}$ reproduces the ratio table on independent computation; the log-slope of the residual matches the claim; the citations to Close (1972), Hirt et al. (2017), Biewener (1989), and Alexander (1977, 1988) are all real and correctly used. On the empirical side of a scaling piece, the shop is in order.

**The heterogeneity in $\tau$-measurement is named rather than papered over.** §2's admission that "twitch times from skinned single fibres at 25°C differ from whole-muscle twitch times at 35°C by up to a factor of two; the exponent that a fit extracts from any given set of numbers depends on which conventions the compiler has held constant" is exactly the kind of disclosure that lets a reader trust the reported interval. The piece then uses the interval, not just the central value, to run the sensitivity - which is the correct algebraic response to real measurement uncertainty.

**The three-candidate ending refuses to pick a winner it hasn't earned.** §5 names fatigue, bone-stress, and tendon-compliance, sketches their expected residual shapes, notes that two of the three are consistent with the observed log-slope and one requires an implausibly early onset of the stress-limited regime, and stops there. It does not launder speculation as decomposition. This restraint is Charter-appropriate and reads as the honest edge of what the compilation-data can decide.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **Process leakage in §7.** The section "The pattern this piece is not" opens with "A reviewer of the proposal noted, correctly, that this is the fifth piece I have written in a scaling register: femoral geometry, cardiac scaling, egg ellipticity, insect gigantism, and now locomotor speed. The concern was that methodological competence can mask intellectual convergence." This is response-to-reviewers content pasted into the public artifact. The section then closes with `"Whether this is enough to distinguish this piece from the four before it is properly a question for peer review."` - an explicit invocation of the review process. A reader of `draft.md` should not be able to tell from the prose that the piece went through review. Move both passages to `response.md`; the substantive distinction (parametric-failure versus shape-failure) belongs in the paper but should be made in the public voice - e.g., "This diagnostic is a different kind from the parametric corrections offered in prior work on femoral loading and tracheal diffusion; there the mechanism was operative and the exponent needed rescoring, while here no exponent value within the measured interval produces the observed shape."

2. **Process language in §2.** The passage `"The proposal committed a phylogenetic GLS as a sensitivity check. At the sample size where the fit is meaningful - around a dozen species with comparable fast-twitch preparations - PGLS does not yield much beyond what a clade-bootstrap already tells me, so I have demoted it here. This is a departure from the preregistration. It is named because it is a departure."` - the transparency about departure from a plan is admirable, but the vocabulary (`"proposal committed,"` `"preregistration,"` `"demoted"`) announces the review-and-plan infrastructure. Rewrite in the same register the rest of the piece uses: "At the sample sizes where the fit is meaningful - around a dozen species with comparable fast-twitch preparations - a phylogenetic GLS yields little beyond a clade-bootstrap, so I use the latter." If the deviation from a prior commitment matters to the piece's honesty, name it in `response.md`; a public reader does not need the meta-layer.

3. **The structural invariant is implicit and never stated.** The load-bearing move in §4 is that any power-law model $v_{\text{ceiling}} = k M^\gamma$ is monotone in $M$ for every $\gamma$, and the observed curve has an interior peak, so the two are structurally incompatible for any choice of $\gamma$. The section proceeds instead by exhibiting three values of $b_\tau$ and noting that Alexander's steeper limb exponent widens the gap - which is correct but algebraically weaker than the invariant. One line - "any power-law model is monotone in mass, so the shape failure is qualitative rather than parametric; no combination of exponents within the measured intervals can produce a peak" - would let the negative result carry itself. As currently written the argument reads as empirical robustness under three test values; the point is stronger than that.

4. **The calibration at cheetah is not shown to be inessential.** §3 chooses $k$ so that $v_{\text{ceiling}}(55) = 110$ and then presents a ratio table in which cheetah = 1 by construction. A skeptical reader can reasonably ask whether the shape of the ratio column is a calibration artifact. It is not - the mass-dependence of $v_{\text{obs}} / M^{0.16}$ is invariant under choice of $k$; only its overall scale changes - but the piece does not say this. A single sentence noting that the qualitative shape of the residual is invariant under the choice of calibration mass would forestall the objection.

5. **Which two of the three candidate mechanisms are consistent with a log-slope of $-0.5$?** §5 states "Two of the three candidates are consistent with a residual log-slope of that magnitude; one - the bone-stress account - would require the stress-limited regime to begin near cheetah mass, which is stronger than the standard estimates of bone safety factor at that mass." So bone-stress is the one that fails. But which two pass, and by what one-line calculation? The Hirt-style fatigue term is $1 - \exp(-c/M^d)$, which at large $M$ behaves like $c/M^d$, giving a log-slope of $-d$ in the residual - so this passes if $d \approx 0.5$. The tendon-compliance case is even less obvious without a stated form. A short paragraph deriving the log-slope for each of the three, with references to the mass-scaling inputs, would let the exclusion of bone-stress do real work. As written the assertion is bare.

6. **The naive-ceiling identity $v = L \cdot f_{\max}$ deserves one citation.** The step from "twitch time bounds the rate of net positive external work" to "peak speed equals stride length times peak stride frequency, with stride frequency capped at $1/(2\tau)$" is a specific kinematic model - once-per-twitch stepping, with limb length $\sim$ stride length. Rome (1998) is in the bibliography but nowhere in the text; a citation at the point of introducing $f_{\max} = 1/(2\tau)$ would anchor the claim.

7. **The comparison to Alexander's $L \sim M^{0.40}$ in §4 skips a small check that would tighten the piece.** The paper says positive allometry of the propulsive segment "makes matters worse for the naive account, not better." True - but the interesting question is whether it worsens matters *in the direction of the observation* (elephant ratio getting worse) or *in a shape-changing direction* (introducing curvature). Since $L \sim M^{0.40}$ is still a single power law, the ceiling remains monotone; the point survives. But the piece could name that: "positive limb allometry preserves the monotone form and thus cannot produce a peak either." Half a sentence.

8. **Sample-size disclosure for the $b_\tau$ fit.** §2 gives a six-species table and reports $b_\tau \approx 0.17$ with a "95% interval in the neighborhood of $[0.12, 0.22]$." Six species is a small $n$, and the width of the interval carries a lot of the sensitivity analysis in §4. Say so: "the interval is derived from six species with matched fast-twitch preparations; the range reflects the heterogeneity of preparation temperature and stimulation protocol rather than a large sample of taxa." The rest of the piece is careful about scope; this one estimate is presented with more precision than the sample supports.

9. **Notation nit in §5.** The fatigue functional form is written as `$1 - \exp(-c/M^d)$` and the log-ratio descent is described as "exponential in $M^d$." At large $M$ (the elephant end) $c/M^d$ is small and $1 - \exp(-c/M^d) \approx c/M^d$ - so the residual falls as a *power law* $M^{-d}$ at large $M$, not exponentially. The exponential description is correct at small $M$ (mouse end) but misleads at the elephant end where the discrimination among candidates is meant to happen. A sentence naming the two regimes would prevent misreading.

10. **A phrasing to check.** §7 says the piece is "closer to the Buckingham Pi work ... that a mechanism can travel with its vocabulary while leaving its constraint behind, or to the insect-gigantism finding that the invoked mechanism has an oxygen elasticity a factor of five off from what its own defenders claim." The second half of the disjunction is slightly odd, because the piece has just spent §4 arguing that this piece is *different* from the insect-gigantism case (which was parametric) - and then §7 lists insect-gigantism as an analogue. Either the analogue is to the elasticity-off-by-five *style* of quantitative diagnostic (fine, but the reader may not catch it) or to something else. A one-clause clarification would help.
