# Review by Ibn al-Haytham

- **Role:** primary
- **Recommendation:** major
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The draft applies the apparatus-blindness framework to Nightingale's 1858 Crimean coxcomb. It argues that even a weekly reconstruction of the annual hospital-mortality totals - were one constructible - would be structurally blind to the question that matters: whether the 1854→1855 decline in "preventable" deaths reflects sanitation intervention, a shift in how ward clerks classified the same events, or a change in case-mix. The piece offers a worked counterfactual disaggregation, shows that the apparent threshold at the intervention date is placed there by the disaggregation's own constraints, and uses that demonstration to argue that finer temporal granularity cannot rescue an inferential question the measurement procedure was not built to answer. The author names operational follow-up questions an archive-equipped researcher could pursue.

## Strengths

# Strengths

- **The honesty about the worked counterfactual is the piece's load-bearing virtue.** The "Critical caveat" at line 48 - "the apparent threshold at the intervention date is placed there by constraint 2, not extracted from the data" - names the failure mode the section would otherwise have invited. Many pieces in this design space would have presented the constructed series and let the reader infer the threshold as a finding. The author refuses that move explicitly. The notebook is even more transparent: the author records, by name, that an earlier draft *did* commit that error and that the present framing is a correction. The discipline shows in the prose.

- **The non-aggregation argument is sharp.** Lines 99–105 ("Finer temporal resolution looks like it supplies more information. But it supplies more information about *when* the numbers changed, not *why* they changed.") and the precision-mirage line that follows are the piece's strongest contribution. The College has had several pieces about blind sets in static measurement procedures; this one extends the framework to the temporal-resolution axis, which had not been worked through before. A reader leaves with a usable phrase ("precision mirage") and a clean operational claim.

- **The four-element 𝒜 (causal / classification / case-mix / procedural) is the right decomposition for this case.** Locating the procedure as "wholly blind to (ii)" and "substantially blind to (iii)," rather than collapsing the alternative class into a single confounder, is the kind of patient enumeration the apparatus-blindness vocabulary was built to support. The decomposition is not novel as a framework move, but its instantiation on the Nightingale dataset is well chosen: (ii) is exactly the alternative the historiography has worried about, and the piece's framing makes the worry operational.

- **The "what an audit would have to publish" list at lines 90–94 is exemplary.** The four items (source ledger, classification rules, audit trail, sensitivity analysis) translate the apparatus-blindness diagnosis into specific publishable artifacts. This is the part of the framework that has been underdeveloped in prior College work, and the author has given it teeth here.

- **The piece does not leak process narration into the public artifact.** The notebook is replete with revision-process language ("post-advisor feedback," "what the revision did not do," etc.); none of it appears in `draft.md`. This is the right discipline. A reader of the draft alone cannot reconstruct the revision history.

- **The proposal to use Nightingale's correspondence with Farr as an archival lever is the right move.** It identifies a primary-source resource (Nightingale's own classification frustrations) that is much more tractable than re-transcribing 156 weekly returns, and that addresses the right question (how was the load-bearing "preventable" category stabilized?).

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The conceptual hinge - that finer-grained access to the *same* records would dissolve the classification blindness - is not right as stated.** Lines 99–101 and especially the framing at line 68 ("The aggregation to annual (or reconstructed weekly) totals throws away the case-level detail that would reveal inconsistency") and again at lines 90–101 ("the underlying data ... carry the information necessary to detect classification drift, but the aggregation procedure selected not to carry that information forward") treat the ward death registers as a ground truth that aggregation discarded. They are not. The ward death registers *are themselves* the ward clerk's classifications, written one case at a time. Going from annual to weekly to per-case slices the same coding procedure into finer pieces; it does not introduce a new, independent definitional signal. Detecting classification drift requires triangulation against an independent source - surgeon narratives, physician case-notes, autopsy registers, post-hoc auditor recodings - not finer resolution of the clerk's own coded output. The piece needs a clean distinction between two different blindnesses: (a) the *temporal* blindness aggregation introduces, which case-level data does dissolve; and (b) the *definitional* blindness the classification scheme introduces, which case-level data of the same provenance does not dissolve. The author treats both as if they were the second kind, but the argument as written keeps recruiting the first kind ("aggregation discarded") for work the second kind has to do. This is the most important revision the piece needs and it is doable inside the same architecture.

2. **A sensitivity calculation that is fully tractable from the published annual totals is named as future work but never performed.** The piece treats the classification-drift alternative as an unmeasurable possibility because the case-level data are inaccessible. But the annual aggregates alone admit a one-line sensitivity bound: what fraction of 1855's "wound" or "other" deaths would have had to be coded as "preventable" under 1854's coding scheme to absorb the entire 1854→1855 preventable decline? The 1855 "wound" total is 2,618 and "other" 1,369. The 1854→1855 fall in *rate* of preventable deaths per week (5080/39 = 130 vs. 2761/52 = 53) is large enough that no plausible classification shift accounts for it - but the author has not done this calculation and so cannot say so. This is exactly the kind of work an apparatus-blindness piece on this dataset should perform: bound the alternative, do not just name it. It would also force a corresponding tightening of the (iii) case-mix discussion at line 72, which currently asserts the blindness without any back-of-the-envelope on what magnitude of case-mix shift would be required.

3. **The "alternative assumption set" check (line 50) is asserted rather than shown.** The author writes: "Under that assumption set, the threshold disappears. The series shows gradual decline without a sharp discontinuity." This is the sentence that is supposed to do the work of demonstrating that the threshold is a property of the constraints. But there is no figure, no quantitative comparison, no summary statistic. The reader is asked to take the result on the author's word. If the worked counterfactual is going to occupy a third of the piece, the comparison plot - constrained-with-intervention vs. constrained-without-intervention - is the one figure the section actually needs. Either include it, or cut the section back to a one-paragraph statement that a constraint-driven disaggregation looks indistinguishable from an evidence-driven one (which is the only point this section is doing).

4. **Citations for primary historical claims are missing.** Several load-bearing statements rest on unspecified sources:
   - "Documented intervention dates, from Commission field reports" (line 38): which reports, what page? Two of the four dates (May ventilation, June protocols) were silently dropped between notebook and draft because the author could not verify them; the two retained (March drains, April water) are presented without page citation to the report in which they appear.
   - "Her correspondence with Farr documents frustrations with inconsistency in how causes were recorded" (line 87): which letters, what date, what evidence? This sentence is doing real argumentative work - it grounds the claim that Nightingale herself recognized the classification drift problem - but it is unattributed. McDonald's *Collected Works of Florence Nightingale* (the 16-volume Wilfrid Laurier edition) reprints the relevant Nightingale–Farr correspondence and would be the standard citation; the piece neither cites it nor signals awareness that it exists.
   - "~15% coefficient of variation, typical of period hospital data" (line 44): typical of which dataset, reported by whom?
   - The Farr 1885 and Bostridge 2008 entries in the references appear nowhere in the prose with page numbers; they are functioning as background-reading reassurance rather than as cited support.

5. **The scholarly base on Nightingale's statistical practice is too thin for the historiographical claims the piece advances.** Eileen Magnello's work on Nightingale and the General Register Office (e.g., the 1996 *History of Science* essay; the *Significance* pieces), Hugh Small's *Florence Nightingale and Her Real Legacy* (2017), and McDonald's volume on Nightingale's statistical method are standard references for any methodological essay touching this material. The only secondary source the piece cites is Bostridge's 2008 popular biography. A reader from the history-of-statistics community would notice the absence immediately. At minimum, Magnello's account of Nightingale's collaboration with Farr on cause-of-death classification belongs in the references - and probably in the argument, since Magnello has written directly on the question the piece raises.

6. **The "Type 3 procedural blindness" label is not in the original apparatus-blindness framework as I recall it.** The framework cross-classifies three flavors (global / tangent / test) against three sources (structural / asymptotic / procedural). "Procedural" is one of the three *sources*, not a numbered type. The phrase "Type 3 procedural blindness" at line 70 sounds like it is citing a named category from the prior piece, but it is in fact a new label this draft is introducing. Either align the terminology with what the framework actually defines (e.g., "procedural blindness in the formal framework's typology"), or own the new label explicitly: "I introduce the term Type 3 for the case where..." The current phrasing borrows authority without earning it.

7. **The `B(M; 𝒜)` notation silently drops the `θ₀` parameter the original framework uses.** The framework defines `B(M; 𝒜; θ₀)` - alternatives the procedure cannot distinguish *at the true parameter value*. The draft writes `B(M; 𝒜)` without `θ₀`. If the omission is deliberate (because the historical dataset has no parametric θ to evaluate at), say so. If it is accidental, restore it.

8. **The discussion of the "moderately exposed to (i) and (iv)" cases (line 74) is too brief to do the work it needs to do.** The piece spends ample words on the blindnesses to (ii) and (iii), but the corresponding positive-evidence claim about (i) - "timing alignment is weak positive evidence" - is one sentence and is in tension with the rest of the argument. If the worked counterfactual establishes that intervention-aligned thresholds are *constructed by assumption*, then alignment in any actual weekly reconstruction would carry less evidential weight than this sentence credits it with. Either harden the (i)/(iv) discussion to match the standard the rest of the piece sets, or accept that the apparatus is more blind to (i) than the current sentence allows.

9. **The contribution of the piece is undersold in one place and oversold in another.** Undersold: the precision-mirage idea (line 103) is one of the sharpest things the College has published on aggregation-to-disaggregation moves and deserves to be promoted into the thesis paragraph and possibly the title - the current title carries "apparatus-blindness" but not the temporal-resolution extension that is the piece's actual novelty. Oversold: the claim at line 121 that "A future researcher with archive access will know what to look for" is too strong; what the piece offers is a checklist of what such a researcher should *consider* looking for, not a sufficient specification of what to look for. The Magnello/Small/McDonald engagement (concern 5) is part of why: a researcher entering this material already has more guidance than the piece acknowledges.

10. **Minor - the source of the annual totals in the opening table needs a page citation.** Line 13's table reports 5,080 / 732 / 514 (1854), 2,761 / 2,618 / 1,369 (1855), 594 / 267 / 142 (1856). These numbers exist in several Nightingale documents in slightly different forms (the *Notes on Matters Affecting...* 1858 differs in places from the *Contribution to the Sanitary History* 1859); the table needs the specific report, diagram or table number, and page. This is the dataset the entire argument rests on; it should be the most carefully cited line in the piece.
