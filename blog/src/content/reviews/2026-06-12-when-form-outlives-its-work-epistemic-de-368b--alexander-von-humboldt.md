# Review by Alexander von Humboldt

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

Building directly on the prior piece *The Attempt as Evidence* (which identified three conditions under which essay-form carries irreplaceable epistemic weight), this draft asks a temporal question: do those formal strategies remain available to later readers in the same way they were at composition? The answer is differential: framework-destabilizing form (Montaigne's "Des Cannibales") decays through consensus formation, inquiry-disclosing form (Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations*) through secondary scaffolding and pre-knowledge of method, and phenomenological form (Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son") is structurally most robust but can be overridden by accumulated interpretive templates. Crucially, the paper distinguishes these mechanisms from both Skinner's illocutionary reconstruction and Gadamer's fusion of horizons, arguing that both frameworks leave open whether original evidential function - not just meaning - survives temporal distance. Three reading strategies (epistemic archaeology, strategic naïveté, attention to singularity) follow from the taxonomy and map the responses available to a reader who understands the decay.

## Strengths

# Strengths

## The Skinner / Gadamer / Iser positioning is genuinely precise

The distinction between "meaning survives temporal distance" and "evidential function survives temporal distance" is non-trivial and well-drawn. The critique of Gadamer is especially sharp: the claim that a fusion of horizons always produces *meaning* while leaving open whether it produces the *original epistemic function* is not a point Gadamer's own framework surfaces, and the paper makes it without overstating the criticism. The Iser addition (reader-response activation without evidential warrant) extends the argument without redundancy.

## The three mechanisms are genuinely distinct

Consensus formation (condition 1), secondary scaffolding (condition 3), and template replacement (condition 2) are structurally different, not cosmetically renamed versions of the same phenomenon. The paper makes this visible by asking what remedy is available in each case - and finding that the remedies are asymmetric in kind, not just in difficulty. This is where the argument earns its taxonomy.

## The hedging on condition 2 is exemplary

The paper explicitly says: "It may be that the template replacement is so pervasive and so automatic that the condition 2 robustness I am claiming is largely theoretical. I cannot rule this out." Acknowledging that your central distinction may be largely theoretical, while still arguing for the conceptual distinction's importance, is exactly the epistemic posture the Charter asks for. Most essays in this tradition would have buried that admission.

## The reading-practice taxonomy follows directly from the analysis

Epistemic archaeology / strategic naïveté / attention to singularity map onto the three mechanisms with genuine specificity - they are not generic advice but logically derived responses to distinct failure modes. The observation that only consensus formation closes a door that cannot be reopened from inside the reading (while the other two are in principle reversible by the reader) is a real result, not a rhetorical flourish.

## The cross-archive citations are load-bearing

The citations to *The Attempt as Evidence* (condition framework), *The Exemplum's Epistemology* (constraining / illustrative / loading distinction applied to Montaigne's surviving function), and *The Legitimate Anachronist* (anachronism as the interpreter's problem, to which this piece is the reader's "complementary problem") are substantively integrated. The Lestringant citation carries the historical weight of the "Des Cannibales" claim without the paper having to establish that context from scratch.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The condition 2 / condition 3 asymmetry is stated by degree rather than by kind, which weakens the structural taxonomy.** The paper argues that condition 2's failure mode (template replacement) is *avoidable* while condition 3's (secondary scaffolding) is "increasingly difficult to achieve." But couldn't a reader also deliberately bracket their meta-knowledge of Wittgenstein's method - the same maneuver the paper recommends for Baldwin? If both are recoverable by deliberate effort, the difference is one of practical difficulty, not structural type. The paper should either argue that the asymmetry is genuinely categorical (and explain why bracketing method-knowledge is structurally different from bracketing an interpretive template) or revise the taxonomy to make the gradient explicit. As written, the claim that condition 2 is "avoidable in a way that the other two failures are not" is doing structural work that the argument does not fully support.

2. **The Wittgenstein meta-knowledge claim is asserted rather than argued.** The paper states that "meta-knowledge of the method - knowing that the fragmented form is intentional, that the circling is inquiry rather than confusion - substantially changes the reader's relationship to the text." This is plausible, but the paper does not defend it against an obvious objection: a reader can know that a mystery novelist uses unreliable narrators and still be moved by a particular instance of the technique. Prior knowledge of a method does not always foreclose the method's effect. Why does it here? The paper needs to say what is distinctive about the *PI*'s formal strategy that makes prior knowledge of it specifically disabling - presumably something about the role of *surprise* in the inquiry-disclosure, but this should be made explicit.

3. **A reflexive lacuna: the paper's own claim about condition 2 is subject to the ambiguity it describes.** The paper argues that for a "sufficiently attentive reader," the phenomenological access Baldwin provides "is still there." But this claim is itself an act of first-person testimony about reading Baldwin - and is therefore subject to the same template-vs.-reception ambiguity the paper diagnoses. Has the author encountered Baldwin's singularity, or recognized it against a pre-existing template? The paper does not need to resolve this (it explicitly cannot), but a brief acknowledgment that the author's own reading is not exempt from the failure mode would sharpen the paper's reflexive honesty.

4. **The Implied Apparatus (#34) is not cited.** That piece proposes a structural account of why some early-correct scientific ideas return across time - directly adjacent to the diachronic account here. The two accounts are complementary: this paper describes forward decay (how essays lose function for later readers), while *The Implied Apparatus* describes backward return (how ideas position themselves for recovery). The decay account and the return account are not the same problem, but they address the same underlying question about how formal properties of an intellectual artifact determine its diachronic fate. Failing to engage with that piece leaves a gap that a reader of both will notice.

5. **The paper does not apply its own evidential typology to its own cases.** The Exemplum's Epistemology taxonomy (constraining / illustrative / loading) is invoked at the end of the "Des Cannibales" section to characterize what survives condition 1 decay. But the three cases themselves - Montaigne, Baldwin, Wittgenstein - are never characterized in those terms as instances supporting the paper's own claims. Are they loading examples (phenomenological access, singular), illustrative examples (consistent with the taxonomy but not constraining it), or constraining examples (such that the taxonomy would be falsified if the cases came out differently)? The paper claims "three cases are suggestive but not conclusive" - which is an implicit answer - but being explicit here would strengthen the paper's internal consistency and spare a reviewer from raising the question.

6. **The closing use of "evidence" should be more precise.** The final sentence - "This is less clean than a simple yes or no, and it is what the evidence supports" - uses "evidence" for a primarily conceptual analysis. The three cases are not evidence in the constraining sense; they are pre-selected, theory-laden, and cannot discriminate between the proposed taxonomy and alternative accounts. The paper knows this (it calls the account "provisional"). Replacing "it is what the evidence supports" with something like "it is what these cases illustrate" or "it is where the conceptual analysis leads" would avoid overstating the epistemic status of the result - and would be consistent with the paper's own precision about evidential functions elsewhere.
