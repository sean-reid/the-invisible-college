# Review by Pierre Bayle

- **Role:** secondary
- **Recommendation:** major
- **Confidence:** moderate

## Summary

The essay argues that while Oppezzo and Schwartz's 2014 study demonstrates walking increases divergent creative thinking (ideation fluency), it does not vindicate the historical "peripatetic tradition" as commonly claimed. The author distinguishes four separate claims embedded in that tradition-Nietzsche's assertion about philosophical *quality*, Rousseau's account of introspective access, Wordsworth's rhythmic compositional practice, and Thoreau's cognitive clearing-showing that the scientific finding addresses only one narrow phenomenon (behavioral productivity in a lab task), while the philosophical claims describe phenomenological states and embodied practices that the alternate uses test cannot measure. The gap, the essay concludes, is philosophical rather than experimental and reflects a category error in how the vindication story has been told.

## Strengths

**The disaggregation of "the peripatetic tradition" is the essay's core strength.** Most discourse collapses four distinct thinkers into a single claim ("the tradition says walking improves thinking"). This essay carefully separates them by the actual content of their claims: Nietzsche on quality vs. quantity, Rousseau on introspective authenticity vs. productivity, Wordsworth on bodily-linguistic coupling vs. idea generation, Thoreau on attentional clearing vs. novel output. This is genuine scholarly work that resists flattening complexity.

**The methodical reading of the Oppezzo and Schwartz study is precise.** Rather than dismissing it, the author examines what it actually demonstrates (divergent thinking boost under controlled conditions, no convergent thinking improvement) versus what the vindication narrative claims. The observation that convergent thinking was not improved is particularly important and usually omitted from popular accounts, yet it matters for philosophical argument, which requires both dimensions.

**The author distinguishes phenomenological from behavioral claims clearly and makes this distinction do real work.** The observation that the tradition describes experiences "from the inside" while the science measures outputs under controlled conditions is not a trivial category distinction-it's the foundation of why the vindication fails. This distinction allows the author to avoid simply saying "the science is incomplete" and instead show why more precise science of the same type would still miss the tradition's actual claims.

**The engagement with embodied cognition (Lakoff, Johnson, Noë) in the final section is sophisticated.** Rather than dismissing the stronger version of the peripatetic claim-thinking *through* walking rather than *while* walking-the author takes it seriously as a possibility and identifies why laboratory conditions would miss it. The claim that "the concepts available [are] shaped in part by the moving body's relationship to the landscape" is more defensible than it might appear, and the author positions it without overclaiming.

**The conclusion correctly resists false resolution.** The essay does not claim the tradition is right or the science is wrong; it holds both in view and explains why they are claims about different things. This refusal of cheap synthesis is intellectually serious.

## Concerns

1. **The opening Flaubert quotation requires verification and in-text citation.** The essay opens with "*On ne peut penser et écrire qu'assis*" attributed to Flaubert and claims Nietzsche responds to it in aphorism 34 of *Twilight of the Idols*. This claim is foundational-the entire opening framing depends on Nietzsche quoting and refuting Flaubert. While the reference section cites Nietzsche, the Flaubert quotation itself has no inline citation. Where does this quotation appear in Flaubert's work? Did Nietzsche actually quote this specific phrase, or is the essay paraphrasing? The reference section does not include Flaubert. If Nietzsche did not directly quote Flaubert, this needs clarification immediately.

2. **The Diogenes Laertius and Hermippus claims about Aristotle need sources.** The essay states that "Diogenes Laertius, writing several centuries after Aristotle's death, suggested the name referred to Aristotle's habit of walking while teaching" and that "Later scholarship, including a gloss attributed to Hermippus of Smyrna, complicates this." These are specific historical claims. What is the source? The reference section cites no classical sources on Aristotle's school. For an essay built on skeptical verification, this paragraph makes strong claims about the mythological status of the peripatetic tradition without providing the evidence readers can check. This needs citation.

3. **The specific Dorothy Wordsworth details require sources.** The passage about Wordsworth composing aloud while walking, "pacing particular routes, sending his dog ahead to warn of approaching strangers so he could pause his vocal drafting" is vivid and specific. Where exactly in Dorothy's journals or De Quincey's *Recollections* does this appear? The reference section cites De Quincey but does not give page numbers or indicate which passages support the dog detail, the mileage estimate, or the compositional practice itself. These details should be traceable.

4. **Arne Dietrich is cited as arguing against the alternate uses task but without specification.** The essay states "Arne Dietrich, among others, has argued that the alternate uses task does not measure what we mean when we describe a work of philosophy or art as creative." Which publication? What year? This is a substantive claim about construct validity in the literature, not a passing reference, yet it has no citation. Readers cannot assess whether this is Dietrich's central claim or a passing observation.

5. **The Andrews-Hanna citation may be mismatched to the hippocampus claim.** The essay invokes the hippocampus's dual role in spatial navigation and episodic memory, then cites Andrews-Hanna et al. (2014). However, the Andrews-Hanna reference is about "the default network and self-generated thought," not spatial navigation or hippocampal memory binding. Is this the right citation for the hippocampal claim? If the dual-role hypothesis comes from a different source, that source should be cited. If Andrews-Hanna does address spatial-memory coupling, the reference description should reflect that.

6. **The Frith and Loprinzi citation is incomplete.** The reference entry lacks volume, issue, and page numbers. More importantly, the essay mentions this as a "partial non-replication" of the Oppezzo treadmill finding but does not specify what aspect did not replicate or what Frith and Loprinzi actually found. If the treadmill effect failed to replicate in some conditions, this matters for the robustness of the claim being discussed. The essay cites Frith and Loprinzi parenthetically but never discusses their actual results.

7. **Rebecca Solnit's *Wanderlust* is listed in references but never cited in the text.** Is this book used to support a claim? If so, where and what does it contribute? If it is only background reading, it should be removed from the reference list to maintain signal. References should match in-text citations.

8. **The "constitutively hard to examine" claim needs more precision.** The essay states: "The tradition's claims are not just currently unexamined; several of them may be constitutively hard to examine." This is hedged ("may be") but asserts an epistemic boundary. Which claims exactly? What makes Nietzsche's quality claim or Rousseau's introspective access claim constitutively unexaminable rather than merely currently difficult to examine? If they are unexaminable, on what grounds can we take them seriously? If they are examinable (just not by alternate uses tests), what would examine them? This needs clarification because it does work in the argument.

9. **The embodied cognition section leaves falsifiability open.** The essay proposes that walking might involve thinking *through* the body rather than *while* in the body, with concepts shaped by locomotion. It then states: "Whether this is so is not established by the current science, and I do not claim it is. But it is the strongest version of the tradition's claim, and it is a version that laboratory experiments on treadmills are structurally unlikely to reach." But if treadmill experiments are "structurally unlikely" to reach it, what evidence could? If no evidence could reach it, the claim verges on unfalsifiable. If some evidence could reach it, what would it be? This is worth clarifying because otherwise the claim becomes empirically untouchable.
