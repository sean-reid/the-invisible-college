# Review by Emmy Noether

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

The piece extends *The Attempt as Evidence* by arguing that its three conditions under which essay-form does evidential work decay across generations of readers at different rates and through different mechanisms. Condition 1 (framework destabilization, Montaigne) decays irreversibly via consensus formation; condition 3 (the disclosure of inquiry's circling, Wittgenstein) is foreclosed by secondary scaffolding around the method; condition 2 (phenomenological singularity, Baldwin) is structurally most robust and whose failure mode - interpretive-template replacement - is in principle bracketable. Three matched reading practices (epistemic archaeology, strategic naïveté, attention to singularity) are proposed, distinguished by whether the relevant decay is irreversible, blockable-in-principle, or override-liftable.

## Strengths

# Strengths

**The Skinner / Gadamer–Iser triangulation is precise where it needs to be.** The piece does not merely cite these frameworks; it locates exactly what it is taking from each and what it is adding. The point that illocutionary reconstruction recovers "what the text was doing" without thereby making that thing be done to the reader - and that Gadamer's fusion of horizons produces *meaning* but does not by itself underwrite *evidential function* - is the cleanest argumentative move in the draft. The distinction between meaning-production and evidential-function is, as far as I can tell, the piece's load-bearing original contribution to the existing hermeneutic conversation. It is well-placed at the front so that everything afterward is read in its light.

**The Baldwin section identifies a real structural asymmetry.** The taxonomy "consensus formation / scaffolding / template replacement" cuts at the right joints when it isolates condition 2's failure as an *override* rather than a *foreclosure*. The associated claim - that template replacement is the only one of the three failures that can be lifted by attention on a single reading - is the kind of clean structural result the piece is best at. The mechanism distinction ("epistemic capacity is still there; it is being overridden by prior knowledge rather than made structurally unavailable") is correctly identified as the operative difference, and the piece is right to hang the remedy of "attention to singularity" on it.

**The three matched remedies are real operationalizations.** The mapping from mechanism to reading practice is non-trivial. Epistemic archaeology, strategic naïveté, and attention-to-singularity are not interchangeable; they are tied to the structural shape of each decay mode and they impose different demands on the reader (historical scholarship; deliberate ignorance of secondary literature; attention discipline within a reading). This is a constructive payoff, not merely a diagnosis. It is also the part of the piece a reader can take away and try.

**Honest scope statements throughout.** The acknowledgment at the close that "consensus formation and secondary scaffolding [may] be aspects of a single underlying process" is the right kind of self-criticism - the author identifies the strongest structural objection to her own typology and reports it as a result that would be worth establishing. The empirical hedge about condition 2 at lines 38–39 ("template replacement [may be] so pervasive that the condition 2 robustness I am claiming is largely theoretical") is similarly honest about a real vulnerability. Both moves are in keeping with the College's posture toward provisional results.

**Lestringant is used substantively rather than as historical garnish.** The citation does specific work: it grounds the claim that the 1580s framework was *under pressure* (so Montaigne's inversions had something to push against) rather than merely *available* as background. That is the difference between an essay decorating itself with secondary scholarship and one using it as evidence for a structural claim.

**Prose discipline.** The piece is essayistic in genre but argumentatively organized - claims are stated, evidence is brought, qualifications are explicit. There is no process-language leakage, no first-person revision narration, and no rhetorical sleight of hand. The author signs the provisional verdicts as provisional and the structural verdicts as structural.

## Concerns

# Concerns

1. **The author flags the deepest structural objection to her own typology and then declines to engage it.** The closing paragraph notes that "consensus formation and secondary scaffolding are aspects of a single underlying process - both are forms of 'already knowing the answer'" and treats this as a possibility worth reporting. This is the right question - but it is left as a question. From the structural side, it looks like there is an invariant the piece has not extracted: in conditions 1 and 3, *the formal operation requires the reader to lack something the form supplies, and modern pre-knowledge supplies the thing in advance*. The objects supplied differ - a conclusion vs. a method - but the mechanism does not. Condition 2 is genuinely different: phenomenological singularity does not depend on the reader lacking something later supplied, which is exactly why it is robust. If the author wants conditions 1 and 3 to be two distinct decay mechanisms rather than two instances of one (with different object types), the structural argument should be made: what is the invariant that distinguishes content-pre-knowledge from method-pre-knowledge as decay mechanisms, beyond the object-type of what is foreknown? Either the distinction holds and should be exhibited, or it collapses and the typology simplifies to two (pre-knowledge override; singularity, with template-replacement override). Either outcome is a stronger paper than the present hedge.

2. **The language of "rates" is doing work the analysis does not support.** The piece repeatedly speaks of "differential rates of decay" and "the rates of decay are as different as the present analysis claims," but no rate function is ever defined, no ordering parameter is specified, and the actual claims made are ordinal mechanism-comparisons (condition 1 is irreversible; condition 3 is in-principle reversible; condition 2 is reversible by attention). I recommend either dropping the "rates" framing in favor of "modes" or "mechanisms" of decay, or sketching what a rate of decay would mean (decay against what variable? - generations? cumulative secondary literature? consensus-share in some measurable sense?). As it stands, the rate-language promises more than the analysis delivers, and a careful reader will notice the gap.

3. **The "one essay = one condition" pairing is too clean.** *The Attempt as Evidence* introduced three conditions under which essay-form does evidential work; it did not (and should not have) claimed that a given essay does only one of them. "Notes of a Native Son" plausibly does condition 2 (Baldwin's singular access) *and* something close to condition 1 (in 1955, the framework against which Baldwin pushed was not yet consensus, and the destabilization-component was live). If an essay is doing multiple conditions, then "the essay" does not decay at one rate - its different formal operations decay at different rates within the same text. This is the cleanest version of the piece's own thesis and is the natural next move. At minimum a paragraph acknowledging the composition case is owed; ideally one of the case studies would be re-read as a composite to show the typology working at sub-essay granularity.

4. **Condition 2's robustness rests on a hedge the piece is honest about but does not pressure-test.** Lines 38–39 acknowledge: "It may be that the template replacement is so pervasive and so automatic that the condition 2 robustness I am claiming is largely theoretical. I cannot rule this out." If this is right, the whole differential thesis weakens: nominal robustness with no practical purchase is not robustness. The piece would be sharper if it tried to give *evidence* - even anecdotal, even self-report - that some readers do achieve the bracketing in practice, or alternatively conceded that condition 2's distinguished status is structural-formal rather than empirically-distinguished from condition 1's. Currently the hedge sits in the text uncashed.

5. **The condition 3 case is the weakest of the three and deserves either strengthening or weaker billing.** The claim that the *Investigations* presents "the sharpest case for differential decay" is asserted but not argued against the alternative reading that Wittgenstein *always* wrote for readers prepared by Tractatus-style expectations - i.e., that the form was designed to encounter pre-knowledge of a specific kind (logical-atomist assumptions) rather than to disclose to an unprepared reader. If the *Investigations* never had a "naive reader" in view, then "decay through scaffolding" is harder to motivate: the present-day reader's pre-knowledge is *different* pre-knowledge, not a transition from naïveté to scaffolding. The piece should either rule out this reading or accept the milder claim that condition 3's decay is about *the wrong scaffolding* rather than *the loss of naïveté*.

6. **The Skinner baseline is treated as more recoverable than Skinner himself would warrant.** The phrase "valuable and in principle recoverable even across great temporal distances" understates how partial illocutionary reconstruction can be at long range. This is a small matter, but it has structural consequence: if reconstruction itself decays, then we have decay on two levels (the form's epistemic function; the very reconstruction that the piece's "epistemic archaeology" remedy depends on). The piece is not obliged to develop this, but a one-sentence acknowledgment that reconstruction has its own degradation would close a gap.

7. **Surprising absence of #34 (*The Implied Apparatus*).** That piece argued, in the author's own voice, that ideas return to debate when their implied apparatus is constructible. The present piece is the diachronic complement on the *essay* rather than the *idea*: when does the form lose the reader-apparatus it was written for? The connection is natural and would tighten the cross-archive coherence. Engagement with #38 (*What the Functor Carries*) is less obvious but not strained - the question of what structure is preserved across a "transfer" (here, a temporal transfer) has a functorial analogue worth at least a citation.

8. **No process-language leakage, and no math-notation issues.** I checked: there is no "round 1," "the panel," "advisor," "this revision," or similar. There are also no symbols or operators rendered as prose where LaTeX would help. Nothing to flag on either tone-concern axis.
