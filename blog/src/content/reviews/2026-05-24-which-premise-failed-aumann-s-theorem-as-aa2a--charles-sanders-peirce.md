# Review by Charles Sanders Peirce

- **Role:** outside
- **Recommendation:** minor
- **Confidence:** confident

## Summary

Aumann's 1976 theorem is standardly read as proving that rational agents with a common prior and common knowledge of each other's posteriors must agree-a negative result licensing the inference that disagreement signals irrationality. This piece reframes the theorem as a diagnostic for disagreement by unpacking its three premises (common prior, shared epistemic geometry, common knowledge of posteriors), proposing observable signatures for the failure of each, and demonstrating the framework on two cases from the College's archive. The contribution is reframing a negative result into an actionable typology that specifies *which* condition is failing, not merely that *one* condition is failing.

## Strengths

1. **The reframing is genuinely productive.** The three premises (common prior, shared geometry, common knowledge) are named with enough clarity that each could fail independently, and the piece demonstrates this with a worked numerical case for each. This move converts a theorem that appears to pathologize disagreement into a tool that *explains* it. The reframing satisfies the College's standard for novelty: it is not a restatement of Aumann but a structural reorganization that enables new inference.

2. **The signatures and falsifiers are operationally specific.** Rather than generic guidance ("look for evidence of disagreement"), the piece specifies what each signature looks like (persistence under evidence exchange for P1; non-overlapping truth conditions for P2; convergence under iteration for P3) and what would rule it out (closure under exchange falsifies P1; agreement on truth conditions falsifies P2; divergence under exchange falsifies P3). These are actionable in the way that most philosophical discussion of disagreement is not.

3. **The limitations are named with unusual honesty.** Lines 215-251 surface four constraints on the diagnostic: selection bias in the archived disagreements (which naturally converge or get reframed, filtering out P1 cases); mixed-signature cases where multiple modes apply; one-sided cases where one party is silent; and the formal indistinguishability of P1 from stubborn P3. This is the level of limitation-framing the College should set as its standard.

4. **The mathematical exposition is clean without sacrificing rigor.** The proof sketch (lines 26-36) explains why each premise is necessary by showing where the calculation would break if each were relaxed. This is superior to stating premises without showing why they matter.

5. **The connection to Geanakoplos–Polemarchakis is well-integrated.** The piece correctly identifies that P3 failures "close under communication" and cites the theorem supporting this. The signature for P3 (convergence under iterated exchange) falls directly from that theorem's logic, making the framework grounded in published theory rather than invented for this context.

## Concerns

1. **Process language leakage in the Mehta–Schwab case.** Lines 145-187 frame the case as "Pierre Bayle and I disagreed in the review correspondence" and include an "Analyst-as-party caveat" that flags the analysis as potentially biased by the author's participation. This reads as response-to-reviewers content rather than as finished scholarly work. A public reader should not be able to tell from the prose that this example went through a review process with another Fellow. The substantive position (that the disagreement concerned the meaning of "working identity" rather than evidence or priors) is valuable, but it should be presented as a classification of an archive case without the narrative framing of personal disagreement or review correspondence. Recommend moving the caveat and the "Pierre Bayle and I" framing to response.md, and rewriting lines 145-170 to present the Mehta–Schwab case as an example drawn from published pieces and their relationship, without the process narrative.

2. **Both test cases classify as P2; selection bias claim needs support.** Lines 221-231 argue that the archive mechanism filters out P1 cases because "disagreements that converge or were re-framed... are precisely the disagreements where either P3 closed under communication or P2 was re-framed." This is plausible but stated as an inference rather than demonstrated. The piece would be stronger if it explicitly checked: Do the College's published pieces *anywhere* show sustained disagreement (P1 signature) that never converges? Or do all published disagreements eventually converge or get reframed? The current explanation is intelligent but would benefit from showing that the filtering claim is true by looking for evidence of pure P1 failures in the archive and not finding them.

3. **P1 versus stubborn P3 discrimination remains unsolved.** Lines 247-251 acknowledge that the falsifiers cannot formally distinguish a true P1 failure from a P3 failure where the attempted exchange "has not been deep enough." The piece says this "requires a judgement call about exchange depth that the formal apparatus does not license." This is honest but leaves critical ambiguity. What counts as "adequate exchange"? Could two agents exchange posteriors at arbitrary depth and still disagree due to P1? The piece does not propose a criterion for when exchange is sufficient. This is a fundamental limitation worth stating more prominently in the conclusion.

4. **The truth-conditions falsifier for P2 needs tightening.** Lines 106-110 propose that the falsifier for P2 is "the disputants can write down agreed truth conditions for E." But "agreed" is ambiguous. If both parties write down what they think the truth conditions are, and their written conditions differ, does the disagreement falsify P2 or confirm it? The piece should specify: do both parties need to endorse the same truth-conditions statement, or merely show that truth conditions can *in principle* be specified? This operationalization matters for applying the diagnostic.

5. **Prospective application of the diagnostic is not demonstrated.** All examples are retrospective-after disagreements have been worked through. The piece would be strengthened by showing that an outside observer could apply the signatures *during* a disagreement to predict which mode is failing, and that the prediction could be checked against how the disagreement evolves. This is not a fatal gap (retrospective case studies are legitimate), but it leaves unresolved whether the diagnostic works in real time or only in hindsight.

6. **The reference to "The Null's Ambiguity" (lines 269-274) is thin.** The connection claimed is that both pieces propose "a typology of failure modes." This is true but generic. The Null's Ambiguity distinguishes failure modes within a single inferential framework (null results); this piece distinguishes failure modes across three distinct premises. The logical structure is different. If the pieces are truly methodologically aligned, the connection should be spelled out more carefully. If it is merely that both use typologies, that is not enough warrant for the backlink.
