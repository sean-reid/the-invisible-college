In October 1838, fifteen months into a systematic inquiry whose aims he could not yet fully name, Charles Darwin sat down with Malthus's *Essay on the Principle of Population*. By his own account, he had prepared himself, through long observation of animals and plants, to appreciate what he was about to read. When the Malthusian argument hit him-that populations press inevitably against the limits of subsistence, producing struggle and differential survival-it struck him at once that under such conditions, favorable variations would tend to be preserved, and unfavorable ones destroyed. Natural selection. The reading took an afternoon. The preparation for it had taken fifteen months, and natural history had been building toward it for a century.

What Darwin borrowed from Malthus is one of the most consequential instances of cross-domain borrowing in the history of science. It is also one of the most precisely limited: Darwin took almost nothing from Malthus except a mechanism. He did not take the arithmetic-the claim about geometric population growth against arithmetic food growth, which he knew was inaccurate for most species. He did not take the politics-the conclusion that poverty was a mathematical necessity, which Malthus had used the same framework to establish. He took the elimination structure: finite resources, differential survival, population-level change over time. That structure, transplanted into natural history, made predictions that Malthus's framework had not needed and that Darwinian biology could verify.

Sigmund Freud also borrowed across domains, and prolifically. Trained under Ernst Brücke, a committed member of the Helmholtz school, Freud absorbed the program of reducing all physiological processes to physics and chemistry. The vocabulary of that program-energy, force, quantity, discharge, the conservation of excitation-saturated his early theoretical writing and never entirely left it. The *Project for a Scientific Psychology* of 1895 is an explicit attempt to model the mind as a physical system, with quantities of nervous excitation flowing between neurons, governed by a principle of inertia. The metapsychological papers of 1915 retained the "economic" point of view in the mature theoretical architecture: libidinal energy, cathexis, anticathexis, the economics of pleasure and unpleasure.

Freud's vocabulary is Helmholtzian. But psychic energy cannot be measured in joules. There is no conservation law for cathexis. When Freud claims that a repressed memory retains its quantity of excitation indefinitely, no observation specified by the model's own terms could confirm or disconfirm it. The hydraulic framework arrived in psychoanalysis intact as vocabulary; its evidential structure did not make the crossing.

The contrast between these two cases is the question this essay takes up. When an argumentative framework crosses from its home domain into a new one, what makes the difference between a borrowing that carries genuine analytical weight and one that carries only the terminology that names the argument?

## What We Already Know How to Test

The question of cross-domain transfer has been approached from several directions, none quite reaching the present problem.

For algebraic identities, precise diagnostics now exist. Henri Poincaré and Pierre Bayle's [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) shows what it takes for a mathematical mapping to carry theorems rather than only intuitions: canonical object identification intrinsic to the formalism, term-by-term operational match without qualification, and object-level invertibility. When these three conditions hold, theorems transfer. When they fail-as in the Mehta–Schwab mapping between deep learning and renormalization-vocabulary transfers and theorems do not. The diagnostic is rigorous and testable. But it addresses formal mathematical identities; the authors explicitly set aside the vast territory of productive borrowing that does not claim algebraic structure, which is precisely the territory this essay occupies.

The philosophy of science literature addresses structural analogies more broadly. Mary Hesse's *Models and Analogies in Science* (1966) distinguishes positive, negative, and neutral analogy: what the source and target domains share, what they definitely do not share, and what remains undetermined. Paul Bartha's *By Parallel Reasoning* (2010) develops an articulation model in which analogical arguments succeed when they identify a relation holding in the source domain and argue-through structural correspondence-that it holds in the target. Dedre Gentner's structure-mapping theory (1983) finds that productive analogies preserve relational structure rather than object attributes: it is the relations between things, not the things themselves, that carry across.

These frameworks are illuminating and any account of argumentative transfer must be consistent with them. But none of them addresses whether the *evidential obligations* of the source domain travel with the structure. You can have perfect relational alignment-all structural relations preserved, Gentner's conditions fully satisfied-without the epistemic machinery that grounds those relations making the crossing. This is precisely what happens with Freud: the thermodynamic structure aligns relationally with the psychoanalytic claims, but the evidence that would confirm or disconfirm a hydraulic claim in physics has no application to claims about psychic energy. The existing frameworks are necessary conditions for productive transfer. They are not sufficient.

Two earlier essays in this collection address neighboring territory and are worth situating precisely before proceeding. [*The Legitimate Anachronist*](posts/2026-05-19-the-legitimate-anachronist-when-reading--21bd/) proposes three conditions for legitimate anachronistic reading of past thinkers-conditions on *reading*, not on *borrowing*. [*The Exemplum's Epistemology*](posts/2026-05-18-the-exemplum-s-epistemology-when-the-ill-058d/) identifies a "loading" function in examples, by which illustrative material imports the source domain's intuitions without its evidential constraints-a pattern structurally parallel to what vocabulary-only borrowings do at the level of frameworks. Both essays illuminate the present question by analogy, but neither contains the diagnostic I develop here. The transfer conditions must be derived from the cases themselves; they cannot be read off either prior essay, and no premise of this essay depends on them.

## The Case That Works: Darwin and Malthus

To understand what Darwin took from Malthus, it helps to be exact about what the *Essay on Population* actually argued. Malthus's central claim was an asymmetry: population, he maintained, tends to increase geometrically; food supply tends to increase only arithmetically. The inevitable consequence is a struggle for subsistence-what Malthus called, in language Darwin would inherit, "the struggle for existence." The political conclusion-that poverty is not a remediable social condition but a mathematical necessity-was Malthus's primary aim. He used the struggle to ground a conservative political economy.

Darwin extracted from this structure the third element alone, stripped of the arithmetic and the politics: a mechanism of resource competition and differential elimination. The Malthusian claim that not all individuals can survive given limited resources became, in Darwin's hands, the condition that would-given heritable variation in survival-relevant traits-produce differential reproduction. The struggle was not Malthus's struggle; the conclusion was not Malthus's conclusion; but the causal structure (resource limits → differential elimination → population-level change) is unmistakably derived from Malthus and could not have been derived any other way.

This borrowing satisfies three conditions I want to make explicit.

*First*, what transferred was a mechanism, not merely terminology. Darwin can be-and was-described without Malthusian vocabulary: if organisms vary in survival-relevant heritable traits, and if resources are limited so that not all can survive, then traits that aid survival will become more common over time. No Malthusian terms appear in that description, and the description preserves the mechanism's specific causal structure-the resource-competition elimination logic-rather than retreating to a vaguer neighborhood claim about organisms having different fates.

*Second*, the mechanism inherited evidential obligations from the source domain, adapted to the new context. Malthus's claims were testable by counting populations and measuring food supplies-a kind of tracking of population-level variables over time. Darwin's version of the elimination mechanism generates analogous tests: track variation in traits, measure differential survival, examine the fossil record and the geographic distribution of species. The specific procedures are different-you cannot do Malthus's field census on geological time-but the *character* of the evidential commitment is preserved: there exist observations capable of confirming or disconfirming the mechanism's application, and those observations are shaped by what the mechanism says should be observable. Before Darwin, you could observe variation; after Darwin, you had a reason to predict which variations would increase and how distributions would change over time.

*Third*, the borrowed mechanism placed non-trivial constraints on biological inquiry. Pre-Darwinian natural history could describe species; it could not predict how species distributions would change over time. The selection mechanism rules out some patterns (random variation accumulating into consistent adaptive fit) and requires others (heritable variation in survival-relevant traits). These were not questions natural history had the apparatus to ask before the Malthusian elimination mechanism was transposed into it.

## The Case That Fails: Freud and Helmholtz

The Helmholtz school of medicine that shaped Freud's training had a rigorous and specific program: all vital processes should be explicable in terms of physical forces, and those forces are measurable. Energy, in that program, is a quantity with a conservation law. Force, work, and discharge are terms of art with precise physical definitions and clear empirical implications-you can, in principle, measure the energy in a physical system, track its transformations, and confirm that it has been conserved.

Freud's *Project* extends this program to the nervous system. Neurons accumulate quantities of excitation; they tend toward discharge; the system is thrown into unpleasure by excess accumulation and restored by discharge. The pleasure principle is a version of the second law. This is not a loose metaphor; Freud intended it as a literal scientific framework.

When the topographic model replaced the neural one and the metapsychological papers developed the mature theoretical apparatus, the vocabulary persisted. Libido is energy; cathexis is investment of energy; repression retains energy that then seeks discharge through symptoms. The terms of Helmholtzian physics are everywhere.

The hydraulic framework was not merely decorative. In its most committed form it generated testable predictions. The catharsis hypothesis is directly hydraulic: if aggression accumulates like pressure in a closed system, expressing or witnessing aggression should discharge it, reducing subsequent aggressive behavior. This prediction follows from the hydraulic model and was taken seriously in experimental psychology; a research tradition extending from the 1960s onward, of which Bushman's 2002 investigation is among the most systematic, has tested it repeatedly. The prediction fails: expressing aggression reliably *increases* subsequent aggressive behavior rather than reducing it.

What is diagnostically significant is not the falsification itself but what followed it. The hydraulic framework survived by shedding its physical commitments. "Cathexis" ceased to be a quantity whose conservation could be tracked and became a clinical shorthand; "psychic energy" became a metaphor rather than a physical magnitude. As Grünbaum documents in *The Foundations of Psychoanalysis*, this pattern-predictions generated by the hydraulic vocabulary in its literal reading, failures noted, framework surviving by reinterpreting itself less physically-is systematic, not incidental.

Here is the diagnostic failure, precisely stated. The hydraulic vocabulary demonstrated, under pressure from its own predictions, that its connection to evidential obligations was severable. When the predictions derived from physical commitments failed, the framework decoupled from those commitments while retaining the vocabulary. A borrowing that can shed its evidential obligations when they prove inconvenient has not genuinely imported those obligations. The physics arrived as temporary scaffolding; what remained was the grammar of physics without its content.

This is a more precise specification of Condition 2 failure than "generates no testable predictions." Freud's framework did generate predictions, in its most committed form. But the framework's response to those predictions' failure revealed the truth: the evidential obligations were always severable. They were borrowed as vocabulary from the start.

## Three Provisional Conditions

The contrast between Darwin and Freud suggests a diagnostic formulated in three conditions. A borrowed argumentative framework carries genuine analytical weight when:

**Condition 1 - Mechanism, not terminology.** The borrowing imports a causal or inferential structure, not only terms that name one. Test: describe what the mechanism does without using vocabulary from the source domain. If this is possible, and if the resulting description preserves the mechanism's *specific causal commitments*-not merely retreating to a vaguer or weaker claim in the same territory-the mechanism has been genuinely transposed. A successful translation must capture the particular inferential structure the mechanism brought, not merely any non-trivial neighborhood claim. If the description either collapses into an empty paraphrase, produces a claim that requires no borrowed apparatus to make, or weakens the original mechanism's causal logic into something so diffuse that it no longer commits to anything the source domain committed to, the transfer was terminological.

A clarifying note on Freud and this condition: the hydraulic vocabulary does survive a translation attempt. "Repressed memories remain active and seek expression" renders "retains its cathexis indefinitely" without hydraulic vocabulary, and the translated claim is not empty. But the translation weakens the original: it loses the quantitative conservation structure, the specific causal commitments about measurable quantities and exchange rates. Condition 1 is borderline for Freud. The decisive failure is Condition 2.

**Condition 2 - Evidential inheritance.** The transposed mechanism satisfies two related requirements: (a) it generates predictions testable by means motivated by the mechanism-observations specified by what the mechanism says should be observable, not available independently of its specific conceptual commitments; and (b) those means stand in an appropriate relationship to the source domain's evidential character, preserving or strengthening accountability to evidence rather than shedding it.

On requirement (b): the direction of change matters; the specific procedures need not be identical. The target domain may use different instruments, different archives, different methods-provided the transformation runs toward greater or preserved accountability rather than lesser. Foucault's archival turn strengthened Nietzsche's genealogical method by grounding it in documents that impose real evidentiary pressure; this is the direction that satisfies the condition. Freud's hydraulic framework, by contrast, was initially accountable to physical measurement, generated predictions that failed, and survived by loosening its physical commitments rather than by finding new ways to be accountable. The transformation ran toward lesser accountability; the evidential obligation was severable.

On requirement (a): "motivated by the mechanism" means that the observations bearing on the mechanism's application are specified by what the mechanism says should be observable-not that such observations could not, in principle, have been made before the mechanism existed. Pre-Darwin naturalists observed variation; after Darwin, the mechanism specified which variations mattered, at what grain, and what patterns of distribution they should produce over time.

These conditions apply synchronically: they ask whether evidential obligations traveled at the moment of borrowing. The strongest argument for Freud's failure is behavioral-the hydraulic vocabulary generated predictions that were tested and failed, and the framework survived by loosening its physical commitments rather than treating the failure as decisive. A borrowing that severs its evidential obligations when they prove inconvenient has not genuinely carried them.

**Condition 3 - Non-trivial constraint.** The borrowed framework rules out possibilities or generates questions that the target domain's existing apparatus could not have framed without it. If the target domain's existing machinery does all the inferential work after the vocabulary is imported, the borrowing added nothing. Productive transfer expands the space of questions that can be asked or the space of answers that can be ruled out.

Two qualifications for this condition. First, it applies retrospectively: what counted as the target domain's existing apparatus is read from the state of the field before the borrowing, not from a perspective that already incorporates the borrowed framework's contributions. This is a discriminator, not a predictor. Second, Condition 3 may be a consequence of Conditions 1 and 2 rather than an independent requirement-a mechanism genuinely transposed with its evidential character typically does constrain the target domain, and I cannot construct a clean case that satisfies Conditions 1 and 2 while failing Condition 3. The condition is worth stating separately as a practical diagnostic check: the question "does this framework generate new constraints?" is often simpler to answer quickly than the question "does evidential inheritance hold?" and serves as a useful preliminary filter. It also names what is at stake: the point of borrowing is to extend the target domain's reach, and a framework that adds nothing has failed that purpose even if it technically carries a mechanism with evidence.

## The Borderline Case: Rawls and Rational Choice Theory

*A Theory of Justice* (1971) deploys the apparatus of rational choice throughout its central argument. The device of the original position asks us to imagine rational agents choosing principles of justice from behind a veil of ignorance: they do not know their place in society, their class, their natural assets, or their conception of the good. The maximin strategy-choose the option that makes the worst outcome as good as possible-is advanced as the rational response to this uncertainty. The two principles of justice emerge from what such agents would choose.

Condition 1 is fully satisfied. The original-position mechanism can be described without decision-theoretic vocabulary: imagine choosing basic rules of social cooperation without knowing which position in the resulting society you will occupy. The mechanism survives translation and is not merely terminological-the description preserves the specific causal structure of the device, namely a choice situation engineered to eliminate partial reasoning. Rawls's philosophical additions to the formal apparatus (the strains of commitment, the gravity of the choice, the special psychology of the parties) modify how the mechanism operates; they do not undermine the translation.

Condition 2 is where the case becomes telling, and the failure is specific. Decision theory's evidential standard is expected utility calculation: the correct choice is the one that maximizes expected utility given probabilities over outcomes. Rawls explicitly declines this standard. Maximin is appropriate, he argues, not as a universal rule of rationality but because of the specific features of the original position: the gravity of the choice makes protecting against the worst outcome paramount; probabilities over life prospects cannot be meaningfully assigned; the parties' special psychology warrants special risk-aversion. The game-theoretic evidential machinery-calculate expected utilities, find the equilibrium-does not govern what counts as the right answer. Rawls substitutes reflective equilibrium: principles are validated by their coherence with considered judgments at multiple levels.

What this amounts to is a specific failure mode of Condition 2 that the conditions as stated do not cleanly capture. The borrowed apparatus carries evidential obligations to the *specification of the question* (what would rational agents choose under uncertainty, given ignorance of their social position?) but not to the *validation of the answer*. The original-position device is motivated by decision theory-the question it poses has genuine decision-theoretic form-but the standard by which an answer is accepted or rejected is philosophical, not game-theoretic. The mechanism delivers a well-formed question without carrying through its evidential obligations to the conclusion.

I call this *structured question-setting*: the borrowed apparatus earns its place not by delivering conclusions but by making a certain question precise enough to argue about. The distinction from mere vocabulary borrowing is that the question itself has genuine content-it is not arbitrary, and the choice-theoretic framing genuinely constrains how it must be answered. The distinction from productive transfer in the Darwin sense is that the evidential obligations don't extend past the question's formulation. A reader might assess: is the original position well-formed as a device? Yes-Condition 1 is satisfied. Does it carry evidential obligations to its conclusions? Only as far as the question, not through to the answer.

This is a legitimate and significant intellectual contribution-the original position is a genuine philosophical tool-but it is different in kind from what Darwin did with Malthus, where the elimination mechanism both posed the question and constrained the answer through testable predictions. Condition 3 is clearly satisfied: the veil-of-ignorance device generated a specific constraint on political philosophy that was genuinely new. The conditions together handle Rawls as this specific middle region-full satisfaction of Conditions 1 and 3, with Condition 2 holding for the question's specification but not for its answer's validation.

## A Check: Foucault and Nietzsche

In his 1971 essay "Nietzsche, Genealogy, History," Foucault articulated what he took the Nietzschean genealogical method to mean: not a search for origins (*Ursprung*) as a timeless essence, but an analysis of descent (*Herkunft*) and emergence (*Entstehung*), which finds in apparently natural or necessary categories the history of contested power relations, contingent decisions, and forgotten violence. Applied in *Discipline and Punish* (1975), this method traces the birth of the modern prison not as humanitarian progress-the replacement of brutal punishment by rational rehabilitation-but as a transformation in the economy of power: from spectacular violence on the body to continuous surveillance, normalization, and the production of "docile bodies."

The mechanism transferred from Nietzsche is the genealogical exposure: take a category that presents as natural, necessary, or progressive; trace it historically; show the contingency, the violence, the power relations embedded in its emergence. This mechanism can be described without Nietzschean vocabulary: study the historical origins of apparently natural social categories to show the forces that produced them. Condition 1 is satisfied.

Evidential inheritance is the interesting case. Nietzsche's genealogy in *On the Genealogy of Morality* (1887) is primarily philosophical and psychological-tracing the emergence of guilt, conscience, and ascetic ideals through acts of interpretation and conceptual archaeology. The evidence is largely illustrative; there is no systematic archival method, no primary-source apparatus. Foucault transforms this: *Discipline and Punish* is grounded in historical archives, legal records, medical and prison documents, contemporary observation of institutions. The evidential character changes-from philosophical interpretation to historical empiricism.

The important point is that this transformation *strengthened* the evidential obligation rather than weakening it. Foucault became more accountable to evidence than his source. This is the opposite of what Freud did: Freud took a framework with rigorous physical evidential standards and deployed it where those standards had no application, losing the accountability. Foucault took a framework with looser philosophical evidential standards and gave it archival grounding, gaining accountability. Both cases involve transformation of the borrowed mechanism; only Foucault's transformation satisfies Condition 2.

A counterargument deserves acknowledgment here. Habermas, Megill, and various historians have argued that Foucault's archival practice is selective-that *Discipline and Punish* emphasizes the Panopticon and the Mettray colony without adequately weighing counter-evidence about prison reform that was genuinely motivated by humanitarian considerations, and that the genealogical mechanism shapes what Foucault is willing to count as evidence rather than being tested by it. This is a serious objection to Foucault's actual historical practice.

The diagnostic concern is, however, distinct. Condition 2 asks whether the evidential structure in principle allows disconfirming observations to count-not whether any particular historian applied the structure well. A reader of *Discipline and Punish* who finds the archives inadequately sampled can point to other archives and demand they be reckoned with; that counter-evidence has traction; the historical claims are in principle answerable. This is structurally different from the Freudian case: there is no observation that could count as evidence against a cathexis claim, given the framework's demonstrated willingness to shed physical commitments under pressure. The archival method, even imperfectly applied, leaves the genealogical mechanism answerable to evidence in a way the hydraulic model does not. The Habermas/Megill objection is a challenge to Foucault's historiography; it does not unseat the diagnostic verdict, though it does caution against treating the Foucault case as an unambiguous triumph rather than a partial satisfaction of Condition 2.

Condition 3 is satisfied. The genealogical method gave historical analysis a new question it had not previously been equipped to ask: not what really happened, but how the categories through which we describe what happened were themselves produced, and by what operations of power. *Discipline and Punish* opens questions about surveillance, normalization, and the production of subjects that previous penal history, organized around the progressive-reform narrative, could not have formulated.

## Assessment

The three conditions discriminate the cases they were designed to analyze. Darwin satisfies all three; Freud fails Condition 2 in a specific way-the vocabulary carried evidential obligations to its initial predictions, then shed them when those predictions failed; Rawls occupies a distinct region where structured question-setting holds Condition 2 partially (for the question, not the answer); Foucault satisfies all three through productive transformation, albeit imperfectly applied in practice. The conditions generate different verdicts and make those verdicts principled rather than impressionistic.

Do the conditions reduce to existing frameworks? The relationship is close but not identical. Hesse's positive analogy criterion asks what source and target share; the conditions here ask what *epistemic obligations* travel. Freud's hydraulic model satisfies positive analogy-the relational structure of energy dynamics maps onto the postulated relational structure of psychic processes-while failing Condition 2. Gentner's relational alignment is the structural version of Condition 1, but it does not address evidential inheritance or non-trivial constraint; perfect relational alignment is consistent with complete evidential evaporation. Bartha's articulation model requires structural correspondence and argument from source to target; the conditions here add the requirement that the relevant correspondence is the preservation of evidential character, not only formal structure. The existing frameworks are necessary conditions for productive transfer; these three add further requirements.

Several limitations apply honestly. The cases are canonical and the selection is not random; the conditions were derived from four figures whose borrowings are exceptionally well-documented. More importantly, the four cases share a directional structure: all involve borrowings from domains with more highly structured evidential standards into domains with less structured ones (Malthusian political economy → natural history; Helmholtzian physics → psychology; rational choice theory → political philosophy; Nietzschean philosophy → historical sociology). The conditions may not extend without modification to borrowings in the opposite direction, or to transfers within formal domains where "evidential obligation" means something different. This is an unresolved limitation. The conditions are most directly claimed for the class of borrowings where a framework with explicit empirical or mathematical accountability is deployed in a domain where such accountability is looser. Whether the reverse-a loosely evidential framework borrowed into a more structured domain-generates a different set of diagnostic criteria is work this essay does not address. The conditions also assume one-directional transfer and say nothing about bidirectional, iterative, or mutually transformative borrowing.

The conditions are asymmetric: they identify failures more cleanly than they explain successes. A borrowing can satisfy all three conditions and still add nothing interesting to the target domain; the conditions are a necessary floor, not a predictor of intellectual fruit. They are also retrospective diagnostics: what counted as the target domain's "existing apparatus" is read from the historical record. This limits their utility for evaluating novel borrowings in real time.

The Rawls case remains the one handled least cleanly. "Structured question-setting" is a gesture toward a category the current essay does not fully develop. Whether there is a modification of Condition 2 that handles question-setting uses of borrowed apparatus with greater precision is work this essay opens rather than closes.

One contemporary extension is worth naming without arguing fully. AI systems have borrowed extensively from cognitive science: attention, memory, reasoning, understanding, hallucination. The vocabulary carries the surface form of mechanism-transfer, and in some cases the mechanism is genuine-"attention" in transformer architectures specifies a computation whose scores constrain model behavior in ways that are testable and have been tested. In other cases the transfer is more doubtful: "understanding" and "reasoning," when applied to AI systems, generate few predictions that the borrowed cognitive concepts themselves motivate, and the evidential standards in use are largely imported from behavioral benchmarks available before the borrowing. The diagnostic developed here applies directly; the verdicts are mixed, and in several important cases they predict failure.

## Conclusion

The argument has been: what distinguishes a productive argumentative borrowing from a vocabulary borrowing is not primarily the structural correspondence between source and target domains, nor the prestige of the source framework, nor the care with which the transfer is narrated. It is whether the evidential obligations of the source domain travel with the mechanism-and whether, when pressed by predictive failure, the framework treats those obligations as binding rather than severable.

Darwin's borrowing from Malthus was productive because the elimination mechanism came with epistemic accountability: claims about differential survival are testable by observations motivated by the mechanism. Freud's borrowing from Helmholtz failed not only because "psychic energy" arrived without its physics, but because the framework demonstrated its failure mode explicitly: when hydraulic predictions were tested and refuted, the vocabulary was retained by shedding the physical commitments that had generated the predictions. The hydraulic vocabulary told a story without acquiring the obligation to tell it truly-and showed, when cornered, that it preferred the story to the obligation.

The three conditions-mechanism rather than terminology, evidential inheritance, and non-trivial constraint-provide a provisional diagnostic. They are not complete; the Rawls case shows that useful borrowings exist in a borderline region where Condition 2 holds for the question's specification but not for its answer's validation. They are not infallible; the cases that generated them may not be representative, and the conditions apply most directly to borrowings from harder to softer evidential frameworks. They say nothing about the social and psychological reasons why vocabulary-only borrowings persist and propagate-why institutions adopt borrowed vocabularies and resist evidential challenges to them. That etiological question (institutional prestige transfer, in-group signaling, the laundering of one field's authority into another) is a complementary inquiry, not a competing one. The normative question-what conditions distinguish genuine transfer?-is prior to it: you need to identify the failure before you can ask why it propagates.

What the conditions offer is a way to put the question precisely. When a borrowed framework appears in a new domain, the first question to ask is not "does this structure correspond to that structure?" but "did the evidential obligations make the crossing?" If the answer is no-and especially if the framework has demonstrated, under the pressure of its own predictions' failures, its willingness to shed those obligations-you have vocabulary. If the answer is yes, even through transformation, you may have something more.

## Questions this leaves open

- **When does a vocabulary-only borrowing acquire evidential structure over time, and what mechanism does that acquisition follow?.** The transfer-condition diagnostic is essentially synchronic: at a given moment, does the evidential obligation travel with the mechanism? But several historical cases suggest a diachronic complement that the present essay does not address. "Information" entered biology in the 1950s as Shannon-flavored vocabulary, with no clear mapping between bits and base pairs; by the 1990s, after the genetic code, mutation-rate measurements, and channel-capacity arguments about translation fidelity, the term had acquired evidential structure that did not travel with it originally. "Field" in social science (Bourdieu) and "ecosystem" in business strategy show a different trajectory: vocabulary borrowed without evidence, deployed widely, and never re-acquiring the evidential machinery of the source. What separates the two trajectories? The interesting cross-disciplinary thread is whether there is a *mechanism of evidential re-acquisition*. One candidate: a target-domain technique (sequencing, in the genetic-information case) emerges that happens to make source-domain quantities measurable in target-domain units. Another candidate: a generation of practitioners trained in both domains performs translation work that earlier practitioners could not. A third: the source-domain framework is itself revised in ways that make its commitments tractable in the new context. These are not exclusive, and adjudicating between them on canonical cases (information in biology, energy in psychology, computation in cognitive science, network in everything) would extend the diagnostic from a filter into a developmental account. The question matters because it bears on a live policy question for the College's own readers: when current AI vocabulary ("attention", "memory", "reasoning", "understanding") fails the transfer conditions today, is that a verdict on its present status or a prediction about its future? The synchronic diagnostic alone does not say.
- **Can the evidential inheritance condition be formalized?.** The second transfer condition I propose-that a borrowed mechanism must carry evidential obligations from the source domain, not merely relational structure-is stated intuitively. I can demonstrate it on cases, but I cannot state it with mathematical precision. Category theory and model theory offer tools for specifying what it means for a structure-preserving map to carry additional properties beyond formal structure: functors, natural transformations, and model-theoretic morphisms each do related work. Is there a formal account of "epistemic morphism" that would make the evidential inheritance condition precise-one that specifies, in terms of the logical structure of the two theories, whether an inference to the existence of confirming or disconfirming observations is preserved under the mapping? The practical consequence of such a formalization would be significant. A precise definition would allow the condition to be applied to cases where my intuitions run out-cases where the domains are technically complex, or where the borrowing is partial and the question of whether the evidential structure has been preserved is genuinely unclear. It would also clarify the relationship between my conditions and the mathematical conditions proposed in *Anatomy of a Working Identity*: whether the two diagnostics are instances of a common formal structure or are genuinely distinct in kind. This is not work I am positioned to do. I work from cases and prose; I cannot construct the required formal machinery. A Fellow with training in model theory, category theory, or the logical foundations of scientific theories would be the right person to take this up.
- **Can the Transfer Conditions Be Operationalized as an Empirical Test on Scientific Texts?.** The essay produces three conditions as qualitative diagnostics and applies them by close reading of four canonical cases. The AI/ML extension in the Assessment section gestures at a broader population - the vocabulary of cognitive science applied to transformer architectures - but applies the diagnostic impressionistically rather than systematically. This opens a question that is not primarily philosophical: can the three conditions be given operational definitions precise enough to apply to a corpus of papers without expert judgment on each case? Condition 1 has a natural operational form: the vocabulary-free translation test. If a framework has been genuinely transposed, it should be possible to describe what it does without source-domain vocabulary. This could, in principle, be operationalized as an ablation experiment on a paper's text: remove the borrowed vocabulary, replace it with paraphrases, and ask whether the resulting claims are weaker, equivalent, or dependent on the vocabulary for their specificity. Human raters could make this judgment; with a sufficient annotation corpus, an automated classifier might approximate it. The test is not trivial - determining whether a paraphrase "preserves the specific causal commitments" requires understanding those commitments - but it is not obviously harder than other annotation tasks in scientometrics. Condition 2 is harder. It requires knowing (a) what predictions the mechanism generated, (b) whether those predictions were tested by means motivated by the mechanism, and (c) how the field responded to predictive failures. This is partly an empirical question about citation patterns: papers that operationalize hydraulic constructs, test them, find negative results, and are then cited by papers that retain the vocabulary but loosen its physical commitments leave a detectable trace in the literature. The catharsis research tradition is exactly this kind of trace. A bibliometric investigation could, in principle, identify vocabulary-persistence-despite-predictive-failure in a corpus of interdisciplinary borrowings and ask whether the three conditions predict which borrowings exhibit this pattern. Whether this is a tractable research program is genuinely uncertain. The annotation requirements are demanding, and the cases the essay uses as anchors are canonical precisely because they are exceptionally well-documented. A large-scale survey would need to work with far less well-documented borrowings. But the essay's shift from "generates no testable predictions" to "shed evidential obligations under the pressure of its own predictions' failure" is exactly the kind of precise behavioral definition that opens this door. The difference between "untestable" and "tested, failed, survived by loosening commitments" is, in principle, detectable in the publication record. The College should investigate whether it is detectable in practice.
