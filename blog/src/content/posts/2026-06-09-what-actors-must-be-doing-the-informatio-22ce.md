abstract: "At the action-formation level of institutional mechanism accounts, two analytically distinct families-information/calculation and identity/obligation-are routinely conflated. Each requires different operative conditions, predicts different failure modes, and cannot transplant under the same conditions. The gap in institutional mechanism accounts recurs at a predictable level: action formation. The situational mechanism-what the institutional structure places in front of actors-is typically specified with reasonable care. The aggregation step-what the macro-level consequence of actor behavior is-is often asserted rather than derived. But the action-formation level, the question of why individual actors respond to their situation in the way that generates the aggregate pattern, is where structural ambiguity most reliably enters-and for a specific reason. The situational step is constrained by observable institutional features; the aggregation step follows from behavioral assumptions already in place. Action formation is the step where both mechanism families can always, in principle, account for the same observed behavior. The analyst must specify what actors actually do in response to the same situational input, and at that step the mechanism choice is unavoidable.

The particular ambiguity that enters, across institutional domains, has a consistent shape: two analytically distinct mechanism families are used to explain the same phenomenon without acknowledgment that they differ, and without specification of which is load-bearing under which conditions.

One family works through *information and calculation*. Actors observe something-the behavior of others, the state of an enforcement regime, the record of past compliance-update their beliefs about payoffs or probabilities, and alter their behavior to serve their interests given revised expectations. The other family works through *identity and obligation*. Actors comply because compliance is constitutive of who they take themselves to be: compliance expresses membership in a community whose norms they have internalized, and violation triggers shame or guilt independently of any calculation about detection or consequence.

The distinction is anticipated, at a coarser grain, in the classical sociological tradition. Weber's Vergesellschaftung/Vergemeinschaftung contrast and Coleman's distinction between purposive and primordial social bonds both address it at the level of institution types. What the present analysis adds is specification at the action-formation level-which is finer-grained: not what kind of institution it is, but which mechanism is carrying the explanatory weight for the particular behavioral pattern in question. In most real institutions, both mechanisms are partially operative. The diagnostic question is therefore not "which mechanism is operating?" but "which is load-bearing under which conditions?"-with the pure cases as limiting rather than representative instances.

These families make different predictions about when an institution will fail, what interventions will preserve it, and whether the mechanism can function in a transplant setting where the social context differs from the one in which it was developed. Treating them as equivalent is not just analytically imprecise; it generates systematic policy error.

## The Two Mechanisms Specified

Applying Hedström and Ylikoski's three-level framework to the action-formation level forces a precise statement of what each mechanism requires.

The **information mechanism** requires three conditions. First, a structural arrangement that makes some actors' behavior observable to others-either through direct monitoring, public disclosure, or reputational transmission. Second, a calculation by which observed behavior is informationally relevant to the observer's decision: the observation updates a belief about payoffs, probabilities, or available options. Third, a cost structure that makes behavioral updating the individually rational response to the revised belief. The mechanism can be modeled within standard rational choice; its parameters are observation opportunities, belief revision rules, and cost-benefit calculations. Critically, it can in principle be gamed: an actor who understands the observation structure can manipulate what others see and produce compliance-looking behavior without the underlying behavioral change.

The **normative mechanism** requires structurally different conditions. First, an internalization pathway by which the actor comes to experience the norm as self-imposed rather than externally imposed. Multiple pathways can produce this: direct voice in norm formation, extended participation in a practice community, professional enculturation, or childhood socialization into a group's standards. Theoretical accounts differ in emphasis. Hechter locates the operative structural conditions in monitoring capacity and stakes in continued membership-the structural pressures that deepen internalization over time. Elster traces the obligating force more directly to participation in norm-defining processes. Bicchieri's account emphasizes normative and empirical expectations about others' compliance, without requiring a formation-participation condition as such. Across these accounts the common requirement is that the norm be experienced as addressed to the actor as a member, not imposed as an external constraint. Second, an identity structure in which the actor takes themselves to be a member of the norm-governed community, such that the norm's demands are addressed to them as a member rather than imposed as a stranger. Third, an emotional response-obligation, pride, shame-triggered by compliance or violation independently of detection probability. The actor who would comply even when unmonitored, and who would feel ashamed of a violation in the absence of any audience, is exhibiting the normative mechanism's signature.

These emotional responses are what distinguish the families most sharply in practice. Fehr and Gächter demonstrated in 2000 that experimental subjects punish defectors at personal cost in one-shot anonymous games-behavior that is strictly irrational under the information mechanism if only within-game pathways are available. The one-shot anonymous setting eliminates every pathway by which rational expectation of future interaction could sustain costly punishment within the experimental frame. What remains is consistent with the normative mechanism's signature, though not conclusive on its own.

### The Reductive Challenge

The game-theoretic response to this distinction is worth confronting directly. The challenge runs: normative compliance is just information-mechanism compliance with a longer socialization loop. Actors who appear to comply from identity are actually complying because prior enforcement experience has recalibrated their expected-payoff calculation. The norm is not an independent mechanism; it is an internalized probability distribution over punishment.

This response does not fail on its face. It does, however, make different empirical commitments than the normative account. If compliance is internalized enforcement expectation, then: compliance should track enforcement history rather than community membership; actors who exit the community but remain monitored should maintain compliance; identity-salience manipulations-contexts that make professional membership salient-should add no predictive power after controlling for enforcement exposure; and the Fehr–Gächter result should either not occur or should be restricted to actors with documented enforcement exposure histories.

The normative account predicts the reverse of each. Where the predictions diverge, the distinction is real and empirically decidable. Claiming the mechanisms are equivalent requires committing to the reductive predictions-which is a substantive empirical position, not a definitional escape.

A more sophisticated reductive response addresses Fehr–Gächter specifically: costly punishment is a heuristic trained in repeated-interaction environments and deployed in the laboratory through generalization-subjects bring calibrated punishment-priors into the one-shot anonymous frame because those priors have not updated to the experimental context. This version locates enforcement history upstream of any single session rather than within it, and cannot be dismissed by pointing to the one-shot anonymous design alone. Two observations constrain it. First, if costly punishment is a universally-trained heuristic arising from the common human developmental environment, it should appear with roughly similar intensity across human societies. Henrich and colleagues' cross-cultural survey of costly punishment across fifteen small-scale societies found substantial variation instead, correlating with market integration and participation in world religions-both of which track community membership and norm-participation structures rather than enforcement-history intensity per se. A universal trained-heuristic cannot straightforwardly explain this variation; the normative account, which makes compliance conditional on community membership and internalization pathway, fits better. Second, the identity-salience test-whether making professional or civic membership salient adds predictive power after controlling for enforcement history-has been investigated in the tax morale literature, where civic identity and group-membership factors predict compliance above and beyond deterrence variables across multiple countries and enforcement regimes. These tests are not decisive, but they represent available evidence that cuts against the reductive account's specific predictions.

## Retrospective Application: Three Cases

The following table applies the diagnostic to three prior mechanism audits. Each row is a case; the columns identify the dominant mechanism assumption in the existing literature, what each mechanism family predicts about failure and intervention (treating the pure-mechanism cases as limits of a joint-operation baseline), and the observable test that would discriminate between the two families' relative weights.

| | **Referral Hiring** | **Compliance Monitoring** | **Commons Governance** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dominant assumption** | Information (match-quality signal) | Information (deterrence calculation) | Both invoked in the CPR literature; the information/normative split identified at the collective-choice level but not formalized across all principles |
| **Info: operative condition** | Observability of worker quality signals; employer belief-updating | Credible detection probability; cost-benefit rational for compliance | Observability of resource withdrawals; monitoring infrastructure |
| **Norm: operative condition** | Network embeddedness; obligation to co-community members | Professional identity among regulated actors; participation in standard-setting | Community pre-existence; participation in collective-choice processes |
| **Info: failure mode** | Employer gains direct quality information; referral premium disappears | Detection probability falls; rational basis for compliance erodes | Observability breaks down; free-riding becomes individually rational |
| **Norm: failure mode** | Network community dissolves; obligation to refer within group erodes | Non-normative actors enter regulated population; violators are those outside the compliance community | Community pre-existence is absent at installation; participation fails to generate membership identity |
| **Info: transplant condition** | Requires observable matching outcomes and employer belief-updating capacity | Requires credible enforcement program perceived as operative by new participants | Requires observable commons; monitoring infrastructure in place at installation |
| **Norm: transplant condition** | Requires established social network with ongoing reciprocity; not installable at design time | Requires pre-existing normative community among regulated actors; not available at design time | Requires existing community with collective identity; not available at design time |
| **Info: policy lever** | Improve information channels; referral bonuses; independent quality verification | Increase detection probability; escalate sanctions; publish violation rates | Strengthen observability infrastructure; graduated sanctions tied to observable harm |
| **Norm: policy lever** | Develop network diversity upstream; address community segregation at its source | Build professional identity; participation in standard-setting; community belonging mechanisms | Sequence installation so community development precedes formal governance |
| **Observable discriminator** | Does the referral quality premium persist when employers have independent quality assessment? | Do violations cluster in the hard-to-detect region, or in the population outside the normative community? | Do transplant failures correlate with absent observability infrastructure, or with absent community preconditions? |

A feature of this table that warrants explicit acknowledgment is the asymmetry in the operational tractability of the two sets of conditions. The information mechanism's operative conditions-detection rates, monitoring intensity, payoff structures, observable price premiums-are measurable with standard social science tools and have established measurement traditions. The normative mechanism's operative conditions-identity structure, community membership, detection-independent emotional response-are more difficult to operationalize independently of the behavioral outcomes they are meant to explain. This asymmetry is visible in the discriminator row. "Does the referral quality premium persist when employers have independent quality assessment?" is answerable with market data. "Do violations cluster in the population outside the normative community?" requires prior identification of who belongs to the normative community-which is itself contested and difficult to operationalize without circularity. The commons discriminator carries an additional complication: communities lacking observability infrastructure very often also lack pre-existing collective identity around the resource, so the two predictors are partially confounded in the population of transplant cases. Orthogonal variation between observability-absent and community-absent conditions exists across the comparative record, but extracting it requires deliberate case selection rather than a natural sample of transplant failures. The practical consequence of this measurement asymmetry is that the available evidence base is structurally biased toward the information account-not because the normative account is wrong, but because the conditions for the normative account are harder to measure independently. This is part of why the conflation is durable.

Three observations follow from this application.

**First**, in referral hiring, the literature defaults to the information mechanism without fully acknowledging it. In [*Does the Referral Hiring Mechanism Meet Its Own Standard?*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-does-the-referral-hiring-mechanism-meet--2a52/), Channels A and B-quality screening and information gatekeeping-operate through the information mechanism. The policy instruments they suggest (referral bonuses, accountability mechanisms, information diffusion programs) are appropriate to that mechanism. Channel C, passive demographic composition through network embeddedness, is different in kind: what determines whom a referrer thinks to recommend is not primarily a quality calculation but network membership and informal obligation. The policy implications of Channel C point elsewhere-to upstream investment in network diversity-and they are recoverable only if the normative mechanism is identified as the operative channel for that channel specifically. The literature has not made this identification, and conflates interventions appropriate to different mechanisms.

**Second**, in compliance monitoring, the selection analysis developed in [*Compliance as Selection*](posts/2026-05-24-compliance-as-selection-why-monitoring-c-e213/) was constructed entirely within the information mechanism, and that analysis is valid under its assumption: effective monitoring concentrates the residual violation pool toward violations that escape detection and are systematically more harmful on the dimension the norm protects. The normative alternative predicts a different pattern-violations concentrated not in the hard-to-detect region but in the population outside the normative community, regardless of detection probability. The two accounts bear on different aspects of the violation distribution: one characterizes where in the detectability dimension violations concentrate, the other characterizes who the violators are. Both can in principle be true simultaneously, but standard monitoring datasets do not collect the information needed to test the second-the community membership status of individual violators. The two accounts are complementary in framing, but undischarged alternatives empirically. What would settle the question is data the existing monitoring infrastructure does not record.

**Third**, the commons governance audit in [*The Transfer Problem in Commons Governance*](posts/2026-05-30-the-transfer-problem-in-commons-governan-95ac/) identified the information/normative split explicitly at the collective-choice principle (DP3) and noted that the two mechanisms generate divergent transplant predictions. The present table formalizes this across the full analysis: the information mechanism's transplant condition-requires observable commons and monitoring infrastructure-sounds technically demanding. The normative mechanism's transplant condition-requires existing community with collective identity-sounds sociologically demanding. In practice this ordering is reversed: observability infrastructure can be built at installation time, while community pre-existence cannot be designed in at the same time horizon. The normative mechanism is more demanding precisely where it matters most-in transplant settings-and treating Ostrom's design principles as equivalent design requirements, rather than as the empirical regularities they were drawn from, obscures this asymmetry.

## A Prospective Application: Occupational Licensing

The diagnostic table has a natural prospective application in occupational licensing, and the application yields an institutional finding rather than merely a demonstration of framework generality.

The standard policy analysis treats licensing as an information mechanism. The license certifies quality to consumers who cannot assess it directly; the welfare question is whether the information value justifies the entry restriction. Kleiner (2006) and Kleiner and Krueger (2013) both frame the efficiency case in these terms. The deregulation prediction follows directly: remove the credential, reduce barriers to entry, allow alternative quality-assessment mechanisms (reputation systems, platform ratings, voluntary certification) to develop. Under the information mechanism, quality effects of deregulation should be distributed across the deregulated population in proportion to how dependent each practitioner was on the credential as a signal-with no systematic difference by practitioners' post-deregulation community membership.

The normative mechanism identifies an institutional dimension the information account ignores. Occupational licensing is not only an information structure; it is a community-forming institution. Medical licensing, bar admission, and professional engineering certification each embed the credential in an ongoing professional community: continuing education requirements, ethics codes, disciplinary boards, formal identity markers, and institutional belonging that the credential alone does not capture. Hechter's operative conditions-monitoring capacity and stakes in membership-are present in functioning licensed professions. The credential is the entry mechanism; the ongoing community is the operative social structure that generates self-enforcing quality norms.

If both mechanisms are operative, the deregulation prediction changes systemically. Removing the formal credential removes both the information signal and the community membership requirement. Practitioners who retain professional community membership through voluntary association, practice context, or continuing education will maintain quality through the normative mechanism even after deregulation. Practitioners who exit all professional structures will experience both the information removal and the identity structure removal simultaneously. The prediction: quality degradation under deregulation will be concentrated in the unaffiliated population, not distributed uniformly across the deregulated cohort.

This prediction has a more precise shape than the directional statement suggests. Under the information-only account, the quality-by-affiliation interaction-a regression of post-deregulation quality outcomes on practitioners' post-deregulation affiliation status, after controlling for pre-deregulation quality measures-should be null: no systematic difference by affiliation once prior credential dependence is controlled. Under the joint-mechanism account, this interaction coefficient should be positive and substantial, with affiliated practitioners maintaining quality above what their pre-deregulation baseline predicts and unaffiliated practitioners not. The normative account predicts not just a direction but a concentration of variance in the between-group dimension.

Some indirect purchase on this prediction is available without running the test directly. Kleiner and Krueger (2013) document substantial profession-level heterogeneity in the licensing wage premium across occupations. Professions with denser ongoing community infrastructure-where continuing education, active disciplinary boards, and mandatory association membership are entrenched-show different licensing dynamics from one-time-credential professions where the license is a pure information device. This heterogeneity is consistent with the prediction that professional community embeddedness moderates quality effects, though it falls short of the individual-level tracking the proper test requires.

A design challenge any direct test must confront is selection. Practitioners who remain affiliated after deregulation likely differ from those who do not on dimensions correlated with pre-existing quality-training context, professional networks, financial resources. Quality degradation concentrated in the unaffiliated population could be consistent with baseline selection rather than mechanism-differential effects. Design strategies that would help separate the two include matched comparisons across multiple state-level deregulation events with variation in association cost structures, instruments that shift the cost of post-deregulation affiliation without shifting practitioner quality, and within-practitioner panel tracking with affiliation status as the moderator across the deregulation event. The test is feasible; it requires design decisions the existing literature has not made.

The institutional finding is this: if licensing institutionalizes professional identity-and the architectural features of major licensed professions suggest it does-then the deregulation policy debate has been misspecified in a particular direction. It predicts quality effects that are too uniform (ignoring the normative mechanism among the persistently affiliated) and misattributes the mechanism of professional quality maintenance to information rather than identity in ways that make quality preservation in deregulated markets seem to require only alternative signaling infrastructure. These errors are durable because the information mechanism is tractable, its policy implications are specific, and the data needed to test the normative alternative have not been collected.

## The Diagnostic as a Practitioner Tool

The information/normative distinction is not specific to the three cases audited here. It is a structural feature of any mechanism claim in which compliance is the operative behavioral unit. Any institutional account that explains compliance through monitoring, detection, signaling, or incentive alignment is invoking the information mechanism. Any account that explains compliance through professional identity, community belonging, moral internalization, or obligation is invoking the normative mechanism. The ambiguity appears whenever both families of language are in use in the same body of work without specification of which is load-bearing.

The diagnostic table provides a checklist. The operative question for any new case is not which mechanism sounds more appropriate but which mechanism's conditions are met: Is there an observability structure and cost structure that makes information-channel updating individually rational? Is there an internalization pathway and community membership that makes normative compliance identity-constitutive? In many real institutions, both conditions are partially met, and the analytical work is not to choose between the mechanisms but to specify which is load-bearing under which conditions and which is the residual.

The two uses of the diagnostic have different robustness profiles. The prescriptive use-identifying which design tools are appropriate for a new institutional installation-is relatively secure, because the transplant asymmetry forces a specific choice: the information mechanism's conditions can be constructed at design time, the normative mechanism's cannot, not at the time horizon that institutional designers typically operate. The normative mechanism is cultivable over longer scales-professional schools, ethics codes, and ritualized practice can build it over generations-but that is a different problem from what a policy designer can address within a project cycle. A designer who misidentifies which mechanism is available at the relevant time horizon will deploy the wrong instruments, and no adjustment to monitoring intensity or incentive design can substitute for community preconditions the normative mechanism requires.

The diagnostic use-identifying which mechanism is operative in a functioning institution-is less secure precisely because the normative mechanism's conditions are harder to operationalize independently of the behavioral outcomes they are meant to explain. The measurement asymmetry documented in the table discussion biases the available evidence base toward the information account. This is not a reason to abandon the diagnostic use; it is a reason to hold diagnostic conclusions with appropriate uncertainty and to treat the information account as the default requiring some evidence to overturn, not as the residual.

The cases where both uses converge most usefully are transplant settings: institutions being designed new, transferred across social contexts, or rebuilt after disruption. Here the normative mechanism is not yet established even if the institutional rules are formally in place. The information mechanism can be activated at installation time; the normative mechanism requires community pre-existence or a deliberate sequence in which community development precedes formal rule installation-at timescales that typically exceed a design cycle. The licensing finding crystallizes this: even if both mechanisms are operative in established licensed professions, the deregulation policy debate has proceeded as if only one is available, producing predictions about quality effects that are too uniform and policy prescriptions miscalibrated to the mechanism actually maintaining quality. Specifying which mechanism is load-bearing, and at which time horizon it is available, is the minimum discipline this distinction requires.

## Questions this leaves open

- **Are there scaling laws on the size of a community within which the normative mechanism remains load-bearing?.** In biology, the surface-to-volume ratio fixes the regime in which diffusion alone can sustain a body; above a threshold size, organisms require active transport - a different mechanism kicks in not because evolution selected for it but because the previous mechanism geometrically cannot scale further. The information/normative split in institutions has a candidate analogue. Monitoring-based enforcement scales linearly (perhaps worse, with attentional cost) in the size of the regulated population. Identity-based compliance, depending on whether one believes Dunbar-style cognitive limits on community recognition, may have a hard upper bound around 150–500 members, or a softer logarithmic decay as group size grows. If this is right, then "which mechanism is load-bearing" is not just a matter of which conditions are met (the draft's framing) but of which conditions *can possibly be met* at a given community size. A licensed profession of 10,000 members may operate via something that looks like the normative mechanism only because subsidiary identity structures (regional bar associations, specialty societies) carve the population into pieces small enough for community recognition to hold. The architectural prediction is then sharper than the draft's: as the regulated population grows past the scale at which face-to-face recognition is feasible, the institution must either fragment internally or revert to information-mechanism dependence. The Reformation, modern medical specialty boards, and the proliferation of subreddit moderator structures may all be instances of the same scaling response. A College piece testing this would need to operationalize "community size" (formal membership? frequency of encounter? recognition capacity?), and to find institutional cases that have grown across the candidate threshold without internal fragmentation. The negative cases - institutions that grew past the threshold and broke - would be diagnostic.
- **Does the Normative Mechanism Survive Algorithmically Mediated Institutions?.** Platform-mediated institutions - gig labor markets, platform-based professional services, algorithmically managed commons governance experiments - present a structural challenge to the normative mechanism that this piece does not address. The piece argues that the normative mechanism requires participation in norm-setting (voice), identity as a community member, and an emotional response triggered by compliance or violation. Platform-mediated institutions typically provide none of the first: norm-setting is opaque, algorithmic, and non-participatory. Whether identity and obligation can form under these conditions is not answerable from the institutional literature analyzed here. The relevant traditions are computational social science (what does community membership mean when community is algorithmically curated?) and the sociology of platform labor (what identity structures emerge among gig workers who lack formal professional membership?). If normative mechanisms cannot form in algorithmically mediated settings, the prediction is not merely that these platforms will exhibit information-mechanism failures - it is that any institutional function that depends on the normative mechanism will fail in these settings regardless of how well the information structure is designed. This has a specific policy implication for regulatory approaches to platform labor that the information-mechanism framing of most regulatory debates would miss.
- **When Can the Normative Substrate Be Cultivated Faster Than a Generation?.** The revised piece is careful to relocate the designable/non-designable contrast to a time-horizon claim: the normative mechanism is cultivable across generational scales (professional schools, ethics codes, ritualized practice) but not at the time horizon institutional designers typically operate. This is the right framing, but it leaves the interesting question untouched. There is empirical variation in how fast a normative community can form. Military boot camp, medical residency, and certain religious novitiates compress identity formation into months rather than decades; ethnographic accounts of cooperative formation in war-affected communities (Bauer et al. on conflict-induced cooperation) report measurable identity shifts on year-scale, not generational. A morphologist would say: form responds to slow constraints but not all slow constraints are equally slow, and the constraint can sometimes be relaxed by intensifying the stimulus. What features distinguish institutions where a normative substrate can be installed at compressed timescales from those where it cannot? Candidates include intensity of immersion, removal of competing identities, ritualized performance with witnesses, and stakes in mutual fate. The College has the right intellectual furniture for this question: the diagnostic table specifies what the normative mechanism requires; the open question is which of those operative conditions are amenable to accelerated cultivation and which are not. The current piece's structural finding - that the normative mechanism's installation horizon is the binding constraint - would be substantially strengthened by a companion analysis of when that horizon can be shortened, and at what cost. This is a piece a future Fellow could write directly against the framework this one establishes.
