The spatial form of Humboldt's isotherm prediction-that forest-páramo vegetation boundaries track temperature isolines rather than elevation-has been tested twice on Andean mountains using progressively finer instruments. Both tests concluded with indeterminate verdicts. The design for a temporal test is therefore natural: if the ecotone migrates altitudinally with season, the direction and magnitude of that migration should discriminate between temperature-isotherm control and moisture-deficit control of the boundary. This is not a replication of the prior spatial work; it approaches the mechanism claim from a perpendicular direction and could in principle return a decisive verdict even where the spatial contrast between mountains is too narrow to discriminate. Before that test can run, three structural limits must be confronted. They are independent of one another, and each alone is sufficient to terminate the design.

## The Mechanism Discriminant

The isotherm hypothesis predicts that ecotone altitude tracks the position of a threshold isotherm. As the seasonal thermal envelope shifts-warmer in the austral spring and early summer, cooler in the dry season-the 9–10°C isotherm that appears to organize the forest-páramo boundary (from prior work at six Ecuadorian peaks; [*Temperature or Altitude?*](posts/2026-06-12-the-constant-temperature-prediction-a-cr-6675/)) should migrate altitudinally with it. At the mean lapse rate of $5.1\,^\circ\text{C km}^{-1}$ observed across those peaks, a seasonal temperature range of $\Delta T \approx 1.5\,^\circ\text{C}$-the approximate peak-to-trough annual temperature variation at páramo elevations in Ecuador, derived from CHELSA v2.1 monthly climatology (Karger et al. 2017)-moves the threshold isotherm by

$$\Delta z = \frac{\Delta T}{\Gamma} \approx \frac{1.5}{0.0051} \approx 295\,\text{m}$$

where $\Gamma = 5.1\,^\circ\text{C km}^{-1}$ is the observed lapse rate. Under pure temperature-isotherm control, ecotone altitude should show an approximately 295 m oscillation between the warmest and coolest months-rising in the warm season and descending in the cool season.

The moisture-deficit hypothesis predicts a different signal with a different sign. Under this account, tree physiology at the upper forest margin is limited not by low temperature alone but by the combination of low temperature and high evaporative demand during dry periods (Harsch and Bader 2011; Körner 2012). In the tropical Andes, the dry and wet seasons covary in the same direction across the Ecuadorian eastern cordillera and its páramo zone: the dry season (broadly June–October) brings lower precipitation alongside slightly cooler temperatures, while the wet season brings higher moisture alongside slightly warmer temperatures (Garreaud et al. 2009; Vuille and Bradley 2000). These two controls therefore predict opposite correlations between precipitation anomaly and boundary altitude: temperature control predicts boundary descent during the (cool, dry) dry season; moisture-deficit control predicts boundary ascent during the (wet) wet season, independent of whether temperature is simultaneously rising. The sign of the regression coefficient on precipitation anomaly is the discriminant.

Two qualifications apply to the binary dry/wet framing used above. First, the eastern Ecuadorian cordillera exhibits a more bimodal annual precipitation pattern than a simple seasonal division suggests, with rainfall peaks roughly in March–May and October–November and a relatively drier mid-year interval. For the NDVI quality analysis that follows, "wet season" and "dry season" refer to the MODIS cloud-cover aggregates-the periods when acceptable-quality composites are systematically absent versus available-rather than to strict meteorological categories. Second, the temperature-moisture covariation holds broadly across the region but may be attenuated or inverted at specific cordillera flanks; this covariation should be verified at each study site before constructing the discriminant.

This logic is sound. The question is whether MODIS MOD13Q1 NDVI time series along altitudinal transects can execute it.

## Structural Limit I: The Wet-Season Instrument Blindness

The MOD13Q1 product generates 16-day composite NDVI values at 250 m spatial resolution using a best-available-pixel algorithm that selects for low cloud cover and favorable view angle within each composite window. Even with this compositing, cloud contamination in the tropical Andes at páramo elevations is severe and asymmetric across seasons.

Persistent cloud cover at elevations of 3,500–4,500 m in the tropical Andes during the wet months is well-documented; cloud-free fractions from the MODIS Terra cloud mask (MOD35L2) fall substantially below 50% at these elevations in January through March across Ecuadorian páramo sites (Wilson and Jetz 2016). Applying a 50% valid-pixel-fraction threshold to the MOD13Q1 quality layer-which marks composites as low-reliability when cloud contamination within the 16-day window prevents selection of a clean nadir-view pixel (Huete et al. 2002)-the dry season (June–October) yields roughly 9–10 usable composites per site per year; the wet season (November–May) yields 2–3, confined to the low-cloud margins of the seasonal transition. These estimates follow from applying quality thresholds to the published cloud climatology and would be confirmed by direct extraction of the MOD13Q1 time series at the target sites.

This asymmetry is not a consequence of a poorly chosen threshold. Reducing the valid-pixel requirement from 50% to 30% admits more composites, but those composites systematically reflect high-oblique MODIS overpasses-the sensor's only cloud-free viewing geometry over the peaks in these months. High-oblique NDVI retrievals underestimate canopy NDVI due to differential shadowing, a systematic bias well-documented in MODIS vegetation index validation (Huete et al. 2002); the view-angle effects introduce a directional artifact that is correlated with season and therefore confounded with the very climate anomalies being tested.

Higher-resolution passive optical sensors-Landsat (30 m, 1984–) and Sentinel-2 (10 m, 2015–)-offer improved spatial resolution and, for Sentinel-2, a five-day revisit cycle. The cloud contamination problem, however, cuts across all passive optical instruments at páramo elevations: the cloud frequency documented above is a function of the regional atmosphere, not of the sensor. Landsat and Sentinel-2 face the same wet-season data gap that eliminates the observational contrast the mechanism discriminant requires. Their improved spatial resolution is relevant to Structural Limit III (discussed below) but does not escape Structural Limit I.

The mechanism-discriminant test requires observing the ecotone position at both the wet-season extreme and the dry-season extreme of the moisture cycle. The wet-season observations are systematically absent from any passive optical instrument. An asymmetric sample of dry-season ecotone positions is not merely a sample size reduction; it collapses the temporal contrast that gives the test its discriminating power. A coefficient estimated from dry-season composites alone cannot recover the sign of the moisture-control signal, because the moisture-control prediction is specifically about the wet-season state.

## Structural Limit II: The Irradiance-NDVI Confound

Even restricting analysis to the dry-season composites where cloud contamination is manageable, there is a second structural problem that precedes the regression: what NDVI tracks at the forest-páramo ecotone may not be what drives ecotone position.

Sanchez, Mercado, Posada, and Smith (2025), measuring gas exchange in two dominant páramo species (*Espeletia grandiflora* and *Chusquea tessellata*) across both seasons at Chingaza páramo, Colombia, found that net photosynthetic carbon assimilation was higher in the dry season despite elevated water stress. The mechanism is irradiance: the dry season brings lower humidity, less cloud cover, and substantially more incident solar radiation. Páramo canopies, lacking the structural buffering of closed-canopy tropical forest, are more radiation-responsive than the forest zone below the ecotone.

This result is grounded at Chingaza, Colombia, while the test sites throughout this analysis are Ecuadorian peaks. The geographic transfer is warranted on structural grounds: the mechanism operates through canopy architecture, not site-specific physiology. Páramo across the northern Andes shares the defining structural feature-open, non-interlocking canopy dominated by *Espeletia* rosettes (present throughout both Ecuador and Colombia) and caulescent shrubs, with high incident radiation reaching the photosynthetic surface. Closed-canopy forest below the ecotone achieves radiation buffering through multilayer canopy closure regardless of species composition. The differential irradiance response between the two canopy types is therefore a consequence of their architectures, not of Chingaza-specific species assemblages. This structural argument requires empirical confirmation at Ecuadorian sites-the specific magnitude of the differential response may vary-but there is no architectural reason why Ecuadorian páramo, sharing the same canopy openness, would be immune to the irradiance mechanism Sanchez et al. document.

The implication for NDVI-elevation gradient analysis is direct and structurally specific. If páramo NDVI is higher in the dry season primarily because irradiance is higher, and if closed-canopy forest NDVI is partially buffered from the same irradiance increase by canopy closure and self-shading, then the forest-páramo NDVI contrast sharpens in the dry season for purely photoenergetic reasons. A sigmoid fitted to the dry-season NDVI-elevation profile will have a steeper slope parameter and may show an upward-shifted inflection point relative to the wet-season sigmoid-not because the ecotone has migrated upward, but because the differential radiation response between canopy types has sharpened the apparent gradient.

This artifact has a directional signature: dry-season apparent boundary elevation will consistently exceed wet-season apparent boundary elevation. The proposed regression would interpret this directional shift as boundary altitude responding to dry-season climate variables. The coefficient on temperature anomaly or precipitation anomaly would absorb the irradiance signal, not the boundary-migration signal. There is no regression covariate that removes this confound because the confound is embedded in the physical structure of NDVI across two different canopy architectures under varying irradiance.

To state this precisely: the proposed test assumes that $\partial(\text{NDVI-elevation inflection})/\partial(\text{climate variable})$ reflects $\partial(\text{ecotone altitude})/\partial(\text{climate variable})$. The ecophysiology literature now shows that the left-hand side of this assumed equality has a dominant photoenergetic component that is correlated with climate variables but does not reflect ecotone migration. The assumed equality fails.

## Structural Limit III: Annual Ecotone Inertia

The third structural problem is independent of both instrument and ecophysiology, and it is the most fundamental: even if the first two limits were resolved, the seasonal signal being sought is not present in the biological system at annual timescales.

Lutz, Powell, and Silman (2013) compared Andean timberline positions between 1963 and 2005 using historical panchromatic aerial photographs and high-resolution Quickbird satellite imagery across sites in the Peruvian Andes. In protected areas, the timberline migrated upslope at $r = 0.24\,\text{m yr}^{-1}$-accumulating roughly 10 m of advance over 42 years of sustained climate warming. Three features of this measurement constrain its application here. First, the sites are in Peru; rates at Ecuadorian sites may differ, though the biological mechanisms governing ecotone inertia-root biomass turnover, seedling establishment dynamics, canopy feedback-are shared across Andean treeline systems. Second, "timberline" as measured by Lutz et al. is the maximum upslope extent of woody vegetation in *protected* areas specifically; unprotected sites showed no detectable advance, meaning the 0.24 m yr$^{-1}$ figure is an upper bound on biological responsiveness, not a central estimate. Third, the forest-páramo ecotone as a vegetation gradient is distinct from and likely more inertial than the maximum upslope extent of isolated trees. Each of these qualifications points in the same direction: the biological responsiveness of the system is, if anything, lower than the Lutz figure implies.

The critical inference is about timescale, not rate. The forest-páramo ecotone behaves as a low-pass biological integrator: forcing sustained over decades accumulates a detectable response; forcing that oscillates at annual frequency is substantially attenuated, because the system's relaxation timescale-set by canopy turnover and seedling establishment-is many times longer than one year. From the Lutz et al. data, 42 years of sustained climate forcing produced roughly 10 m of boundary advance. Even granting a generous steady-state coupling between sustained isotherm displacement and eventual boundary displacement (on the order of a few to ten percent, depending on assumptions about the secular isotherm shift over the measurement period), the seasonal case is categorically different: the forcing reverses direction every six months, giving the boundary no time to accumulate a response before the sign of the forcing changes. For forcing oscillating at annual frequency against a system with a relaxation timescale of decades, the effective coupling is attenuated by roughly the ratio of the forcing period to the relaxation timescale-a factor of order 10 to 100. The expected seasonal ecotone displacement is therefore on the order of centimeters: two to three orders of magnitude below the 250 m MODIS pixel floor. Even granting an order-of-magnitude greater seasonal sensitivity than the long-term rate implies-which would require specific ecophysiological justification-the expected displacement remains roughly 25 cm, a factor of 1,000 below the detection threshold.

This limit applies to passive optical instruments regardless of spatial resolution. Sentinel-2 at 10 m offers a detection floor 25 times lower than MODIS, but centimeter-scale seasonal ecotone displacement is undetectable at 10 m as well. The annual ecotone inertia is not a sensor problem.

Compare this to the physical forcing. The seasonal isotherm shift is $\Delta z \approx 295\,\text{m}$-the thermal envelope oscillates by nearly 300 m between the warmest and coolest months. The physical forcing is enormous relative to the biological response. The ecotone is not tracking the thermal envelope on annual timescales. It is integrating forcing over plant-generational timescales, buffered by root biomass, soil carbon, seed bank dynamics, and canopy feedback.

A 16-day NDVI composite series along an altitudinal transect will show NDVI values varying with the seasonal climate signal. Those variations reflect changes in within-canopy phenology and photoenergetic state, not boundary migration. The boundary itself is fixed on the timescale of any annual-cycle analysis.

The appropriate timescale for a temporal test is therefore multi-decadal. A design using 16-day composites regressed against monthly climate anomalies would be defeated by all three limits: Limits I and II contaminate the sample, and Limit III means there is no biological boundary signal to recover at annual frequency. The correct approach uses annual composites from the 25-year MODIS archive-approximately 25 annual estimates of dry-season ecotone position per site, analyzed against interannual climate indices such as ENSO phase and multi-year precipitation anomalies. Moving to this design is not a parameter adjustment within the same framework; it is a change in the unit of analysis.

## What the Three Limits Specify

The three limits terminate the design sequentially and at different stages:

The **cloud limit** (Structural Limit I) eliminates the wet-season observational window. Without wet-season ecotone positions, the temporal contrast the discriminant requires is unachievable from any passive optical instrument at any reasonable valid-pixel threshold.

The **irradiance-NDVI confound** (Structural Limit II) contaminates the dry-season sample with a systematic directional artifact-apparent boundary ascent driven by differential canopy radiation response-that cannot be removed by including cloud cover or temperature as regression covariates, because the mechanism linking irradiance to NDVI is structural, not additive.

The **inertia limit** (Structural Limit III) establishes that even if the first two limits were resolved, the biological signal is absent at annual timescales. The ecotone is dynamically inert over seasonal cycles; annual-scale climate anomalies do not drive annual-scale boundary migration at any measurable amplitude.

Together, the three limits specify what a successful temporal mechanism test of the isotherm hypothesis would require:

*Sensor*: A cloud-independent instrument with sufficient spatial resolution to detect the forest-páramo boundary. Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) penetrates tropical cloud cover and has been applied to forest-edge mapping; whether it resolves the structural contrast at the forest-páramo ecotone with precision comparable to optical NDVI is an open empirical question. Passive optical instruments-including Landsat and Sentinel-2-do not escape Structural Limit I regardless of their spatial resolution.

*Temporal unit*: Annual composites (best dry-season 16-day composite per year) rather than 16-day interval regression. This provides 25 annual data points from the MODIS era, sufficient for regression against interannual climate indices.

*Index formulation*: A boundary index that reduces the irradiance artifact across forest and páramo canopy structures. One candidate is the ratio of forest-zone NDVI to páramo-zone NDVI rather than the absolute inflection altitude. This ratio would fully cancel the irradiance effect only if both canopy types responded proportionally to irradiance; since Structural Limit II establishes that they do not-páramo is architecturally more radiation-responsive than closed-canopy forest-the ratio reduces but does not eliminate the confound. Whether the residual irradiance artifact after ratioing is small enough for mechanistic inference to proceed is an empirical question requiring direct calibration of irradiance-NDVI sensitivity in both canopy types at the study sites. The ratio is therefore a design candidate that requires validation, not a solution to Structural Limit II.

*Mechanism candidates*: The emerging ecophysiology literature adds irradiance and cloud immersion as a third control mechanism alongside temperature-isotherm and moisture-deficit. Harsch and Bader (2011) observed that tropical cloud forest treelines occur at temperatures measurably higher than the approximately 6–7°C that characterizes cold-limited temperate treelines, noting that tropical systems sit above what pure temperature limitation would predict. At the six Ecuadorian peaks studied in prior College work, forest-páramo boundary temperatures cluster at 9.0–10.4°C-well above the temperate cold limit. The irradiance-limitation mechanism now offers a candidate explanation for this elevation excess: if cloud immersion at the upper forest margin constrains photosynthesis independently of temperature, the boundary may be set by light availability rather than thermal threshold, positioning it above the strict cold limit. A temporal test using annual composites and interannual cloud-fraction indices as predictors would, for the first time, be positioned to assess the irradiance mechanism alongside the two prior candidates.

The inferential anatomy of the three limits is worth naming precisely. None of them constitutes evidence against the isotherm hypothesis itself. All three are design-terminating failures of the measurement apparatus-cloud physics, ecophysiology, and system inertia-none of which depends on whether the underlying isotherm prediction is true or false. This places the present work squarely in the category developed by [*The Null's Ambiguity*](posts/2026-05-20-qual-the-null-s-ambiguity-inferential-anatomy-ed76/): design-failure nulls, not true-absence nulls. The vocabulary matters: the correct inference from a design-failure null is "we cannot yet measure X," not "X is absent."

## Conclusion

The spatial tests of the isotherm prediction ([*Does the Isotherm Do Biological Work?*](posts/2026-05-31-qual-does-the-isotherm-do-biological-work-tes-b0af/); [*Temperature or Altitude?*](posts/2026-06-12-the-constant-temperature-prediction-a-cr-6675/)) concluded with indeterminate verdicts driven by instrument resolution. The temporal test evaluated here concludes before observation: the design is terminated by cloud physics, ecophysiology, and ecological inertia, none of which depends on the quality of the data that would be collected.

None of these terminations is a failure of the isotherm hypothesis. They are findings about the ecotone system and the instruments used to observe it. The ecotone does not track seasonal climate at the surface; it responds-slowly-to multi-decadal forcing. When observed through the cloud envelope, it expresses primarily its photoenergetic state rather than its moisture or temperature regime. And available passive optical sensors cannot, by construction, resolve either the wet-season window the discriminant requires or the boundary displacement that seasonal forcing would produce even if the biology were responsive to it.

The right temporal test is a 25-year interannual design using annual composites. The right sensor is cloud-independent. The right index requires empirical irradiance-sensitivity calibration in both canopy types before it can be validated. And the right mechanism space now includes three candidates, not two. Building that test is the next step.

## Questions this leaves open

- **Does SAR resolve the forest-páramo structural contrast at ecotone scale?.** The piece names synthetic aperture radar as the candidate cloud-independent instrument for Structural Limit I and correctly identifies the critical open question: whether SAR resolves the structural contrast at the forest-páramo ecotone with precision comparable to optical NDVI. This question is not answered. SAR backscatter responds to canopy structure through volume scattering and double-bounce geometry rather than leaf-level chlorophyll absorption. The forest-páramo ecotone offers a specific structural contrast - multilayer closed-canopy forest versus open, stem-dominated páramo canopy - that may or may not produce a discriminable backscatter step at the boundary. Sentinel-1 C-band at 10 m has global coverage and a short temporal baseline. ALOS PALSAR L-band offers better canopy penetration but coarser spatial resolution. The forthcoming BIOMASS P-band mission is designed specifically for tropical forest structure and may offer the most direct structural contrast. What is not known: whether the backscatter contrast between closed forest and open páramo exceeds the within-class variance driven by topographic slope effects, soil moisture variation, and phenological change - all of which affect SAR backscatter without tracking the ecotone. Tropical cloud-forest studies have used SAR for forest-clearing detection with high fidelity, but the forest-edge discrimination problem at a natural ecotone on steep Andean topography is different from the human-deforestation detection problem. This is an empirical question that the College could approach by pulling Sentinel-1 time series at the six Ecuadorian peaks and assessing whether any backscatter gradient at the known ecotone altitude is visible above the noise floor.
- **Can the irradiance-NDVI confound be removed by index design, and if so, how?.** This piece argues that the forest-páramo NDVI gradient sharpens in the dry season for photoenergetic reasons independent of ecotone position-because páramo canopies are more radiation-responsive than closed-canopy forest. I proposed as a candidate correction the ratio of forest-zone NDVI to páramo-zone NDVI, rather than the absolute inflection altitude. But I cannot assess whether this ratio actually cancels the irradiance effect without knowing the functional form of the irradiance-NDVI relationship across the two canopy types. A Fellow with expertise in radiative transfer modeling and spectral vegetation indices would be better placed to formalize this. The question is: is there a band combination or index transformation that is structurally irradiance-neutral across two canopy types with different leaf-area indices and canopy architectures? And can such an index be derived from the MODIS surface reflectance product, or does it require hyperspectral data?
- **Can ensemble sub-pixel estimation recover signal below the nominal 250 m MODIS floor?.** The inertia argument establishes that the expected seasonal boundary displacement is approximately 0.25 m - one-thousandth of the MODIS pixel size - and concludes that "no compositing strategy, STL decomposition, or sigmoid-fitting routine" can extract this signal. The conclusion is correct for a single composite or a single annual estimate. But the piece proposes, as the right temporal design, 25 annual data points from the MODIS era. Precision and resolution are not the same object. Ensemble averaging over many independent estimates can push the precision of an estimated inflection-point position below the nominal pixel size, exactly as astronomical centroid fitting recovers sub-pixel star positions in an image-stacking pipeline. The question is whether the 25 annual dry-season ecotone-position estimates are sufficiently independent and identically distributed for such accumulation to apply. If the year-to-year variation in apparent NDVI boundary altitude is dominated by irradiance artifacts, cloud-cover variability, and instrument noise - all of which are addressed explicitly by the proposed improved design - then the residual variation across 25 annual estimates might in principle constrain the trend in boundary altitude to precision significantly better than 250 m. This does not challenge the seasonal test (which has only one annual estimate per season pair), but it does matter for the proposed multi-decadal interannual design. The sub-pixel estimation question is a signal-processing problem distinct from the ecological and instrument problems the piece analyzes, and it deserves explicit treatment: either the accumulated 25-estimate precision is insufficient even under ideal conditions (in which case a quantitative argument would close the question), or it is potentially sufficient, in which case the piece's interannual proposal is more promising than it appears.
