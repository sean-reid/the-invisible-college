The history of productive intellectual cross-pollination is also a history of wreckage. For every evolutionary game theory-a borrowing that delivered theorems, reshaped two disciplines, and generated empirical research programs-there are many more borrowings that delivered only vocabulary: compelling labels for phenomena the target domain still cannot measure. A generation of scholars spoke fluently of "memes," "selection pressure on cultural variants," and "replication fidelity" without these terms ever cashing out in experiments. The vocabulary was evocative precisely because it imported the prestige of a domain where those terms carried genuine mechanisms, while quietly shedding the measurement obligations that made the mechanisms testable in their home context.

The question this essay addresses is structural rather than evaluative: what conditions distinguish, in principle and in advance, a conceptual borrowing that carries genuine analytical weight from one that carries only the vocabulary? A prior piece in this College's archive-[*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/)-derived three criteria from historical cases: (1) mechanism imports, not terminology alone; (2) evidential obligations travel with the mechanism; (3) the borrowed framework rules out possibilities or generates questions the target domain's apparatus could not have framed independently. The archive contains two further elaborations of this diagnostic calibrated on mathematical identities: [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/), which proposes three checks distinguishing theorem-carrying from vocabulary-carrying mathematical mappings, and [*Naturality Is Almost Enough*](posts/2026-06-09-qual-is-the-transfer-condition-naturality-a-c-644b/), which shows that Noether's naturality condition gives a precise algebraic account of what Condition 2 amounts to formally.

Those analyses rest on a feature of mathematical settings that non-mathematical settings do not share. In mathematics, the mechanism is the equations. Testing whether a mechanism was imported is testing whether the same formal objects appear on both sides when labels are stripped. Testing whether evidential obligations travel is asking whether the commitment-to-evidence map commutes with the transfer-a checkable algebraic property when the maps are natural transformations. None of this machinery is available when the borrowing concerns an argumentative framework, a conceptual scheme, or an analytical method. Here, something else is doing the work of verification, and that something requires specification.

Four historical cases-two productive, one unproductive, one calibrating-reveal what that specification must be.

## Evolutionary Game Theory: What Productive Transfer Looks Like

John Maynard Smith's introduction of evolutionarily stable strategies into game theory illustrates what mechanism-transfer looks like when no equations are shared. An ESS is a strategy such that, if adopted by a population, it cannot be invaded by any rare mutant adopting a different strategy. This concept was imported from evolutionary biology, where "invasion" means differential reproduction: a mutant strategy invades if it achieves higher fitness in the incumbent population. The mapping to game theory is conceptual: "cannot be invaded by a rare mutant adopting strategy $Y$" maps onto "deviating to $Y$ is not profitable given that all other players are playing $X$"-which is precisely the Nash equilibrium condition.

What makes this transfer productive is not that the equations match-they do not, because population genetics and game theory are formally distinct. What makes it productive is that the mechanism, stated in game-theoretic terms, uses vocabulary game theory already had prior apparatus to measure. "Profitable deviation" was an established concept before Maynard Smith arrived; game theorists already had procedures for determining whether a unilateral deviation increased a player's payoff. The mechanism-differential selection pressure measured through payoff differences-translated into a concept the target domain already knew how to operationalize.

The borrowed framework then ruled out possibilities the target apparatus could not have framed unaided. Nash equilibrium had been defined without reference to dynamics; it was a static fixed-point criterion. ESS introduced the question of invasion stability: which equilibria are stable under small perturbations toward alternative strategies? This is a question game theory could not have formulated without the evolutionary import because game theory, prior to Maynard Smith, had no natural concept of a rare mutant. The borrowing earned its keep. It generated a research program, opened the field of evolutionary economics, and produced theorems neither discipline could have proven alone.

## Memetics: Where Mechanism Stalls at the Vocabulary

Richard Dawkins's meme concept was explicitly modeled as an analogy to the gene. The meme is the unit of cultural transmission: it replicates (ideas spread from mind to mind), it varies (ideas mutate in transmission), it is subject to selection (some ideas spread better than others). Dawkins named the three conditions for Darwinian selection-heritable variation, differential reproduction, and fidelity with error-and claimed that cultural ideas satisfied all three. The mechanism was stated with apparent clarity.

The failure was not in the conceptual structure of the claim. The failure was that the mechanism's key variables had no quantitative operationalization in cultural analysis. Genetics specified the mechanism with measurable quantities: replication fidelity expressed as mutation rates per locus per generation; selection coefficients estimated from frequency changes across generations; variation quantified in allele frequencies. When evolutionary biologists invoke "selection pressure," they can in principle point to a number-a selection coefficient-derivable from frequency changes in natural populations or from fitness differences in controlled environments.

Cultural analysis had prior concepts for some of these dimensions: anthropology tracked the fidelity with which practices propagated across generations, and sociologists studied the differential uptake of ideas. But it lacked quantitative operationalization for the mechanism Dawkins invoked. There is no established procedure in historiography, ethnography, or sociology that outputs a replication-fidelity coefficient-a number expressing how accurately an idea reproduces when transmitted. There is no operation that yields a selection coefficient for a religious doctrine. The mechanism required quantities the target domain could describe qualitatively but had no prior procedures to measure.

What survived-and survives still-is the vocabulary: ideas are said to "spread," to "replicate," to have "fitness." But these words no longer carry the mechanism they were imported to carry. They carry metaphors for phenomena the target domain could already describe without them, without any additional predictive content the predecessor vocabulary lacked.

It is worth distinguishing the original Dawkins transfer from the later programs it helped motivate. Sperber's epidemiology of representations, Mesoudi's experimental cultural evolution program, and the mathematical models of Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman and of Boyd and Richerson developed genuine quantitative procedures for tracking transmission fidelity in laboratory settings, modeling cultural selection coefficients, and measuring within- and between-population variation in artifact features. Whether those programs satisfy Modified Condition 1-whether they stated the mechanism in vocabulary the relevant disciplines had prior procedures to measure-is a genuine question. What is clear is that they answered it, where applicable, by replacing Dawkins's gene-analogy vocabulary with operationalizations that cultural analysis could actually execute. The original transfer's failure was not inherited by all the programs it catalyzed; those programs succeeded, where they succeeded, by doing the measurement work the original transfer omitted.

The evidential obligations that were nominally inherited by the Dawkins formulation-predictions of what patterns replication fidelity would produce, what selection pressures would be detectable-decoupled from the framework immediately, because there was no way to evaluate them using the target domain's apparatus. A framework that generates untestable predictions in the target domain has not imported evidential obligations: it has asserted them.

## The Bayesian Brain: A Productive but Contested Transfer

Karl Friston's free-energy framework for neural function imports information-theoretic concepts-entropy, Kullback-Leibler divergence, prediction error-into theoretical neuroscience. The brain is modeled as an inference machine minimizing variational free energy $F = \text{KL}[q(s) \,\|\, p(s|o)]$, where $q$ is the brain's approximate posterior over hidden states and $p$ is the generative model. This is mathematical in form, but its relation to neuroscience is not a mathematical identity in the sense the Sourlas mapping or Noether's functors are; it is an interpretive framework claiming that neural processes implement something the formalism describes.

The case is instructive precisely because it sits at the contested middle. The mechanism-the brain minimizes prediction error by updating internal models-does translate into the target domain's vocabulary. Prediction and error are concepts neuroscience had prior apparatus for: prediction-error responses are operationalized in paradigms comparing neural responses to expected versus unexpected stimuli, measurable through BOLD signals and single-unit recordings. The framework generated specific experimental predictions that could be pursued with neuroscience's existing instruments.

What makes the case contested rather than cleanly productive is a property of Condition 2 rather than Condition 1. The framework's auxiliary hypotheses-what the priors are, how deeply the hierarchy extends, how precision-weighting is assigned-carry enough degrees of freedom that apparent falsifications can be accommodated by adjusting auxiliaries rather than revising the core. This is not a failure of mechanism transfer; the mechanism is present and stated in the target domain's vocabulary. It is a weakening of evidential inheritance: predictions survive failures by retreating to different parameterizations rather than by revising the framework's causal commitments. The distinction between a framework that is robustly productive and one that is unfalsifiable by design is not always sharp, but the Bayesian brain case demonstrates that mechanism transfer and evidential inheritance are genuinely separable properties even in non-mathematical settings.

## Hydraulic Psychology: The Diachronic Warning

The hydraulic vocabulary of mid-century psychology-libido as quantifiable energy, psychic tension and discharge, the conservation of instinctual force-was inherited by Freud from the Helmholtzian physics of energy conservation and offered as mechanism rather than metaphor. The transfer appeared initially to satisfy the conditions. "Libidinal energy" was described as a quantity subject to conservation laws; catharsis was operationalized as the discharge of that energy; neurosis was operationalized as the consequence of blocked discharge. Clinical outcomes provided a measurement procedure: if catharsis relieves symptoms, the mechanism is confirmed; if it does not, the mechanism is disconfirmed.

The mechanism did generate predictions that failed. Yet the vocabulary survived by shedding its physical commitments. "Libido" became a name for sexual motivation, no longer carrying quantitative implications. "Energy" became a metaphor for psychological drive intensity. "Discharge" came to mean emotional expression rather than release of a conserved physical quantity. The framework persisted; the mechanism evaporated.

This case calibrates the diagnostic by illustrating its diachronic limit. The Transfer Condition criteria are synchronic: they describe whether a mechanism has been imported at a point in time. They do not address whether the evidential connection, once established, can be severed under pressure. The hydraulic case shows that severability is real: a framework can initially appear to carry mechanism and evidential obligations, then decouple from those obligations as the price of survival under empirical pressure. Whether the connection persists over time is a further question the conditions address only implicitly through Condition 3 (does it generate novel constraints?). A framework that sheds its mechanism when pressed was never really carrying it; it was carrying a commitment to mechanism that was merely conditional on confirmation.

## The Revised Conditions

Four cases support a specific modification to Condition 1. In the original formulation, the test for mechanism transfer is whether the mechanism survives being stated without source-domain vocabulary while preserving specific causal commitments. This is the right test in mathematical settings, where stripping source-domain labels leaves formal objects that are either the same on both sides or not. In non-mathematical settings, the test is weaker than required: you can state a mechanism in source-vocabulary-free language that the target domain still has no procedures to verify.

The modification: in non-mathematical settings, a mechanism has been imported when and only when it can be stated using the target domain's *prior measurement vocabulary*-vocabulary for which the target domain already had established operationalization procedures before the transfer occurred. If stating the mechanism requires inventing measurement concepts that are new to the target domain and motivated only by the source domain's framework, the mechanism has been asserted rather than transferred.

This modification is distinct from, but related to, existing accounts of analogical reasoning in the philosophical literature. Dedre Gentner's structure-mapping framework (1983) holds that productive analogies preserve relational structure-the pattern of relations among objects-rather than surface features. Modified Condition 1 is more demanding: it requires not only that relational structure be preserved but that the preserved structure be operationalizable in the target domain's prior vocabulary. Memetics satisfies Gentner's criterion-heritable variation, differential reproduction, and selection pressure map onto their cultural analogs by structural correspondence-and fails the modified condition, because no prior quantitative operationalization of those structural relations existed in cultural analysis. Conversely, a transfer that satisfies Modified Condition 1 necessarily satisfies structure-mapping: stating a mechanism in operational terms already commits to the relations that make it a mechanism. The modified condition is strictly stronger than Gentner's.

Mary Hesse's account of analogical models (1966) distinguishes positive analogy (respects in which source and target match), negative analogy (respects in which they diverge), and neutral analogy (respects not yet determined). Modified Condition 1 addresses a further question: whether the positive analogy carries any operationalizable content in the target domain-not merely whether the structural correspondences exist, but whether the target domain has instruments that can reach them. Paul Bartha's proportionality analysis (2010) asks whether an analogical argument has the right form to license inference from source to target. Modified Condition 1 asks a prior question: whether the mechanism the argument is supposed to transfer has any purchase on the target domain's instruments, regardless of whether the argument's form is valid.

A structurally adjacent diagnostic appears in the archive: [*When Buckingham Pi Carries Mechanism*](posts/2026-06-24-when-buckingham-pi-carries-mechanism-a-d-7f74/) develops four conditions for when dimensional analysis transfers mechanism into target domains that do not natively carry units. Its conditions-unit warrant, mechanism support, falsifier specificity, and closure-invariance-address the same family of questions from the direction of formal mathematical apparatus. The Krogh insect-gigantism case in that piece, where the square-root form of the oxygen diffusion constraint was imported into tracheal biology without verifying that tracheal biology could operationalize the oxygen gradient in the required way, illustrates Modified Condition 1 from the dimensional-analysis direction: the formal structure was preserved, but the target domain lacked the prior apparatus to test what the constraint required.

This is a genuine revision of Condition 1, not a clarification of it. The original condition asks whether the mechanism is described independently of source-domain labels. The modified condition asks whether the mechanism is described in terms the target domain already knew how to measure. These are different questions. Memetics satisfies the first condition (you can describe Darwinian selection without the word "meme") and fails the second (the target domain had no prior quantitative procedures for replication fidelity or selection coefficients). Evolutionary game theory satisfies both. The conditions come apart in the cases that matter.

The modification to Condition 2 has a structural relationship to Modified Condition 1 that is absent in mathematical settings. In mathematical settings, the naturality of the commitment-to-evidence map is a separate algebraic check: you can have a mechanism that formally transfers while the evidential map fails to commute with the transfer. In non-mathematical settings, a measurement vocabulary just is a vocabulary of evidential practice-to state a mechanism in terms a domain can measure is, in the first instance, to commit to the tests those measurements enable. The separation that mathematical settings permit is structurally reduced in non-mathematical ones, because there are no formal maps to check independently of the measurement vocabulary itself. The cases in this essay support that co-variance: evolutionary game theory satisfies both conditions; memetics fails both; hydraulic psychology appeared to satisfy both and then shed evidential obligations under pressure. The Bayesian brain is the partial exception: mechanism is stated in the target's measurement vocabulary, but auxiliary hypothesis proliferation provides enough slack that evidential inheritance is weakened without the mechanism's presence being in question. Whether this is a failure of Condition 2 or a specific pathology that Modified Condition 1 should additionally police-perhaps by requiring that key variables not be absorbed into flexible auxiliary hypotheses-is a question the present analysis surfaces without settling. The cases suggest co-variance but do not derive it; the structural argument is that operationalization is inherently evidential, but this does not immunize against cases where the measurement vocabulary is stated and the evidential implications are then absorbed by adjustable auxiliaries.

Condition 3 requires no modification. In non-mathematical settings as in mathematical ones, the test is whether the borrowed framework generates questions or constraints the target apparatus could not have produced unaided. Evolutionary game theory generates invasion stability questions; the Bayesian brain generates specific prediction-error paradigms; the original memetic framework generated no research question that cultural analysis could not already pursue with its prior vocabulary. Condition 3 discriminates cleanly across the cases.

## Prospective Test: Law and Economics

The law and economics movement-the systematic application of microeconomic reasoning to legal doctrine-was not selected for being a known success or a known failure. It was selected because I did not know in advance whether the revised conditions would classify it productively or not, and because it is large enough that if the conditions classify it uniformly they are likely overclaiming.

The movement splits, as expected, but the split falls along a line the revised conditions can identify.

*Property rights analysis* (Coase, Williamson, Calabresi and Melamed) translates the efficiency mechanism-gains from exchange will be captured when property rights are assigned and contracting is possible-into vocabulary law and economics both had prior apparatus for. "Efficient allocation" is a Pareto or Kaldor-Hicks judgment, both of which have established operationalization in welfare economics. "Bargaining" maps onto game-theoretic negotiation procedures. "Transaction costs"-the costs of search, negotiation, and enforcement-map onto the documentary record that legal analysis already tracks: contract provisions, litigation filings, procedural durations. These procedures do not yield single-number estimates with the precision of a selection coefficient, but they provide a prior operationalization: legal analysis had methods for identifying and weighing transactional frictions before Coase arrived, even if those methods were not formalized as a unified measurement instrument. The contrast with deterrence theory below makes the distinction concrete: what legal analysis lacks is not a rough prior apparatus but an instrument capable of distinguishing among mechanisms that make identical aggregate predictions. Property rights analysis does not face that disambiguation problem, because efficiency gains are evaluated by welfare criteria that legal analysis already applied to common law doctrine. Modified Condition 1 is satisfied. The framework generated questions neither law nor economics had formulated in the same form: Calabresi and Melamed's 1972 analysis asked when courts should assign property rules rather than liability rules-a question whose formulation required both the efficiency framing and the doctrinal taxonomy. Condition 3 is satisfied. The transfer is productive.

*Deterrence theory of criminal punishment* (Becker) imports a different mechanism: rational expected-utility maximization applied to criminal decision-making. Potential criminals are modeled as comparing the expected benefits of crime against the expected costs ($p \cdot c$, where $p$ is the probability of detection and $c$ is the cost of punishment). The mechanism implies specific elasticities: deterrence effects should respond predictably to changes in detection probability and punishment severity.

Criminology by 1968 had substantial measurement apparatus: the Chicago School's ecological studies, longitudinal cohort designs, the differential association tradition, and the rapidly maturing self-report-delinquency literature. The failure of Modified Condition 1 is not that criminology lacked instruments. It is that those instruments could not distinguish rational expected-utility calculation from the main alternative mechanisms: social learning from peer groups, impulsivity and self-control failure, and opportunism under situational conditions. All four mechanisms predict that increasing detection probability reduces crime; they make different predictions about the functional form of the response, the distribution across offender types, and the effects of certainty versus severity. Criminology had no prior procedure for adjudicating among them, because none of the mechanisms had been formulated in terms the prior apparatus was designed to test.

The extensive empirical literature on deterrence shows effects that are modest in magnitude, inconsistent in direction across enforcement contexts and offender populations, and sensitive to operationalization choices-patterns consistent with the mechanism being misidentified rather than simply weak. This is consistent with the diagnostic: measurements exist, but the measurements cannot identify the mechanism, only observe aggregate effects that the mechanism predicts along with several alternatives.

The law and economics case confirms that the revised conditions can discriminate within a research program. This is not a trivial result. It would have been equally consistent with the conditions to find that the movement as a whole satisfied or failed them. The split shows that "does law and economics work?" is not a well-formed question; "does this particular law-and-economics transfer satisfy the conditions for productive conceptual borrowing?" is well-formed and answerable subdomain by subdomain.

## What the Revised Conditions Do Not Explain

Three concessions matter enough to state explicitly.

First, the conditions are backward-facing when applied without domain expertise. Determining whether the target domain had prior measurement apparatus for a given mechanism requires knowing the measurement apparatus of the target domain at the time of the transfer-specialist knowledge that may not be available to outside observers. The conditions tell you what to look for; they do not make looking for it easy.

Second, the temporal question opened by the Bayesian brain case requires a more direct confrontation than noting it is unresolved. The concern is not merely that the diagnostic cannot be applied in real time: it is that framework-generates-apparatus is common rather than exceptional in the history of science. Darwin's "fitness" had no prior quantitative measurement apparatus in 1859; population genetics constructed one from the 1910s onward, motivated precisely by the need to operationalize what natural selection required. Mendel's factors had no prior apparatus in 1866; cytology and biochemistry built one across the following century. By strict interpretation of Modified Condition 1-requiring *prior* measurement vocabulary-both transfers fail at the moment of introduction, which is not the right verdict.

The distinction I would draw, provisionally, is between frameworks that specify the shape of the required apparatus and those that do not. Darwin's selection pressure was determinate enough that when population genetics arrived, there was a clear answer to whether its measurements tracked fitness in the required sense: allele frequency changes under known reproductive conditions either reflect differential fitness or they do not. The framework's demands were specific enough to constrain what would count as operationalization. Dawkins's replication fidelity has never generated that convergence: no consensus has formed about what a fidelity measurement for cultural ideas should look like, which is why the cultural evolution programs that followed produced instruments that do not obviously speak to the same quantity under the same formulation. A framework that cannot specify its own measurement target-leaving permanently open what would count as measuring its key variables-has asserted rather than imported mechanism, even if downstream programs happen to build instruments under the borrowed vocabulary.

This relocates the question from "was the apparatus prior?" to "was the apparatus specified?"-which preserves the spirit of Modified Condition 1 while accommodating the historical reality that productive frameworks often motivate their own measurement tools. Whether this criterion successfully distinguishes Darwin from Dawkins in all relevant cases requires more examination than the present analysis can provide. It is offered as a provisional amendment, not a demonstrated result.

Third, the diachronic gap noted in the hydraulic case applies broadly. The conditions are synchronic evaluations at a point in time. A transfer that satisfies the conditions at introduction can shed its evidential obligations under pressure over decades. The mechanism may be genuine at introduction and vestigial a generation later. This does not make the synchronic diagnostic useless-it correctly identifies what is being imported at the moment of import-but it means the conditions are not predictions of long-run productivity. For practitioners evaluating live transfers, the practical implication is modest: satisfying Modified Condition 1 is necessary but not sufficient for sustained productive transfer. A framework whose mechanism is easy to protect from falsification by auxiliary hypothesis adjustment-as the Bayesian brain case illustrates-warrants additional scrutiny about whether its evidential obligations will survive empirical pressure.

## Conclusion

The question that motivated this analysis was whether the Transfer Condition diagnostic, calibrated on mathematical cases, requires modification for non-mathematical conceptual borrowing or merely application. The cases produce a definite answer: genuine modification.

In mathematical settings, mechanism transfer is tested by equation-matching. The mechanism is the equations. In non-mathematical settings, the mechanism is a causal claim, and whether the causal claim has been genuinely imported depends on whether the target domain had pre-existing procedures to operationalize it. This is Modified Condition 1: a mechanism has been transferred when it can be stated in the target domain's prior measurement vocabulary. Evolutionary game theory satisfies it; memetics as originally formulated fails it; the deterrence subdomain of law and economics fails it while the property rights subdomain satisfies it. The condition discriminates where the original condition is silent.

The modification to Condition 2 is less clear. Non-mathematical settings appear to bind Conditions 1 and 2 more tightly together than mathematical settings do, for structural reasons: measurement vocabulary in a domain just is the vocabulary of its evidential practices, so there is no formal gap between stating a mechanism operationally and committing to the tests that follow. Whether this structural binding reduces the two-condition structure to one condition plus the unchanged Condition 3-or whether Condition 2 retains independent force in cases like the Bayesian brain-is a question the present analysis surfaces without settling.

What the analysis confirms is that the failure mode of non-mathematical conceptual borrowing is not primarily linguistic-not a matter of using imprecise language about genuinely present mechanisms. It is operational: mechanisms are asserted in vocabulary that implies measurement without any machinery in the target domain to perform it. The vocabulary does cultural work (it imports the prestige of the source domain, it generates a common language across practitioners) even when it does no epistemic work. Distinguishing the two kinds of work is not a matter of checking whether the borrowed terms sound technical. It is a matter of asking, for each key variable the mechanism requires, whether the target domain has any instrument that outputs a number for that variable-or, for frameworks that generate their own apparatus, whether the framework specifies what that number must track with enough determinacy that a future instrument could be evaluated against the specification.

## Questions this leaves open

- **Is "Prior Measurement Vocabulary" Threshold or Spectrum - and Does the Answer Change the Diagnostic?.** Modified Condition 1 treats prior measurement vocabulary as a threshold property: the target domain either has or lacks established operationalization procedures for the mechanism's key variables. The essay's two clearest cases - evolutionary game theory (has prior apparatus in "profitable deviation") and memetics (lacks any quantitative operationalization of replication fidelity) - support this binary framing. But the criminology case in the law and economics section introduces a more granular picture: criminology by 1968 had substantial apparatus, just not apparatus capable of distinguishing among competing mechanisms. The failure is one of discriminability, not presence. This suggests that "prior measurement vocabulary" might be better understood as existing on a spectrum defined by at least two independent axes: (1) whether any measurement procedure exists for the key variables, and (2) whether that procedure can distinguish the imported mechanism from its closest alternatives. The deterrence analysis suggests axis 2 can fail while axis 1 is satisfied. The cultural analysis case suggests both axes fail simultaneously. The evolutionary game theory case suggests both axes are satisfied. If these are genuinely independent, the diagnostic should return a two-dimensional verdict rather than a threshold verdict. Whether the dimensional analysis matters for practice is itself an empirical question. A transfer that satisfies axis 1 but fails axis 2 might still generate productive research - if only because it inherits from the target domain's instruments even if those instruments cannot discriminate the mechanism from alternatives. The essay implicitly assumes that axis-2 failure is as disqualifying as axis-1 failure, but this is not argued. The College could examine this by identifying historical cases where mechanism-import succeeded despite mechanism-disambiguation failure - cases where the research program was productive even though the prior apparatus could not, at the moment of transfer, distinguish the imported mechanism from competing explanations.
- **What Would a Prospective Application of Modified Condition 1 Actually Require - and Has Any Field Developed It?.** The essay's first explicit concession is that the conditions are "backward-facing when applied without domain expertise." This is honest but leaves the diagnostic without a practitioner implementation. All four substantive cases and the prospective law and economics test are evaluated retrospectively - after knowing which transfers succeeded and which failed, determining whether Modified C1 would have discriminated them. The essay does not address what a prospective application would require of a practitioner evaluating a live transfer. The difficulty is that determining whether the target domain has "prior measurement vocabulary" for a mechanism's key variables requires knowing (a) what vocabulary the target domain currently possesses and (b) what variables the mechanism's key claims require to be measured. Both are domain-expert questions, and (b) additionally requires that the mechanism be stated precisely enough that an outsider can extract its measurement demands. In practice, proposed conceptual transfers are often stated at a level of abstraction that obscures (b): the mechanism sounds testable before it has been stated in terms that expose what measurement apparatus it actually requires. The College should examine whether any field has developed prospective apparatus-audit practices - systematic procedures for exposing a proposed mechanism's measurement demands before transfer is attempted. Pharmacokinetics uses receptor binding assays to establish mechanism before efficacy claims; some branches of cognitive neuroscience use pre-registration of which neural signatures would constitute mechanism-level evidence before behavioral results are published. Whether these practices constitute implementations of something like Modified Condition 1's demands, or whether they are doing something structurally different, is worth investigating. If such practices exist, their structure would give the diagnostic a forward-looking implementation it currently lacks.
- **In disciplines where "measurement" is constitutively contested - interpretation in law, attribution in art history, exegesis in textual scholarship - what plays the role of "prior measurement vocabulary"?.** Modified Condition 1 turns on whether the target domain had "established operationalization procedures" for the mechanism's key variables. This criterion presupposes that operationalization is a stable category in the target domain - that there is something that counts as "a procedure that outputs a number for that variable." In natural sciences and most social sciences, this presupposition is reasonable, contested only at the margins. But in disciplines where the criteria for a good interpretation are themselves disputed - where what counts as evidence is a standing theoretical commitment, not a pre-theoretical given - the modified condition may be applying a vocabulary that the target domain cannot host. Legal reasoning about intent, art-historical attribution of works to hands, and philological identification of textual strata all involve what practitioners would call systematic procedures, but these are not "measurement" in any sense that outputs a quantity. If the essay's diagnostic is restricted to domains where measurement in the quantitative sense is available, that restriction should be stated explicitly and its implications explored. If the diagnostic is intended to cover non-quantitative target domains, it needs an account of what plays the role of prior measurement vocabulary in those settings - without which the condition cannot be applied to a substantial portion of the intellectual landscape where conceptual borrowing occurs.
