The transfer condition asks when an argumentative borrowing carries its conclusions across domains rather than just its terminology. Three informal criteria - mechanism rather than vocabulary, evidential inheritance, non-trivial constraint - have been proposed for it. They look like they should reduce to a single algebraic statement: that a commitment-to-evidence map commutes with the transfer. This piece tests the reduction by explicit construction. The result is split. The middle criterion, evidential inheritance, is exactly naturality in a refined category whose evidential morphisms preserve observational content. The first and third criteria do not collapse into it - they are independent conditions of different kinds, a precondition on existence and a non-degeneracy constraint. The three together do not compress to one.

## The category

Let a *domain of inquiry* $D$ be a triple
$$D = (\mathcal{M}_D,\ \mathcal{E}_D,\ r_D)$$
where $\mathcal{M}_D$ is a set of *mechanisms* (causal or inferential structures the domain countenances), $\mathcal{E}_D$ is a set of *evidential acts* (procedures whose outcomes can confirm or refute claims), and
$$r_D : \mathcal{M}_D \longrightarrow 2^{\mathcal{E}_D}$$
is the *commitment map* assigning to each mechanism the set of evidential acts the mechanism is accountable to.

A *transfer* from $D$ to $D'$ is a pair $(\phi, \psi)$ with $\phi: \mathcal{M}_D \to \mathcal{M}_{D'}$ on mechanisms and $\psi: \mathcal{E}_D \to \mathcal{E}_{D'}$ on evidential acts. Composition is componentwise. This is the category **Dom**.

Two functors $\mathbf{Dom} \to \mathbf{Set}$ are then in play. The *mechanism functor* $M$ sends a domain to $\mathcal{M}_D$ and a morphism to $\phi$. The *evidence-powerset functor* $E$ sends a domain to $2^{\mathcal{E}_D}$ and a morphism to the direct-image map $2^\psi$. The commitment map $r$ is a candidate natural transformation $M \Rightarrow E$.

## The first attempt fails

Naturality of $r$ along $(\phi, \psi)$ is the demand that, for every $m \in \mathcal{M}_S$,
$$r_T(\phi(m)) \;=\; 2^\psi(r_S(m)).$$
Said in a diagram:

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
\mathcal{M}_S & \xrightarrow{\;\phi\;} & \mathcal{M}_T \\
{\scriptstyle r_S}\!\downarrow & & \downarrow\!{\scriptstyle r_T} \\
2^{\mathcal{E}_S} & \xrightarrow{\;2^\psi\;} & 2^{\mathcal{E}_T}
\end{array}
$$

The picture is right; the category, as just stated, is wrong. Morphisms in **Dom** are arbitrary pairs of set functions, and so for any $\phi$ one can construct a $\psi$ that makes the square commute trivially: send every element of $\mathcal{E}_S$ to a single element of $r_T(\phi(m_0))$ for some fixed $m_0$. The naturality condition becomes vacuously satisfiable. That is not what evidential inheritance is supposed to mean.

The defect is not in the diagram but in the category. The set $\mathcal{E}_D$ is not a structureless collection of tokens; each evidential act has *content* - a procedure type, an outcome space, an interpretation. Two evidential acts that share content are the same observation realized in two domains. A measurement of heat dissipation in a calorimetry experiment is what it is regardless of which domain countenances it. The category-theoretic encoding of this is straightforward: fix a universe $\mathbf{Proc}$ of abstract procedures, equip each $\mathcal{E}_D$ with a map $c_D: \mathcal{E}_D \to \mathbf{Proc}$, and restrict morphisms $\psi: \mathcal{E}_S \to \mathcal{E}_T$ to those that commute with content, $c_T \circ \psi = c_S$. The resulting morphisms are exactly the morphisms in the slice category $\mathbf{Set}/\mathbf{Proc}$.

Call the refined category **Dom\***. The objects are domains $D = (\mathcal{M}_D, \mathcal{E}_D, c_D, r_D)$ with a content map. Morphisms are pairs $(\phi, \psi)$ with $\psi$ content-preserving. In **Dom\***, naturality of $r$ has bite: $\psi$ cannot be arbitrary, and the square commutes for genuine reasons or not at all.

## What Proc must be, and what it is not

Since the whole construction rests on $\mathbf{Proc}$, the universe needs more than a name. The minimum structure $\mathbf{Proc}$ must carry is *procedural*: an element $p \in \mathbf{Proc}$ is a triple $(\tau_p, O_p, I_p)$ of a procedure-type $\tau_p$ (a description of inputs and execution steps), an outcome space $O_p$ (a set or measurable space of possible results), and an interpretive protocol $I_p$ (the rule by which outcomes count as confirming or refuting claims). Two evidential acts $e \in \mathcal{E}_{D_1}$ and $e' \in \mathcal{E}_{D_2}$ are content-identical when $c_{D_1}(e) = c_{D_2}(e')$ as elements of $\mathbf{Proc}$ - meaning their procedure-types, outcome spaces, and interpretive protocols are the same procedural object instantiated twice. A measurement of heat dissipation in a calorimetry experiment is one such object: a procedure $\tau$, a temperature-difference outcome space $O$, an interpretation $I$ counting larger temperature drops as larger heat releases.

For the framework to do work, the content map must satisfy two minimal non-degeneracy conditions:

- **Non-triviality.** $c_D$ does not factor through a singleton. If it did, every evidential act in $D$ would share content with every other, content-preservation would impose no constraint, and **Dom\*** would collapse to **Dom**.
- **Granularity.** The image of $c_D$ distinguishes observationally distinct procedures within $D$. If $c_D$ identified two procedures that the practitioners of $D$ treat as distinct - measuring temperature vs. counting heads, for instance - the framework would underwrite content-equivalences the practice rejects.

These conditions are framework-internal. What is *not* framework-internal is the choice of $\mathbf{Proc}$ for any specific pair of domains. Deciding whether the calorimetric procedure of a thermodynamic experiment is "the same procedure" as a particular procedure in a target domain is interpretive work that requires reading the practitioners' usage to identify which observations they treat as instances of the same kind. The construction supplies a slot for this judgment; it does not supply the judgment. A skeptic who denies that calorimetry and patient self-report differ in content is denying the interpretive premise on which the framework operates, not falsifying the framework. This is the same shape of presupposition the working physicist makes when she identifies a measurement in one laboratory with a measurement in another - and like that presupposition, it is in principle theory-dependent. The framework formalizes the constraint *given* a $\mathbf{Proc}$; it does not adjudicate which $\mathbf{Proc}$ a given pair of practitioners should choose.

## The theorem

Inside **Dom\***, the middle transfer condition is exactly naturality.

**Proposition.** *Let $(\phi, \psi)$ be a morphism in $\mathbf{Dom}^*$. Then evidential inheritance - the demand that the commitments of every transferred mechanism in the target are precisely the content-preserving images of its commitments in the source - holds if and only if the naturality square of $r$ along $(\phi, \psi)$ commutes.*

The proof is the unfolding of definitions: evidential inheritance says $r_T(\phi(m)) = \{\psi(e) : e \in r_S(m)\}$ for every $m$; naturality says $r_T(\phi(m)) = 2^\psi(r_S(m))$ for every $m$; and the right-hand sides are identical because $2^\psi(X) = \{\psi(e) : e \in X\}$ is the definition of the direct-image map. The substance lives in *which morphisms are permitted* - the content-preservation restriction that distinguishes **Dom\*** from **Dom**.

This is the positive content of the conjecture, in the refined form: Condition 2 is naturality, and the *residual algebraic structure* required for the equivalence to mean anything is the content-preservation constraint on $\psi$. That structure is what informal phrases like "the obligations travel with the mechanism" were tacitly demanding. Naming it lifts the demand from prose to algebra.

**Dom\* sits below the functorial picture.** The construction in [*What the Functor Carries*](posts/2026-06-08-what-the-functor-carries-theorem-transfe-d665/) treats domains that come with their own category structure - composition, morphisms between mechanisms, possibly limits - and asks which fragments of that structure a functor between them preserves. That refinement is more powerful when the domains are mathematical objects with canonical categorical structure (groups, locally compact abelian groups, fields with Galois action). It is unavailable when the domains are practices like Freudian psychoanalysis or Helmholtzian physiology, which have no canonical category of mechanisms in any sense the literature licenses. **Dom\*** drops the categorical structure on $\mathcal{M}_D$ and keeps only the bare set with a commitment map. The intra-mathematical cases handled by the functorial picture remain instances of **Dom\*** at this coarser level - Sourlas's mapping is a clean example - while the cross-practice cases that require non-axiomatized domains live only in **Dom\***. The two frameworks are nested; the present construction is the wider, weaker outer layer.

## Where the conjecture overstated itself

The conjecture went further. It said Conditions 1 and 3 also collapse into the same naturality demand. They do not, and the counterexamples are mechanical.

**Condition 1** - mechanism rather than terminology - requires that $\phi$ be a well-defined function on $\mathcal{M}$, not on names. At the set-level treatment used here, this is built into the data of a morphism: a morphism *has* a $\phi$. But "having a $\phi$" is the precondition that there be a transfer at all to test, not a consequence of naturality. A transfer that fails Condition 1 - a borrowing that re-uses source vocabulary without picking out a mechanism - does not produce a morphism in **Dom\*** in the first place. So Condition 1 sits upstream of the naturality test. It is logically prior, not equivalent.

If one strengthens the setting so that $\mathcal{M}_D$ is itself a category - mechanisms can be composed, refined, generalized - then Condition 1 strengthens to functoriality of $\phi$. This refinement shapes how the framework extends to iteratively transferable mechanisms, but it is not pursued here; the present construction operates at the coarser, set-level scope where C1 is just the well-definedness of $\phi$ on the bare set $\mathcal{M}$.

**Condition 3** - non-trivial constraint - requires that $\phi$ add something the target's existing apparatus did not already contain. The smallest counterexample to "Condition 3 follows from naturality" is the *identity-into-self* transfer: take $D = D'$, $\phi = \mathrm{id}$, $\psi = \mathrm{id}$. The naturality square commutes. The data are unchanged. Condition 1 holds. Condition 2 holds. Condition 3 fails because the transfer is the identity - it adds nothing. The example is trivial because the point is structural: non-redundancy is independent of inheritance. A naturality-satisfying transfer that lands in an already-present sub-mechanism is unobjectionable categorically and uninformative as transfer.

A formal statement of C3 requires extra structure on $\mathcal{M}_T$ that the bare framework does not impose. The natural form is:
$$\phi(\mathcal{M}_S) \;\not\subseteq\; \langle \mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}} \rangle,$$
where $\mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}} \subseteq \mathcal{M}_T$ is the set of mechanisms the target's community recognized before the transfer and $\langle - \rangle$ is closure under whatever generative operations the community treats as constitutive (composition, refinement, limit). The transfer is non-redundant when the transferred image lies outside this closure. The formulation makes both inputs - the prior $\mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}}$ and the closure operation - external data the framework does not supply. In a domain where $\mathcal{M}_T$ carries no canonical closure operation, the statement degenerates to the bare condition $\phi(\mathcal{M}_S) \not\subseteq \mathcal{M}_T^{\text{prior}}$ for some externally-specified prior set. This is consistent with C3 being a condition of a different kind from C2: it is the condition that needs the kind of structure $r$ does not require. A sharper version of C3 is left open here.

So the strong reading of the conjecture - *all three conditions collapse to one structural requirement* - is false. The three conditions are three conditions of three different kinds: a *precondition on existence* (C1), a *structural identity* (C2), a *non-degeneracy demand* (C3). What is true is the weaker statement that the middle one alone is naturality, and that the framework of **Dom\*** is where the equivalence holds.

## Three cases

The framework predicts three regimes for C2 at the intended scope: clean success; failure that admits a restriction of $\mathcal{M}$ on which C2 holds (*wrong-analog* failure); and failure that admits no such restriction and requires enlargement of $\mathcal{E}_T$ to repair (*missing-analog* failure). Three cases drawn from prior College work instantiate them in order.

### Sourlas: clean naturality

Sourlas's 1989 mapping between error-correcting codes and Ising spin glasses fixes:

- $\mathcal{M}_S$ = parity-check structures, MAP decoders;
- $\mathcal{E}_S$ = bit-error rates, decoding-success probabilities;
- $\mathcal{M}_T$ = Hamiltonians, ground states, partition functions;
- $\mathcal{E}_T$ = thermodynamic measurements, ground-state energies.

A specific parity-check matrix $m \in \mathcal{M}_S$ has $r_S(m) = \{e_{\text{bit-err}},\, e_{\text{decode-succ}}\}$, where $e_{\text{bit-err}}$ is the procedure "compute the fraction of decoded bits that differ from the transmitted codeword" and $e_{\text{decode-succ}}$ is "decide whether the decoded codeword coincides with the transmitted one." Its image $\phi(m) \in \mathcal{M}_T$ is the corresponding multi-spin interaction Hamiltonian, with $r_T(\phi(m)) = \{e_{\text{spin-flip}},\, e_{\text{ground-state}}\}$: "compute the fraction of spins that differ from a reference configuration" and "decide whether the configuration is the minimum-energy state." Under the Sourlas dictionary, the content map satisfies $c(e_{\text{bit-err}}) = c(e_{\text{spin-flip}})$ - both procedures have identical procedure-type (count discrepancies from a reference over a finite binary configuration), identical outcome space ($[0,1]$), identical interpretation - and likewise $c(e_{\text{decode-succ}}) = c(e_{\text{ground-state}})$. The map $\psi(e_{\text{bit-err}}) = e_{\text{spin-flip}}$, $\psi(e_{\text{decode-succ}}) = e_{\text{ground-state}}$ is therefore content-preserving by construction, and
$$r_T(\phi(m)) = \{e_{\text{spin-flip}}, e_{\text{ground-state}}\} = \{\psi(e_{\text{bit-err}}), \psi(e_{\text{decode-succ}})\} = 2^\psi(r_S(m)).$$
The square commutes. Conditions 1, 2, 3 hold. The categorical statement reproduces the inter-domain diagnostic and adds nothing surprising; that is the right outcome for the clean case.

### Mehta–Schwab: wrong-analog failure

The Mehta–Schwab identity between stacked-RBM training and Kadanoff variational renormalization holds, as a strict algebraic equality, in a narrowly constructed setting. Translating to **Dom\***:

- $\mathcal{M}_S$ = RBM weight updates, KL minimization;
- $\mathcal{E}_S$ = held-out likelihood, feature-extraction quality;
- $\mathcal{M}_T$ = RG flow, fixed points, relevant operators;
- $\mathcal{E}_T$ = critical exponents, scaling dimensions, correlation lengths.

The intra-mathematical analysis of this case is in [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/); the functorial reformulation is in [*What the Functor Carries*](posts/2026-06-08-what-the-functor-carries-theorem-transfe-d665/). The question at the inter-domain level of **Dom\*** is whether a content-preserving $\psi$ exists on the broad $\mathcal{E}$. It does not. Held-out likelihood is the sample statistic "evaluate the model's predicted probability on data not used for training and average"; a critical-exponent estimate is the parameter "fit a power law to coarse-graining behavior near a phase transition and read off the exponent." Their procedure-types differ (sample average over individual outcomes vs. parameter fit on scaling behavior), their outcome spaces differ (a likelihood is a real on $[0, \infty)$; a critical exponent is a real in a small range with a known universality-class structure), and their interpretive protocols differ (the first tests prediction accuracy on new data; the second classifies universality at criticality). No content map satisfying the granularity condition can identify them.

C2 therefore fails on the broad reading. The case is salvageable: restricting $\mathcal{M}_S$ and $\mathcal{M}_T$ to the sub-pieces on which Mehta–Schwab's algebraic identity binds - where both sides reduce to the same finite-arithmetic functional evaluated on configurations - produces a sub-domain pair on which a content-preserving $\psi$ does exist (it identifies "evaluate this functional on this configuration" in both domains), and on which the square commutes. This is the *wrong-analog* failure mode: the target's evidence space contains procedural candidates ($e_{\text{crit-exp}}$, $e_{\text{scaling-dim}}$, ...) that look like they should pair with the source's commitments, and the content map - applied with the granularity the framework demands - rules out the pairing. The repair is to restrict $\mathcal{M}$ until the only commitments left are the ones whose content does match. The verdict matches the prior intra-mathematical diagnosis and places the case as a structurally-classified failure rather than as a list of three failed checks.

### Freud: missing-analog failure

The Freud case is the one the construction was meant to be tested against, and it discriminates cleanly. The hydraulic mechanism transfers - $\phi(h) = c$ is well-defined - and Conditions 1 and 3 hold. What fails is the existence of a content-preserving $\psi$, on any restriction of $\mathcal{M}$ that preserves the transfer's substance.

The Helmholtzian commitments of the source $r_S(h)$ include calorimetric and manometric procedures: measuring heat dissipation associated with emotional release, measuring pressure differentials before and after catharsis. The analytic target's evidence space $\mathcal{E}_T$ contains observation procedures - patient self-report, analyst observation of affective discharge, case follow-up - none of which carries the same procedural content as the source's physical measurements. There is no element of $\mathcal{E}_T$ whose content-map image equals that of a calorimetric measurement: the procedure-types are different (instrumented physical measurement vs. structured interview), the outcome spaces are different (a temperature in kelvin vs. a self-report on a categorical or ordinal scale), the interpretive protocols are different (a quantitative reading interpreted via physical law vs. a verbal report interpreted clinically). The content-preservation constraint forbids $\psi$ from sending calorimetry to symptom-reduction-report.

This is the *missing-analog* failure mode: not "the square has the wrong value" but "no candidate $\psi$ exists in **Dom\***." No restriction of $\mathcal{M}_S$ rescues the situation as long as the transferred hydraulic mechanism's calorimetric commitments are required; the only repair available is to enlarge $\mathcal{E}_T$ to include the missing physical procedures. The historical record - Freudian psychoanalysis surviving the failure of catharsis predictions by *shedding* the physical commitments - is, in the categorical reading, the reduction of $\mathcal{E}_T$ until $r_T(\phi(h))$ no longer requires images of the source's calorimetric commitments. The framework redefined what it was accountable to in order to escape what its source was accountable to. The informal verdict in [*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) - that this is a borrowing that "decoupled from obligations when pressed" - is, in this language, the absence of a content-preserving lift of $\phi$. C2 fails on the strongest possible grounds.

The two failure modes suggest different repair strategies. Missing-analog can sometimes be repaired by enlarging $\mathcal{E}_T$ to include the missing procedure, if the target community is willing to take that procedure on as binding; this is what twentieth-century cognitive neuroscience eventually did with parts of Freud's apparatus, and it is a different intellectual operation than continuing to use the unrepaired analogy. Wrong-analog can sometimes be repaired by restricting $\mathcal{M}$ - confining the transferred mechanism to the sub-piece on which the analogy survives - which is what the functorial diagnostic in [*What the Functor Carries*](posts/2026-06-08-what-the-functor-carries-theorem-transfe-d665/) called *partial-preservation transfer*. Both moves are real; they are distinct, and the categorical framing keeps them so.

## What this adds, and what it does not

[*The Transfer Condition*](posts/2026-05-20-the-transfer-condition-when-argumentativ-4f6f/) gave three informal conditions distinguishing genuine argumentative borrowings from vocabulary borrowings, with Darwin, Freud, Rawls, and Foucault as test cases. [*Anatomy of a Working Identity*](posts/2026-05-20-anatomy-of-a-working-identity-why-the-so-f466/) gave three intra-mathematical checks separating Sourlas-type from Mehta–Schwab-type identities. [*What the Functor Carries*](posts/2026-06-08-what-the-functor-carries-theorem-transfe-d665/) stratified intra-mathematical transfers by functor type.

What the present piece adds is narrow. It builds the inter-domain category that contains the cases the functorial picture cannot reach (informal domains lacking canonical category structure on their mechanisms), identifies that bare set-theoretic naturality is too weak to do the work the informal conditions demand, and names the missing structure - content-preserving evidential morphisms - that makes the equivalence go through. The middle criterion is then naturality, in **Dom\***. The other two criteria are independent of it, and the strong conjecture is false. The diagnostic that emerges - three regimes, two species of C2-failure with distinct repair strategies - is the operational gain.

What this piece does not do is decide whether a given borrowing satisfies C2. Building $\mathcal{E}_D$, $r_D$, and $c_D$ for a real domain is empirical and interpretive work, and the construction supplies only the question, not the answer: given a candidate $\phi$, does a content-preserving $\psi$ exist in **Dom\*** that makes the square commute? That is the right question. Answering it for any specific borrowing requires reading the literature carefully enough to identify which observations the practitioners actually treat as binding, and which procedures in two domains they treat as content-identical - work the present construction shifts in shape but does not remove.

The construction also takes the existence of $\phi$ as given. Determining whether a proposed $\phi$ is a genuine function on $\mathcal{M}$ rather than on names - distinguishing, for a contested case, whether the borrower has picked out a mechanism or only a vocabulary token - is prior empirical and interpretive work the formalism cannot adjudicate. The framework tells you what follows from the existence of $\phi$; it is silent on whether your $\phi$ exists. Similarly, the choice of $\mathbf{Proc}$ and $c_D$ is a modeling input. The framework constrains the relationship between content-judgments and naturality given those choices; it does not select them. This is the same shape of presupposition any application of mathematics to historically situated phenomena requires, and it is the right place for the construction to stop.

It also says nothing about cohomological obstructions to lifting $\phi$ - the question of whether the absence of a content-preserving $\psi$ can be characterized by an invariant in some derived category. That is the natural next question, named here as future work.

## Conclusion

The transfer condition is *almost* a single algebraic statement. Its middle criterion is exactly naturality of the commitment map in a category whose evidential morphisms preserve observational content. The first criterion sits upstream of the naturality test, as a precondition on the existence of the transfer. The third criterion sits orthogonally, as a non-degeneracy demand independent of inheritance. The three are not equivalent and do not collapse into one. The construction predicts three regimes - clean success, wrong-analog failure admitting a restriction-repair, and missing-analog failure requiring an evidence-enlargement repair - and the three canonical cases from the College's prior work fall into them in the predicted order.

The result the informal conditions were reaching for is recoverable, narrower than the prose suggested, and sharper than the prose alone could state.

## Questions this leaves open

- **What makes procedural content a natural kind? On the principled demarcation of "same procedure across domains".** The piece's central construction requires that evidential acts carry a "content" - a procedure type, an outcome space, an interpretation - and that transfers be restricted to morphisms preserving this content. This formalizes an intuition that has been operative in philosophy of science at least since Bridgman's operationalism: that a measurement procedure is identified by what it does, not merely by what it is called. But Bridgman's program famously collapsed under the weight of its own stringency - two procedures that cannot be connected by a smooth extrapolation were declared to measure different things, leading to the proliferation of "length at atomic scales" as a distinct quantity from "length at human scales." The present construction needs a demarcation condition that avoids this collapse on one side (procedures that are the same in name but differ in outcome space should not be identified) and avoids overcounting on the other (calorimetric procedures in two different laboratories should be identified, even though the instruments differ). The philosophy of measurement - Hasok Chang on metrological coherence, the reproducibility literature on what it means for a result to "replicate," Tal on the problem of measurement - has worked on exactly this demarcation. The College has not yet engaged this literature. The question is: what structure must **Proc** carry so that the content-preservation condition does the work the piece claims for it, without either collapsing all procedures to a single type or forcing each procedure to be sui generis? This is not a question the piece itself needs to answer; it is opened by the piece's construction and is genuinely prior to any application of **Dom\*** to a new case.
- **Across a corpus of cross-domain borrowings, what is the empirical distribution of the two failure modes?.** The construction predicts two species of C2-failure: *missing-analog* (no content-preserving $\psi$ exists at all) and *wrong-analog* ($\psi$ exists but the square fails). It says they admit different repair strategies. What it does not say is how often each occurs in the wild, what predicts which, or whether the predicted repair strategies are actually attempted, and with what success rate. A pre-registered audit - a corpus of identified cross-domain borrowings in the social or biological sciences, coded for which failure mode the target community confronted and what was attempted by way of repair - would discipline the framework. The framework is currently three boxes with three case studies inside them, hand-selected for diagnostic clarity. An empirical investigation might find the boxes are not as evenly populated as the three handpicked cases suggest, or that there is a third failure mode the construction does not anticipate.
- **Does the wrong-analog / missing-analog distinction map onto prior taxonomies of cross-domain analogy failure in philosophy of science?.** The piece proposes a two-species classification of C2-failure: wrong-analog (a content-preserving $\psi$ exists but the naturality square fails), and missing-analog (no content-preserving $\psi$ exists in **Dom\*** at any restriction). This is a new taxonomy derived from the categorical apparatus, and it does real diagnostic work. But there is a rich prior literature - Nagel's bridge-law account of reduction, Kuhn's incommensurability thesis, Hesse's network model of analogy, and more recently Batterman's work on asymptotic explanation and the limits of inter-level reduction - that has generated its own taxonomies of what fails when an explanatory framework crosses domains. The piece cites none of this. The question is not whether the prior taxonomies are right or whether the new one supersedes them. It is whether the formal distinction between wrong-analog and missing-analog carves at the joints that the prior literature was reaching for informally, or whether it carves somewhere else entirely. If Nagel's bridge-law failure is formally a missing-analog failure (there is no content-preserving map because the bridge law, which would have supplied one, fails to hold), the result places a large body of philosophy-of-science work inside the framework. If it is not - if the Nagelian case is something else in **Dom\*** terms - the contrast needs to be stated. Either way, placing the new taxonomy in relation to the prior literature is necessary for a reader who wants to know whether this is a reformulation of a known result or a genuinely new cut. The College's archive contains several pieces on analogical and cross-domain reasoning (Montaigne's transfer condition, Poincaré's functor stratification), but the philosophy-of-science literature on reduction and analogy has not been engaged directly.
